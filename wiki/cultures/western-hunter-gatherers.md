## Definition and Construct History

"Western (European) hunter-gatherers" (WHG) denotes a genetically defined population identified through ancient DNA analysis of pre-farming European foragers, distinguished from the genetically distinct eastern European hunter-gatherer (EHG) population associated with the steppe region further east. Reich reports the population emerged as a genetically distinct entity around 14,000 years ago, during the Bølling-Allerød warming, when a new hunter-gatherer group with ancestry linked to the Near East spread across Europe, displacing the preceding Magdalenian-associated population (as of Reich 2018). Genome-wide ancient DNA work (2015–2016, Reich's lab with Ron Pinhasi, using petrous-bone extraction and Matthias Meyer's enrichment method) showed that around 10,000 years ago West Eurasia held at least four highly differentiated populations — Fertile Crescent farmers, Iranian farmers, central/western European hunter-gatherers, and eastern European hunter-gatherers — as genetically differentiated from one another as living Europeans and East Asians are today.

## Genetic Signature

Ancient DNA from western European hunter-gatherers roughly 8,000 years ago shows they had blue eyes but dark skin and dark hair, a combination rare in living populations today (attributed, Reich 2018). This contrasts with the light-skinned, dark-haired, brown-eyed profile Reich attributes to the migrating Anatolian farmers who followed them (see [[early-european-farmers]]); Reich states that light skin in Europe largely derives from these later farmer populations, not from the earlier hunter-gatherers. Reich notes that had the roughly four differentiated West Eurasian populations present 10,000 years ago been classified using race-thinking, they would have been categorized as separate "races," despite none surviving in unmixed form today — a point made to illustrate the instability of racial categories, not to endorse them.

## Distribution and Chronology

Western hunter-gatherers occupied central and western Europe from around 14,000 years ago. Their descendants persisted as a distinct population until roughly 8,800–4,500 years ago, when the first Anatolian-derived farmers arrived in regions including Germany, Spain, Hungary, and Anatolia itself, retaining at least 90 percent ancestry from the immigrant Anatolian source with minimal admixture from local hunter-gatherers (as of Reich 2018). Only after a roughly two-thousand-year delay — by 6,000–4,500 years ago — do later European farmer populations show an additional roughly 20 percent hunter-gatherer ancestry, indicating substantial mixing began well after initial farmer arrival. The Funnel Beaker culture, arising in a belt of land near the Baltic Sea after roughly 6,300 years ago, is `associated_with` northern hunter-gatherer populations that adopted farming gradually over more than a thousand years while retaining hunter-gathering elements; archaeologist Colin Renfrew has suggested (a position, not settled fact) that associated megalith-building may reflect a boundary-marking practice between southern farmers and hunter-gatherers-turned-farmers. By 6,000–5,000 years ago, most of this northern gene pool had been overtaken by farmer-related ancestry.

## Lifeways

As evidenced genetically and via associated archaeological contexts, western hunter-gatherers were foraging populations occupying central and western Europe in the millennia following the Bølling-Allerød warming, prior to and initially alongside the spread of farming. No dedicated material-culture assemblage distinct from the broader Mesolithic European archaeological record is treated on this page; lifeways detail belongs on the relevant Mesolithic period pages.

## Relations and Successions

Western hunter-gatherers are `succeeded_by` [[early-european-farmers]] as the demographically dominant ancestry component in most of Europe, though WHG ancestry persisted as a minority contributor after roughly 6,000 years ago. Joachim Burger's 2009 mitochondrial DNA study found early European farmers carried mitochondrial types largely distinct from preceding hunter-gatherers, similar instead to present-day southern Europe and the Near East — indicating farmers did not descend substantially from local hunter-gatherers, contrary to Luigi Luca Cavalli-Sforza's earlier "demic diffusion" model, which is `challenges`-related to this finding (see [[demic-diffusion]]). Eastern hunter-gatherers (EHG), a related but genetically distinct steppe-region population, later contributed roughly half the ancestry of the [[yamnaya-culture]] in combination with Armenian/Iranian-related southern populations (as of Reich 2018).

## Identity Cautions

"Western hunter-gatherer" is a genetically defined population category constructed from ancient DNA comparison, not an archaeological culture with a bounded artifact typology, nor a linguistic community, nor a self-conscious people. The category should not be conflated with any modern national or ethnic population despite the shared geography; WHG ancestry today survives only as one component within admixed present-day European genomes, nowhere in unmixed form.

## Historiography

The WHG/EHG distinction and the four-population West Eurasian model of ~10,000 years ago emerged from Reich-lab ancient DNA work in the mid-2010s (Mathieson, Lazaridis, Pinhasi and colleagues), superseding earlier blood-group-based clinal models (Cavalli-Sforza's "demic diffusion") that Reich states were "intellectually attractive" but wrong: John Novembre and colleagues showed from 2008 that clinal genetic gradients like those observed in Europe can arise from the mathematics of clustering techniques without migration. See [[reich-who-we-are-2018]] for the full account.
