# Qijia Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The Qijia culture was first identified by J. G. Andersson at Qijiaping, Guanghe County, Gansu, in
the 1920s, while he was searching for the Western origins of Yangshao painted pottery (as of Liu &
Chen 2012, quoting the source). Andersson initially proposed Qijia as an indigenous precursor
culture *preceding* Yangshao in the far west, implying a remote Western origin for Yangshao
pottery — a claim Xia Nai's 1946 stratigraphic fieldwork (Academia Sinica expedition) overturned,
demonstrating Qijia was in fact *later* than Yangshao. This refutation is documented on
[[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]] as an episode in the Western-origin-vs-multiregional
debate over early Chinese civilization.

As currently understood, Qijia is a Bronze Age culture dated ca. 2200–1600 BCE, distributed over
roughly 700 km east–west by 600 km north–south across western Shaanxi, Gansu, Ningxia, eastern
Qinghai, and central-southern Inner Mongolia. More than 1,000 sites have been identified and about
20 excavated (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material.** Qijia yielded China's earliest known remains of domesticated equines, often found
together with sheep and cattle as grave offerings (Flad et al. 2007, as cited in Liu & Chen 2012).
More than 100 copper/bronze objects have been recovered from at least 10 Qijia sites — mostly pure
copper early on, with lead-bronze, tin-bronze, lead-tin-bronze, and arsenical-copper alloys also
present, produced by both hammering and casting. Despite widespread metal use, metal objects were
*not* the primary prestige marker in Qijia mortuary ritual; jade/stone ritual objects (bi discs,
cong tubes, huan rings) and pottery served that role — Huangniangniangtai alone yielded 260
jade/stone ritual objects across a third of 62 excavated burials, with one tomb (M48: an adult male
flanked by two females) containing 83 stone bi discs, a jade pendant, 304 jade/stone waster pieces,
and 10 pottery vessels. Qijia jade forms (cong, huan, segmental bi) are argued by some scholars to
derive from Longshan-culture prototypes in southern Shanxi, indicating east-to-west cultural
influence (attributed; Liu & Chen 2012, citing multiple site comparisons).

**Linguistic.** No linguistic evidence is reported; `language_affiliation: [[unknown]]`.

**Genetic.** Recent mtDNA analysis (Gao 2009) found the Qijia population had close genetic
affinity with the Tianshanbeilu population in eastern Xinjiang (as of Liu & Chen 2012). This is an
`associated_with` relationship between two archaeological-culture-linked skeletal samples — it
does not equate the two cultures, their languages, or any named people. Separately, skeletal
analysis of 291 burials at Liuwan, Ledu, Qinghai (ca. 2500–1900 BCE) shows continuity in physical
type from the preceding Banshan/Machang populations through Qijia — "racially Mongoloid, most
similar to East Asian types" (Pan & Han 1998, as cited in Liu & Chen 2012) — evidence favoring local
biological continuity over wholesale population replacement at this site.

## Distribution and Chronology

Settlement survey in the Hulu River region documents a 370% rise in site count (80 to 376) from
the preceding Lower Changshan culture (ca. 2800–2200 BCE) to Qijia, while average site size fell
61% (10 to 3.9 ha) and deposit thickness fell 38% — a pattern read as reflecting demographic and
settlement-organization change accompanying the culture's spread. Qijia's floruit coincided with a
cold-dry climatic period dated 4300–3700 cal BP (Wu & Liu 2004, as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Lifeways

Mixed farming-pastoral economy with domesticated sheep, cattle, and (earliest in China) equines;
metallurgy using both hammering and casting; jade/stone ritual production and consumption
concentrated at sites like Huangniangniangtai. Liu & Chen note that if suttee (companion burial)
did occur at Liuwan, the physical-type continuity data make cultural adaptation, not steppe
population migration, the more likely explanation (attributed interpretation).

## Relations and Successions

`associated_with` Tianshanbeilu (mtDNA affinity, not identity). No material has been found west of
Xinjiang in the steppe region proper, so despite arguments for two-way interaction (Li 2003; Mei
2003), Liu & Chen conclude "the cultural influence from the steppe to Qijia appears to have been
dominant" (attributed interpretive conclusion, as of Liu & Chen 2012). Qijia-style ceramics and
burial customs appear at [[zhukaigou-culture|Zhukaigou]] in the Ordos (Phases III–IV), generally
interpreted as reflecting migration of Qijia population into that region (Linduff 1995; Neimenggu
and Ordos 2000) — see Zhukaigou's Relations section. Siba culture pottery in the Hexi corridor
shows Qijia influence alongside Machang-variant Majiayao ancestry; compositional analysis links
Zhukaigou's earliest metal objects to Qijia metal-forming traditions, leading Liu & Chen to suggest
Qijia people "may have brought the metallurgy techniques into the Ordos" (attributed).

**Bronze-metallurgy origins debate.** A long-standing dispute among Chinese archaeologists
concerns whether Qijia (and by extension early Chinese) bronze metallurgy arose through
independent/indigenous innovation (Sun & Han 1997; Zhang 1987) or through diffusion from the West/
Eurasian steppe (An 1993b; elaborated by Fitzgerald-Huber 1995 and Mei 2003 via typological
resemblance to Okunev, Minusinsk/Altai, and Seima-Turbino metal forms). Both positions are recorded
as live scholarly positions, not adjudicated here; see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]
for the broader civilizational-origins framing this dispute sits within.

## Identity Cautions

Qijia is an etic archaeological-culture construct defined by ceramic, metal, and mortuary-ritual
traits; it is not equated in this source with any named ethnolinguistic group. The mtDNA affinity
with Tianshanbeilu is a population-genetic association between skeletal samples, explicitly not an
equation of cultures, languages, or peoples. Technology and domesticate transmission from western
Eurasia (bronze/copper metallurgy, sheep/goat, cattle, horse) documented for Qijia is a
**technology/domesticate transmission**, evidenced materially and via mtDNA on specific skeletal
samples — it must not be read as identifying a single migrating population or language group (as
of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Historiography

Discovered by Andersson in the 1920s within a since-refuted "Western origins of Chinese
civilization" framework; its chronological position was corrected by Xia Nai's 1946 fieldwork, an
episode treated in Chinese archaeological historiography as foundational to the discipline's
methodological maturation (see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]). Liu & Chen (2012)
synthesize subsequent survey, metallurgical-compositional, and mtDNA work.
