## Definition and Construct History

Gallinazo (also Virú, in Larco Hoyle’s older nomenclature) names the north-coast Peruvian ceramic and settlement tradition that, as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]], marks "the emergence of a new cultural order that eventually gave rise to the Moche." Rafael Larco Hoyle’s 1940s mortuary-pottery sequence placed Cupisnique → Transitory Cupisnique → Salinar → Virú/Gallinazo → Mochica/Moche (five phases). Moseley stresses that singular corporate styles form only after the corporate bodies they serve exist; therefore Gallinazo developments must be examined to understand Moche origins, and the two cultures **partially overlapped in time**.

The label is an etic archaeological-culture construct (ceramics, adobe architecture, settlement hierarchy), not a proven ethnic identity or unitary state.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material (as of Moseley 2001):**

- **Ceramics:** utilitarian wares evolved from Salinar (oxidizing fire); elite wares used **negative/resist** painting (carbon smudge after wax shielding) — a borrowed technique producing a coherent elite style. Broader distribution of elite wares from Chicama to Santa is read as shared aesthetic tastes if not marriage/political alliances among governing gentry; also present in Lambayeque.
- **Architecture:** commoners relegated to cane walls on stone footings; **adobe** reserved for kuraka quarters and corporate centers. Bricks initially handmade, later mass-produced mold-made adobes (four-sided cane molds). **Segmented construction** — vertical seams separating slabs of mortared bricks not bonded to contiguous slabs — is interpreted as mit’a-like modular labor organization with Preceramic mesh-bag (*shicra*) antecedents.
- **Monuments:** vast terraced platforms perched on hills (Licapa in Chicama; Cerro Orejas and near Cerro Blanco in Moche — latter later obscured by Moche capital). Peak-top "Castillos" of Santa and Tomaval (Virú); Santa Castillo as primary political center with ~70-ha residential area, >3,000 people estimated.
- **Gallinazo Group (Virú type site):** numerous collapsed adobe mounds over 4–5 sq. km; standing structures estimated ~30,000 rooms — no comparable Gallinazo center elsewhere (as of Moseley 2001); scholars propose a regional political center of a loosely confederated realm.
- **Emblem continuity:** distinctive club with pike-like end and pointed-disk mace head appears on a Gallinazo reed-boat warrior vessel and becomes a major Moche emblem.

**Linguistic / genetic:** none in Moseley 2001.

## Distribution and Chronology

North coast of Peru: better studied in Río Virú and southern drainages; distribution of elite wares Chicama–Santa; presence in Lambayeque. By the first century BC, Moseley reports dramatic population growth sustained by large valley-neck canals and lower-valley reclamation; agriculture expanded to limits in smaller basins; prehistoric population thought to have peaked in Virú and Santa. Hierarchical settlement — many farmsteads/hamlets → fewer secondary/tertiary centers → single primary center of exceptional dimensions — is thought to reflect valley-wide systems based at local primary centers.

Dry times in the first centuries AD: sierra people encroached on canyon lands held by Gallinazo; coastal alliances may have been fostered by drought. More centralized governance arose late Gallinazo as drought waned: Cerro Blanco population gained control over Río Moche then political integration with larger Chicama; linkages forged less by conquest than by coalition through marriage and kin alliances among local kuraka who adopted the new corporate style **Moche**. Continuities in settlement, monumental architecture, molded adobes, extended burials, and utilitarian ceramics make late Gallinazo and early Moche "basically indistinguishable" except for corporate art and ideology (as of Moseley 2001).

## Lifeways

Canal-based farming at valley necks and lower valleys; adobe corporate architecture and elite metallurgy prerogatives already foreshadowed in Salinar (Cerro Arena; Virú elite headquarters with copper slag). Moseley’s valley-size model of coastal state consolidation: first act of consolidation played out earliest in small Casma (Sechín Alto), then larger Virú (Gallinazo), then moderate Moche drainage (Moche, later Chimor); failure to march north into Lambayeque suggests great valley size fostered internal fragmentation.

## Relations and Successions

- Succeeds Salinar / late Early Horizon north-coast orders; builds on Cupisnique-era irrigation landscape.
- Gives rise to / overlaps early [[moche-culture]] — Gallinazo as corporate predecessor; Moche as new elite ideology/art package adopted by kuraka coalitions.
- Dry-period sierra encroachment and coastal confederation as concurrent political ecology (attributed).

## Identity Cautions

Gallinazo ≠ Moche ≠ a single state ≠ a language. Negative-resist elite ware distribution is not automatically political unification. "Gallinazo Group" as regional capital is a scholarly proposal from site scale, not a demonstrated empire. Partial chronological overlap with Moche means phase boundaries are style-ideological, not clean ethnic replacements.

## Historiography

Larco Hoyle’s ceramic succession still structures north-coast narratives; Virú Project–era work underpins the Gallinazo Group estimates Moseley cites. Moseley’s 2001 synthesis places Gallinazo at the center of Moche origin arguments (corporate body before corporate style). Collection coverage: Moseley 2001 only so far; primary Virú Project monographs not yet ingested.
