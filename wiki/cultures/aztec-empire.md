# Aztec Empire

## Definition and Construct History

The **Aztec** (Mexica-led Triple Alliance) imperial polity of central Mexico ended with the Spanish conquest of Tenochtitlán. As presented by [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski (2007)]], Aztecs were among the last northern/western invaders of central Mexico; legends (Spanish-recorded) cast them as rag-tag foragers/primitive agriculturalists forced into lake swamps; allies of Tepanec Atzcapotzalco then turned on them ~AD 1427, bringing most of central Mexico under control by savage warfare; garrisons from Pacific to Gulf and conquests to the Guatemalan border. “Aztec” is an external/modern cover term—not identical to all central Mexican peoples or to every tributary subject.

In Easter week 1519 Cortés landed on the Veracruz coast and began the campaign ending in crushing defeat of indigenous Aztec civilization. Old and New Worlds had had so little prior contact they differed physically and linguistically and were unaware of each other—yet Cortés encountered cities, markets, irrigation, slavery, temples, pyramids, roads, pottery, gold, textiles he could understand in terms of urban Spanish experience.

Despite profound ideological differences (Spanish abhorrence of Aztec death/human-sacrifice preoccupations; Aztec incomprehension of aspects of Roman Catholicism), Spanish and Aztecs were—W&O argue—fundamentally alike culturally: hierarchically organized, class-structured, expansionist empires with state religions and economies of intensive agriculture and specialized craft production.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material / urban (as of Wenke & Olszewski 2007).** Capital Tenochtitlán: massive pyramid/court complex (now under Mexico City); island location best understood via central role in redistributive networks. AD 1519 population estimates 200,000–300,000 (5× London then); Valley of Mexico late Aztec population 1–2 million. Empire nearly “invisible” outside the capital without roads, administrative buildings, or walls (Hodge via W&O)—contrast with [[inka-empire|Inka]] road systems.

**Agriculture.** Between ~AD 1300 and 1520 large-scale drainage of Valley of Mexico into productive plots; **chinampas** (layered weeds, mud, feces, garbage; initially floating mats per documents) yielded up to four crops/year of maize, beans, squash, tomatoes. Michael Smith: hierarchical marketing system with intense local specialization.

**Social organization.** Divine king; nobles (*pillitin*) of royal house; commoners in *calpulli* clans (multiple lineages, shared village/ward, war units, common land, tax units, shrines)—*calpule* leader as imperial-people link; *pochteca* professional merchants; landless peasants/slaves at bottom (field labor, menial tasks, mass sacrifice).

**Religion and sacrifice.** Theology of successive creations requiring constant effort including human blood/heart sacrifice to forestall sun’s extinction; Spanish may exaggerate numbers but tens–hundreds of thousands annually seem inescapable and widely supported (as of W&O 2007). Michael Harner’s cannibalism-as-protein thesis noted as untestable/insufficiently evidenced given rich maize-beans diet; Europeans had a political agenda in depicting savagery; human sacrifice nonetheless important across early complex cultures. Later Aztecs believed [[teotihuacan]]’s pyramids had been built by giants and that gods were buried beneath them.

**Linguistic / genetic.** Nahuatl imperial language ≠ multiethnic subject mosaic; four-way non-identity applies.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Postclassic background.** After ~AD 900 Toltecs dominated Valley of Mexico (Aztec legends claim Toltec descent and NW origin, possibly Chichimeca involvement); major center Tula with twin stone pyramids, ball court, Quetzalcóatl temple, ~4.6 m warrior statues; trade/military outposts to northern/western Mexico and exports to Arizona/New Mexico; interaction with Chichén Itzá; Tula destroyed ~AD 1156 by northern invaders; later Tarascan state; League of Mayapán AD 1250–1519 in Maya zone.

**Imperial peak.** From ~1427 central Mexican control; Pacific-to-Gulf garrisons; Guatemalan-border conquests. Capital-focused tribute empire rather than road-and-wall landscape signature.

**Conquest and demographic collapse.** Cortés left Cuba 1519 with ships, men, arms, horses; advantages of non-Aztec diplomacy, horses, cannons, war dogs, esprit de corps; Moctezuma initially welcomed Spanish under delusion they were returning gods, then kidnapped/jailed; alliances with non-Aztec peoples; Moctezuma and many killed at Tenochtitlán; last Aztec king hanged 1524. Heartland population from >1 million to probably <70,000 within 150 years from war, smallpox and other European diseases, slavery, and related plagues. Spanish military technology, diplomacy with non-Aztec groups, and introduced diseases effectively ended indigenous Mesoamerican civilizational development; New World peoples lacked iron tools/weapons and large domesticated draft/transport animals fundamental to Old World imperialism (metals used in ornaments; llamas in South America only).

## Lifeways

Intensive chinampa and drained-field agriculture; hierarchical markets; militarized expansion and tribute; *calpulli*-based commoner organization; *pochteca* long-distance merchants; state religion of solar maintenance through sacrifice; class stratification from divine king to slaves. Craft production highly specialized and integrated (textbook structural likeness to Spanish empire).

## Relations and Successions

- **Successor in a long Mesoamerican [[complex-society|complex-society]] sequence:** [[olmec-culture]], Formative Oaxaca, [[teotihuacan]], [[classic-maya]], Toltec Tula, then Aztec.
- **Compared with:** Spanish empire (structural likeness despite ideology); [[inka-empire]] (different imperial landscape signature; both contact-period New World empires); Old World primary states in textbook “single developmental pattern” conclusion for Mesoamerica vs Mesopotamia despite ideological difference from Spanish/Western traditions.
- **Ch.13 conclusion (W&O position):** Olmec, Teotihuacán, Oaxacan, Maya, Aztec locations explained by exceptional productivity under simple farming + maize-beans-squash; irrigation, circumscription, interregional exchange as stimuli; striking parallel to Mesopotamia as single developmental pattern.

## Identity Cautions

- **Not a single ethnic people.** Empire incorporated multiethnic subjects; “Aztec” is a cover term; Mexica of Tenochtitlán are one core among Triple Alliance partners and tributaries.
- **Four-way non-identity.** Nahuatl ≠ all archaeological central Mexican material ≠ genetic population ≠ every tributary polity’s self-identification.
- **Contact-period sources** are colonial as well as Indigenous—Spanish political agenda in depicting “savagery” is explicitly flagged in the textbook.
- **Sacrifice numbers** are contested in scale; textbook treats large-scale sacrifice as real while cautioning exaggeration and rejecting Harner protein thesis as under-evidenced.
- **Moctezuma “returning gods”** motif is Spanish-period narrative as presented—treat as attributed ethnohistory, not transparent indigenous psychology.

## Historiography

Textbook Mesoamerica chapter Postclassic/conquest sections (as of Wenke & Olszewski 2007), combining Spanish chronicles, archaeological Valley of Mexico settlement/chinampa research (Smith marketing model; Hodge “invisible empire”), and comparative civilization framing. Conquest historiography and specialist urban archaeology of Tenochtitlán outstanding for densification; demographic collapse estimates are mid-2000s textbook figures.

### Adams — terminal conquest state (1966, attributed)

In *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]*, the Aztec realm is the **terminal trade-and-tribute conquest state** of the central Mexican sequence, homotaxially paired with the Akkadian realm ([[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]]; [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]]). Adams stresses: *calpulli* labor/tribute models extended to the emergent state; steps to kingship (Itzcoatl land grants, succession changes, book-burning, Moctezuma purge of nobles); *pochteca* as war pretexts, spies, and royal protégés; palace redistributive power; Triple Alliance fragility and thin political controls beyond the core; "empire" an unsuitable label for both Akkadian and Aztec patterns. Stratification: *pilli* / *mayeque* / slaves; conquest estates; corporate kin still viable but encapsulated in class structure. Militarism bound to trade-and-tribute (Chapman via Adams); sacrificial elaboration and royal war insistence densified as ethnohistoric reading as of 1966 — not wiki-voice demography.


### Trigger — city-state system + tributary hegemony (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|UEC]]*) places the Valley of Mexico / Aztecs among his seven early civilizations as a **[[city-state]] system** with hegemonic tributary overlay — **not** a [[territorial-state]]. Highest agricultural intensity ranking in his sample (chinampas); warrior values and large-scale sacrifice included in the cross-case religious package (mutual human–deity dependence via sacrifice). Date-stamped ~2001 secondary synthesis. Study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]; comparison: [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]].
