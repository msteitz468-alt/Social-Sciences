# Botai Culture

## Definition and Construct History

The **Botai culture** (with related **Tersek** west of the Ishim) is an etic construct for a short-lived, geographically restricted settlement system of northern Kazakhstan (~**3700–3000 BCE**) defined by extreme horse-bone dominance and large house-pit settlements. V. Zaibert excavated the type site. [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]] (*[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]*, 2007) characterizes Botai as “a settlement of specialized hunters who **rode horses to hunt horses**” — a peculiar economy that, he argues, existed only in this window and region. The construct is central to [[horse-domestication]] and [[bit-wear-analysis]] debates; it is not a self-identified people and is **not** Anthony’s PIE homeland population.

## Material / Linguistic / Genetic Signature

**Material / faunal:** Botai — **>150 house-pits**, ~300,000 animal bones, **99.9% horse**; other species sparse (large bovid, elk, deer, boar, bear, beaver, saiga, gazelle). Tersek Kozhai 1: horses **66.1%** of ~70,000 bones. Lithic/settlement shift from pre-3700 Atbasar (small ephemeral camps, diverse game, microliths) to large settlements, tens–hundreds of thousands of bones, bifacial blades, polished stone weights (Anthony: rawhide rope manufacture).

**Bit wear (Anthony & Brown):** Of 42 Botai P2s examined, 19 measurable; **5** with significant bevels (3, 3, 3.5, 4, 6 mm) representing ≥3 horses, found in different places (not one intrusive pit); 12% of full sample / 26% of measurable P2s — too high for rare natural malocclusion; polished facets like soft bits. Kozhai 1: 2 of 12 P2s with 3 mm facets. Anthony: “reasonably certain that horses were bitted and ridden in northern Kazakhstan beginning about **3700–3500 BCE**.”

**Linguistic:** Not assigned to IE. Anthony’s Ural River frontier separates Pontic-Caspian herding from eastern foragers for ~2,000 years; Botai sits east of that cultural border.

**Genetic:** Not treated in Anthony 2007.

## Distribution and Chronology

Restricted to the **northern Kazakhstan steppes**, ~3700–3000 BCE only. When [[afanasievo-culture|Afanasievo]] groups crossed the Ural toward the Altai (~3700–3400 BCE), intervening north Kazakh steppe was largely unsettled by those migrants as indigenous Botai-Tersek emerged; only three intermediate kurgan cemeteries (Ubagan I, Verkhnaya Alabuga, Karagash) mark the path.

## Lifeways (as evidenced)

Anthony’s model: **mounted horse hunting** of predominantly wild herds, with a minority of bitted/ridden animals. Sandra Olsen’s arguments (reported by Anthony): whole carcasses regularly butchered in settlement over centuries — hard for pedestrians to drag ~800-lb carcasses repeatedly; site on open ridge slope (not a natural trap); house-pit excavation 32 dung-rich soil “must have been the result of redeposition of material from stabling layers” — implies corralling. 1:1 sex ratio and all ages including fetuses (used by opponents as mass wild-herd kills) Anthony reinterprets as requiring **herd drives** that capture both stallion-harem and bachelor bands living far apart — “impossible on foot.” No sheep; few/no cattle at Botai — mounted **foragers**, not pastoralists like western neighbors.

## Relations and Successions

- `preceded_by` Atbasar-type foragers who hunted wild horses, deer, aurochs without stockbreeding.
- `associated_with` western Pontic-Caspian herders: Anthony argues Botai likely **acquired** domesticated-management ideas (and riding) from western neighbors who had cattle, sheep, and probably horses for ~1000 years before 3700–3500 BCE; riding “probably did not begin in northern Kazakhstan” but in the [[steppe-hypothesis-debate|PIE homeland region]] (Pontic-Caspian), possibly before 4200 BCE.
- Contemporaneous with Final Eneolithic steppe cultures west of the Ural ([[sredni-stog-culture|late Sredni Stog]], Repin, late [[khvalynsk-culture|Khvalynsk]], early [[maikop-culture|Maikop]] contacts).
- Not ancestral to [[yamnaya-culture|Yamnaya]] or Andronovo in Anthony’s narrative; different economic and linguistic path.

## Identity Cautions

- Culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people. Botai material culture is **not** equated with Indo-European speech.
- Scholarly split (Anthony 2007): **for** domesticated/ridden horses — Zaibert, Makarova, Sandra Olsen; **against** (all wild) — Ermolova, Levine, Benecke & von den Dreisch (narrow size variability; 1:1 sex ratio; lack of riding pathologies on a small vertebral sample). Anthony allows both sides partly right if **75–90%** of bones are wild hunted horses and a minority were ridden.
- Do not import the retracted [[dereivka]] bit-wear claim into Botai; Botai bevels are a separate sample and context.
- Post-2007 genetic and lipid/residue work on Botai horses may revise or refine Anthony’s 2007 picture — date-stamp claims.
