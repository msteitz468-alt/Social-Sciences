## Definition and Construct History

"Assyrians" designates the population and state centered on the city of Assur on the
Tigris, and later on Kalhu (Nimrud) and Nineveh, whose kings ruled an expanding
Mesopotamian and eventually Near Eastern empire from the Middle Assyrian period
(mid-2nd millennium BCE) through the empire's collapse in 612–609 BCE. Per
[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], the political entity is well documented through
royal inscriptions, administrative archives, and the palace-relief corpus, but "Assyrian"
as an ethnic or genetic category is far less secure than as a political/linguistic one —
the population was, especially from the 9th century BCE onward, substantially built up
through mass deportation of conquered peoples (see below), making any simple
Assyrian-people = Assyrian-genetic-population equation unwarranted. The label covers a
continuous political and religious tradition (the cult of the god Ashur, dynastic
continuity at Assur) more securely than it covers a stable ethnic community.

## Material / Linguistic / Textual Signature

- **Linguistic**: Assyrian is a dialect of Akkadian, written in cuneiform; from the 8th
  century BCE, Aramaic (written in the Aramaic alphabet on perishable media) increasingly
  displaced Akkadian in everyday and even some administrative use, driven partly by the
  mass deportation of Aramaic-speaking populations into the Assyrian heartland (Roux;
  attributed).
- **Material**: royal capitals at [[assur]], [[nimrud]], [[nineveh]] (and, briefly,
  Dûr-Sharrukîn/Khorsabad under Sargon II); the defining Assyrian visual signature is the
  orthostat palace relief (see Art, below), plus lamassu gate-guardian sculpture, glazed
  brick, and the Nimrud ivories.
- **Textual**: an exceptionally dense royal-inscription and administrative-archive record,
  cross-checkable in places against the independent Babylonian Chronicle — Roux flags
  several instances where Assyrian royal inscriptions and the Chronicle disagree (see
  Historiography, below); royal annals are explicitly "not really historical but
  propaganda and votive texts" in Roux's own methodological framing, a caution the wiki
  extends to all Assyrian royal-inscription material used as an evidence class.

## Distribution and Chronology

**Middle Assyrian period.** Ashur-uballit I (1365–1330 BCE) freed Assyria from Mitannian
vassalage and took the titles "Great King" and "King of the Universe," corresponding as an
equal with Egypt's Amenophis IV. Successors (Enlil-nirâri, Arik-dên-ilu, Adad-narâri I,
Shalmaneser I [1274–1245 BCE], Tukulti-Ninurta I
[1244–1208 BCE]) extended Assyrian power across the Jazirah, into the former Mitannian
sphere, and briefly over Babylon itself — Tukulti-Ninurta I captured the Kassite king
Kashtiliash IV and briefly ruled Babylon through puppet princes before being assassinated
by his own son. Tiglathpileser I (1115–1077 BCE) campaigned as far as
the Mediterranean and fought the Ahlamû-Aramaeans "twenty-eight times"; his death (by
murder, per Roux) opened a roughly 166-year "dark age" of Assyrian decline (1077–911
BCE) under Aramaean territorial pressure.

**The revival and the early empire.** Roux frames Adad-nirâri II (911–891 BCE) as the
pivotal figure: "Assyria awoke in 911 B.C." He expelled Aramaeans from the Tigris valley
and Tûr 'Abdin, recovered eastern Jazirah territory, and secured roughly 80 years of peace
with Babylonia by treaty. Roux explicitly argues the resulting empire was **not a planned
enterprise** but accreted from interwoven motives — defense of the Tigris core and trade
routes, the economic profitability of raiding after the ~1200 BCE Near Eastern collapse,
royal ambition, and a religious ideology in which the god Ashur's enemies were the king's
enemies (attributed interpretive synthesis, not settled consensus).

Ashurnasirpal II (883–859 BCE) campaigned widely — suppressing an
Aramaean revolt on the Khabur, conquering the Aramaean kingdom of Bît-Adini, and reaching
the Mediterranean in 877 BCE via Karkemish, though Roux notes this triumphal march was in
practice "another razzia," not a conquest, since the Neo-Hittite and Aramaean princes were
caught by surprise and offered no resistance. He founded a new capital at Kalhu
([[nimrud]]). His inscriptions record punishments of flaying, impalement, immurement, and
burning reserved chiefly for rebels and nobles; Roux, while quoting this material at
length, adds his own comparative judgment that "the Assyrians never indulged in systematic
genocides," contrasting Ashurnasirpal's targeted cruelty with the Israelites' reported
"purely ethno-cultural" destruction of the Amalekites. **This comparative claim is Roux's
own contestable historiographic judgment and is recorded here as an attributed position,
not as wiki-voice fact** — it rests on reading royal-inscription rhetoric against a
separate biblical tradition, and a specialist might weigh the comparison differently.

Shalmaneser III (858–824 BCE) devoted 31 of 35 regnal years to war,
including the inconclusive Battle of Qarqar (853 BCE) against a large western coalition
(Damascus, Hama, and Arab and Phoenician contingents) and repeated failed attempts to take
Damascus; he received tribute from Jehu of Israel (Black Obelisk), the first biblical
figure named in cuneiform. His reign ended in a four-year civil war; Roux notes "the
empire had yet to be conquered" even at this stage, and a roughly century-long "Assyrian
Eclipse" followed (weak kings 781–745 BCE), during which the rival kingdom of Urartu rose
to prominence.

**The Neo-Assyrian empire proper.** Tiglathpileser III (744–727
BCE) is called by Roux "founder of the Assyrian empire" (his own historiographic
judgment). From 738 BCE he restructured conquered territory into provinces under a
governor (shaknu) or district lord (bêl pihâti), added a permanent standing army (kisir
sharruti) alongside the older conscription levy, and made mass deportation systematic
policy: reported figures include 30,000 Syrians and 18,000 Aramaeans deported in 742–741
BCE alone, and Tiglathpileser's own campaigns displaced tens of thousands more. Roux's own
extrapolation — a cumulative ~4.5 million persons deported over three centuries of the
practice — is his estimate, not a directly attested figure, and is recorded here as such.
Deportees were explicitly not enslaved; inscriptions describe them as "counted among the
people of Assyria" with the same duties and rights as native Assyrians. Roux suggests,
speculatively, that the resulting "Aramaization" of the Assyrian population — reinforced
by an increasingly non-Assyrian-born standing army — may have `contributed_to` (not
`caused_by`) the empire's eventual collapse; this causal claim is explicitly attributed
and flagged as speculative.

Under Sargon II (722–705 BCE), Sennacherib (704–681 BCE),
Esarhaddon (681–669 BCE), and Ashurbanipal (668–627 BCE)
— the Sargonid dynasty — the empire reached its greatest extent, briefly incorporating
Egypt under Esarhaddon and Ashurbanipal. Sargon II founded the new capital Dûr-Sharrukîn
(Khorsabad, founded 717 BCE) but died in battle before its long occupation could take
hold; his successors preferred [[nineveh]]. Sennacherib made Nineveh the imperial
capital and, in 689 BCE, destroyed Babylon in an unprecedented act — his own inscription
(quoted by Roux) states: "I attacked it and, like a storm, I overthrew it… Its
inhabitants, young and old, I did not spare… I turned it into pastures… I removed the
dust of Babylon." Sennacherib was assassinated in 681 BCE; ancient tradition read this
as divine punishment for the sacrilege against Babylon — a moralizing frame from the
sources themselves, not a wiki-voice causal claim. Esarhaddon rebuilt Babylon as
atonement and, in 671 BCE, conquered Egypt, defeating Pharaoh Taharqa. Ashurbanipal
maintained a dual kingship with his brother Shamash-shum-ukîn over Babylon for sixteen
years before a devastating Babylonian civil war (652–648 BCE); he also destroyed Elam
definitively in 639 BCE, sacking Susa and desecrating royal tombs. Roux's own summary
judgment is that Ashurbanipal's apparent triumph after 639 BCE masked severe structural
exhaustion — Egypt permanently lost, Elam gone as a check but also as a buffer,
Babylonia devastated and resentful, and the rising Median kingdom unnoticed as the
coming threat (attributed synthesis).

The empire collapsed rapidly: [[nineveh]] fell to a Babylonian-Median coalition in 612
BCE (see that page), and a residual Assyrian state at Harran was extinguished by 609 BCE.

## Lifeways: The Assyrian State

The king's title ("Great king, mighty king, king of the Universe, king of the country of
Assur") framed him as the human agent of the god Ashur. Succession was hereditary in
principle but required the endorsement of the royal family and nobility, and several
successions were violent. Crown princes trained in the "House of Succession" (bît
redûti) at Tarbisu. Administration ran through provincial governors (bêl pihâti/shaknu),
district chiefs (rab alâni), and town "mayors" (hazannu) with local Councils of Elders,
bound to the crown by the adû loyalty oath. Divination — astral movements, extispicy,
dream interpretation, bird flight, and more — functioned as an official state "science,"
but Roux stresses (attributed) that Assyrian policy remained fundamentally realist:
omens provided "a general set of circumstances" for royal decisions the king made
freely, and kings are known to have solicited repeated omens until a favorable reading
appeared — divination as legitimation as much as guidance.

A striking ritual institution was the **substitute-king** device: in times of danger
(such as an ominous eclipse), a commoner was enthroned in the king's place — one
attested case names Damqi, son of a court official, chosen by a prophetess in a trance,
married to a court lady, and then killed along with his wife after a brief "reign,"
substituting for Shamash-shum-ukîn. The true king, meanwhile, underwent purification
rites (fasting, ritual shaving) during the danger period.

Economically, land was nominally royal, worked under ilku-service obligations, though a
Harran census and land-sale contracts attest private landownership too. Internal trade
is poorly documented because everyday Aramaic records were kept on perishable parchment
and papyrus that have not survived — an explicit archive-survivorship bias (Evidence-Class
Framework, sampling frames as content). The population was divided into free persons,
state/landowner dependents (mushkênu), and legally-rights-bearing "slaves"
(debt-bondage and war captives, who could rise to administrative posts); official
terminology framed the whole population as being at the king's service — "the national
industry of Assyria: war," in Roux's phrasing.

## Lifeways: The Assyrian Army

From Tiglathpileser III, the army had three tiers: a professional standing army (kisir
sharruti, drawn substantially from non-Assyrian-born peoples — Aramaeans, and later
Median, Cimmerian, Arab, and Elamite auxiliaries), conscripted troops under ilku duty,
and reserves. Equipment ranged from light bowmen and slingers to heavy mail-armored
lancers; cavalry expanded relative to chariotry over the course of the Neo-Assyrian
period (attributed to campaigning against mounted Medes in mountainous terrain). Siege
warfare — moats, earthworks, tunnels, siege towers, battering rams — is well attested.
Roux ventures a 7th-century mobilizable-force estimate of 400,000–500,000 (reserves
excluded); this figure is his own speculative extrapolation from sparse and inconsistent
inscriptional numbers (Ashurnasirpal cites 50,000; Shalmaneser III fields 120,000 at
Qarqar) and should not be treated as a documented count. The main evidentiary basis for
reconstructing Assyrian tactics is the palace-relief corpus and the Balawat bronze
gates, since royal inscriptions give little real tactical detail — a methodological
point Roux makes explicitly.

## Assyrian Art

Roux calls relief sculpture "the greatest and most original achievement of the
Assyrians" (his own aesthetic and historical judgment) — sculpture in the round, by
contrast, he judges comparatively weak (the British Museum's Ashurnasirpal statue is
"conventional, lifeless" in his assessment). The orthostat-relief technique is
attributed to Hittite origin, elaborated by Assyrian craftsmen in limestone. Its
distinguishing feature is subject matter centered on the human king rather than
divinized or god-scaled figures (contrast Egyptian relief); gods appear mainly as
symbols (spear-in-altar, winged disc). Roux reads this as deliberate political
propaganda designed to inspire "respect, admiration and fear" — an interpretive claim,
not a neutral finding. The Nimrud ivories (see [[nimrud]]) are, in Roux's words, "the
richest site of all" for this material, much of it tribute or booty from western
provinces and stylistically Egyptian-, Phoenician-, and Syrian-influenced; their
subject matter — women, animals, processions — is explicitly "emphatically peaceful,"
a striking counterpoint to the war-dominated stone reliefs.

## Relations and Successions

- **`descends_from` / continuous with:** the Old Assyrian city-state of [[assur|Assur]];
  per Roux, later Assyrian power was substantially built by incoming [[amorites|Amorite]]
  dynasts (an interpretive claim — see Identity Cautions).
- **`preceded_by`:** vassalage to [[hurrians|Mitanni]] (broken under Ashur-uballit I).
- **Chief rivals:** [[hurrians|Mitanni]], Babylonia under the [[kassites|Kassites]] and
  later the [[chaldaeans|Chaldaeans]], Urartu, Elam, and Egypt's Kushite dynasty.
- **Absorbed:** the [[aramaeans|Aramaean]] and [[hittite-empire|Neo-Hittite]] states of
  Syria and the Jazirah; deported Aramaic-speakers reshaped the imperial core.
- **`succeeded_by`:** the [[chaldaeans|Neo-Babylonian (Chaldaean)]] empire, whose founder
  Nabopolassar allied with the Medes to destroy [[nineveh|Nineveh]] in 612 BCE; devastated
  Assyria was not rebuilt.

## Identity Cautions

"Assyrian" here names a political and linguistic-cultural tradition centered on the cult
of Ashur and the cities of Assur, Nimrud, and Nineveh — it is not a stable genetic
population. Mass deportation policy from Tiglathpileser III onward deliberately mixed
conquered peoples into the Assyrian population on equal legal footing, and by the late
empire much of the population, including much of the army, was Aramaic-speaking and
non-Assyrian-born. Do not read "Assyrian" material culture, language, and any notion of
biological descent as a single coherent unit; per this wiki's four-way non-identity
rule, treat any equation among them as a claim requiring argument, not an assumption.

## Historiography

Roux explicitly notes a source-reliability problem running through Assyrian history:
royal inscriptions are self-glorifying propaganda (his own methodological point,
excluding annals from "real" history), and in several documented cases directly
conflict with the more neutral Babylonian Chronicle — for example, Sargon II's
inscriptions claim victory at the 720 BCE Battle of Dêr, while the Chronicle states the
Assyrians were defeated by Elam alone (Sargon later had a rival's monument at Nimrud
re-inscribed with his own "radically different version," an explicit case of ancient
propaganda). Similarly, Sennacherib's own annals frame the 689 BCE Battle of Hallulê as
an Assyrian victory, but Roux judges "it was in fact a near-defeat." The later tradition
of a catastrophic Assyrian disaster before Egypt under Sennacherib (biblical, Herodotean,
and Berossus accounts of plague or divine intervention near Pelusium) is, per Roux,
"very controversial and rejected by most scholars" — a live source-reliability question
about the Sennacherib-Egypt campaign, not a settled fact either way.

Assyriology's own historiography has swung between two poles that Roux himself pushes
back against: a "pack of wolves" image of Assyria as purely warlike, and — in his own
counter-framing — an emphasis on the Assyrians as "less thirsty of blood than of
knowledge and culture," pointing to the systematic scribal preservation project behind
[[nineveh|Ashurbanipal's Library]]. Both framings are attributed positions; the wiki
records them as such rather than adjudicating.

**Collection coverage note**: this page rests entirely on a single secondary synthesis,
[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]]; no primary Assyriological monograph or specialist
handbook has yet been ingested. Treat all interpretive claims above as Roux's own
until cross-checked against additional sources.

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] provides a
current university-textbook synthesis that both confirms and analytically extends the
Roux-derived narrative above, treating the Neo-Assyrian empire as a governing
**structure** rather than only a sequence of campaigns.

**Middle Assyrian origins as territorial-state formation.** Van De Mieroop dates
Assyria's emergence from a mere city-state to a directly-controlled territorial state to
a specific sequence: **Assur-uballit I** (1353–1318, per Van De Mieroop's chronology —
a Middle Chronology date differing slightly from the 1365–1330 given above from Roux;
both rest on the same synchronistic evidence and the discrepancy is a normal
chronology-convention variance, not a factual dispute) established firm control over the
Assyrian heartland after centuries as, at most, a [[hurrians|Mitanni]] vassal, then
exploited the Hittite destruction of Mitanni to annex its eastern territories and open
direct diplomatic relations with Egypt as an equal — provoking Babylonian protest that
Assyria was "our vassal." Three long-reigning successors — Adad-nirari I, Shalmaneser I,
and **Tukulti-Ninurta I** — then converted the former Mitanni/Hanigalbat zone east of the
Euphrates from vassalage into directly administered Assyrian territory, garrisoned and
governed rather than merely raided: this, per Van De Mieroop, is the actual emergence of
Assyria as a territorial state rather than a city-state. Shalmaneser I is also credited
with introducing mass deportation/resettlement as a control technique within conquered
Hanigalbat; Tukulti-Ninurta I extended it to deporting Syrian populations into the
Assyrian heartland for labor — the origin point, in this account, of the practice later
central to Neo-Assyrian imperial policy. (Attributed; Van De Mieroop.)

**A three-stage typology of imperial integration.** Van De Mieroop proposes an explicit
analytic model of how Assyria absorbed foreign polities, escalating through **vassal
state** (local ruler retained, tribute paid), **puppet state** (a more pliable local
figure installed on the throne), to **province** (direct rule by an Assyrian governor) —
with escalation triggered by "acts of disobedience" rather than blanket annexation. He
explicitly argues against reading the empire as driven by simple territorial appetite:
"the empire cannot be considered as driven by a mere desire to acquire territory. It was
a structure that aimed at maximizing resources for its core." The Israel/Samaria case
illustrates the full sequence in miniature: voluntary tribute under Menahem, a
pro-Assyrian puppet (Hoshea) installed after a rebellion, then outright annexation as
the province of Samaria under Sargon II following further disobedience, with **27,290
people deported** by Sargon's own count and foreign settlers moved in to create "a less
homogeneous population that was more docile toward Assyria" — followed by active
economic redevelopment (estates, Akkadian administrative restructuring, rebuilding in
Assyrian style), not mere punitive abandonment. (Attributed; Van De Mieroop, citing
Sargon's inscriptions.) Judah and other buffer states, by contrast, were deliberately
left as vassals against Egypt, and Phoenician city political structures were preserved
because their trade mattered to Assyria — the empire's policy was calibratedly flexible,
not uniform.

**Mass deportation as demographic/integrative policy.** Beyond the Samaria case, Van De
Mieroop cites a scholarly estimate of **roughly 4.5 million people deported over the
empire's roughly 250–300-year span** (a single cited estimate, attributed and treated as
provisional, not a directly attested count — consistent with Roux's own separately
derived ~4.5 million estimate above, though the two figures rest on different scholarly
extrapolations and should not be read as independent corroboration of one another).
Deportation served simultaneously as a tool of destroying resistance capacity, supplying
labor for imperial building/agriculture, and reshaping the conquered population's
loyalties — with the side effect, cited elsewhere in this ingest (see
[[aramaeans|Aramaeans]]), of spreading Aramaic language and script empire-wide via
displaced Aramean populations.

**Personalistic-loyalty administration.** Van De Mieroop stresses that Assyrian
imperial governance ran on **personal loyalty rather than codified bureaucratic
contract**: officials held office at the king's pleasure, not by hereditary right, and
positions were explicitly not legally protected. The **systematic replacement of
hereditary high officials with eunuchs from the mid-8th century** (beginning under
Tiglath-pileser III) is read as a deliberate anti-nepotism mechanism — eunuchs could not
found dynasties, so provincial and central offices reverted to the crown rather than
passing to heirs. **Loyalty oaths (adê)** extended beyond officials to the entire
population: Esarhaddon had "the people of Assyria, great and small, from coast to coast"
swear to accept Assurbanipal's succession. Provincial governors (shaknu) and city
mayors (hazannu — sometimes multiple per city, possibly deliberately, to prevent any
one official accumulating local power) headed administration; offices blended
military, administrative, and religious duties with no separation comparable to later
bureaucratic states (Van De Mieroop explicitly cautions against reading this through a
later, e.g. Ottoman, comparandum). Vassal treaties were sworn on both Assyrian and the
vassal's own gods — Van De Mieroop states explicitly "there was no religious
intolerance," and temple-sacking/looting of divine images functioned as political
domination, not forced conversion.

**Cosmological kingship and "calculated frightfulness."** Imperial ideology cast the
king as agent of the god Assur, whose rule embodied cosmic order against the "chaos" of
the unruled world; foreigners were dehumanized in official rhetoric ("images of
swamp-rats or bats"). This ideology was transmitted not only through largely
inaccessible royal inscriptions but through victory parades, public readings of
campaign accounts, the deliberately awe-inspiring planned layout of new imperial
capitals, and palace relief programs depicting the king as "master of the world." Van De
Mieroop uses the scholarly shorthand **"calculated frightfulness"** for a named
terror-tactic complex — massive force, selective sieges, and public torture/mutilation
display deployed to induce rapid surrender — consistent with, and extending, the palace
propaganda function already noted above from Roux. Sargon II's building inscription for
Dur-Sharrukin is read as deliberately echoing the Babylonian Epic of Creation's language
for Marduk's cosmic ordering, equating royal city-building with divine cosmos-building.
(Attributed throughout; Van De Mieroop.)

**A structural account of the 612 BCE collapse.** Van De Mieroop frames the fall as
resulting from causes internal to the imperial structure itself, not only external
attack: (1) governance quality was hostage to a single ruler's capacity, and succession
crises (notably Assurbanipal vs. his brother Shamash-shuma-ukin) were "extremely
disruptive"; (2) the empire's own "rapacious attitude" — deportation and heavy tribute
demands — made rebellion attractive to conquered populations whenever an opportunity
arose; (3) once tributary supply was cut by successful revolt, the oversized imperial
core cities (Nineveh, Kalhu) could not sustain themselves; (4) much of the Assyrian
heartland's own population by this point consisted of deportees with limited loyalty to
the Assyrian state. Van De Mieroop's own summary: "that empire had always been built on
a weak basis, and fissures could undermine the whole system." The documented sequence —
Assurbanipal's death (631/630, contested date), a succession struggle, Nabopolassar's
626 Chaldaean proclamation in Babylon, Median attacks from 615, the sack of Nineveh in
612, and the final extinction of Assyrian resistance at Harran by 605 — is treated as
the working-out of this structural weakness rather than a sudden, externally-imposed
catastrophe. At Nineveh, the conquerors' destruction was selective and ritual, not
indiscriminate: specific figures on wall reliefs (the king; the soldier depicted
beheading the Elamite king) were deliberately defaced (eyes and ears cut) before palaces
were burned — probably Medes ritually avenging perceived Elamite kin — and the
Babylonian king reportedly took ashes of Nineveh home "to avenge Sennacherib's
destruction of Babylon," an explicit reciprocal-atrocity framing already present in the
ancient sources themselves.

**Debate 12.1 — why did the Assyrians build an empire? (positions surveyed, none
adjudicated by Van De Mieroop as settled).** Van De Mieroop surveys the historiography of
this question without resolving it: a **personality/ideology position** (royal
megalomania, jealousy of Syro-Phoenician wealth, or a royal ideology demanding annual
campaigning to impose order on a "chaotic periphery"); a **religious position** (the god
Assur as king required the mortal king to enlarge the state by divine command); a
**no-rationale/self-preservation position** (a Cold-War-era study explicitly noted by
Van De Mieroop as itself a product of its moment — "provoked by the Soviet invasion of
Hungary in 1956" — arguing the Assyrians had no coherent mission, only expansion for its
own sake); a **defensive-economic position** (reaction to the Aramean threat and to
resource access); and a **general Mesopotamian-commercial-imperative position** treating
Neo-Assyrian expansion as one instance of a recurring Mesopotamian pattern (Akkadian, Ur
III, Old Babylonian, Middle Assyrian) driven by a resource-poor core on major trade
routes — a reading Van De Mieroop notes "detracts from the special character of the
Neo-Assyrian empire." Van De Mieroop's own preference is for historicizing the question:
reasons for expansion likely changed across the empire's 250-year span, with core and
periphery adapting dynamically to one another — stated as a preferred framing, not a
resolved consensus.

**Historiographical note — a discredited racial explanation (reflexivity item).** Among
the positions surveyed, Van De Mieroop specifically flags Wolfram von Soden's 1937
argument that Assyrian militarism reflected an "Aryan joy of battle" transmitted via
"Nordic blood" through Indo-European contact with the Mitanni elite — an explicitly
Nazi-era racial-science reading of Assyrian imperialism. Van De Mieroop states this
position is "now fully rejected." **This is recorded here strictly as a documented,
discredited episode in the history of Assyriological scholarship, not as any kind of
live explanatory position** — it illustrates how 20th-century racial ideology could
penetrate ANE historiography, and belongs in this page's historiographical record
precisely because of its total rejection, not its content.
