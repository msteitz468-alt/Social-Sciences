# Grandes Écoles

## Definition and Scope

The **grandes écoles** are France's elite higher-education institutions, separate from and
opposed to the open-access universities: entry by competitive examination (*concours*) after
intensive preparatory classes (*classes préparatoires* — *khâgne* for humanities, *taupe* for
science), leading to the *grands corps* of the state and the summits of business. The dualist
structure (grandes écoles/universities; preparatory "pupils" vs university "students") is the
system's defining feature — per [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]], "it is the relationship of
systematic opposition between the two categories of institution that gives rise to the
originality of each," an analogue of the British public-school/grammar-school divide.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

The French case is historically specific, but Bourdieu (and Wacquant's foreword) argue by
homology that every advanced society has functional equivalents refracted through national
histories — the US Ivy League and elite boarding schools, Oxbridge — while warning against the
"fallacy of misplaced concreteness" of seeking exact institutional counterparts (there is no
American ENA).

## Theoretical Treatments

*(All attributed — Bourdieu, [[bourdieu-state-nobility-1996|The State Nobility]]; survey data
1965–69, restudied 1984–85.)*

- **A field, not a list of schools.** The grandes écoles form a genuine [[social-field|field]]
  with detectable field-effects (each school's transformations react to the others'). Its
  structure is **chiasmatic**, homologous to the [[field-of-power]]: an academically dominant
  but socially/economically dominated pole (ENS-Ulm) versus a socially/economically dominant but
  academically weaker pole (HEC, Sciences-po, ENA), with Polytechnique at the intersection.
  Recruitment matches consecrated destination: teachers' sons at ENS, higher civil servants'
  sons at ENA, business heads' sons at HEC.
- **Grande porte / petite porte.** The field institutes "distinct, rigid, and definitive
  discontinuities among competitors whose... abilities are actually in continuous distribution" —
  a major gate (top schools, definitive elite status from adolescence) versus a minor gate
  (petites écoles, IUTs, facultés) homologous to the upper/petty bourgeoisie divide.
- **Production apparatus.** The preparatory classes are "genuine total institutions": a
  "forcing ground" of urgency, discipline, ranked competition, and "symbolic confinement" whose
  *organization* (not content) inculcates the dispositions of power — culminating in the
  concours as a [[rites-of-institution|rite of institution]] consecrating a
  [[state-nobility|school nobility]]. Esprit de corps — "the reflex complicity of
  well-orchestrated unconsciouses" — is "the precondition for the constitution of cultural
  capital" as a collective resource.
- **Structural history.** "Palace wars" among fractions of the dominant class are fought through
  schools: HEC's symbolic-capital ascent from 1881; ENA's post-1945 conquest of higher public
  service at ENS's and Polytechnique's expense (read as threatening "the death knell for the
  independent intellectual" of Sartre's type). "Sanctuary schools" (management schools, INSEAD
  and the like) absorb bourgeois demand displaced by academic competition, converting academic
  failure into marketable, legitimated capital.
- **Persistence.** The 1984–85 restudy found the field's structure "very similar" to the 1960s
  despite May 1968 — with the social distance between the two poles *widening*.

## Key Studies

- [[bourdieu-state-nobility-study]] — the charter analysis (surveys 1965–69; 84-institution and
  21-school correspondence analyses; 1972 CEO study; 1984–85 restudy).
- Institutional self-histories exist in quantity but are, per Bourdieu's "Discourse of
  Celebration" appendix, overwhelmingly written by alumni and staff — a source-reliability
  caution for any future ingest.

## Debates

The meritocracy question — whether the concours democratized elite recruitment or re-clothed
inheritance as merit — is the standing dispute; Bourdieu's "Blindness" appendix documents his
polemic against Kesler's pro-ENA reading of the same data. Not yet promoted to a `debates/`
page; single-source coverage so far.
