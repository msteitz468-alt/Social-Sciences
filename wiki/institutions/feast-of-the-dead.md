## Definition and Scope

The Feast of the Dead ("the Kettle" to the Huron) was a communal reburial ceremony in which a village's dead — excluding those who died violent deaths — were disinterred from their individual raised tombs and reburied together in a large ossuary pit. Bruce Trigger, in *The Children of Aataentsic* (1976; 1987 reprint), describes it as held roughly once every ten to fifteen years per major village, typically timed to a village's relocation, since the existing cemetery could no longer be tended once the settlement moved. Bodies of the dead from satellite villages and from friends in other Huron villages were included; only closely allied tribes were invited to mingle bones with the host community. (FINDING, attributed)

The rite was believed to release souls — previously bound to the cemetery — to travel west to the land of Iouskeha and Aataentsic, passing a standing rock (Ecaregniondi) and a spirit (Oscotarach) who removed the brains of the dead; the souls of the very young, the very old, and those who died violently (suicides, war dead) were excluded from this village-of-souls. (FINDING, attributed)

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Trigger (attributed) argues the Feast developed as a distinctively middle-Iroquoian ritual, beginning with ossuaries ("bone pits") first appearing in the late Pickering culture. It differs from the individual-grave burial practiced by the Iroquois proper, the Susquehannock, the St. Lawrence Iroquoians, and possibly the proto-Huron of the Trent Valley. Neutral ossuaries are generally smaller than Huron ones, with as many as eleven found around a single site — suggesting to Trigger that ossuary burial may not have served the same integrative function among the Neutral as among the proto-Huron.

An unusually early possible instance of communal reburial appears at the Serpent Mounds site (Rice Lake): three secondary-burial pits, each containing 15–29 bodies, associated with early Iroquoian times but with no nearby permanent village — suggesting to Trigger that villages "may have been less of a ritual focus" in the Pickering culture than later. Ossuary evidence at the Juntunen site (upper Great Lakes, c. 1200–1400 CE) — 34 bodies, some articulated, some disarticulated — is cited by Trigger as "an unexpectedly early date for the possible diffusion of the Feast of the Dead among the northern Algonkians."

The best-studied middle Iroquoian ossuary, the Fairty ossuary (near the Robb site, Toronto outskirts, dated to about the end of the Middleport substage), contained over 500 bodies. Using mortality-rate modeling with an assumed ten-year interval between burials (Anderson 1964), Trigger infers a regional population of roughly 1,500–2,500, while noting substantial uncertainty (a doubled village-lifespan estimate would halve the population figure). Only a stone scraper and a single shell bead were recovered as grave goods, indicating limited prehistoric wealth or trade compared with the historic period. (FINDING, attributed)

## Theoretical Treatments

Trigger frames the Feast as "in effect a microcosm of the structures and values upon which Huron society as a whole was based," arguing its main social function was to reinforce goodwill and cooperation across otherwise disparate clan segments, villages, and tribes through the literal commingling of bones. (TRIGGER-CLAIM, attributed)

Under the fur trade the ceremony underwent marked florescence rather than displacement of an older form. At the 1636 Feast of the Dead held at Ossossane on the occasion of that village's relocation, Brébeuf recorded over 1,200 gifts on display, with twenty beaver robes (200 skins) set aside to thank visiting headmen. The Ossossane ossuary itself was lined with forty-eight ten-skin beaver robes, and grave goods included marine shell beads, projectile points, pigments, textiles, pipes, a complete conch shell, glass beads, iron knives, rings, and two to three decomposed copper kettles (Kidd 1953). A separate, unnamed ossuary yielded up to twenty-six French kettles, sixteen conch shells, iron axes, copper bracelets, glass beads, metal arrowheads, and iron cups. Historic ossuaries thus contain markedly more offerings than prehistoric ones. (FINDING, attributed)

Harold Hickerson (1960, as cited by Trigger) interprets the fur-trade-era Feast as central to confederacy-wide and Huron-Algonkian trade-network solidarity. Harold Innis (as cited by Trigger) is invoked for the further point that the periodic interment of scrap metal and valuables removed European goods from circulation, sustaining demand given already-high attrition and use rates. Trigger's own reading (attributed) treats the fur-trade elaboration of the Feast as the florescence of a pre-existing Huron tradition — its expanded scale paralleling earlier Huron political integration — rather than a new departure; he notes that the Feast's centrality for expressing cross-tribal solidarity appears to have been unique to the Huron among Iroquoian peoples. By the period covered in Trigger's concluding chapters, he treats the elaboration of the Feast (alongside increasing Iroquois grave-goods deposition) as evidence of growing ritualism accompanying confederacy expansion in the fur-trade era.

Not all commentary in the sources is uncritical: some "Old Men and notables" reportedly "secretly took possession" of many fur robes at the 1636 Ossossane Feast (Thwaites 10:303), and Brébeuf claimed the poor lost everything at such ceremonies while the rich lost comparatively little — a claim Trigger flags as possibly an unfounded Christian condemnation of the non-Christian custom rather than an established fact. (POSITION, attributed)

## Key Studies

The single richest documented instance is the 1636 Feast of the Dead at Ossossane, described at length in the Jesuit Relations and analyzed by Trigger; see [[sainte-marie-among-the-hurons]] and the Huron mission chapters of Trigger's *Children of Aataentsic* for the surrounding context of Jesuit residence and epidemic mortality that occasioned it. An illustration of the rite, Plate 8 in Lafitau (1724), depicts Brébeuf's account but is flagged by Trigger as showing "the artist's macabre fantasies" (dangling articulated skeletons) — an explicit source-reliability caution about visual sources of the ceremony.

## Debates

**1636 Ossossane relocation and the Chaudron/Brûlé reburial dispute.** The 1636 relocation of Ossossane occasioned an Attignawantan Feast of the Dead at which councils considered disinterring and reburying the Frenchmen Guillaume Chaudron and Étienne Brûlé with the Attignawantan dead, framed as "the most sacred of alliances" through joint burial. Brébeuf declined the proposal on doctrinal grounds — Christians and non-Christians could not be buried together — but offered a compromise: rebury Chaudron and Brûlé near the ossuary provided all baptized Huron dead were buried together in a separate grave. The Huron accepted, though Trigger notes the compromise likely unsettled Huron notions of unity achieved through commingled burial. In the event, the separate-ossuary plan for baptized Huron fell through, and fifteen to twenty baptized Huron bodies were buried without Christian rites in the common Ossossane pit. (FINDING, attributed)

A further complication that year: a quarrel between the headmen of the five northern Attignawantan villages and the rest of the tribe — over exclusion from secret councils and uneven distribution of epidemic-reparations — led the five northern villages to hold a separate Feast of the Dead in 1636, distinct from the southern ceremony at Ossossane. Trigger treats this, together with other evidence, as reason to doubt whether the Feast was strictly a tribal-level ritual as opposed to one multiple villages traditionally held separately, cautioning against assuming Brébeuf's single account reflects the sole or normal pattern. (TRIGGER-CLAIM, attributed)

**Confederacy expansion and ritualism.** Trigger situates the Feast's fur-trade-era elaboration, together with increasing Iroquois grave-goods deposition, within a broader pattern in which Huron and Iroquois confederacies "appear to have expanded to include more tribes, if they did not come into being at this time" as a political response to trade-goods competition — an extension of existing tribal-level institutions rather than a novel structural invention, in which increased ritualism served to promote political and social integration. (TRIGGER-CLAIM, attributed)
