# Headhunting

The ritualized taking of human heads in raiding — documented on this wiki so far through the [[ilongot|Ilongot]] case (northern Luzon, Philippines), via Renato Rosaldo's *[[rosaldo-ilongot-headhunting-study|Ilongot Headhunting, 1883–1974]]*. Other regional complexes (Borneo, Melanesia, Taiwan, Naga Hills) await sources.

## Definition and Scope

*(Ilongot case, attributed to Rosaldo; fieldwork 1967–74, narrated events 1883–1974.)* Among the Ilongot, headhunting (*ngayu*, raid) was ambush killing — *ka'abung* (killing by deception), *ta'neb* (waiting in ambush), *begbeg* (rushing a house at dawn) — followed by severing and **throwing away** the victim's head. Raiding parties (4–40 men) were recruited along bilateral kin networks centered on an aggrieved man; retaliation targeted any member of the offending [[bertan]], collective liability being the feud's signature. Ambush, condemned by colonial observers as cowardice, was defended by raiders as a moral act safeguarding their brethren.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Single-case so far; the Ilongot complex itself varied internally by epoch — see the periodization below. Rosaldo's account is explicitly a caution against treating "headhunting" as one timeless custom.

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Coming of age, not trophy-taking (Rosaldo).** Taking a head ended novice (*siap*) status and conferred the red hornbill earrings of an "arrived" man; every peer sought at least one head, and Ilongots framed the act as "throwing away" a burden — grief, insult, the "weight" of novice status — not as acquiring soul-stuff. Rosaldo likened it to piacular sacrifice. Peer envy (see [[liget]]) drove chains of head-takings in quick succession ("like people walking along a path"): in one ~30-day span in 1952, five of Rosaldo's ten-man cohort took heads.
- **A historical practice, not a timeless one (Rosaldo).** Between Jones's fieldwork (1908–9) and Rosaldo's, headhunting "flourished and then ceased altogether three different times." Rosaldo's reconstruction: full bloom under the late-Spanish *kakastila*; constabulary pacification after Jones's 1909 murder (Turnbull's punitive expedition — which itself collected seven Ilongot heads); tranquility 1910–18; resurgence 1919–28 as colonial "loyal margins vs. rebellious center" policy fused with feud; quiet 1929–35; gradual return 1936–41; peak violence 1942–45 (including beheadings of Japanese soldiers); postwar moratorium 1945–49 (*pedeg*, "still, stopped"); resurgence 1950–54 during the Huk era (*ka'ukbu*), when Battalion Combat Teams armed Ilongots and promised impunity — including a 1954 ambush in which ~80 Ilongot men beheaded nine Huk guerrillas; decline thereafter; definitive end with the September 1972 martial-law declaration and firing-squad rumors.
- **Grief and rage (M. Rosaldo; R. Rosaldo 1989).** Bereavement intensified *liget*; a head was "a place to carry one's anger." After 1972 foreclosed raiding, New Tribes Mission conversion served some men as the alternative means of coping with grief (Rosaldo's Afterword).

## Key Studies

[[rosaldo-ilongot-headhunting-study]] — raid mechanics documented in detail: pre-raid divination in which a "person of knowledge" chants to lure victims' "hearts," which perch like birds on the raiders' earlobes (the future site of the earrings); women's farewell and celebration songs; the novice's ritual utterance as he throws the head; the victory call ('a 'ee 'u 'u, one shout per head).

## Debates

**The "bridal gift" myth.** Rosaldo explicitly refuted the durable claim (from Jenks 1905, echoing Campa 1891 and Savage 1904, still in the Manila press in 1963) that an Ilongot man had to present a severed head to his bride: heads were thrown away, presented to no one. Rosaldo (1978a; 1980) treated the myth as an exoticizing, "bestializing" stereotype — a documented case of ethnographic misrepresentation. Colonial-era moral framing of ambush as "cowardice" (Jordana y Morera 1885) versus the raiders' own ethics is recorded on the study page.
