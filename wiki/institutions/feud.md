# Feud

> First-class treatment in [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Evans-Pritchard, *The Nuer* (1940)]] as the load-bearing institution of [[ordered-anarchy]]. Mediated by the [[leopard-skin-chief]].

## Definition and Scope

The **feud** is regulated, reciprocal violence (or the threat of it) between kin-based groups as a means of obtaining reparation for injury — paradigmatically homicide — in the absence of a state monopoly on force. In Evans-Pritchard's Nuer analysis (attributed), it is precisely *not* lawless: "Blood-feuds are a tribal institution, for they can only occur where a rule of law is recognized since they are the way in which reparation is obtained. Fear of incurring a blood-feud is, in fact, the most important legal sanction within a tribe." A case counts as feud (Nuer *ter*) only within a tribe, where compensation is negotiable; between tribes there is *mac* (war, no compensation), and against the Dinka *pec* (raiding) — all under *kur*, fighting in general.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

The Nuer case is the type-specimen of the **segmentary blood-feud**: only the minimal lineages of slayer and slain are directly liable to killing, but "through common residence, local patriotism, and a network of kinship ties, the whole sections participate in the enmity." Settlement runs through the [[leopard-skin-chief|leopard-skin chief]] and a payment of ~40–50 head of cattle, with mortuary ritual (*hir*). Yet the Nuer held that "a feud never ends": those who accept cattle remain *jiter* (at feud) even without overt hostility, and a posthumous son is *gat ter*, "a child of feud," raised to avenge. Comparative variants — Mediterranean vendetta, Germanic *wergild*, Bedouin blood-money — share the compensation-or-vengeance structure; this page will accrue cases as sources are ingested.

A pointed **negative case** is the [[tiv|Tiv]] of central Nigeria. [[bohannan-paul|Bohannan]] reports (attributed, *[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-study|Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv]]*, fieldwork c. 1949–1953) that "institutionalized feud did not exist in Tivland": a killing led not to regulated kin vengeance but to open **war between lineages**, after which "the score was counted and remembered on each side" and carried into the next war. Bohannan flagged this as a comparative puzzle — the Tiv resemble the Nuer and the Bedouin in segmentary organization yet lack the feud — and reserved it for a projected monograph. Correction, where it came, came in the idiom of witchcraft and magic (*tsav*), not blood-vengeance (see [[self-help-law]]).

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Structural-functional (Evans-Pritchard, attributed).** The feud is "a political institution... an approved and regulated mode of behaviour between communities within a tribe... essential to the political system." Functionally it is "a structural movement between political segments by which the form of the Nuer political system... is maintained" — a device sustaining the fission/fusion equilibrium of the [[segmentary-lineage-system]]. Its settleability is proportional to **structural distance**: "Feuds are settled with comparative ease in a restricted social milieu where the structural distance between the participants is narrow, but... more difficult to settle as the milieu expands, until one reaches intertribal relations where no compensation is offered or expected."
- **Later readings** treat the feud less as a homeostatic mechanism and more as an arena of strategy, history, and change (part of the [[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]), and question whether the "self-balancing" account idealizes what is often escalating violence.

## Key Studies

[[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Evans-Pritchard, *The Nuer* (1940)]] — the founding structural analysis.

[[gluckman-custom-and-conflict-study|Gluckman, *Custom and Conflict in Africa* (1955/56)]] — “The Peace in the Feud”: men quarrel under some customary allegiances but are restrained by other, cross-cutting allegiances; greater division in one area of society tends toward greater cohesion in a wider range, *provided* a general need for peace and a shared moral order (attributed). Uses Nuer, Colson’s Tonga, and modern industrial analogies; generalizes beyond segmentary systems to the mechanism of [[cross-cutting-conflicts]]. Black-Michaud’s *Cohesive Force* remains a named later successor not yet ingested.

## Debates

Whether the feud "maintains order" (functionalist) or expresses and escalates conflict; and, for the Nuer specifically, whether Evans-Pritchard's mechanism overstates the neatness of segmentary opposition ([[segmentary-lineage-model-debate]]). Gluckman’s “peace in the feud” is the classic British anthropological formulation of the cohesion thesis — still contested for underplaying unredressed violence and historical change.
