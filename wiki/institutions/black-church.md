# Black Church (Negro Church)

## Definition and Scope

In *[[du-bois-souls-of-black-folk-1903|The Souls of Black Folk]]* (1903), chapter X (“Of the Faith of the Fathers”), [[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]] analyzes the **Negro church** (period term retained in attributed quotation; page title uses both historical and current labels) as the central social institution of African American life. He argues:

> “The Negro church of to-day is the social centre of Negro life in the United States, and the most characteristic expression of African character.”

Scope in *Souls*: from African priestly/clan religion through plantation worship to post-Emancipation Baptist/Methodist dominance and the institutional city church — not a full contemporary denominational census beyond figures he cites.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Du Bois’s account is U.S.-specific with an African historical root. He traces: African social base (clan priest, nature-worship) → plantation revolution (priest becomes preacher; Voodooism then Christian veneer) → Baptist and Methodist dominance; African Methodist Episcopal organization; Northern churches severing white affiliations. He claims the Negro church **antedates** the monogamic Negro home in institutional sequence under slavery. Southern rural churches lean on schoolhouses as twin centers (illustrated also in the Tennessee vignette of ch. IV).

## Theoretical Treatments

**Du Bois (1903 *Souls*), attributed:**

- **Triad of slave religion:** the **Preacher** (most unique Negro personality type), the **Music**, and the **Frenzy** (“Shouting”).
- **Multifunction beyond worship:** Sunday-school, societies, mutual aid, politics, social life — “central club-house”; in great city form (Bethel, Philadelphia), a “government of men.”
- **Near-universal membership** in the South as social necessity where the church is the only open social centre.
- **1890 census figures** (as he reports): large counts of organizations and communicants — use as Du Bois’s cited stats, not re-verified here.
- **Ethical double life** under the [[the-color-line|color-line]]: streams toward hypocrisy vs. radicalism; “The price of culture is a Lie” as defense of the weak; awaiting a new religious ideal between extremes.

**Du Bois (*Religion in the South*, c. 1906; *[[du-bois-essays|Essays]]*), attributed:**

- Paired with *Economic Revolution in the South*: earning a living and attitude toward life are “two sides of the same problem”; essence of Southern religion is the **ethics of slavery and emancipation**.
- Slavery as economic mistake and moral crime, with historical excuses that must be remembered without falsifying truth.
- Genealogy of Christian legitimation: heathen-conversion rationale → baptism-does-not-free laws → later **race caste** replacing status slavery; distinct denial of human brotherhood under the cotton kingdom.
- Early Black spiritual leaders (Hosier, Leile, Bryan, Lott Carey, Chavis, Evans, et al.) as exceptional upward stretch until ethical paradox of teaching vs. control.
- Preacher-led revolt fear (Vesey medium of Charleston congregations; Nat Turner) → waves of laws forbidding Black preaching, literacy, and unsupervised worship after 1831.
- Postbellum Southern Church paradox (as Du Bois frames it): clings to worn orthodoxy while daily life “crucifies ‘Niggers’”; leaven of better ethics drowned by Southern social tyranny and Northern indifference.

**Talented Tenth link:** college-bred preacher as reformatory opportunity where the older preacher was “ignorant and often immoral” (Du Bois’s wording, 1903 essay).

Functionalist note: the church supplies integration and leadership under caste exclusion from other public institutions — Du Bois’s claim, not a general wiki-voice law of religion.
