# Indirect Rule

## Definition and Scope

**Indirect rule** (also Native Administration) names the British colonial practice of governing subject populations **through** indigenous or invented political institutions — chiefs, emirs, native courts, native treasuries — rather than solely through direct European district officers. In the Asad volume it is the principal administrative form to which British social anthropology was articulated as useful knowledge: mapping "tribes," customary law, and political structure so rule could run through local authorities.

This page treats indirect rule as a **political institution of colonial governance**, not as an African traditional system. Lackner (1973) insists that the claim that traditional systems developed "along their own lines" under indirect rule is a **mystification**: using a system for British ends necessarily distorted it.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

*(From Lackner and related Asad essays, attributed.)*

| Setting | Form | Outcome (as argued) |
|---|---|---|
| Northern Nigeria (Emirates) | Hierarchical Islamic polities under Emirs — model origin for Lugard's system | Seemed to "fit" indirect rule |
| Southern Nigeria after 1914 amalgamation | Northern model exported (Native Courts 1914; Native Authorities 1916) | Disastrous mismatch in East |
| South-East (Ibo and neighbours) | Warrant Chiefs / Native Courts without traditional chiefly template | Failure; Women's War / Aba Riots 1929 → commissions → demand for anthropological knowledge |
| South-West | Obas accountable to title-holder councils | Limited success; taxation slow |
| Wider British Africa | Functionalist holism and equilibrium models | Feuchtwang/Willis: fit with pragmatic self-administration knowledge |

Lugard's **interventionist** reading (transmit orders through rulers; engineer change) vs successors' **non-interventionist** preference ("along their own lines") is a documented internal colonial dispute (Lackner via Crowder/Nicolson).

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Administrative theory:** Lugard and successors theorised practice after the fact; "no Guide to Indirect Rule" — empirical improvisation then doctrinal justification (Lackner).
- **Functionalist anthropology:** Feuchtwang and others argue functionalism supplied knowledge forms suited to indirect-rule problematics (bounded tribal units, political structure, custom). Faris reads Nadel's Sudan work as functionalism as **control ideology** under *pax Britannica*.
- **Critical anthropology (Asad ed. 1973):** Indirect rule is the institutional hinge of the [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] for the British case — not proving every ethnographer was a hired agent, but showing structural demand for certain kinds of knowledge.
