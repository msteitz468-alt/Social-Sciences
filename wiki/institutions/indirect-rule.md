# Indirect Rule

## Definition and Scope

**Indirect rule** (also Native Administration) names the British colonial practice of governing subject populations **through** indigenous or invented political institutions — chiefs, emirs, native courts, native treasuries — rather than solely through direct European district officers. In the Asad volume it is the principal administrative form to which British social anthropology was articulated as useful knowledge: mapping "tribes," customary law, and political structure so rule could run through local authorities.

This page treats indirect rule as a **political institution of colonial governance**, not as an African traditional system. Lackner (1973) insists that the claim that traditional systems developed "along their own lines" under indirect rule is a **mystification**: using a system for British ends necessarily distorted it.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

*(From Lackner and related Asad essays, attributed.)*

| Setting | Form | Outcome (as argued) |
|---|---|---|
| Northern Nigeria (Emirates) | Hierarchical Islamic polities under Emirs — model origin for Lugard's system | Seemed to "fit" indirect rule |
| Southern Nigeria after 1914 amalgamation | Northern model exported (Native Courts 1914; Native Authorities 1916) | Disastrous mismatch in East |
| South-East (Ibo and neighbours) | Warrant Chiefs / Native Courts without traditional chiefly template | Failure; Women's War / Aba Riots 1929 → commissions → demand for anthropological knowledge |
| South-West | Obas accountable to title-holder councils | Limited success; taxation slow |
| Wider British Africa | Functionalist holism and equilibrium models | Feuchtwang/Willis: fit with pragmatic self-administration knowledge |

Lugard's **interventionist** reading (transmit orders through rulers; engineer change) vs successors' **non-interventionist** preference ("along their own lines") is a documented internal colonial dispute (Lackner via Crowder/Nicolson).

### European antecedent — indirect rule as the pre-modern norm (Tilly 1990/1992, attributed)

In *[[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and European States]]*, [[tilly-charles|Tilly]]
generalizes the concept far beyond the colonial case: before the French Revolution, "all but the
smallest [European] states relied on some version of indirect rule" — governing "through
powerful intermediaries who enjoyed significant autonomy, hindered state demands that were not
to their own interest, and profited on their own accounts from the delegated exercise of state
power" (clergy, landlords, urban oligarchies, tax farmers; the Ottoman timar system as his
extended case). Its economy: it "made it possible to govern without erecting, financing, and
feeding a bulky administrative apparatus"; its risks: "disloyalty, dissimulation, corruption,
and rebellion" — predation by intermediaries and rebellion led by them. War's escalating
demands drove the transition to [[direct-rule]]. Keep the two literatures distinct but linked:
the colonial-administration institution documented here is, on Tilly's reading, a specific
instance of a general governance form. See [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]].

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Administrative theory:** Lugard and successors theorised practice after the fact; "no Guide to Indirect Rule" — empirical improvisation then doctrinal justification (Lackner).
- **Functionalist anthropology:** Feuchtwang and others argue functionalism supplied knowledge forms suited to indirect-rule problematics (bounded tribal units, political structure, custom). Faris reads Nadel's Sudan work as functionalism as **control ideology** under *pax Britannica*.
- **Critical anthropology (Asad ed. 1973):** Indirect rule is the institutional hinge of the [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] for the British case — not proving every ethnographer was a hired agent, but showing structural demand for certain kinds of knowledge.
- **Kuper 2015 (attributed):** Lugard’s *Dual Mandate* (1922) and on-the-ground social engineering (tribe/chief/district template); Malinowski’s 1927 note that Indirect Rule was a “Complete Surrender to the Functional Point of View”; elective affinity and loose symbiosis rather than consistent spy-collaboration — [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] Position 9; [[kuper-anthropology-and-anthropologists-2015]].
- **Stocking 1995 (attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]], *[[stocking-after-tylor-1995|After Tylor]]*, Ch. 8):** cautions against reading Lugard's own text as functionalist-friendly. Lugard's *The Dual Mandate in British Tropical Africa* (1922) — written partly as a response to Leonard Woolf's Labour Party critique *Empire and Commerce in Africa* — classified African peoples in explicitly social-evolutionary and racialist terms and cited almost no anthropological authors beyond Henry Maine, Everhard Im Thurn, and Grafton Elliot Smith. Stocking states plainly that the text, "rather than foreshadowing functionalist anthropology," reads as "redolent of the racialist evolutionism of the later nineteenth century" — an explicit authorial judgment distancing indirect-rule *ideology* from functionalist *theory*'s supposed complicity. This sits against Malinowski's own private assessment (undated notes, c.1928–29) that Lugard, had he "wanted to control scientific Anthropology to fit his imperial idea," "couldn't have done anything but to create [the] Functional School" — i.e., Malinowski himself asserted the theory–policy correspondence Stocking treats cautiously. See [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] Position 10.
