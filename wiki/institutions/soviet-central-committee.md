# Soviet Central Committee (CPSU)

## Definition and Scope

The **Central Committee** (CC) of the Soviet Communist Party was the party's leading body between congresses: elected at party congresses, formally superior in party rules to the Politburo and Secretariat, and in research practice the clearest institutional roster of the national political elite. Mawdsley and White (2000) define the Soviet elite for research as **all full and candidate CC members, 1917–1991** (**N=1,932** across **twenty-two** Central Committees elected 1917–1990).

Not identical to the Politburo (inner leadership) or the entire party membership. Membership mixed: top leaders; job-slot office-holders (ministers, regional first secretaries, military and police chiefs, ambassadors, editors, large-enterprise directors, youth and trade-union leaders, Academy of Sciences president, occasional writers); and, from the Khrushchev era, a small share of **token** worker/peasant/intelligentsia representatives.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Other communist parties had analogous central committees. This page covers the **Soviet/CPSU** case as documented in [[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-2000]]. Size ranged from 1917 (21 full + 19 candidates) to the 1986 peak (307 full + 170 candidates); 1990 cut to 412 full members with the highest turnover of the era (88%).

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Official / Leninist:** leading organ under [[democratic-centralism]]; congress verifies, removes, and names the CC (Lenin at 9th Congress, epigraph in Mawdsley & White Ch. 1). Stalin's "areopagus" metaphor; late-Soviet "brain of the party."
- **Job-slot institutionalism (Daniels; Mawdsley & White):** CC as map of ranked offices ([[job-slot-system]]); maturity by mid-1930s; survival of the map through Purge-era personal destruction.
- **Elite / class critiques:** bureaucracy (Trotsky), [[new-class]] (Djilas), [[nomenklatura]] (Voslensky)—competing labels for the group the CC crystallises at its top tier.
- **Decision forum:** plenums as elite assemblies—used by Stalin; decisive in **1957** against the Anti-Party Group; reactivated under Gorbachev; never fully institutionalised against the top leadership (Mawdsley & White).

## Debates

How much real power the CC held versus Politburo/Secretariat/personal dictatorship; whether the purge-era CC was a rubber stamp; whether a late-Soviet democratised CC could have stabilised reform (authors' Ch. 6: unlikely given political tradition); whether post-1991 "reproduction" of this elite into Russian capitalism is best read as continuity or conversion.

## Carr 1979 on early CC politics

[[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*, Chs. 5–8, 12) narrates the CC as the formal peak that **atrophied** as Politburo, Orgburo, and secretariat (from 1919) and the general secretaryship (Stalin, April 1922) took day-to-day power. Lenin's Testament proposed expanding the CC to 50–100 to reduce split risk — a remedy Carr judges unlikely to touch the root problem. Opposition fights (Trotsky 1923; united opposition 1926–27) were decided by control of the machine and congress majorities more than by free CC deliberation. Complements [[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study]] long-run prosopography with early-period political narrative.
