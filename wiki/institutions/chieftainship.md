# Chieftainship

Recurrent African (and wider) political form: office of chief or king as a node of authority, often combining administrative, judicial, military, and ritual roles—or, in acephalous systems, the *absence* of such institutionalized office as a comparative baseline. Central case material in *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* (1940): Zulu, Ngwato, Bemba, Ankole, Kede (Group A–type centralized systems); Tallensi dual *na’am* / *tɛndaan*; Logoli–Vugusu leadership without chieftainship rights. This page synthesizes **attributed** APS treatments; it does not assert a single universal chiefship type in wiki voice.

## Definition and Scope

What counts as “chieftainship” is itself an analytic problem APS forces open:

- **Institutionalized office:** Hereditary or appointed political head with defined rights—tribute, judicial fines, labour, land allocation, war leadership, ritual for the common good—backed by force, mystical sanction, or both. Exemplars in APS: Zulu king and tribal chiefs (Gluckman); Ngwato *kgosi* (Schapera); Bemba territorial chiefs and paramount Citimukulu (Richards); Ankole Mugabe and client chiefs (Oberg); Kede Kuta and delegates (Nadel); Tallensi *na’ab* holding *na’am* (Fortes).
- **Polar / dual office:** Tallensi *tendaana* (Custodian of the Earth) is homologous with chiefship yet opposed—ritual orientation to the Earth, taboos inverse to chiefs’, no colonial political status—yet Fortes treats the *pair* as the central factor of Tale political organization, not chiefship alone.
- **Non-chieftainship leadership:** Wagner’s Logoli and Vugusu: political authority “inarticulate,” not linked to clearly defined rights and privileges “usually associated with institutionalized chieftainship”—no tribute rights, law enactment monopoly, raid call-up as office rights, formal installation of clan heads.

Colonial **Indirect Rule** and Native Authority systems created or remade “chiefs” as administrative agents (Fortes on Tallensi headmen-as-chiefs; Schapera on Protectorate Proclamations; Gluckman on Zulu under European rule). Scope of this page therefore includes: (1) indigenous office forms as of documentation moments; (2) dual and non-chiefly authority; (3) colonial chiefship as a distinct historical variant—not a transparent window on the precolonial past.

**Servicean [[chiefdom]] (distinct concept):** [[service-elman-r|Service]] (*[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*) uses **chiefdom** as a general evolutionary *level* — permanent redistributional coordination and hereditary rank — not as a synonym for every society that has a “chief.” A society may have chieftainship (APS Group A office) without fitting Service’s chiefdom type; Service’s type may include ranked polities outside the African APS sample. Keep the two analytic languages separate; cross-link via [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]] and [[chiefdom]].

### Group A–type cases (centralized)

- **Zulu (Gluckman):** Nation as allegiance to the king over hundreds of clans; king owns the land; regiments centralized about the capital uniquely in southern Africa; chiefs hold delegated judicial/administrative powers without control of age-regiments; dual ideology of royal brothers as support and rivals; mystical national rites vested in the *office* so that even slain tyrants’ spirits become sources of good. Colonial period: nation divided under white magistracy and tribal chiefs under European overrule.
- **Ngwato (Schapera):** Chief as ruler, judge, law-maker, economic director, formerly priest/magician and war leader; hereditary principle through wards and leading families; absorption of heterogeneous *bafaladi* under nuclear dynasty; Kgama’s legislative reforms create dual “traditional” and “Kgama’s” law; 1934 Proclamations subordinate chiefship to Administration approval and curtailed tribunals—supreme authority the District Commissioner’s line.
- **Bemba (Richards):** Rank based on kinship to chief; territorial *icalo* chieftainship; chief’s person affects land prosperity; *bakabilo* as hereditary ritual councillors and check on the paramount; integration through supernatural sanction and loyalty to paramount rather than age-regiments; white ordinances remake machinery.
- **Ankole (Oberg):** Kingship founded in Abachwezi/Ruhinda ideology and Bagyendanwa drum; Bahima clientship (*okutoizha*) welds pastoral freemen into state; Bairu serfdom and tribute as domination mechanism; Oberg’s label “conquest state”; British rule abolishes clientship/serfdom/slavery as political relationships.
- **Kede (Nadel):** Explicit State criteria—territorial non-tribal dominion, central administration, privileged ruling personnel; Kuta almost absolute via control of promotions; minority riverain rulers over *kintsoji*; moral sanction from Tsoede-invested first Kuta and “Rule over the Water”; vassalage to Etsu of Nupe with remitted taxes.

### Group B and borderline cases

- **Tallensi (Fortes):** No one with authority over all Tallensi; *na’am* purchased by competing lineage segments and mystically vested via Mamprusi elector (Kuna’aba) without feudal administrative rights; chief *primus inter pares* (Toŋraana’s modern claim to rule all Tallensi = distortion); chief and *tendaana* polar opposition with joint responsibility “to prosper the community”; chiefs/*tindaanas* without over-rights to farm-land rent; colonial force monopoly remakes chiefs as Administration agents and strips *tindaanas* of political status—headmen of chief-less clans become “chiefs.”
- **Logoli and Vugusu (Wagner):** Clan-level sovereignty; tribe as consciousness of unity without central overrule; leadership multifactorial and non-exclusive; **explicit contrast** with institutionalized chieftainship’s rights (tribute, law-making, raid call-up, stranger residence control).

## Theoretical Treatments

### Fortes and Evans-Pritchard (editors)

In the APS Introduction (and comparative programme of the volume), Fortes and Evans-Pritchard organize cases into **[[group-a-group-b-political-systems]]**: Group A societies with centralized authority, administrative machinery, and judicial institutions (chiefs/kings); Group B societies without government in that sense, ordered through segmentary lineage and balanced opposition ([[ordered-anarchy]], [[segmentary-lineage-system]]). They emphasize [[mystical-values-of-office]]—attachment to mystical symbols of office as binding force often stronger than utilitarian interest—and warn that designating autonomous political groups is always partly arbitrary (overlapping segmentary relations even in Group A). Political structure is not the whole social structure; clans, age-sets, ritual associations, affinity, and trade stretch beyond it. (Editorial claims owned mainly by intro extraction; case authors instantiate the frame.)

### Case authors (attributed)

- **Gluckman:** Kingship as national symbol and barrack-centered military establishment; scale gradient of ties (kinship at homestead → political bonds at tribe → military identity at nation); chiefs as symbols of tribal autonomy *within* federation.
- **Schapera:** Constitutional ethnography of chiefship under dual authority; hereditary family principle; legislation and public assemblies as living checks; colonial law as transforming not merely surrounding the office.
- **Richards:** “Dogma of descent” and identification of successor with predecessor; land prosperity tied to chief’s person and ancestors; *bakabilo* as institutionalized restraint; Bantu comparative typology of ruling-lineage concentration of power.
- **Oberg:** Conquest-state formation from pastoral/agricultural contact; clientship as created political bond (not only kinship); ideology (Abachwezi, drum) as sanctification of structure; classification of clientship vs. serfdom/slavery as contractual vs. compulsory.
- **Nadel:** Explicit State definition (Lowie, Oppenheimer); three supports of Kede chieftainship (mythic descent, economic monopoly, kin offices); absolute power via promotion dependence as functional for mobile river colonies—and its weakness (no legitimate check).
- **Fortes (Tallensi):** Polar *na’am* / *tɛndaan* as central political factor; office owned by maximal lineage (“chiefship belongs to all of us”); purchase of *na’am* as “indirect ballot” under economic equality; rejection of feudal analogy for Mamprusi investiture; ritual ideology as conceptual and sanctioning medium of structure; colonial monopoly of force as system-transforming.
- **Wagner:** Shift from political *institution* to political *function*; definition of political unit by consciousness of unity where no central authority; multifactorial “inarticulate” authority as negative definition of chieftainship.

## Debates

- **Is “chief” a vernacular category or colonial administrative invention?** APS case authors document both indigenous office vocabularies (*kgosi*, *na’ab*, Mugabe, Kuta, etc.) *and* colonial elevation of headmen and Native Authorities. Tallensi *tindaanas* lose political status while chiefs gain force-backed tax powers (Fortes)—a key instance of colonial reweighting of dual offices.
- **Group A / Group B typology:** Productive scaffold and later target of critique (history, class, scale, colonial co-production). Record as editors’ and volume’s frame; do not treat the binary as timeless taxonomy.
- **Mystical values vs. material sanctions:** APS stresses office mystique; case chapters also detail cattle, tribute, regiments, river tolls, and Administration guns. Weight both evidence classes; mechanism claims stay attributed.
- **Conquest and stratification:** Oberg’s conquest-state / Hima–Iru model (and period racial language) is a live historiography problem for Great Lakes studies—keep as attributed APS position.
- **Segmentary politics without chiefs:** Whether Tallensi dual office and Kavirondo clan leadership belong under “chieftainship” at all is an analytic choice; this page includes them as **comparative boundary cases** required by the APS corpus.

## Historiography

APS (1940) is a charter comparative statement of British Africanist political anthropology on chiefs and kingship. Later Manchester School conflict analysis (Gluckman), legal anthropology (Schapera’s wider corpus), Fortes’s Tallensi monographs, and postcolonial critiques of Indirect Rule chiefship are **not yet ingested**—update this page when they enter the collection rather than reverse-reading them into APS.
