# Occupational group (corporation)

## Definition and Scope

In *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* Book III ch. 3, [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] proposes the **occupational group** (*corporation*, association of workers of the same sort cooperating in the same function) as the modern secondary group that can supply [[social-integration|integration]] and [[social-regulation|regulation]] once family, locality, and religion no longer suffice. Identity of origin, culture, and occupation is “the richest material for common life.” Three-fold advantage: omnipresent, ubiquitous, control extends to the greatest part of life — not intermittent like politics, follows the worker unlike family.

Not merely a private interest combination: it must become a **definite, recognized organ of public life** with real functions (insurance, aid, pensions, dispute resolution, contract conditions, restraining exploitation) and a moral individuality (customs, rights, duties).

### The 1902 argument (*Division of Labor*, 2nd-edition preface)

The corporative argument's fullest statement is the preface Durkheim added to the second edition of *[[durkheim-division-of-labor-1893|The Division of Labor]]* (1902), "Some Remarks on Occupational Groups" (attributed throughout):

- **Diagnosis** — modern economic life is in a state of "juridical and moral **[[anomie|anomy]]**": occupational ethics in commerce and industry exist "only in the most rudimentary state," unlike the developed professional ethics of law, medicine, the army, or the priesthood. Because nothing "restrains the active forces and assigns them limits," conflict is settled by "the law of the strongest" and "the state of war is necessarily chronic." This anomic state is "the cause... of the incessantly recurrent conflicts... of the economic world."
- **Anomie and liberty** — the unregulated market's "liberty" is illusory: "liberty... is itself the product of regulation." Only social rules prevent the strong from exploiting the weak.
- **Why the occupational group** — neither the market's own spontaneity, nor the State (too remote, and economic life "too specialized" for state competence), can supply the needed regulation. Only "a group intimate enough with [an occupation] to know its functioning" can regulate it: the corporation, "formed of all the agents of the same industry, united and organized into a single body."
- **Not a guild revival** — Durkheim rebutted the "historical anachronism" charge: he traced corporations from the early Roman Republic through the Empire's forced hereditary *collegia* to the 11th–12th-c. European revival, arguing a recurrent institution answers "a durable and profound need," so the 18th-c. suppression (Turgot) left that need unmet. Historically corporations were **moral milieux**, not mere interest groups — religious communities (patron cult, common banquets), mutual-aid/burial societies, quasi-familial bonds — "heir of the family" for occupational functions.
- **Modern shape** — reorganize on a **national** scale to match the national/international market (the medieval corporation's tight bond to the commune caused its obsolescence); make it a recognized secondary organ between State and individual, since "a society composed of an infinite number of unorganized individuals... constitutes a veritable sociological monstrosity"; political representation could shift toward occupational constituencies, the corporation becoming "the elementary division of the State."
- **Not a panacea** — distributive justice (ending inherited inequality — "so long as there are rich and poor at birth, there cannot be just contract"; see [[forced-division-of-labor]]) is a distinct, additional requirement, but corporative reform is "the first condition" for other reforms' efficacy.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Durkheim cites Greco-Roman and medieval corporative longevity as evidence of historical need; the French revolutionary suppression without replacement, he argued, replaced corporative egoism with more corrosive individual egoism — “the only demolition of all those then accomplished which we have to regret.” Old local closed autonomy was unfit for a unified nation; remedy is enlarge base and reconnect to national life under state influence, not abolish.

## Theoretical Treatments

| Reading | Claim (attributed to Durkheim 1897) |
|---|---|
| **Remedy for egoistic suicide** | Restore group grip so the individual feels bound to a collective existence that precedes and survives him |
| **Remedy for anomic suicide** | Supply collective forces that regulate economic appetites; set equitable shares; moderate strong/weak; prevent production’s “morbid fever” |
| **vs State alone** | State hypertrophied yet impotent; too distant and levelling; individuals as “liquid molecules” |
| **vs territorial decentralization** | Local patriotisms cannot be resuscitated; only **occupational decentralization** multiplies moral centers without splitting national unity |
| **vs religion for economic regulation** | Religious contempt for riches / passive resignation unadapted once earthly interests dominate collective life |
| **Political organization** | Electoral college by corps rather than territory only works if corps are first real moral individualities |

Program limit: no detailed corporative blueprint in *Suicide* — seek germs in the past and “set resolutely to work.”
