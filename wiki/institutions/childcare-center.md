# Childcare Center

Center-based care and preschool for young children. In this wiki, the primary analytical treatment is [[small-mario-luis|Mario Luis Small]]'s multi-method study of New York City centers and urban mothers (*[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains]]*, 2009; [[small-unanticipated-gains-study]]). Beyond the manifest function (childcare/preschool), centers are **routine organizations** that can [[organizational-broker|broker]] social and organizational ties for parents — often as **unanticipated gains** relative to the enrollment motive.

## Definition and Scope

Formal organizations providing group care/education for young children, distinct from informal kin care or in-home babysitting. Small's case: licensed NYC centers (five boroughs; ~1,683 licensed at 2004 survey frame; survey n=293 directors). National comparison: Fragile Families mothers of young children in large U.S. cities.

Funding/sector types in Small's NYC material include Head Start, ACS city-funded centers, private nonprofits, and for-profits — with systematically different parent-involvement and organizational-tie profiles.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

*(Not covered in depth by the ingested source — U.S. urban focus.)* Small documents the late-20th-century U.S. rise in maternal labor-force participation and young-child center enrollment (Ch. 2 figures). Comparative institutional regimes (e.g. European universal preschool) are outside the book's primary data.

## Theoretical Treatments

*(Attributed — Small 2009.)*

- **Opportunities:** pickup/drop-off policy, field trips, parties, shared spaces structure whether strangers meet.
- **Inducements:** fund-raisers, cleanups, hiring committees, parents associations induce cooperative interaction.
- **Trust and obligations:** repeated noncompetitive, homophilous encounter; emergency contact lists as formal support networks.
- **Organizational ties:** validation, storage, referral, collaboration with external agencies (health, education testing, social services, goods).
- **External drivers of brokerage:** state mandates (esp. Head Start family-services model), large nonprofits as higher-order brokers, professional norms of holistic care, neighborhood location as targeting signal.

Centers vary sharply: some generate many friendships and rich organizational ties; others function more as terminals with thin parent interaction.

## Debates
