# Prison

## Definition and Scope

The **prison** (penitentiary, house of detention) is a closed institution that holds persons under legal authority, typically as punishment, remand, or correction. This page is primarily fed by [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s genealogical account in *[[foucault-discipline-and-punish-1977|Discipline and Punish]]* (1975/1977) — a history of the **birth of the prison** as the penalty *par excellence* of modern societies, and of the [[disciplinary-power|disciplinary]] techniques that make it operable. Adjacent theoretical treatment: Goffman's [[total-institution]] (via the [[mental-hospital]] and *[[goffman-asylums-study|Asylums]]*), which analyzes the interaction order of closed establishments rather than the genealogy of penality.

Foucault's scope is early modern–nineteenth-century **Western Europe, especially France**, with comparative glances at England, the Low Countries, and the early United States (Walnut Street). It is not a global or contemporary mass-incarceration ethnography.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

- **Foucault (attributed):** contrasts (1) sovereign **scaffold** penality (public torture-execution); (2) reformers' dream of a **punitive city** of signs and analogical penalties (Beccaria et al.); (3) the **disciplinary prison** that actually won. Within the prison form: Auburn (congregate work, silence) vs. Philadelphia (cellular isolation) as rival techniques of transformation.
- **Models he cites:** Amsterdam Rasphuis; Ghent; English reformatory prisons; Walnut Street (Philadelphia); Mettray (agricultural colony / pure discipline, 1840).
- Cross-cultural and contemporary carceral systems (colonial prisons, US mass incarceration, etc.) are largely **outside** the ingested source — coverage gap.

## Theoretical Treatments

### Foucault — political technology of the body (attributed)

- The prison form **antedates** its systematic use in the penal codes; it was elaborated as a general apparatus to render individuals docile and useful before the law defined detention as the penalty *par excellence*.
- **Double foundation of self-evidence:** (1) juridico-economic — deprivation of liberty as equal, quantifiable penalty; (2) technico-disciplinary — the prison as apparatus for transforming individuals (like a strict barracks, school, workshop).
- From the outset, penal imprisonment covered both deprivation of liberty **and** a technical corrective task — not liberty-loss first and reform later.
- **Complete and austere institution:** isolation; penal labour (as discipline and moral form of wages, not primarily production); modulation of penalties according to progress; independence of the carceral apparatus relative to pure judicial sentence.
- **Delinquent vs. offender:** the penitentiary produces a biographical, characterological object (the *delinquent*) that differs from the juridical subject of the offence; "twin brothers" of justice and the prison — the prison takes "vengeance" on justice by imposing its own knowledge-power.
- **"Failure" reversed:** ask what is *served* by failure — maintenance of delinquency, recidivism, closed delinquent milieux, post-sentence brandings. Prison, Foucault proposes, succeeds extremely well in **producing delinquency** as a usable, supervised form of illegality within a police–prison–delinquency circuit (→ [[carceral-archipelago]], [[instrument-effects]] as related reading of functional "failure").
- Prison is **not endogenous** to the reformers' semiotic penal system; it comes from the colonization of legal punishment by [[disciplinary-power]].

### Goffman — total institution (attributed)

Goffman treats prisons among [[total-institution|total institutions]] (alongside asylums, monasteries, barracks): batch living, mortification of the self, underlife. Different program — mid-twentieth-century US ethnography of interaction, not genealogy of modern penality. Complementary: Foucault on *how the form was historically assembled*; Goffman on *how closed institutions are lived*.

## Debates

- **Humanization vs. redistribution of power:** Foucault's central polemic against progress narratives of penal reform (open interpretive dispute with legal-historical humanization accounts).
- **Functionalism of delinquency-production:** whether reading prison failure as useful over-closes explanation (critical reception).
- **Goffman vs. Foucault:** interaction order vs. genealogical power — rarely a formal "debate page," but a standing contrast of methods and objects.
- Contemporary mass incarceration / racialized carceral state: distinct research programs; do not silently equate with Foucault's 19th-c. European "birth of the prison."
