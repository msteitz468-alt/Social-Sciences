# Temple Economy

The Sumerian temple economy denotes the institutional role of temple estates as major landholders, employers, and redistributive hubs in Mesopotamian city-states, from the Early Dynastic period through Ur III, per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]]. Its scope and structure were significantly revised by later historiography, discussed below.

## Definition and Scope

Roux explicitly critiques an older "temple-city" model — the theory that all city-state land was temple property and all inhabitants were temple servants or clients — as a **historiographic overreach** that arose from the chance survival of unusually large archives specifically from the Baba temple at Girsu ([[lagash]]), treated as if representative of the whole economy. Modern reappraisal of Presargonic tablets, per Roux, shows a more mixed economy in which temple-land, palace-land, and private landownership coexisted.

Temples' national gods were said to "ideally own" their city-state — Lagash "belonged" to Ningirsu, Umma to Shara, Ur to the moon-god Nanna — but Roux describes this explicitly as a "fictitious concept," an ideological/legal fiction rather than a description of literal ownership. This distinction matters: temple ownership language in royal and administrative inscriptions should not be read in wiki voice as documenting literal property title.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

**Early Dynastic Sumer.** Temple-land is estimated by Roux at about one-third of arable land around a city, divided into three categories: "land of the Lord" (feeding priests and officials), "food land" (allotted to farmers and officials, revocable, not full ownership), and "plough land" (let to tenants for one-seventh to one-eighth of the harvest share). The temple economy redistributed goods as wages or rations to large numbers of dependent laborers — mostly women, also men and slaves — working in temple workshops (milling, spinning and weaving, brewing, cooking, gardening); temple farmers could be conscripted for war or public works (fortifications, canals, sanctuaries). Occupational specialization was extensive, per tablets from Girsu and Shuruppak: separate shepherds for male and female donkeys, fishermen divided by water type (fresh, brackish, sea), even a "corporation" of snake-charmers with its own chief, overseen by a bureaucratic apparatus of scribes, controllers, overseers, and chief inspectors (*nu-bànda*) and superintendents (*agrig*) under the high priest (*sanga*). Trade with foreign countries "as far away as Afghanistan and the Indus valley" was, per Roux, largely in the hands of the temple or palace.

The palace (*é-gal*) formed a second major economic unit, less well documented than the temple; a Shuruppak tablet of roughly 2600 BCE records the ruler maintaining 600–700 soldiers with equipment, and rulers occasionally purchased land from wealthy individuals or officials via attested contracts. Private-property contracts, more numerous in Akkad than in Sumer proper, show individuals of varying rank freely selling, exchanging, donating, or leasing land, livestock, and slaves, with plot sizes varying hugely by status — from 240-plus hectares for a high official down to 1–6 hectares for a low-status vendor such as a civil servant, currier, or stone-cutter.

Uruinimgina, the last *ensi* of [[lagash]] (late 24th century BCE per Roux's chronology), issued well-known reforms recorded in his own inscription: reducing abusive taxation on burials and weddings, revoking corrupt officials, and restoring land and property "encroached upon" back to the temple estate of Ningirsu — framed as "freeing the citizens of Lagash from usury, monopoly, hunger, theft and assault." Roux notes these reforms had no lasting effect: Lagash fell to Lugalzagesi of Umma regardless, within Uruinimgina's reign. A broader Early Dynastic trend, per Roux, was the gradual separation of Palace from Temple authority — by roughly 2400 BCE, Entemena of Lagash was no longer the city's high priest, that office having passed to a separate *sanga*, Dudu, per Entemena's own inscription.

**Ur III state-redistributive economy.** Under Shulgi and Amar-Sin, emphasis shifted decisively from Temple toward Palace, with the king's authority framed as personal and as a mandate from Enlil, conditioned on respecting tradition; the king nominally owned all land and goods on Enlil's behalf, but in practice held directly only "food land" in Ur's home territory, with Palace income from taxes, duties, tribute, and gifts funding temple-building, canal work, and public works empire-wide. The "bala" tax system rotated monthly levies (chiefly livestock) from each *ensi*, collected at Puzrish-Dagan/Drehem near Nippur, alongside tribute (*gun*) in silver, cattle, and hides from outlying provinces and vassals, and gifts from foreign rulers — all recorded by Drehem scribes. State-run large-scale agriculture (wheat and barley) operated under a "prefect" (*shabra*) with supervisory staff; roughly 255,000 hectolitres of wheat were harvested around Girsu in Amar-Sin's second year alone. State-run "industrial" production covered textiles, leather, and flour (though not metalwork, which remained with private artisans): roughly 15,000 women were employed in Girsu's textile industry, and a Girsu cereal-processing unit produced some 1,100 tons of flour per year plus bread, beer, oil, grindstones, and pots, employing 134 specialists and 858 skilled workers (breakdown: 669 women, 86 men, 103 teenagers). International trade was state-controlled; "merchants" (*damgar*) were mostly civil-servant intermediaries, and silver functioned as an exchange standard/quasi-money but was hoarded by officials and circulated only with Palace authorization.

Roux issues an important methodological caution here: the Drehem, Ur, Nippur, Girsu, and Umma archives together constitute a vast administrative corpus — roughly 40,000 tablets published as of his writing, with as many or more unpublished — but document **only state-institutional transactions**; the private sector remains "completely occluded," and the sheer volume of material plus uncertain Sumerian titulature mean, in his words, "it will be a long time before" a coherent socioeconomic synthesis is possible. This caution caps confidence on all Ur III economic claims drawn from these archives.

Ration and wage scales varied by age, sex, and rank: a minimum unskilled factory ration was 20 liters of barley per month plus 3.5 liters of oil and 2 kg of wool per year, supplemented seasonally with dates, beans, spices, fish, meat, and clothes; an *engar* (chief ploughman/agricultural expert) received double rations plus a food-land plot and kitchen garden, potentially enough surplus to buy servants or a house. Interest rates on barley loans reached 33%, and the poorest sometimes hired out children or a wife as debt-bondage servants — Roux's summary judgment (attributed): "a large proportion of the Sumero-Akkadian population lived rather miserably."

**Amorite-period reduction.** Under Amorite dynasties following Ur III's collapse, per Roux, temples were reduced to (in his own phrase) "land-owners among other land-owners, tax-payers among other tax-payers," as the broader economy shifted toward private large landed estates worked by tenant farmers, craft workshops, and merchant associations (*karum*) operating with state capital but private profit-sharing (see [[amorites]]).

## Theoretical Treatments

The "temple-city" model (all land as temple property) represents an early 20th-century scholarly generalization from a single, unusually well-preserved archive; Roux's explicit revision of this model — grounded in reappraisal of the broader Presargonic tablet corpus — is itself a documented historiographic correction, not merely additional nuance, and should be flagged as such wherever the older model appears in secondary literature.

## Key Studies

Primary evidentiary bases: the Baba-temple archive at Girsu ([[lagash]]); the Drehem/Puzrish-Dagan administrative archive and related corpora from Ur, Nippur, Girsu, and Umma (Ur III); Uruinimgina's reform inscription; Entemena's silver-vase inscription.

## Debates

The scope and representativeness of the temple economy remains bounded by archive survival bias (Baba-temple for Early Dynastic Lagash; Drehem/Ur/Nippur/Girsu/Umma for Ur III) — in both periods, Roux cautions that the surviving textual record documents institutional/state transactions far better than any private sector, and generalizations beyond the documented state sector should be treated with corresponding caution.

## Society and Economy (Postgate 1994)

### The temple as household, and a source-critical caveat

*(Attributed throughout to [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)* Postgate treats the temple, alongside the palace, as a "household" or estate — an analytic borrowed from the general anthropological notion of the great household/*oikos*: both institutions hold land, maintain dependent staff and personnel systems, run storage and production facilities, and redistribute income to staff. Postgate distinguishes their character rather than collapsing them: the temple functions as a communal, religious identity-symbol with charitable and banking functions and diffuse ideological legitimacy, while the palace is a secular administrative and military authority pursuing what Postgate himself calls an "exploitative strategy" — though he notes "the two institutions often appear similar in practice." Postgate does not use "great organisations" or "two households" as a fixed technical term; this is his own structuring device (treating temple and palace economy as parallel case studies), not a named school of thought.

Postgate explicitly, calibratedly rejects the older "temple-city" model that a generation of scholarship (following Deimel-vintage analysis of the Bau/Baba Temple archive at Girsu, popularized by V. Gordon Childe's claim that at Lagash the temple owned all the land and employed the entire population) had built into orthodoxy until the 1950s: "This extreme view is now discredited. We cannot any longer maintain that because the temple collected commodities and distributed them to its dependants the entire economy operated through 'redistribution,' or that the priests controlled all agricultural production and commercial activity" (as of Postgate 1994). Postgate roots his skepticism in explicit source criticism: the Baba Temple archive — roughly 1,600 tablets — was excavated without full archaeological control, subsequently looted and scattered across collections worldwide, and the main studies of it (Deimel's above all) date to the 1920s; a single, unusually large and prematurely systematized archive should not be read as representative of the whole Early Dynastic–Akkadian economy. This historiographic dispute — old "temple-city"/redistribution orthodoxy versus Postgate's revised, more limited reading, with Diakonoff's extended-family/commune emphasis and Nissen's skepticism about the antiquity of southern temple dominance as further live, explicitly non-adjudicated positions — is routed to [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]] rather than resolved here.

### Landholding, prebends, staff

Temple landholding followed the three-part organization detailed on [[land-tenure]] (Deimel's schema): land under direct temple cultivation by the temple's own workforce; "prebend fields" assigned to office-holders for sustenance, scaled to the importance of the office; and surplus land rented out to private individuals. Temple personnel — priests and priestesses, scribes, administrative staff, craftsmen, water-carriers, doorkeepers — received offerings and temple income as redistributed pay rather than these serving mostly as literal offerings to the gods. Over time (already visible in Ur III, further elaborated in the Old Babylonian period) many temple posts became hereditary, purchasable, or rentable sinecures termed prebends (*isqum*, "lot" — a term borrowed by Assyriologists from medieval Europe), divisible in principle to as little as one day's tenure per year, with individuals sometimes holding several offices simultaneously. Postgate distinguishes "indivisible" posts (administrative or specialist roles — scribes, craftsmen — requiring continuity and expertise, not prebendary in this fragmentary sense) from "divisible" cultic posts (e.g. *gudu4*/*pašīšum*, "priest of god X") that were more freely subdivided; the *sanga*/*šangûm* (temple head) is sometimes rendered "temple-administrator" rather than "priest," reflecting the office's administrative character.

### Herds, workshops, agriculture

The agricultural base was barley agriculture on irrigated land, with cattle used primarily for traction (the seed-plough) rather than meat, and sheep and goats kept mainly for milk and wool (usually herded together); pigs were kept mainly for fat. Old Babylonian shepherd contracts show flock sizes from 4 to 200-plus animals (ewes usually the majority), with terms fixed by an annual productivity ratio — Larsa/Rim-Sin state-flock contracts assumed a 15% expected loss on adult ewes offset against an 80% expected birth rate, and roughly 2 minas (~1 kg) of wool expected per animal per year; the shepherd kept milk products, a wool quota, any animals surplus to the ratio, plus food/clothing rations and a subordinate's pay. Institutional flocks operated at large scale: the Ur III Drehem (Puzriš-Dagan) archive records totals over 60 months including 347,394 sheep and goats and 28,601 oxen, and a single Early Dynastic IIIa tablet from Abu Salabikh records roughly 14,000 sheep and goats. Cattle required substantial barley supplementation (unlike sheep and goats, which can subsist on grazing alone); Ur III administrators calculated oxen's barley ration together with seed corn when costing cultivation. Postgate states plainly that the extent and rationale of large-institution cattle-herding beyond traction remains uncertain — dairy productivity recorded at Ur institutions was "poor and... not of paramount concern," and he declines to adjudicate between possible motives (leather production, specialized late-Old-Babylonian commercial production) with confidence.

### Rations, offerings, and banking/charitable functions

Ration and wage scales for temple dependents varied by role. Free-will offerings (*arua*) — distinct from regular consumed cult offerings — became permanent temple property: animals, silver rings, boats, grindstones, wool, bitumen, and, notably, people (prisoners of war dedicated by kings; women and children dedicated by ordinary individuals). Ur III *arua* texts recording war-captive dedications show a marked absence of adult male captives, which Gelb infers (an attributed inference, not directly evidenced) may reflect that adult male captives were often killed or mutilated rather than enslaved.

The temple also performed banking and charitable functions beyond cult, per Postgate: the Code of Hammurapi obliges the temple to ransom a captured village member; the Nanna Temple at Ur functioned as a bank, supplying trading capital for merchants voyaging to Tilmun (Bahrain) in Ur III/Old Babylonian times, with merchants making thank-offerings (e.g. silver boats) on safe return; at Assur, merchants dedicated trading profits to temples as a form of inviolable reinvested capital. Charitable practice is attested at Khafajah's Sin Temple and Sippar's Šamaš Temple, where some barley loans to individuals were interest-free against a normal rate of one-third, implying famine relief; temples are also recorded purchasing children at no specified price, apparently absorbing orphans, illegitimate children, and other social "rejects." Temples additionally derived revenue from judicial and ceremonial services — serving as fora for solemn oaths and judicial proceedings, and hiring out the "journey of the divine weapon" (bringing a god's symbol out to settle boundary disputes or harvest-share distributions), the right to which fee was itself a saleable prebend.

### Domestic-economy calibration (Polanyi coexistence, not pure redistribution)

Postgate adopts Polanyi's tripartite typology of economic integration — reciprocity, redistribution, market exchange — but stakes out an explicitly calibrated, anti-pure-redistribution position: the three modes "are not mutually exclusive," and "one of the complexities of early Mesopotamian society is precisely the co-existence of these three modes and their variations" (as of Postgate 1994). This directly qualifies a Polanyi-style pure-redistribution reading of the Mesopotamian economy; see [[redistribution]] for the concept-level treatment. After the fall of Ur III, Postgate argues the merchant class (organized as the *kārum*, "quay/wharf," a merchant association) stepped into the administrative vacuum left by the collapsed bureaucratic state, continuing to organize both foreign trade and internal redistribution on its own initiative; by the Larsa/Hammurapi period the head of the *kārum* (*wakil tamkārī*) mediated palace–merchant relations, and the *kārum* could act as a local legal/administrative authority even in non-commercial matters.

## Kemp — New Kingdom Egyptian temple economy (2018)

*(Attributed — [[kemp-barry-j]], *[[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]* Ch.6.)*

Distinct from the Mesopotamian temple-estate debates above: Kemp’s New Kingdom temples are **corporate institutions** with stone monumental cores, fortress-like enclosure walls, service buildings, land and personnel (“Estate of Amun” scale), processional boat shrines, and buffers of storage (e.g. Ramesseum). Public access is limited; votive “places of the ear” and outer shrines handle popular approach. Temple cult is dual — hidden fixed images and **revealed portable barques** — and processions (Opet, Beautiful Festival of the Valley) stage king–god partnership for urban crowds. Local Old/Middle Kingdom temples had often been mayoral adjuncts in mudbrick towns; the NK brings giant stone temples into cityscapes (Thebes/Karnak type). Economic weight: redistribution and employment rival palace channels; Ch.7 still insists private markets and traders operate beside temple/state provisioning.

Use as Egypt case of religious–economic institution; do not equate with Sumerian “temple-city” models without argument.

## Wilkinson densification (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wilkinson-toby]], *[[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]].)*

Amun estate wealth and Theban priesthood as political actors (especially TIP dual rule); temple building as royal legitimacy (Hatshepsut, Rameses II); late periods see temples as continuity under foreign kings. Complements Kemp’s NK corporate-temple model with narrative of priestly power versus king.
