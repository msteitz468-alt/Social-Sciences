# Comintern (Communist International)

## Definition and Scope

The **Communist International (Comintern)**, or Third International, was founded in Moscow in March 1919 as the organizational centre of world communist parties. [[carr-edward-hallett|Carr]] describes the founding congress (~50 communists, 35 mandates from 19 countries): manifesto drafted by [[trotsky-leon|Trotsky]]; theses by [[lenin-vladimir|Lenin]] denouncing bourgeois democracy and proclaiming the [[dictatorship-of-the-proletariat]]; executive (IKKI) with Zinoviev as president and Radek as secretary (*[[carr-russian-revolution-lenin-stalin-1979]]*).

Carr's thesis: though founders thought in internationalist terms, Comintern was "not a fusion of national communist parties… but the harnessing of a number of weak, embryonic foreign groups to an essentially Russian organization."

## Cross-Cultural Variation

### West (Carr Ch. 2, 9, 18)

- **Second congress (July 1920):** 200+ delegates; work in unions and parliaments; British CP to seek Labour affiliation; **21 conditions** of admission for a single disciplined international party.
- **Splits:** USPD–KPD merger (Germany); French SFIO → PCF; Italian PSI split → PCI — hailed as triumphs as the postwar wave receded (March 1921 German fiasco).
- **Diplomacy vs revolution:** Rapallo and secret military collaboration with Germany coexisted with failed 1923 Hamburg rising; Curzon ultimatum (1923); de jure recognition wave 1924; Zinoviev letter; Locarno isolation; Arcos raid and British break (May 1927).
- **Third period (late 1920s):** Social-Fascism line against social democracy; Carr links disasters in Germany and elsewhere to this ultra-left turn under [[socialism-in-one-country]] orthodoxy.

### East (Carr Ch. 10)

- **Colonial theses (1920):** Alliance of national and colonial liberation movements with Soviet Russia; support national-revolutionary movements even of bourgeois-democratic character in backward countries.
- **Baku congress of peoples of the East:** ~2000 delegates, mostly Central Asian/Muslim; anti-British theme; limited lasting results. Far Eastern congress (Moscow 1922) weaker.
- **China:** Joffe–Sun (1923); Borodin; CCP–KMT [[united-front]]; May 30th movement 1925; northern expedition; **Shanghai massacre April 1927** and collapse of Soviet involvement — nadir of eastern policy in Carr's arc.
- **Peripheral:** Mongolia as Soviet-patronized republic; Japan largely impenetrable; limited Middle Eastern traction.

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Dual function (Carr):** World revolution as Marxist duty **and** defensive weapon of the Soviet regime — compatible only while European revolution seemed imminent; after civil-war survival, "security and stability" quietly displaced imminent international upheaval.
- **Bolshevization (fifth congress):** Transmission of what was "international and of general significance in Russian Bolshevism" to sections — reinforced by socialism in one country (USSR as unique exemplar).
- **Opposition critique:** Trotsky's 1928 programme critique from Alma-Ata blamed socialism in one country for Comintern disasters (Carr).
