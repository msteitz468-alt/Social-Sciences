# Plantation

## Definition and Scope

An agro-industrial estate form organizing large-scale cultivation (and often primary processing) of export crops under concentrated control of land, labor, and capital. In [[mintz-sidney-w|Mintz]]'s treatment of sugar (*[[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985|Sweetness and Power]]*, 1985), the plantation is not merely a farm but an early industrial organization of time, discipline, and technical process — a [[factory-in-the-field]]. New World plantations were "the invention of Europe," overseas experiments staffed for the most part with enslaved Africans from the early sixteenth century, later also with contract laborers from India, China, and elsewhere.

**What counts as a case (Mintz):** sugar estates with mills/boiling-houses producing for metropolitan markets; contrasts with peasant smallholding and with traditional small brown-sugar makers who sell to local poor consumers. See also [[sugar-plantation-complex]] for the wider circuit.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

*(From Mintz 1985.)*

| Variant | Features (as Mintz recounts) |
|---|---|
| Mediterranean / Arab-era | Cane and sugar-making after Arab expansion; Sicily, Spain, Maghrib, islands — precursor epoch |
| Atlantic islands | Madeira, Canaries, São Tomé — bridge to New World scale |
| New World slave plantation | Early 16th c. onward; enslaved African labor; British Caribbean takeoff (Barbados+) supplying England |
| Post-emancipation estate | Indenture and legal constraints on freed people's land/voting/collective bargaining (British West Indies as Mintz narrates) |
| 20th-c. corporate cane | e.g. Puerto Rico south coast for North American market; manual field labor still widespread in 1948 |
| Traditional brown sugar | Jamaican loaves; Haitian *rapadou*; declining economic but persistent cultural importance |

Dual structure *within* single societies is integral: modern mass production and archaic small works coexist.

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Mintz (1985):** plantation/mill as industrial form; slaves as false commodities in a circuit that also proletarianizes metropolitan consumers of sugar; production and consumption interdigitated; outside meaning of planter and free-trade politics.
- **[[anthropological-political-economy]] / [[wolf-eric-r]]:** plantations as sites of labor mobilization within expanding colonial and capitalist fields of force.
- **[[taussig-michael]] (1980):** southern Cauca Valley sugar agribusiness as the site where free-peasant use-value production meets wage labour and [[devil-contract|devil-contract]] lore; cane as "Green Monster" / "God of the landlords" in a 1972 peasant broadsheet he quotes; plantation work as metaphysically evil relative to own-plot agriculture ([[taussig-devil-commodity-fetishism-study]]).
- **Political economy pamphlets (Child, Thomas, etc., as Mintz cites):** plantation production as national wealth, tax base, stimulus to manufacturing, and "civilizing" project — outside-meaning discourse.

## Debates

Whether plantations are feudal, capitalist, or hybrid articulations; how far "factory in the field" generalizes beyond sugar; Williams thesis on capitalism and slavery (engaged in Mintz's secondary literature); post-emancipation labor control vs free labor ideology; whether plantation devil lore is systematic anti-capitalist critique or envy/risk regulation (Taussig vs functionalist reductions).
