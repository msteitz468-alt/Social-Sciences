## Definition and Scope

David Reich, drawing on the sociological literature he cites, defines caste as a group that interacts economically with outsiders but segregates itself socially through endogamy (as of Reich 2018) — a wiki-voice brute description of the definitional criterion, not a contested finding. In India, caste operates at two nested levels: *varna*, a broad four-plus rank system (Brahmins, Kshatriyas, Vaishya, Shudras, plus Dalits/"Scheduled Castes" and "Scheduled Tribes"), and *jati*, a minimum of roughly 4,600 (by some accounts up to around 40,000) individually named endogamous groups, each assigned a varna rank. Caste was legally abolished in India in 1947, but Reich states it continues to shape marriage and socialization choices today (as of Reich 2018).

Reich documents a structurally comparable system among the Ari of highland Ethiopia, an intricate caste arrangement of three genetically and socially differentiated endogamous subgroups — Cultivators, Blacksmiths, and Potters (as of Reich 2018).

## Cross-Cultural Variation

**India.** Jati-level endogamy produces population-genetic signatures: genetic differentiation between jati groups living in the same Indian village was found to be typically two to three times greater than that between northern and southern Europeans, and could not be explained by [[ancestral-north-indians|ANI]] ancestry proportion, region, or social status alone (as of Reich 2018). About one-third of Indian jati groups analyzed showed [[founder-event|population bottlenecks]] as strong as or stronger than those documented in Finns or Ashkenazi Jews, a pattern confirmed in a larger dataset of 250-plus jati groups (as of Reich 2018). The Vysya, a middle-caste group of roughly 5 million people in Andhra Pradesh, show a bottleneck dated to roughly 3,000–2,000 years ago and carry a recessive mutation (low butyrylcholinesterase, causing prolonged paralysis under muscle relaxants) at about 20% allele frequency — high enough to affect roughly 4% of the group homozygously (as of Reich 2018).

Caste-based endogamy in India correlates with ancestry, and the pattern is strongly sex-biased: groups of traditionally higher social status carry more [[ancestral-north-indians|ANI]]/West-Eurasian-related ancestry, concentrated on the paternal (Y-chromosome) line, while mitochondrial (maternal) DNA remains largely of local, [[ancestral-south-indians|ASI]]-derived origin even among high-status groups — suggesting West-Eurasian-related males were more highly placed in the caste system and sometimes married lower-ranking local women (as of Reich 2018). See [[sex-biased-admixture]].

**Ethiopia.** The Ari's greater genetic affinity to a 4,500-year-old local ancient individual, relative to other Ethiopian groups, indicates endogamous barriers persisting at least 4,500 years — described by Reich as the strongest evidence of endogamy he is aware of, older than the documented Indian evidence, which so far reaches back only a couple of thousand years (as of Reich 2018).

Reich notes that caste's repressive character prompted several major religious movements — Jainism, Buddhism, and Sikhism arose partly in reaction to it, offering refuge from the system, and Islam's spread in India was aided by conversion among low-status groups (per Reich's account).

## Theoretical Treatments

Two attributed positions on the origin and antiquity of jati-level endogamy are in live dispute; see [[caste-origins-debate]] for the full position statements. In brief: anthropologist [[karve-irawati|Irawati Karve]] argued jati arose from formerly independent endogamous tribal groups incorporated hierarchically by political elites; historian/anthropologist [[dirks-nicholas|Nicholas Dirks]] argued strong endogamy was substantially a British colonial-era innovation rather than an ancient practice. Reich's genetic findings are presented, in attributed voice, as evidence favoring deep pre-colonial antiquity for strict jati endogamy over the colonial-invention account (as of Reich 2018).

Reich, writing as an Ashkenazi Jew, offers an explicit reflexive analogy between his own upbringing within Ashkenazi Jewish endogamy norms and his understanding of how Indian caste endogamy perpetuates itself — a personal, attributed framing, not a claim of structural equivalence (as of Reich 2018).
