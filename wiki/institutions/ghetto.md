# Ghetto (racial residential)

## Definition and Scope

In *[[massey-denton-american-apartheid-1993|American Apartheid]]*, the **black ghetto** is the institutionally maintained, racially homogeneous residential form that isolates African Americans in U.S. cities — not a voluntary ethnic enclave but a product of white prejudice, discrimination, and state policy. Massey & Denton analogize the system to **apartheid** (title metaphor; South African Group Areas epigraph in Ch 1). Distinct from Chicago-school "natural areas" as purely ecological outcomes.

[[wilson-william-julius|Wilson]] (*[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|The Truly Disadvantaged]]*, 1987) treats the contemporary ghetto less as a continuous institutional constant than as a **class-transformed** space: after working- and middle-class black exit, residual neighborhoods become sites of [[concentrated-poverty]], [[social-isolation]], and [[concentration-effects]] producing the [[urban-underclass]]. He contrasts pre-1960 ghetto social organization with post-1970 dislocation levels.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Book's comparative claim (attributed, Massey & Denton): no other U.S. racial/ethnic group has experienced sustained black-level segregation and isolation across the 20th century; European immigrant enclaves were more temporary and permeable.
