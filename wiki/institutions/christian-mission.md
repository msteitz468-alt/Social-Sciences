# Christian Mission

> Analyzed as the cutting edge of colonialism in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991). This page treats the Christian mission as a recurrent social form; its landmark case is the Nonconformist mission to the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]].

## Definition and Scope

A Christian mission is an organized enterprise to propagate the faith among a target population, typically entailing a station, resident evangelists, schooling, and material as well as spiritual intervention. The Comaroffs' study concerns the **Nonconformist** (dissenting Protestant) variant: the **London Missionary Society (LMS**, founded 1795, predominantly Congregationalist) and the **Wesleyan Methodist Missionary Society (WMMS**, founded 1813).

## Cross-Cultural Variation

The Christian mission is a widespread but historically specific social form. Its variants differ sharply in their relation to power: **Catholic** missions (with their ritual, iconographic, and sacramental emphasis — the Comaroffs contrast Spanish Mexico's "theater" with the Nonconformists' "theater of Protestant industry"); **settler- and state-allied** missions (openly aligned with plantation or colonial authority); **faith missions**; and the **Nonconformist evangelical** mission examined here. Across cases, missions vary in whether they preceded, accompanied, or followed formal colonial rule, and in whether they buttressed or corroded indigenous authority — the LMS/WMMS among the Southern Tswana did the latter largely by unintended effect. The comparative record links this page to [[southern-tswana]] and, more broadly, to the anthropology of colonial encounters.

## Social Origins (attributed)

The Comaroffs argue the mission "cannot be separated from the Industrial Revolution and the rise of the lower middle classes" (Beidelman): its evangelists were "the dominated fraction of a dominant class," drawn from the anomalous social niche between the rising bourgeoisie and the new proletariat. Of seventeen LMS/Methodist missionaries to the Southern Tswana before 1860, twelve came from Scotland or northern England and thirteen from industrializing or proletarianized areas; their origins were peasant, artisan, clerk, and laboring-poor. Figures like **Robert Moffat** (apprentice gardener, son of a ploughman) and **David Livingstone** (a cotton-mill "piecer" at ten) exemplify the type. The mission societies deliberately favored men of "mechanic arts" over scholar-priests, giving Tswana evangelism "a pragmatic cast." Their biographies were "the very embodiment of the spirit of capitalism" (cf. [[protestant-ethic-thesis]]), and their civilizing mission "retraced their own journey through British society."

## Theoretical Treatments

Read side by side (attributed): a **[[protestant-ethic-thesis|Weberian]]** reading treats the mission as a vehicle of rationalized, ascetic bourgeois culture; a **political-economy / conflict** reading (Bundy, Trapido, Etherington) treats it as an agent of capital penetration, peasant differentiation, and labor supply; a **[[historical-anthropology|historical-anthropological]]** reading (the Comaroffs) treats it as the cutting edge of a [[colonization-of-consciousness]] that worked through everyday form rather than doctrine or coercion. These are complementary as much as rival — the Comaroffs fold the political-economy findings into their account while insisting on the neglected dimensions of culture and consciousness.

## How the Mission Worked (attributed)

The Comaroffs' central claim: the mission's power lay less in overt politics — on which "the Nonconformists were ineffectual players" — than in "the diffuse processes of the civilizing mission," reshaping "the taken-for-granted surfaces of everyday worlds" (the [[colonization-of-consciousness]] via [[the-long-conversation]]). Quoting Etherington: "[Settlers] merely wanted the Africans' land and labour. Missionaries wanted their souls." The mission occupied a structurally **interstitial, contradictory** role — championing indigenous rights alienated it from settlers, yet its whiteness left it "a colonizing culture" and, in [[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci's]] terms, "the dominant group's deputies."

## The Mission and Chiefly Authority (attributed)

By trying to claim a bounded "spiritual" realm while leaving "secular" chiefship intact, the mission committed a category error: Tswana *bogosi* was "the total, indivisible fabric of authority." Encouraging commerce, literacy, and Christian marriage undermined chiefly authority even where missionaries wanted strong chiefs. The confrontation of baptism vs. initiation (*bogwera*) produced "two states" — a "kingdom of Christ" rivaling the chiefship (e.g., Molema's Methodist congregation against chief Montshiwa among the Tshidi-Rolong). Because Nonconformist ideology gave no doctrine on political engagement, the mission's political role was **indeterminate**; "political parsons" (Philip, Moffat, Mackenzie, Ludorf) drifted into secular affairs with little effect on realpolitik, while serving as cultural brokers who taught chiefs the language of treaties, sovereignty, and "nation" — feeding the later construction of Tswana ethnicity and, ultimately, apartheid's "traditional" structures.

## Reliability of the Mission Archive

The missionary record is the study's chief evidence and its chief problem: produced by interested parties, "differently re-presented" for relatives, mission boards, and philanthropists, and constructed as rhetoric (the "heroic journey" genre, the confessing self). The Comaroffs read it via [[historical-ethnography]], against the grain, and retain period terms ("native," "heathen") "not in our own voice but in that of the evangelists." Scholars dispute mission efficacy sharply — from Denoon's "no impact" through Horton's "incidental catalysts" to Elphick's "revolutionaries" — with rough consensus (Strayer) that missions were "hardly an independent motor of social change."
