# The State

## Definition and Scope

The **state** is the political institution that, in [[weber-max|Weber]]'s canonical formulation (*[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]*, 1919), **"successfully claims the [[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of the legitimate use of physical force]] within a given territory."** Weber's decisive move is to define the state **by its means, not its ends**: "there is scarcely any task that some political association has not taken in hand," so the state cannot be defined by what it does — only by the specific means (physical force) peculiar to it. This wiki treats the state as a **recurrent social form** (an institution) whose *concept* is carried by the [[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of legitimate violence]] and whose *authority* is analyzed through the three types of [[legitimate-domination|legitimate domination]]. What counts as a case, and who says so, is itself theory-laden: Weber's definition is sociological, not juridical, and admits degrees of "success."

Historically, `distribution: widespread` (not universal): stateless societies — bands, segmentary lineage systems, acephalous polities — organize order without a specialized monopoly of legitimate force; the state is one historically emergent solution, not a human universal.

## Cross-Cultural / Historical Variation

Weber's *Politics as a Vocation* supplies a developmental typology of the state as an administrative order (attributed voice):

- **Estate (Ständestaat) order** — the administrative staff (vassals, prebendaries) *owns* the means of administration in its own right and shares domination with the lord.
- **Patrimonial and bureaucratic-absolutist states** — the prince directs administration through personally dependent, propertyless staff, progressively **expropriating** the autonomous bearers of executive power (a process Weber explicitly parallels to the capitalist's expropriation of the independent producer).
- **The modern rational-legal state** — completes the "separation" of officials from the means of administration; a compulsory association organizing domination, run by expert [[bureaucracy|officialdom]].
- **City-states / free communes** — the Occidental exception where princely, tradition-sanctified authority was absent; the seedbed of the free demagogue.
- **Plebiscitary leader-democracy** — the modern mass-democratic form in which a party machine and a plebiscitary leader stand atop the officialdom (see [[plebiscitary-leader-democracy]]).

## Theoretical Treatments

- **British political anthropology (Fortes & Evans-Pritchard 1940, attributed).** *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* defines Group A societies as those with government (centralized authority, administrative machinery, judicial institutions) and stratified power — "primitive states" if the state is defined by governmental institutions. [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] denies the State as sovereign entity over and above individuals ("fiction of the philosophers"); political organization is the aspect of total organization concerned with control and regulation of physical force. Group B "stateless" systems remain political under that definition. See [[group-a-group-b-political-systems]], [[ordered-anarchy]], [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].
- **Fried neo-evolutionary political anthropology (1967, attributed).** *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|The Evolution of Political Society]]* defines the state as the complex of institutions by which the **power of society is organized on a basis superior to kinship**, whose primary function is to **maintain an order of stratification** (differential access to basic resources). Claims paramountcy in naked force; legitimacy is ideology blended with power but never sufficient alone. Distinguishes [[pristine-state|pristine]] from [[secondary-state|secondary]] states; every pristine state traverses [[stratified-society|stratification-without-stateship]]. Rejects Hoebel’s universal-state thesis and Almond & Coleman “nooks and crannies” abolition of the nonstate/state dichotomy. Sequence: [[egalitarian-society]] → [[rank-society]] → stratified → state ([[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]; Hub: [[hubs/theory/egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]).


 (attributed, side by side)

- **Weberian.** The state = territorial monopoly of *legitimate* force; legitimacy grounded in belief (traditional / charismatic / legal-rational); the modern state as rationalized bureaucratic domination. Political leadership (personal responsibility, the will to fight) is distinguished from administration (impartial execution *sine ira et studio*).
- **Marxian / Engelsian origin theory.** For [[marx-karl|Marx]] and [[engels-friedrich|Engels]], the state is an instrument of class domination arising with [[private-property]] and class society (see [[historical-materialism]]). In *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]* (1884/1891), Engels argues the state is **not** a power imposed on society from without (contra Hegel’s “reality of the moral idea”); it is a product of society at a stage when society is cleft into irreconcilable class antagonisms it cannot exorcise — a power *apparently* above society that moderates conflict within “order,” arising from society yet alienating itself from it. Two distinguishing marks versus the gentile ([[gens]]) constitution: (1) **territorial** membership (not blood); (2) a **special public force** (armed men, prisons, coercive institutions) no longer identical with the self-armed people. Normally the state is that of the economically dominant class (slave-owners; feudal nobility; capital over wage-labour). **Exceptional** relative independence when warring classes balance: absolute monarchy; Bonapartism of the First and especially Second Empire; Bismarckian Germany — the same [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]] problem as Marx’s *[[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-1852|Eighteenth Brumaire]]* (study [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-study]]), here stated as general theory. Case pathways: Athens, Rome, Germans ([[state-formation]]). Horizon: [[withering-away-of-the-state]] when classes end. The *Manifesto*’s “committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie” is thus one pole; *Origin* + *Brumaire* supply origin narrative and autonomy exceptions. Weber shares separation-from-means analogies but rejects economic reduction of the political.
- **Elite-theoretic.** [[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]] analyzes state power through the [[circulation-of-elites|circulation of elites]] ([[lions-and-foxes]], [[demagogic-plutocracy]]) rather than legitimacy-as-belief.
- **Tocquevillian.** [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]]'s comparative political sociology examines the democratic state's tendencies toward administrative centralization and the [[tyranny-of-the-majority]] / [[democratic-despotism]].
- **Collins's conflict sociology / geopolitics.** [[collins-randall|Collins]] (*[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]*, 1975) defines the state bluntly as organized violence — "the state is, above all, the army and the police" — and derives its structure from weapon, supply, and administration types. His [[geopolitical-theory-of-the-state|geopolitical theory]] explains territorial expansion and collapse from heartland resources and peripheral- vs. interior-position (front exposure), with conquest among bureaucratized rivals producing rapid overextension (Napoleon, Hitler). Extends Weber's military-organizational sociology; feeds [[state-formation]].
- **Tilly (contention + durable inequality).** In *[[tilly-from-mobilization-to-revolution-1978|FMR]]* the state appears via the [[polity-model]] (government + members + challengers) and [[repression-facilitation]]. In *[[tilly-durable-inequality-1998|Durable Inequality]]* (1998), a government is an organization controlling principal concentrated means of coercion in a substantial territory; **politics as usual** involves both [[exploitation]] and [[opportunity-hoarding]]; states create/back [[categorical-pairs]] (citizen/noncitizen, married/unmarried, etc.); inequality's politics is the special case of [[durable-inequality-theory]] when a government is a party. Critical differences from nonstate organizations: territorial organizational priority and concentrated coercion.

- **Appadurai (the postnational, attributed).** In *[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]* (1996), [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]] argues "the nation-state, as a complex modern political form, is on its last legs" — strained by [[deterritorialization]], diasporic loyalties, and mass mediation. The "hyphen that links nation to state… is now less an icon of conjuncture than an index of disjuncture." He coins **"trojan nationalisms"** (territorial nationalism as "alibi" for transnational/subnational aims) and catalogs **"postnational social formations"** (NGOs, refugee bureaucracies, transnational religious and rights movements) organized on "principles… fundamentally postnational and not just multinational." The state's classifying/enumerating apparatus ([[colonial-enumeration]]) itself "creates, revitalizes, or fractures" the ethnic identities it claims merely to record. Distinct from Weber's means-definition: a diagnosis of the nation/state bond's fragility, not of the coercive core. See [[global-cultural-flows]], [[production-of-locality]].

### Trigger — early-civilization states (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|UEC]]*) treats early states as class societies headed by sacred [[kingship]], organized as either [[city-state]] systems or [[territorial-state|territorial states]] (stable alternatives). Law reinforces hierarchy; military combines frontier defense, enrichment, and internal order with career soldiers alongside conscripts; administration is bureaucratic and/or delegational. Not theocracies (priests subordinate to kings). See [[early-civilization]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], [[state-formation]].

### Yoffee — limits of archaic-state power (2005, attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]*) argues the neo-evolutionist image of the earliest state as a large territorial despotism monopolizing goods, services, information, and “true law” is a **myth**. Earliest states (often [[city-state|city-states]]) recombined differentiated groups under new ideologies; rule was still repressive and exploitative, but power was limited, contested, and distant from many daily lives (e.g. Old Babylonian [[agency|naditu/kezertu]] cases). Law codes were political instruments of [[evolution-of-simplicity|simplification]], not successful monopolies replacing assemblies. Distinguish **state** collapse from **civilization** persistence ([[societal-collapse]]). Debate: [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017) treats the early agrarian state as a **fragile, coercive grain-and-population machine** rather than a civilizational magnet. All claims **attributed**; deep-time focus on southern Mesopotamia with comparative asides. Links: [[grain-hypothesis]], [[state-formation]], [[nonstate-spaces]], [[legibility]].

**“Stateness” as continuum.** Scott sees early Mesopotamian polities as *gradually becoming* states — more/less rather than either/or. Weberian monopoly of coercive force alone is inadequate for the earliest cases; he privileges markers of **territoriality and specialized apparatus**: walls, tax collection, and officials. A strong-sense state has a king, specialized administrative staff, social hierarchy, monumental center, city walls, and tax collection/distribution — firmly in place at Uruk by ~3200 BCE and well attested by Ur III (~2100 BCE). Small embryonic towns managing collective affairs are not ipso facto states.

**Modest place in species history.** An evenhanded species history, Scott argues, would give the state a far more modest role than usual: first alluvial states were demographically and geographically tiny (a “rounding error” against global population ~2000 BCE); until the past few centuries large fractions of the globe remained hunter-gatherers, shifting cultivators, pastoralists, and independent horticulturalists; on a generous reading definitive state hegemony begins only ~1600 CE — last ~0.2% of species political life.

**Wobbly, often seasonal institutions.** Early states were rarely formidable Leviathans for long; interregna, fragmentation, and “dark ages” were more common than consolidated rule. For thousands of years after first appearance the state was a **variable**, not a constant, in the life of much of humanity — in places even a seasonal institution (e.g. monsoon Southeast Asia power shrinking toward palace walls). Archaeology and history are biased toward monumental stone builders and state-centric texts; mobile peoples and biodegradable architectures vanish from the record.

**Not magnets — bondage as condition of survival.** Against social-contract visions of the state as attractive magnet of luxury and civil peace, Scott argues early states had to **capture and hold** much of their population by forms of bondage and were plagued by epidemics of crowding. Evidence for unfree labor (war captives, indenture, temple slavery, slave markets, forced resettlement, convict labor, communal slavery/helots) is, he holds, overwhelming; states did not invent slavery but **codified and organized** it as a state project — “tempted to assert: ‘No slavery, no state.’” Population machines in bad repair: control of fertile alluvium is meaningless without cultivators; war prizes captives over territory; walls protect stored grain **and confine** taxpayers (Lattimore on Great Walls; anti-Amorite walls as limits of political control). Peasantries experience the state as a recording/measuring machine — “the man to fear is the tax collector.”

**Ecological confinement.** Early states were confined to well-watered rich soils supporting labor-and-grain concentration; outside (arid lands, swamps, mountains) they might raid but not rule. Typical structure: directly ruled core + variable penumbra + zone outside reach. Where grain and agrarian taxes stop, state power degrades; nongrain livelihoods are fiscally sterile from the tax collector’s view ([[nonstate-spaces]]). Writing and lists (grain, manpower, captives, slaves) are the software of the quartermaster state — bookkeeping for half a millennium before civilizational literature in Mesopotamia.

**Speculative domestication of subjects.** Scott extends expansive domestication (control of reproduction) to slaves, state subjects, and women’s reproduction under patriarchal family and pro-natalist state interest — state projects to domesticate human servitors by force as Neolithic ancestors domesticated sheep and cattle (explicitly speculative; Algaze: “Uruk urban institutions… domesticated humans”).

### Postgate — temple, palace, and provincial administration (1994, attributed)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] contributes a documentary, institutional account of the early Mesopotamian state (c. 3000–1500 BCE), developed from cuneiform administrative and legal texts rather than a general evolutionary model.

**Temple precedes palace, then power transfers.** Postgate's own hedged position (attributed) is that the temple was the earlier and, for a long period, the more prominent institutional landholder, but that a documented tendency runs from roughly 2500 to 1500 BCE toward formal transfer of authority and land-management functions from temple to palace — occurring either "by default" (the palace being better positioned institutionally to recover after political disruption) or by deliberate reform. UruKAgina's reforms are the earliest clear textual expression of temple–palace tension; Šulgi's administrative reorganization of the temples under secular officials (the *šabra*) is, per Postgate, the clearest case of outright "nationalization"; Hammurapi similarly assigned state accountants to temples and transferred judicial functions to royal judges. This transfer-of-power reading connects directly to the historiographic dispute over how much of the early economy the temple ever controlled outright — see [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]].

**Provincial governance under Akkad and Ur III.** Newly annexed former city-states became provinces, typically retaining prior territorial boundaries and the local *ensi* title — now meaning "governor" rather than independent ruler — while adopting existing local administrative machinery, a strategy that flattered local sensibilities while enabling administrative continuity. Higher-level appointments frequently went to outsiders or royal princes. Ur III maintained **two parallel administrative hierarchies**: civilian governorates (the source of most surviving archives, staffed by local elites) and a separate, province-based military hierarchy whose senior members were drawn from circles close to the ruling dynasty for loyalty; Postgate notes "we have no idea whether to expect two separate palaces" per province. He suggests this dual civilian/military structure may help explain how the Isin-Larsa successor states inherited so much administratively and culturally from Ur III despite that dynasty's political and military collapse (as of Postgate 1994).

**"Polarization" and "diffraction."** Postgate proposes a methodological correlation (presented as a testable hypothesis, not an established finding) between political centralization and material-cultural standardization: strong central rule ("polarization") produced uniform metrology (Akkadian and Šulgi-era standardized weights, measures, calendar, and scribal/accounting practice), uniform legal codes, standardized "chancery" script styles, and convergent artistic and technological practice; political fragmentation produced the reverse — "diffraction" — contracted geographic horizons, disintegration of provincial administration, and breakdown of metrological and commercial norms. He flags this ceramic/political correlation as under-researched and proposes 2500–1500 BCE Mesopotamia as a strong test case given its unusual documentary and environmental control (as of Postgate 1994).

**The military separated from civilian administration.** Under Ur III especially, military administration was institutionally distinct from civilian government, which Postgate offers as the explanation for the relative scarcity of dedicated military administrative texts (surviving military-related texts concern mainly conscription and evasion); conscription responsibility was devolved to local communities and civilian officials. In earlier Early Dynastic city-states, by contrast, the same population moved fluidly between agricultural/communal labor and military service as occasion demanded, with no sharp professional-soldier/civilian divide (as of Postgate 1994).

## Kemp — Egyptian provider state and mature state (2018)

*(Attributed — [[kemp-barry-j]], *[[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]*.)*

Kemp treats the Egyptian state as both practical frame for studying culture and culture’s most conspicuous product: myth, symbol, and institutions that manipulate minds and direct lives under **dominance hierarchy** balanced by **deference** (and its rejection). Periodization of state form in his anatomy:

1. **Early state / intellectual foundations** — legitimacy via ideal linear past (king lists), *[[maat|ma’at]]* (order), names/onomastica, god-fusions; local patriarchal order absorbed into larger polity; Nagada–Hierakonpolis–delta competitive path to Dynasty 0 / Menes (conventional frame).
2. **Provider state (OK–MK)** — taxation–redistribution bureaucracy; planned model communities as experiments in social control.
3. **New Kingdom mature state** — institutional **separation** of palace, temple, and army/imperial apparatus; monumental urban temples and processional boat-shrine cult as psychological control replacing some older bureaucratic density; international empire.
4. **Economic man** — private demand, traders, barter, grain as money-like medium alongside state redistribution — a mixed economy, not pure redistribution.

Comparative value for this wiki: a long-run case of state formation, bureaucratic provisioning, and later market-like practices inside a durable cultural style. See [[bureaucracy]], [[temple-economy]], [[amarna]].

## Wilkinson — Rise and Fall narrative of Egyptian state power (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wilkinson-toby]], *[[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010|The Rise and Fall of Ancient Egypt]]* 2010.)*

Wilkinson’s book is a **chronological political history** organized as repeated rise–fall cycles under a durable ideology of **divine kingship** — the claim that monarchy with divine authority was the early rulers’ greatest achievement and remained the only acceptable government form for three millennia. Period arcs (his framing):

1. **Divine Right (Predynastic–OK)** — unification; Early Dynastic state apparatus; Old Kingdom pyramid age as zenith of absolute power ([[giza|Giza]]); OK collapse.
2. **End of Innocence (FIP–SIP)** — civil war; MK reunification and “face of tyranny” (Senusret III framing); Hyksos foreign rule as shattered illusion.
3. **Power and Glory (early–mid 18th Dynasty)** — expulsion; empire; Hatshepsut/Thutmose III; Amenhotep III golden age; Akhenaten’s failed revolution at [[amarna|Amarna]]; [[thebes-egypt|Thebes]] as imperial religious capital; [[memphis|Memphis]] as northern prize of reunifiers.
4. **Military Might (Ramesside)** — army state; Rameses II/Qadesh; Sea Peoples; strikes and tomb robbery; dual rule by ~1069.
5. **Change and Decay (TIP–Cleopatra)** — Libyan/Kushite/Assyrian/Persian/Macedonian/Ptolemaic succession; Assyrian sack of Thebes; Cleopatra’s death ends independent pharaonic tradition (his epitaph).

Complements [[kemp-barry-j|Kemp]]’s institutional anatomy: Wilkinson supplies the narrative skeleton; Kemp supplies bureaucracy, model towns, and Amarna urbanism. Keep moralized labels (“tyranny,” “golden age”) as Wilkinson’s rhetoric.

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

**Ur III was not a totalitarian bureaucratic state — a corrective to the older "temple/palace command economy" reading.** Van De Mieroop states the current-consensus correction directly: "The Ur III state was not a totalitarian regime whose inhabitants were fully subjected to the bureaucracy, so labor had to be recruited by offering sufficient compensation," adding that "the local economies and hierarchies survived quite independently, even if they had to pay taxes and homage to the king at Ur." His evidence: (a) the ease with which regions reverted to local practice immediately after Ur III's collapse — "regions that had been part of the unified state reverted to their local habits without evidence of disruption," which would not have been possible had their economies actually become specialized, interdependent components of one system; (b) traces of private, unrecorded economic activity running parallel to the state archive; and (c) an explicit sampling-bias caution — "almost all the texts derive from the state bureaucracy and describe the state's activities," so the sheer bulk of surviving state records creates a false impression of totalizing control that is an artifact of what got recorded and preserved, not of social reality. This qualifies any wiki-voice reading of Ur III as a command economy on this or other pages; see [[ur]] for the fuller Ur III entry and [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate]]'s independent, compatible reading of a two-sector (state/private) economy.

**Steinkeller's core/periphery model of Ur III (attributed).** Van De Mieroop credits Piotr Steinkeller's model of Ur III as structurally two-part: a heartland of roughly twenty provinces — the territories of the formerly independent city-states of Sumer, Akkad, and the lower Diyala — administered by civilian *ensi*-governors, "often from prominent local families" with hereditary succession; and a militarily-administered "eastern periphery" between the Tigris and Zagros under generals (*shagina*) reporting to a royal chancellor (*sukkal-mah*). Van De Mieroop's point, following Steinkeller, is that Ur III centralization was built ON TOP OF, and depended on, continuity of city-state elites rather than replacing them.

**Shamshi-Adad's "Kingdom of Upper Mesopotamia" as a personalist territorial state (attributed).** Van De Mieroop treats early-second-millennium territorial states — Shamshi-Adad in the north, Hammurabi in Babylonia, Hattusili I in Anatolia — as structurally similar to their rivals, "only more successful," and explicitly the product of "an individual's military successes" that "disintegrated soon after their founders' deaths": an achievement-based, personalist state-formation pattern rather than an institutional one. Shamshi-Adad (acceding at Ekallatum near Assur c. 1830 BCE, taking Assur's throne 1807 BCE, conquering Mari c. 1792 BCE) built the "Kingdom of Upper Mesopotamia" — an etic label Van De Mieroop applies, not an ancient self-designation. He ruled from Shubat-Enlil, installing sons as sub-kings (Ishme-Dagan at Ekallatum in the east; Yasmah-Addu at Mari in the west) while retaining ultimate control via a stream of directive letters, and was highly tolerant of local practice (retaining local titles, restoring local temples, keeping some rulers as vassals). The kingdom collapsed rapidly after Shamshi-Adad's death (Mari fell to Zimri-Lim in 1775 BCE, within roughly a decade of the kingdom's peak).

**The eponym (limmu) dating system.** Shamshi-Adad imposed the Assur eponym (limmu) system — annually rotating officials, originally selected by lot — across his domain (Mari, Tuttul, Shubat-Enlil, Terqa) as a mechanism of administrative unification even where local institutions were otherwise left alone. The system outlived his kingdom: independent successor cities kept coordinating dates via a shared eponym sequence even after political fragmentation, and newly published Kanesh eponym lists now give a nearly complete sequence from roughly 1972 to 1718 BCE — a case of an administrative technology surviving the collapse of the state that introduced it.
