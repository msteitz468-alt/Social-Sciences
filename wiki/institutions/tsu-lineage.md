# Tsu (Lineage) and Yi (Town)

## Definition and Scope

In [[chang-kwang-chih|K. C. Chang]]'s reconstruction of Shang society (*[[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Shang Civilization]]*, 1980), **tsu** (lineage/clan unit) and **yi** (walled town/settlement) are the paired building blocks of the political order. The king stands at the apex of a royal lineage network; subordinate lords and officials hold or found *yi* from which they draw wealth and followers. This is an **attributed analytic model** of late Shang organization, not a census of all Bronze Age East Asian kinship.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Chang links Shang *tsu* organization to comparative notions of ranked or ramified lineage systems (echoing earlier remarks connecting Shang to Polynesian "ramage" analogies in his 1963 synthesis). The Shang case is historically specific in coupling lineage with bronze-ritual monopoly, chariot warfare, and oracle-bone kingship.

## Theoretical Treatments

**Chang 1980 (attributed).** The central focus of Shang civilization in the An-yang data is the king; resources of the landscape (Ch.2 of *Shang Civilization*) are at the king's disposal through a stratified governmental network of *tsu* and *yi*. Royal succession models (Yi-Ting dual structure; Old vs New School readings of the king list) structure internal divisions of the royal house (*ta tsung* / *hsiao tsung*). Landed lords outside the capital held their own walled towns — a pattern Chang reads as continuous into Western Zhou enfeoffment rhetoric in the *Tso chuan*.

**Relation to state theory.** The *tsu*/*yi* network is Chang's middle-level model connecting Anyang archaeology and oracle bones to a multi-nodal state rather than a solid territorial bloc — compatible in spirit with later "gruyere" images of late Shang ([[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]] summarizing Keightley), though Chang's own 1980 emphasis is more hierarchical-royal than decentralized-cycle.

## Debates

Free farmers vs slave labor in agricultural *chung-jen* classes; whether dual-succession (Yi-Ting) models correctly describe royal inheritance; how far *tsu* units map onto archaeological cemetery clusters.
