# Mental hospital

## Definition and Scope

The **mental hospital** (psychiatric hospital, asylum, state hospital) is a residential medical-custodial institution for persons defined as mentally ill. This page is seeded from [[goffman-erving|Goffman]]'s *[[goffman-asylums-1961|Asylums]]* (1961), whose principal case is a large mid-century public hospital — **St. Elizabeths Hospital**, Washington, D.C. (fieldwork 1955–56; somewhat over 7,000 inmates; about three-quarters of patients from the District of Columbia). In Essay 3 Goffman writes of "**Central Hospital**" as the anonymized large public-hospital case continuous with those materials. Goffman treats the mental hospital as a leading example of the [[total-institution]] — not as a clinical monograph on treatment efficacy.

Write in the past tense of the documentation moment when drawing on Goffman: mid-1950s custodial hospital life, not "the" timeless asylum.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

*Asylums* is U.S.-centered with comparative citations to other Anglo-American and European institutional literature. Cross-national variation in asylum history (e.g., European moral treatment, colonial asylums, post-1970s deinstitutionalization trajectories) is largely outside the ingested source — coverage gap.

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Goffman (attributed):** mental hospital as [[total-institution]]; [[mortification-of-the-self]] on entry and in the daily round; [[moral-career]] of the patient (prepatient circuit through inpatient ward system); [[underlife]] of [[secondary-adjustment|secondary adjustments]]; tension between [[medical-model]] personal-service rhetoric and batch custodial practice (Essay 4). Ward system as graded living arrangements expressing status; privilege language as organizational currency.
- Functionalist / medical-professional accounts of therapeutic community and hospital as treatment site — not ingested here; record as competing frames when sources arrive.
- Critical and anti-psychiatry readings used *Asylums* as evidence against coercive psychiatry (reception positions — see [[hubs/studies/goffman-asylums-study]]).

## Debates

- Whether Goffman's total-institution portrait generalizes beyond large custodial hospitals.
- Medical model vs. custodial/organizational analysis of what hospitals *do* (Essay 4).
- Historical: deinstitutionalization and the decline of the mega-asylum change the institution's modal form after Goffman's fieldwork — do not silently update his ethnography into the present.
