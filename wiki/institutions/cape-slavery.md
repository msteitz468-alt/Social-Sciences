# Cape Slavery

## Definition and Scope

Slavery at the Dutch Cape Colony, 1658–1838. Distinct from Atlantic plantation slavery in its sourcing and structure: the first two shipments arrived in 1658 (228 people from Dahomey; 174, mostly children, taken from a Portuguese slaver en route Angola–Brazil), but the trade thereafter drew initially mostly on India and Indonesia, later mainly on the African mainland and Madagascar. By the end of the 18th century roughly 63,000 slaves had been imported, with roughly equal numbers born into slavery over the period, and more than half of Cape Town's householders were slave owners (Shell 1994, via Hall 2000). Abolition came under British rule in 1838; compensation money helped build [[district-six]].

## Cross-Cultural Variation and Structure

- **The Slave Lodge (1655–1714):** the Dutch East India Company's own slave barracks in Cape Town, which functioned in practice as an unofficial brothel; Commissioner van Rheede's 1685 inspection recorded 58 children with European fathers living there. Manumission for mixed-descent women at age 22 required Dutch competence, Reformed baptism, and a 100-guilder fee (Shell 1994).
- **Intermarriage and concubinage:** just over 1,000 former slave and indigenous women married freeburghers between 1652 and 1795 (Heese 1984; Shell 1994); the first recorded mixed marriage was 1656 (Jan Woutersz van Middelburg and Catharina Anthonis van Bengale). Ann Stoler's argument, applied by Hall: concubinage and marriage to manumitted women cut the cost of colony-building by combining domestic and sexual labor.
- **Sumptuary marking:** slave status was written on the body by regulation — slaves went barefoot and hatless; manumitted slaves wore shoes, stockings, and a hat as the mark of freedom (Thunberg, 1780s); the 1755 Cape code and a 1765 restriction on free Black women's dress tiered the whole society's appearance by rank (Shell 1994).

## Theoretical Treatments: Violence and Terror

Attributed to the cited studies: Robert Shell (1994) calculated an average of one slave publicly executed per month across the period of Dutch rule, corpses left hanging as example. After fires in 1735–36 attributed to escaped slaves, fifteen people were executed in 1736 — five impaled, four broken on the wheel, four hanged, two women strangled (Mentzel). Hall reads this through Foucault as sovereign power inscribed on the body — "a paranoid dominant transcript... more a sacrifice than a simple punishment" — and follows Robert Ross (1983) in noting that owners' fear of revolt was disproportionate to the recorded incidence of resistance: a "general crisis of authority" rather than a response to actual rebellion. Orlando Patterson's [[social-death]] supplies Hall's frame for slavery's suppression of the slave's social personhood.

## Key Studies: Everyday Resistance and the Material Record

Slaves left "as faint a trace in the material record as they did in the written documentation" (Hall) — the institution's erasures are themselves the analytical object. Hall's readings of the evidence that does survive: faunal assemblages from slave-occupied sites in the Cape winelands pattern like those of the Chesapeake, suggesting parallel everyday resistance ([[hidden-transcript]]); snoek and other cheap fish marked the slave and underclass diet into the 19th century (Bradlow 1977); the Ziekedwars Street well assemblage preserves the household presence of slaves and servants whom the probate record excludes. Contemporary observers' inconsistencies — Mentzel alternately minimizing slaves' hardship, describing floggings in flat detail, and insisting "even the slaves are quite happy in their bondage" — are treated by Hall as the anxiety of the dominant transcript, not as evidence about slave experience.

## Debates

The 1985 Heese affair — genealogical evidence that leading Conservative Party families had slave ancestors, met with threats of libel suits despite the archival record — shows the institution's afterlife in apartheid-era racial politics (see [[district-six]] and the 1988 tercentenary politics on [[heritage-management]]).
