# Citizenship

> Recurrent political form binding **categories of persons** to **agents of government** through mutually enforceable rights and obligations. Principal wiki source for Tilly’s formulation: *[[tilly-identities-boundaries-social-ties-2005|Identities, Boundaries, and Social Ties]]* (2005), Chs. 11–12 (with ties to *Durable Inequality* 1998).

## Definition and Scope

*(Attributed — Tilly 2005.)*

**Citizenship** designates a set of **mutually enforceable claims** relating categories of persons to agents of governments — a **categorical contract**, analogous in form (not content) to co-author or employer–worker contracts.

Late-modern **ideals** (often violated) that distinguish citizenship from general inequality:

1. A broad **categorical relation** between state agents and a large share of the population under jurisdiction — not merely individual attributes.  
2. Ethnic categories other than official nationality have **no standing** as such for citizenship.  
3. **Degrees** of citizenship exist (minors, elderly in some rights, incarcerated, declared incompetents).

Citizenship is **not** the whole inequality system; a bundle belongs to citizenship only insofar as it distinguishes subjects by relation to the state.

**Twin structure:** every inclusion twins **exclusion** (Ch. 11) — citizen/noncitizen, naturalized/native, eligible/ineligible for office.

### Historical origin — bargains over the means of war (Tilly 1990/1992, attributed)

*[[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and European States]]* supplies the
formation account behind the 2005 categorical-contract definition: "The core of what we now
call 'citizenship'... consists of multiple bargains hammered out by rulers and ruled in the
course of their struggles over the means of state action, especially the making of war."
War-driven extraction forced rulers to bargain — asymmetrically ("at the showdown, cannon
versus staves"), yet even repression of tax and conscription rebellions "involved... public
affirmation of the peaceful means by which ordinary citizens could rightfully seek redress."
Bargains ranged from incorporated urban councils (Amsterdam) through legislated class bargains
(Pitt's 1799 income tax) to representative institutions rulers never wanted but conceded "in
the course of persuading them to raise the money for warfare." [[direct-rule|Direct rule]] and
mass national politics "grew up together"; 19th-century bargaining extended claims into
elections, parliaments, and eventually welfare activity. The converse case: post-1945 states
financed by commodity exports or great-power military aid bypass the loop — "large state
edifices have grown up in the absence of significant consent or support from citizens" — which
is Tilly's mechanism for Third World military rule and his prescription (equitable taxation) for
civilianization. See [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation]].

## Cross-Cultural Variation

| Pattern (Tilly) | Sketch |
|---|---|
| Western Europe ~1750–1850 | Local/municipal citizenship subordinated to relatively uniform **national** citizenship; thinning of older burgher rights; expanded national obligations; shift toward **direct rule** |
| Ottoman | Indirect rule via religious/military/property segments; Western pressure for nationality special statuses; millet legacies complicate generalized citizenship |
| Imperial China (Wong via Tilly) | Virtue-bound officials without the same rights-bargaining path; different mutual-obligation assumptions |
| EU | Rights transferring across states; some rights linking persons to union agents |
| Kazakstan mid-1990s (case) | Contested nationhood and citizenship amid multiethnic demography; limited protected consultation |

No state ever had a single impermeable citizen/noncitizen boundary; all grade rights (age, prison, naturalization, gender, property historically).

## Theoretical Treatments

- **Necessary not sufficient for democracy** (Tilly): without effective citizenship, regimes lack breadth, equality, binding consultation, and protection; but authoritarian regimes can build obligation-heavy citizenship, and clientelistic goods-for-support citizenship **inhibits democratization**.  
- **Mitigation of inequality:** effective citizenship imposes strong obligations uniformly and can mitigate (not erase) political effects of routine social inequalities.  
- **Durable inequality:** citizen/foreigner is a classic categorical pair; politics of inequality is DI when government is a party ([[durable-inequality-theory]]).  
- **Boundary mechanisms:** citizenship is inscribed, activated, transferred via naturalization, erased or relocated in revolutions and annexations ([[social-boundary-mechanism]]).
