# Progressive Taxation

## Definition and Scope

Tax systems in which average (or marginal) rates rise with the tax base—income, estates, or wealth. In *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]*, progressive taxation is the major twentieth-century fiscal innovation that helped compress top income and wealth shares after the 1914–1945 shocks and remains central to the viability of the modern [[social-state]].

Piketty distinguishes:

| Form | Definition (book) |
|---|---|
| Proportional / flat | Same rate for all |
| Progressive | Higher rate for those who earn/own/consume more |
| Regressive | Rate falls for richer (exemption, optimization, evasion, or law—e.g. Thatcher poll tax) |

Standard bases: income, capital (flow and stock), consumption (VAT, excise). Twentieth century added social-insurance contributions (often on labor only; nearly half of French revenue; Denmark finances social spending largely via large income tax).

## Cross-Cultural Variation

### Income tax progressivity (historical rates and paths)

- Progressive income taxes often created around WWI (US 1913, France 1914, Britain 1909).
- **US interwar innovation:** Roosevelt raised top federal marginal rate to more than 80% on extremely high incomes (Hoover top rate only 25%). Piketty treats confiscatory rates on incomes deemed “indecent” as a US invention worth reconceiving—especially for [[supermanagers]].
- **Post-1980 Anglo-Saxon cut:** spectacular decrease in income-tax progressivity in the US and Britain (former leaders after WWII)—Piketty argues this probably explains much of the rise in very highest earned incomes via “pay for luck” incentives when top rates fall.
- At writing (~2013): debate whether Obama could raise Bush top rate (~35%) above Clinton-era ~40%.

### Inheritance / estate tax

- French estate/gift tax from 1791: proportional ~1–2% until progressive reform 1901.
- After 1914–45 shocks, estate rates of 20–30%+ common in rich countries vs nineteenth-century 1–2%—Piketty links this to lasting deconcentration of wealth (harder for successive generations to maintain rank).
- Cross-country estate-rate differences may help explain higher postwar German top capital-income concentration (top estate rates 15–20% vs France 30–40%, as reported).

### Wealth / capital stock taxes

- Existing real-estate property taxes (US, France, etc.): often flat, ignore financial assets and debt; still raise ~1–2% of NI in English-speaking world.
- European progressive wealth taxes (France ISF; Switzerland; Spain; formerly Germany, Sweden) often exemption-riddled; Germany/Sweden abolished annual capital taxes 1990–2010 partly due to archaic non-market design.
- **Piketty’s ideal (POSITION):** progressive annual tax on individual *net* wealth—illustrative 0% below €1m, 1% €1–5m, 2% above €5m; steeper 5–10% above €1bn; optional low rates on modest wealth. EU-wide such a schedule would affect ~2.5% of population and yield ~2% of European GDP (private wealth >5 years GDP, concentrated at top). Not meant to replace the social-state tax mass—at most a modest supplement (few points of NI); primary purpose is to *regulate* capitalism and create a financial cadaster.

### Effective rates and regressivity at the top

Detailed 2010 French study of *all* taxes (Piketty): overall ~47% of NI; bottom 50% pay 40–45%; next 40% pay 45–50%; top 5% and especially top 1% lower; top 0.1% only ~35%—bell curve with **regressivity at the top** because capital income is largely exempt from progressive income tax, outweighing progressive capital-stock taxes. High rates on the poor reflect consumption taxes and social contributions (~three-quarters of French revenue). Estates much less heavily taxed than income—worsens “Rastignac’s dilemma” when lifetime resources include capitalized inheritance.

Tax competition with free capital flows: many governments (esp. small European states) exempt capital income from progressive income tax—race to bottom on corporate rates and financial revenues vs labor.

## Theoretical Treatments

**Piketty (attributed / POSITION where policy):**

1. Progressive income and inheritance taxes partly explain why wealth concentration never fully regained Belle Époque extremes after 1914–45; capital-income tax at ~30% average on capital income in rich countries 1950–80 cut pretax r ~5% to net ~3.5%—enough in his models for major long-run deconcentration matching historical top-centile decline. Effect is to *reshape* wealth distribution (middle class rise), not necessarily reduce total accumulation.
2. Collapse of top rates after 1980 interacts with executive pay-setting: high top rates as protective barrier; low rates as incentive to mischief and political feedback (beneficiaries finance parties/think tanks to keep rates low).
3. Three complementary pillars of an ideal tax system: progressive income tax, progressive estate tax, progressive capital tax (contributive justification—capital better measures capacity of the very wealthy when declared income can be <1/100 of economic income, e.g. Bettencourt—and incentive justification).
4. Taxation preferable to public debt for justice and efficiency: better to tax the wealthy than borrow from them; nineteenth-century high debt with r > g enriched lenders (Britain 1815–1914 primary surpluses greater than education spending).
5. Taxation is preeminently political/philosophical, not merely technical; every major political upheaval has a fiscal revolution.

Other public-finance traditions emphasize efficiency–equity tradeoffs, Laffer effects, and capital flight—not adjudicated here; see [[piketty-capital-debate]].
