# Household

The household as the basic social-economic unit of Mesopotamian society, per
[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]], who treats it as "fundamental" to
understanding third- and second-millennium BCE Mesopotamia and structures an entire
analytical chapter around it. Distinct from — and analytically prior to — the "great
household" scaling of the same organizational logic onto institutional estates (see
[[temple-economy]]).

## Definition and Scope

Postgate imports a foundational distinction from historical family-studies scholarship
(citing Laslett 1972): the **family** (persons related by blood or marriage, whose
outer limits are inherently vague) versus the **household**, defined as a "co-resident
domestic group" whose limits at any given moment are comparatively precise (Laslett
1972, 24, quoted by Postgate). The household, not the family, is Postgate's unit of
analysis, because it is the unit visible in the documentary and archaeological record.

Postgate borrows Laslett's household typology, cautioning against treating
"nuclear" and "extended" as a rigid binary — intermediate forms exist, and a household
could move between types over the life course of its members:

- **simple household** — a married couple and their unmarried offspring.
- **expanded household** — a simple household plus additional relatives (unmarried
  siblings of the household head, a widowed mother, minor brothers).
- **multiple-family household** — two or more conjugal units, related by kinship or
  marriage, co-resident under one roof.
- **extended family** (a distinct, broader concept) — "all relatives in habitual
  contact with a person, irrespective of whether they live with him" (Laslett 1972,
  29–30); this can exist as a network of physically separate simple households rather
  than as co-residence.

A rare direct census-like snapshot survives from a Kish administrative/distribution
list (Postgate's Text 5:3): households there are classified only as "simple" or
"expanded" — **no "multiple-family households" appear**, and slave-girls kept as
concubines (with children) form a "triangular" conjugal unit within an otherwise
simple or expanded household rather than constituting a second conjugal family.

## Cross-Cultural Variation

**Urban–rural contrast.** Postgate argues co-resident multiple/extended-family
households were more a feature of rural than of urban life: urban space scarcity,
combined with property-rights structures, forced expanding urban families to
physically separate, whereas rural families could remain co-resident more easily.
Crucially (attributed), the dissolution of co-residence in cities does **not**
necessarily mean the extended family's social and economic role (in marriage
arrangement, [[land-tenure]]) diminished — it merely becomes harder for archaeology
and administrative documents to detect, since legal/bureaucratic texts prefer naming a
single identifiable owner regardless of underlying kin cooperation.

**Marriage.** Postgate reconstructs a four-stage Old Babylonian marriage process: (1)
betrothal/engagement; (2) reinforcing exchange payments — from the groom's side
(*terhatum*, *kirrum*) and the bride's side (*níg.dé.a*/*biblum*); (3) physical
transfer of the bride to the father-in-law's house; (4) establishment of cohabitation
with the husband. Legally, an Old Babylonian marriage required both a "contract"
(*inim-kešda*/*riksatum*) and a "feast" (*kirrum*) to be valid — per the Ešnunna law
code §§27–8, a year's cohabitation without these formalities does not make a woman a
wife, while performing them does; divorce required the spoken formulae "You are not my
husband"/"You are not my wife" (see [[mesopotamian-law]]). Where a marriage was
childless, the wife could legally supply a slave-girl to bear children on her behalf,
who then counted as "her sons" — Postgate compares this to the biblical Sarah–Hagar
narrative; whether a wife had borne sons (including via a slave surrogate)
significantly affected her position and protection in divorce, since sons bound her
permanently to the patrilineal family regardless of the parties' own wishes.

**Descent and residence.** In Old Babylonian times the normal residential and social
unit was clearly patrilineal and patrilocal; men were identified by patronymic —
Postgate compares this naming practice to Russia and the modern Near East. Producing a
son and heir carried great social and religious weight (old-age support; appeasing the
parent's spirit after death). There is "some evidence" for patrilineal descent already
in the third millennium, though documentation is far better for the Old Babylonian
period (attributed, Postgate 1994).

**Inheritance.** Standard practice on a household head's death divided property (land,
house, furniture, animals, slaves, temple prebends, debts, *ilkum*-service
obligations) among surviving sons under strict patrilineal principle — a dead
brother's sons inherited in his place. A detailed division-of-estate document among
four Nippur brothers (Postgate's Text 5:5) itemizes shares of house, fields, animals,
silver, prebend shares, and household goods, including a documented preferential share
for the eldest brother. The eldest son typically inherited the family house itself
and, at Nippur, temple offices and prebends — partly because the house often contained
the family's ancestral tomb: burial within the house's four walls was common practice
from at least the Early Dynastic period through Old Babylonian times (separate
cemeteries also existed alongside this).

**Ancestor cult.** The *kispum* (or *kisega*) libation ceremony — pouring water and
food offerings at the grave at prescribed intervals, accompanied by prayers naming the
dead — is attested by a full genealogical prayer list (Postgate's Text 5:8) and by
terracotta libation pipes found both in the Royal Cemetery of Ur and in ordinary house
contexts. Royal houses performed public *kispum* ceremonies (e.g., during the month of
Abum); the duty of the eldest son to conduct these rites is explicit only in texts
outside the Mesopotamian heartland (Susa; later Nuzi) but is implicit in Nippur
inheritance documents, where the eldest receives the "offering-table of the shrine."
Domestic shrines are attested archaeologically from the Ur III period onward: a small
rectangular pedestal, waist-high, resembling temple altar blocks with recessed-niche
decoration imitating temple architecture, typically set in a corner of a reception
room near a hearth. An Old Babylonian house at [[ur]] has altars in each corner with
pottery bowls between them, possibly representing *kispum* offerings over graves
beneath the floor. Function: cult of the personal god of the household head or family;
kin-based groups (like cities) had their own patron deities, worship of which
expressed group identity.

**Domestic slaves.** Male and female slaves were regular members of Mesopotamian
households, listed as property in inheritance divisions and frequently sold; "one or
more slaves are found in all partitions of sizeable estates," though households with
as many as ten male plus ten female slaves, or fourteen male plus six female slaves,
were rare. Slave children born in the household (*wilid bītim*, "house-born slave")
belonged to the owner. Slaves were also acquired via war, plunder, or purchase — in the
Old Babylonian period, slaves from mountain-fringe regions were popular, and some
merchants specialized in the slave trade. Runaway slaves were pursued via public
herald announcements, with citizens obligated to report or return escapees; a
distinctive hairstyle or mark (*apputtum*) identified a slave, and manumission
("clearing the forehead") involved formal removal of that mark by a barber, with
penalties for unauthorized removal (Code of Hammurabi §§226–7; see
[[code-of-hammurabi]]). Children of a freed slave were normally free themselves.
Debt-slaves, distinct from chattel slaves, might also temporarily reside in a
household, freed on payment or annulment of the debt.

**Women outside the patrilineal household.** Women falling outside the patrilineal
household framework — widows who did not return to their father's house, women
"going after the man of her heart," prostitutes associated with public urban spaces,
"ale-wives" — are poorly documented, because legal sources by nature focus on
property-holding family units. Orphaned or illegitimate children sometimes ended up in
temples, which did not necessarily protect them from exploitation; temple prostitution
is possibly attested as early as Old Babylonian times, though the clearer (and much
later, "lurid") report comes from Herodotus, more than a thousand years afterward —
Postgate himself flags this as a single very-late source used cautiously for
Old-Babylonian-period inference.

## Theoretical Treatments

**The extended-family land-ownership dispute (position, unadjudicated).** Whether the
extended family held a stronger active economic role — especially joint land
ownership — earlier in the third millennium than in the Old Babylonian period is a
live scholarly dispute among Mesopotamian historians, which Postgate records without
adjudicating:

- **Position A (Diakonoff):** relatives of a land seller appear in the earliest sale
  documents not merely as witnesses but as recipients of purchase payments; some sales
  were made jointly by brothers or relatives. The Obelisk of Manishtushu (Postgate's
  Text 5:4) records fields purchased from large related "brother-owner" groups — up
  to roughly seventeen "grandsons" of a common ancestor, holding 821 iku of land — read
  as evidence for strong joint/extended-family land ownership in the Akkad period, with
  parallels at [[lagash]]. Diakonoff also argued land sale was apparently prohibited
  throughout the Ur III and Larsa kingdoms (no surviving Ur III sale documents survive),
  and cites Old Babylonian Ur field-leases showing joint ownership by groups averaging
  "no less than eight men enjoying full property rights."
- **Position B (Leemans, 1986, quoted):** denies the extended family's continuing
  economic importance in Old Babylonian sources — "property left undivided, or the
  fact that members of the family inherited houses which were adjacent, does not...
  imply any cooperation or interdependence in economic activities."
- **Postgate's own reading:** the divergence stems from differing interpretive
  stances toward documentary bias, not from disputed facts — legal and bureaucratic
  documents prefer naming a single identifiable owner regardless of underlying kin
  cooperation, so the absence of named relatives in Old Babylonian documents does not
  prove the absence of extended-family involvement; conversely, it is hard to disprove
  that family links are merely "resuscitated" for legal-compliance purposes at the
  moment of sale. Practical economic logic favored joint or undivided cultivation:
  dividing land equally among generations of brothers risked fragmentation, and
  alternating fallow (requiring two separate plots) plus shared irrigation-water
  rights were bound up with land title. Two competing inheritance strategies are
  identified in the record: reducing equal division (favoring the eldest son with a
  larger share) or not dividing land at all (communal exploitation).

This dispute could warrant its own `debates/` page if further sources deepen it;
flagged here as a position rather than promoted.

**The "great household" analogy.** Postgate treats temple and palace estates as
structurally analogous to — a scaled-up version of — the domestic household: a
production-and-consumption unit with a head, dependents, and a redistributive economy,
differing chiefly in scale and in the ideological register (communal/religious for the
temple; secular/administrative and military/exploitative for the palace). See
[[temple-economy]] for the institutional-scale treatment; this page covers the
domestic-household register.

## Muslim domestic environment (Insoll 1999)

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Timothy Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]*, Ch. 3.)* A second densification layer expands the household page beyond Mesopotamian oikos analysis into **Islamic/Muslim domestic space** as a religiously and socially structured institution.

**Core thesis.** The domestic structure is not neutral shelter: it is a “physical embodiment of an ideal environment” and a “structuring structure” (Rapoport; Bourdieu; Giddens as Insoll cites them). In Islam the family unit is the primary sphere of the social order; domestic space encodes social codes and gender roles. Primary ideal concerns: **privacy**, protection/seclusion of women (purdah in some contexts), family sanctity, and often dual spheres — a private family/harem domain and a male–communal sphere for guests.

**Model and diversity.** The Prophet’s Medina courtyard house (hijrah year 1 / 622 CE) is treated as the model Muslim household but rests on pre-Islamic west Arabian traditions and dual dwelling/meeting-place functions (linked to mosque origins). Stereotypical courtyard privacy devices (inward orientation, austere façades, angled entrances, guest rooms near the door, *mashrabiyya*) do **not** monopolize privacy architecture — parallel features predate Islam and occur among non-Muslims. Across the spectrum from palaces to tents, Insoll finds a common concern with gender segregation and public/private domain separation **where observed**, while plan alone cannot identify a house as Muslim (package recognition required). Vertical tower houses (San’a), ephemeral tents with dual hearths, reed/palm architecture, military/garrison houses, and diaspora terraces (Bradford) show the same analytic: structuring principle + cultural diversity.

**Thresholds.** Boundaries are created physically, through amulets/prophylactic objects, and through calligraphy; foundation sacrifices and Bektashi threshold symbolism are exceptional overt religious loadings. Vernacular domestic study has been neglected relative to palatial “top-heavy” Islamic archaeology.

**Settlement scale.** Public (mosque, market) / semi-private (gated quarters) / private (house) domains reappear at city scale; egalitarian ummah ideals conflict with hierarchical multi-ethnic quarters that archaeology is suited to investigate.
