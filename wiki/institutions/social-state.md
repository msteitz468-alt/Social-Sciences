# Social State

## Definition and Scope

[[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s term for the modern complex of public education, health, income support/replacement incomes, and related social spending that expanded in the twentieth century—the fiscal–social counterpart to [[progressive-taxation]] in regulating inequality. *État social*: not residual charity but a structure of **rights** and equal access to goods deemed fundamental (health, education, pensions).

**Conceptual core (attributed):** modern redistribution does not mainly transfer explicitly from rich to poor; it finances public services and replacement incomes more or less equal for everyone. Equality for pensions often quasi-proportional to lifetime earnings; for education/health, equal access in principle regardless of income. Philosophical scaffolding Piketty invokes: 1789 Declaration art.1 (“common utility”), Rawls’s difference principle, Sen’s capabilities—abstract consensus with democratic disagreement on extent.

Related concept page in the vault: [[democratic-social-state]].

## Cross-Cultural Variation

### Tax-to-GDP and the fiscal revolution (Fig. 13.1 countries)

| Period | Tax share of national income (approx.) |
|---|---|
| 19th c. – WWI (US, Britain, France, Sweden) | <10% |
| After 1920–1980 expansion | ×3–4 (Nordics more than ×5) |
| 1980–2010 stabilization | US just over 30%; Britain ~40%; Germany ~45%; France ~50%; Sweden nearly 55% |

All rich countries moved from <1/10 of NI in taxes to between a third and a half. With 7–8% of NI a state can fund regalian functions (police, courts, army, administration)—often half military—but little social spending. Today regalian functions still ~one-tenth of NI; broader social functions consume between a quarter and a third.

**Composition of social spending (~25–35% of NI total):**

- Education and health: 10–15% of NI (public covers ~three-quarters of costs in Europe, ~half in US). Primary/secondary almost free in rich countries; higher education expensive especially in US/Britain.
- Replacement incomes and transfers: 10–15% (or up to ~20%); pensions two-thirds to three-quarters of that block. Continental Europe public pensions often 12–13% of NI (Italy, France high); US/Britain 6–7% with sharper middle/top caps. Public pensions are main income for at least two-thirds of retirees; essential to eradicating elderly poverty endemic into the 1950s.
- Unemployment insurance typically 1–2% of NI; means-tested income support <1%—yet politically most challenged.

Piketty’s claim: growth of the fiscal state over the last century basically *is* constitution of the social state.

### Public ownership and mixed economy (linked wealth accounts)

Postwar Europe: significant public industrial/financial assets 1950–1980 then privatizations after 1980. France 1950: government ~25–30% of national wealth; industrial/financial state share >50% 1950–80—“capitalism without capitalists” / state capitalism in the Trente Glorieuses. After 1980 France became “promised land of new private-ownership capitalism”; private wealth ~6 years NI vs public ~1/20th. Germany: Lower Saxony stake in Volkswagen; similar mid-century public share. Britain: public assets high but net public wealth negative in 1950s due to debt >2 years NI.

### Poor and emerging countries

1970–80: Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia governments ~10–15% of NI; intermediate Latin America/North Africa/China ~15–20%—below rich countries at comparable development. Gap widened after 1980 as rich-country taxes rose then stabilized while poor/intermediate tax takes fell (Africa/South Asia from just under 15% to a little over 10% in 1990s)—partly customs-duty loss (~5% of NI) under trade liberalization without replacement tax capacity. China advanced on income-tax modernization; India stuck longer in low-tax equilibrium. **POSITION (Piketty):** post-1980 ultraliberal wave and external experiments share blame; fiscal-social state essential to development; vicious circle of underpaid services and tax distrust.

### Education and mobility

Massive twentieth-century education expansion did **not** compress earned-income inequality long-run (qualifications shifted upward with technology). Intergenerational mobility: lowest reproduction in Nordics, highest in US (correlation ~two-thirds higher than Sweden); France/Germany/Britain intermediate—contrary to older American-exceptionalism sociology for most of the twentieth century. US: bottom two parental-income quartiles’ college share stagnated 10–20% (1970–2010) while top quartile rose 40%→80%; Harvard parents’ average income ~$450,000 (~top 2%). Sciences Po parents ~€90,000 (~top 10% France). **POSITION:** real equality of opportunity in higher education is a key twenty-first-century social-state issue; Nordic publicly financed independence preferred if financing available.

## Theoretical Treatments

**Piketty (attributed / POSITION):**

- Social state is a major democratic achievement to **modernize, not dismantle**; pairs with progressive taxation against return of [[patrimonial-capitalism]].
- No second “great leap” like 1930–1980 expansion expected or desired (would imply 70–80% of NI in taxes by 2050–60); low productivity growth ~1–1.5% makes continuous tax increases unwanted; debates will revolve around organization, modernization, consolidation at roughly constant ratios—especially Europe.
- PAYGO pensions: return = g by definition; with g ~1.5% and r ~4–5%, capitalization tempts but transition leaves a generation with nothing; capital returns too volatile for basic retirement of a whole generation—PAYGO remains part of ideal future social state. Need unified individual-account schemes for complex careers; “public pension is the patrimony of those without patrimony” but modest private nest eggs still wise.
- Health and education have intrinsic civilizational value, not only instrumental skill supply.
- After 2008: state influence already historically high—issue is new tools for financial capitalism and reforming complexity, not simply “more state as in the 1930s.” Central banks averted 1930s-scale collapse but did not solve transparency and inequality.
- Ideal regulatory complement: [[progressive-global-wealth-tax]] plus financial transparency; social state + progressive income tax necessary but not sufficient for globalized patrimonial capitalism.

Distinct from residual US-style targeting and from pure public ownership of capital stock.

## Debates

Sustainability under low growth and aging; European scale of social policy and debt pooling; relation of public debt to private wealth (Europe highest private wealth yet deepest sovereign-debt difficulty); education financing and meritocracy; development-policy conditionality—[[piketty-capital-debate]] and Ch.16 positions.
