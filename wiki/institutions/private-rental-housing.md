# Private Rental Housing

## Definition and Scope

Market provision of residential tenancy by private landlords (individuals and firms), as distinct from public housing and owner-occupation. *[[desmond-evicted-2016|Evicted]]* analyzes the **low end** of the U.S. private rental market as a profit sector extracting high shares of poor households’ income under weak quality enforcement and strong legal tools for [[eviction]].

## Cross-Cultural Variation

Desmond’s evidence is Milwaukee-centered with national AHS and cross-city court comparisons. Landlord–tenant racial matching (most white tenants from white landlords; substantial black-landlord share in black neighborhoods) and trailer-park vs inner-city duplex markets illustrate **segmented** rental markets within one city (MARS; ethnography).

## Theoretical Treatments

| Approach | Claim (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Desmond 2016 | Poverty is relational; landlords and tenants bound in opposed interests; “exploitation” via extractive housing/food/credit markets is central; “The ’hood is good” |
| Public-housing/voucher policy tradition | Subsidize demand or supply to bring rent to ~30% of income; Desmond notes majority of poor renters remain unassisted |
| Laissez-faire landlord framing | Market self-regulation; Desmond counters that exploitation relies on state support (courts, sheriffs, screening records, subsidies, tax policy) |

Business models in *Evicted* (attributed cases): Sherrena’s inner-city portfolio (~36 units; high monthly net from poor tenants); Tobin’s trailer park (selective eviction, collection strategy, “money in the middle”); work-off-rent and informal labor; voucher rent premiums (hedonic estimates ~$49–$70/month in Milwaukee analyses cited in Notes).

## Debates

Universal vouchers vs new construction; landlord participation mandates; regulation of rent ceilings and building codes; legal aid in housing court; nuisance ordinances; homeowner tax expenditures vs housing aid for the poor (Desmond Epilogue positions).
