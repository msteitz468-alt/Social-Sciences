## Definition and Scope

Coleman devotes Chapter 9 of *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990) to reinterpreting collective behavior — riots, panics, crowds, fads — as an aggregate outcome of individually rational actors, contra the classical sociological tradition that posited an "irrational," suggestible, emotion-driven actor to explain these same phenomena while positing a rational, purposive actor for bureaucratic authority. Coleman's stated aim is to derive both kinds of systemic behavior from a single, constant conception of the rational individual, varying only the structure of relations among actors. He defines collective behavior by three common elements: many people carrying out similar actions simultaneously; transient, non-equilibrium behavior; and dependency among individuals' actions rather than independence.

## Measurement

Coleman's core mechanism is the **unilateral transfer of control** over one's own actions to another actor — distinct from ordinary exchange because nothing is given in return, and therefore not tending toward the stable equilibrium that mutual exchange reaches. He operationalizes specific cases through formal game-theoretic reconstructions: escape panics via Mintz's (1951) "wet cones" laboratory experiment and a modified prisoner's-dilemma structure allowing sequential, contingent strategies (illustrated by the 1903 Iroquois Theater fire, 602 deaths); hostile crowds via a reward-structure table (his Tables 9.8/9.9) in which each member's dominant strategy is to act contingent on others (illustrated at length via the Cox Commission's 1968 report on the Columbia University student occupation); and fads/fashions via a three-player leader/follower/mass-media game (Table 9.10), with four structural roles distinguished by direction of control-transfer — leader, follower, amplifier, and independent. He closes the chapter with 11 explicit, falsifiable predictions contrasting escape panics, bank panics, and hostile crowds by heterogeneity of attention or power, crowd size, effects of training, and variability of outcome.

## Patterns and Findings

Coleman argues escape-panic equilibria are asymmetric in stability: an orderly-exit equilibrium is unstable (a deviant who runs gains immediately), while a panic equilibrium is self-policing (a deviant who holds back loses immediately) — explaining why panics are easy to trigger and hard to quell. He predicts that greater heterogeneity of attention or power within a crowd (his example: the visible presence of comedian Eddie Foy during the Iroquois fire) reduces panic likelihood, since concentrated transfer of control to one salient actor can produce coordinated exit. For hostile crowds he predicts the reverse: heterogeneity in normative-constraint thresholds (citing Granovetter 1978) makes eruption *more* likely, since a few low-threshold "wild men" can trigger cascading participation, and visible leadership likewise raises the odds of organized (rather than individualistic) action. Coleman attributes the apparent contradiction between these two predictions to their concerning different independent variables (attention/power heterogeneity vs. normative-threshold heterogeneity) and different outcomes (coordinated exit vs. coordinated norm violation).

Bank runs and stock-market panics are held to differ structurally from escape panics: withdrawal is a single, non-repeated action, so an individual's own move does not affect subsequent contingencies faced from others — others' actions function purely as *information* about institutional soundness (an advisory-trust relation, per Coleman's Chapter 8), not as an interdependent game. Coleman predicts, on this basis, that training or fire-drill-style interventions effective against physical panics should not prevent bank panics. Acquisitive crazes (tulip mania, the Florida land boom, John Law's Mississippi Scheme) are analyzed via the same trust-placement logic as ordinary trust decisions, but with the perceived probability of gain itself dependent on others' continued buying — producing positive-feedback bubbles that can overexpand beyond any objectively rational level; Coleman explicitly flags that his theory, as given, does not explain why bubbles burst. Contagious beliefs (flying saucers, ghosts) are modeled as transfer of control over belief rather than action, drawing on Sherif's (1936) autokinetic-effect experiment and linked to periods of authority breakdown, illustrated with New Guinea cargo cults (citing Berndt 1965 and Lawrence 1967 as anthropological sources, not independently verified here).

## Explanatory Theories

Coleman positions his rational-transfer-of-control account explicitly against Le Bon's (1895) "suggestion and hypnotic effects," McDougall's instinct-contagion theory, Allport's "social facilitation," Miller and Dollard's and Blumer's "circular reactions," and Park and Burgess's "rapport" — reinterpreting all of these as descriptions, from the outside, of what is (from the actor's own side) a rational transfer of control. He treats Smelser's (1963) value-added theory as the most comprehensive prior sociological account but argues it retains an irrationalist premise his framework dispenses with. He also draws on Axelrod's (1984) iterated prisoner's-dilemma tournaments, in which "tit for tat" achieved the highest aggregate score without ever winning an individual match, as support for the escape-panic contingent-strategy model, and on Katz and Lazarsfeld's (1955) two-step-flow-of-communication model for the leader/follower fad dynamic.

## Interaction

Coleman connects collective behavior directly to his systems of [[trust]]: bank and stock panics are treated as a special case of trust dynamics under advisory (rather than interdependent-game) information structures, and closed trust-loops are argued capable of sustaining confidently held collective misjudgments in ordinary consumer purchasing, voting, and public-opinion formation. He also links crowd-driven authority withdrawal to his broader account of authority and legitimacy: a hostile crowd's "milling" phase is a search for a new consensus about where the right of control now lies, distinct from disaster-driven collapse of authority (external events destroy rather than replace existing control) and from institutionalized, authority-sanctioned rule-breaking (ritual orgies, controlled communal drug use) in which control is never actually withdrawn.

## Debates

Coleman's reinterpretation is presented as a direct challenge to the "intellectual disarray" he attributes to earlier micro-foundational theory — the coexistence of a rational-actor model for bureaucratic authority and an irrational-actor model for crowds describing the same persons. He treats this as an unresolved theoretical tension his single-actor-type account is meant to dissolve, rather than as a settled matter; no independent replication or reanalysis of his specific formal predictions is reported in this source.
