# Haitian Revolution

## Definition and Scope

The **Haitian Revolution** (conventionally **1791–1804**) is the slave uprising and war of independence in French Saint-Domingue that produced independent **Haiti** and abolished slavery there. This page is densified primarily from [[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]]'s *[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]* (1995) — a theory-of-history treatment of the revolution as [[unthinkable-history|unthinkable]] and as a **non-event** in much Western historiography — not from a dedicated military or social history. Full campaign chronology, demography, and leadership debates require additional sources.

**Identity caution:** “Haitian Revolution” is a retrospective narrative package; participants and contemporaries used other labels; Trouillot tracks how naming and plot selection are themselves power-laden.

## Measurement

- Political independence (1 January 1804) and legal end of slavery in the former colony.
- For Trouillot: place in world-historical narrative — presence, [[formulas-of-silence|erasure]], or **banalization**.

## Patterns and Findings

### Sketch of process (descriptive, via Trouillot’s synthesis)

Trouillot’s Ch.2 sketch (attributed to his narrative, not independent military history): 1791 uprising; Louverture’s rise and 1801 Constitution; Leclerc expedition and secret French project to restore slavery; capture of Louverture; reconstitution under Dessalines; independence. He stresses a **war within the war** (roughly 1802–03 campaigns): Creole (colony-born) leaders vs **Bossale**/Congo dissidents — including Colonel **Jean-Baptiste Sans Souci** — whose guerrilla pressure shaped realignments before Creole hierarchy reasserted itself (Sans Souci’s assassination). Conventional historiography that credits **yellow fever** over Black military capacity is treated as part of the silencing pattern.

### Unthinkable as it happened (Trouillot, attributed)

> “The Haitian Revolution thus entered history with the peculiar characteristic of being unthinkable even as it happened.”

Planter/manager discourse (e.g. La Barre 1790) held general slave revolt impossible. Trouillot roots this in an **ontology** of race and slavery — degrees of humanity, scientific racism already in Enlightenment landscapes — not mere missing information. No plan for a general insurrection was prepared in Saint-Domingue or elsewhere (as he states).

### From unthinkable to non-event

After 1804, he argues, Western historiography managed the event through [[formulas-of-silence|erasure and banalization]]; Haiti’s nineteenth-century ostracism and decline made the revolution seem improbable; Age of Revolutions corpora often omit or demote the most radical political revolution of the age; specialists may admit it happened yet recycle planter tropes (external French intrigue as sufficient cause; terminology that resists former slaves as main actors). Silence also fits relegation of racism, slavery, and colonialism on the Western historiographic “backburner.”

C.L.R. James’s *Black Jacobins* is noted as **counter-discourse** with limited impact on the structural pattern (Trouillot).

### Haitian silences (internal)

Haitian elite historiography (e.g. Ardouin) can silence the war-within-the-war and Bossale leadership as blemishes on a glorious epic — **silences of resistance** against Western forgetting, and silences of class/color politics within the Black republic. See [[sans-souci-milot]].

## Explanatory Theories

- **Trouillot:** power in the [[production-of-history]]; [[unthinkable-history]]; not a general theory of revolutionary causation.
- Causal debates (disease, French politics, maroon traditions, economic structure) are not settled here — need dedicated military/social histories.

## Interaction

French Revolution and Rights of Man rhetoric vs colonial realpolitik (Mirabeau on deputies counting slaves without enfranchisement); U.S. and European slavery debates; later Haitian state formation (Christophe; Sans-Souci; Citadelle); Atlantic age of revolutions as **selective** narrative.
