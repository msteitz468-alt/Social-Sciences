# Network Inequality

Differential access to useful personal connections and to the resources those connections carry — a stratification channel that [[social-capital-theory|social capital]] research treats as central to well-being. Primary ingested development: [[small-mario-luis|Small]]'s *[[small-unanticipated-gains-2009|Unanticipated Gains]]* (2009; study [[small-unanticipated-gains-study]]), which locates origins of network inequality in **[[organizational-embeddedness|organizationally embedded]]** everyday life, not only in individual networking skill or abstract graph structure.

## Definition and Scope

Inequality in the **size, composition, quality, and usefulness** of people's networks, and in resources reachable through **social** and **organizational** ties. Not identical to income or wealth inequality, though often correlated and mutually reinforcing. Small's distinctive expansion: bureaucratic/organizational ties (center → agency → goods/services) are part of "connections," not only informal friendships.

## Measurement

- Survey friendship and close-friend counts; social isolation (no close friends)
- Support exchanges (including among nonintimates)
- Organizational referral and collaborative-tie counts at the organization level
- Outcome associations (material hardship, selected mental-health measures)
- Qualitative domain structure of ties (standard vs compartmental intimacy)

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed — Small 2009, urban U.S. mothers / NYC centers.)*

- **Granovetter labor-market channel (1974/1994):** *[[granovetter-getting-a-job-study|Getting a Job]]* showed contact-mediated matching associated with better jobs among Newton male PTM workers, and treated unequal possession of the "right" contacts as a structural source of unequal opportunity — one reading of residual "luck" (Jencks). Afterword 1994 prioritizes how networks **produce/reproduce inequities** (gendered local networks, racial referral gaps, lower *returns* to contacts for some Black youth samples) even when contact *use* rates are comparable — see [[personal-contacts]] · [[strength-of-weak-ties-theory]].

- Center enrollment associated with larger networks, lower isolation, and lower material hardship for many mothers — gains larger among the poor when friends formed; organizational ties could help even without social ties.
- Center institutional practices stratified friendship opportunities (events, parents associations, pickup policy, cooperative tasks).
- Strong (close) center friendships were common; many were **compartmental** (child/parenting domain) yet highly useful under time constraints.
- Support could exceed friendship via repeated encounter and formal contact lists.
- Centers in **poor neighborhoods** tended to be **better organizationally connected** (net of need) — a channel that can buffer expected neighborhood disadvantage for the enrolled, while the unenrolled remain exposed.
- Climactic type: the **organizational isolate** — disconnected from childcare centers, religious organizations, schools, associations, and similar routine formal organizations — is "increasingly guaranteed to be left out" in a society structured around formal organizations (Small Ch. 8, deepening Wilson-style isolation).

## Explanatory Theories

| Account | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| Investment / rational SC (Lin and readings of Bourdieu) | Actors invest in ties for returns |
