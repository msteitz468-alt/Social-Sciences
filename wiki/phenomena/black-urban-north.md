# Black Urban North (Du Bois early 1900s)

## Definition and Scope

**Black urban North** here means the social pattern of African American population concentration, occupational structure, and color-line constraint in Northern cities as diagnosed by [[du-bois-w-e-b|W. E. B. Du Bois]] in **“The Black North: A Social Study”** (early 1900s; 1900 census horizon), reprinted incomplete in *[[du-bois-essays|Du Bois: Essays]]*.

Du Bois’s framing: the Negro problem is not the sole property of the South; north of Mason and Dixon’s line lived (as he wrote) about three-quarters of a million people of Negro lineage, nearly 400,000 in New England and the Middle Atlantic States — the focus of an announced **series of papers**. This page records **his** early-1900s observation, not a timeless ethnography of “the Black North.”

**Source truncation:** the ingested essay ends mid-sentence before homes and public institutions; Philadelphia is comparative only (sex ratios), not a full city monography here. Du Bois's full-length treatment of a Northern Black community is [[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-study|*The Philadelphia Negro*]] (1899), now ingested (web-derived) — the founding empirical study of this pattern.

## Measurement

As Du Bois presents (attributed):

| Indicator | Figure (Du Bois / early 1900s) |
|---|---|
| North of Mason–Dixon | ~750,000 |
| New England + Mid-Atlantic focus | ~385,000–400,000; series: 150k (1800) → 225k (1880) → ~385k “to-day” |
| Greater New York Black pop. (1900) | **60,000** (Manhattan 36,000; Brooklyn 18,000) |
| Residential core | >20,000 in Seventh Avenue quadrangle (≈16th–64th/65th); 10,000 north of Park/Harlem; rest scattered |
| Urban vs rural | Nine-tenths of Northern Negroes in cities/towns vs three-fourths of Southern Negroes in country |
| Sex ratios | Philadelphia women:men **6:5**; New York **5:4** (housemaid demand) |
| Employment | ~90% Black men wage earners (Federation of Churches tenement canvass) but wages **~⅓ less** than other nationalities |
| Literacy | ~5/6 able to read and write (≈ foreign immigrants) |
| Occupations | **74%** laborers/servants; **26%** firmer foothold (porters etc. 12%; skilled 10%; business 5.5%) |
| Business capital | $800,000 in 69 leading establishments; >$500,000 co-operative real estate (halls, B&L, church property) |
| Professionals / civil service | ~10 lawyers, 20 physicians, ≥90 civil service; ~35 public-school teachers + 1 principal + 2 special teachers |
| Family strain (19th AD, dense district) | **24%** of mothers widows (vs American 16.3%; only Irish higher) |
| Marriage | Two-thirds of men 20–24 unmarried; five-eighths of young women unmarried |

Method: census-informed [[social-observation]], ward geography, comparative ethnic statistics, capital inventories — not a full modern probability sample.

## Patterns and Findings

**Attributed to Du Bois, *Black North*:**

- **Northern race problem long-standing.** Every Northern state had slaves; at Washington’s Administration, ~40,000 Black slaves and ~17,000 freemen in the section; gradual emancipation left “serfdom of poverty and restricted rights,” not full equality.
- **Migration.** Excess of ages 18–30 signals rapid immigration; **Wilmington riot** alone sent thousands North; as Southern masses awaken or are disturbed by violence, migration will continue.
- **Reception manufactures crime.** Urban conditions frequently make the good bad and the bad professional criminals (Du Bois cites Dunbar’s *Sport of the Gods*); half of heads of families in a dense 19th Assembly District had country-bred parents.
- **Economic position not open efficiency competition.** Race prejudice is “large and decisive”: licensing bars (e.g. 1836 drayman), 1821–war $250 realty suffrage restriction for Blacks, **1863 draft riots** used to exclude Negroes permanently from lines of work.
- **Occupational ladder.** Withdrawal from some menial niches when climb possible; foreign whites fill those places; competition intensifies at the bottom. Civil service is a mobility channel for aspiring youth.

Wiki voice holds only the **brute descriptive** facts of what Du Bois reported and when; causal lines stay attributed.

## Explanatory Theories

- **Du Bois:** Southern race conflict → Northern urban in-migration of mixed streams; Northern stake is practical, not academic; minority labor markets require cooperation with national industry, blocked by caste.
- **Contrast:** myth of assured Northern status and equal chance with native white or foreign immigrant — Du Bois rejects.

- Feeds [[the-color-line]] as national, not regional only.

## Debates

- Northern exceptionalism vs continuous Northern color-line — Du Bois’s side of a still-live historiographic issue.
- Crime as racial essence vs product of reception structure — Du Bois stresses reception while using “professional criminals” language; keep both poles attributed.
