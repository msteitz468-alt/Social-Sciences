# North American Fur Trade

## Definition and Scope

Commercial hunting and exchange of animal pelts linking Indigenous North American producers and middlemen to European mercantile companies and markets, from early contact through the 19th century. [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] (1982, Ch. 6) uses the fur trade as a principal case of how mercantile wealth reorganized [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]] societies without immediately installing industrial capitalism.

## Measurement

Archaeological trade-good horizons (Wolf cites early Niagara frontier European goods), company histories, and ethnohistoric reconstructions of Huron, [[iroquois|Iroquois]], Abenaki, Great Lakes, Plains, Athabascan, and Northwest Coast involvements — all **as synthesized by Wolf 1982**.

## Patterns and Findings

Attributed to Wolf:

- Populations were drawn into specialized production and warfare oriented to trade.
- Middleman positions and ethnogenesis (e.g. Red River Métis) emerged from the trade.
- Parallel Siberian fur trade for Russian empire.
- Classic demonstration that "primitives" were not outside history.

## Explanatory Theories

[[anthropological-political-economy]]; articulation of kin-ordered modes with mercantile capital; contrast with full capitalist mode ([[capitalism-mode-vs-market-debate]]).

## Interaction

Connects to European industrial demand (cloth, guns), imperial wars, and later commodity/labor regimes.

## Debates

Degree of Indigenous agency; whether trade equals capitalism; dependency vs enrichment narratives. Trigger vs formalist-profit-maximizer readings of middleman behavior; whether early French–Huron trade equals “dependency” (Trigger: not total by the 1620s).

## Trigger 1985 densification — French–Huron–Iroquois trade and creative symbiosis (to 1632)

*(All claims attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]], *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* 1985, esp. Ch. 4; densifies Ontario/St Lawrence case beyond Wolf’s continental synthesis.)*

### Alliance geometry and middlemen

- By **1608–15** French direct trade reached Algonkins and [[huron|Hurons]]; summer stations moved upriver undercut Montagnais middlemen; formal French–Huron alliance was required for confidence without Algonkin intermediaries. Algonkins retained Ottawa River toll rights (Kichesipirini “richest and proudest”) and sowed mistrust.
- **Middleman protection curtailed growth**, Trigger argues — contra analyses that assumed Montagnais maximized profits. Montagnais received furs from James Bay to southern Ontario but semi-nomadic life limited goods they would handle; they killed interior Montagnais attempting direct French trade as late as **1635**.
- Pattern: when middleman gains were high and Iroquois-alliance urgency low, inland French–native alliances were blocked (Algonkin opposition to Champlain’s Huron visit; Montagnais blocked Saguenay 1608; Trois-Rivières Indians blocked St Maurice 1610–11).
- **1611 Lachine:** ~200 Indians; Huron chiefs secretly gave Champlain/Gravé shell-bead strings and beaver skins seeking alliance eliminating Algonkin intermediaries, contingent on Champlain visiting the fifty council chiefs. Trigger reconstructs confederacy socialization of Ochasteguin’s route monopoly into shared Arendahronon/Atironta regulation.
- After **1610**, a two-tier network formed: Huron/Algonkin/Montagnais–French vs Iroquois–Dutch. Trigger rejects claims (e.g. Rotstein) that Dutch/French merely locked into pre-existing hostile alliance chains: pre-existing warfare existed, but Huron–Mohawk/Oneida conflict likely **later**, from historically documented fur trade; main earlier patterns involved St Lawrence Iroquoians.

### Nature of Indian trade (Trigger)

- Neighbouring tribes either warred or traded; archaeology shows millennia of trade (often luxury goods) as peace mechanism; intertribal trade required political alliances. Annual Huron–French ceremony reaffirmed alliance; without major chiefs’ ceremony ordinary traders felt uncomfortable.
- Alliances not exclusive: Montagnais/Algonkins traded Dutch until Mohawk block (1620s); [[neutral-iroquoians|Neutrals]] traded both Iroquois and Hurons. Trading partners not invariably military allies; French rarely joined Huron raids after 1615.
- Native traders played European rivals on price; higher fur prices → fewer skins brought (satisfy wants with less effort) — not market incomprehension. Early 1610s ~**50 skins** per Huron trader.
- Transport constrained demand: only ~**200** Huron traders/year (~60 canoes × ~200 lb) could not satiate Huron + partner needs — hence **no total dependence** in the 1620s. Limited carrying capacity removed incentive to expand trade links beyond early-1620s range.
- Trigger: the formalist vs substantivist distinction “obfuscates rather than clarifies” native behaviour in the fur trade. Prestige came from **giving away** exotic goods; clan redistribution strengthened hereditary council chiefs (“big stones” / “stay-at-homes” in early 1630s).

### Huron hub and archaeology of impact

- By **1615** Hurons were hub of upper Great Lakes trade via corn + European goods to northern partners; double exchange (corn for furs outbound; European goods for furs homeward). Huron beavers extinct in tribal territories ~**1630** without disrupting economy or French fur volume — insufficient European goods for neighbours meant expanded partner trade supplied pelts.
- Primary Huron demand: iron knives, awls, axes, arrowheads, copper/brass kettles — not bulk French staples. Glass-bead chronology (Periods I–III) now lets archaeologists track seventeenth-century cultural change across southern Ontario (as of Trigger 1985).
- Claims that fur trade undermined Huron matriliny/matrilocality (Smith; Hayden): Trigger finds **no reliable ethnohistorical support**; matrilineal principles strong in 1640s *Relations*; redistribution strengthened hereditary chiefs.

### Creative symbiosis (to 1632)

- Period before **1632** = **nondirected change** (Linton’s term as Trigger applies it): Europeans few and lacked knowledge to control natives despite Champlain/missionary wishes. Fur trade was basis of all Indian–French relations and (to 1627) sole economic base of meagre Quebec settlement.
- Core problem for natives 1600–32, Trigger argues, was not “adjusting to Europeans” but **incorporating larger/more varied European goods into their societies** — mutual adaptation within the fur-trade framework rather than European-directed domination. Indians trading with French neither feared nor felt inferior; long-contact groups ridiculed French incompetence with languages, canoes, snowshoes.
- After English seizure 1629 and French return **1632**, prior pattern resumed; 1633 ~500 Hurons / >60 chiefs renewed alliance; Champlain’s Jesuit-residence precondition was a **successful bluff** (economic necessity would have continued trade if refused).

## Trigger 1976/1987 densification — origins, prices, and volumes to c. 1650

*(All claims attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]], *The Children of Aataentsic* [[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|1976/1987]]; densifies the earlier sixteenth-century origins and finer-grained price/volume data behind the alliance narrative above, which draws on Trigger's 1985 synthesis.)*

### Sixteenth-century origins on the St Lawrence and Maritimes

- Origins: after 1550, growing English competition in Newfoundland pushed French fishermen toward Nova Scotia and the Gulf of St Lawrence; declining Spanish fishing (owing to Spain's wars with England) opened a new French export market favoring dry-processed cod, further drawing French vessels into the Gulf. French sailors began regular fur trading with the Micmac (Nova Scotia/New Brunswick) and Montagnais (southern Labrador).
- This trade reshaped Maritime Indigenous subsistence: winters previously spent on interior hunting were extended to meet trade-goods demand, requiring supplementation with purchased European food and increasing dependence on European partners. By c. 1600 Maritime groups were also exchanging European goods for agricultural produce and deer skins with Massachusetts Indians, part of a chain some sources suggest extended south toward Virginia before British settlement there.
- Demand shifted from mixed pelts (deer, marten, bear, otter, seal) toward beaver for the European felt-hat trade, drawing traders farther into the Gulf. Basque whalers, already active off Newfoundland by 1527, extended operations to Tadoussac and reportedly traded furs there by 1550; by 1578 some twenty to thirty Basque vessels were whaling in the St Lawrence.
- Tadoussac's centrality had a prehistoric basis: the Saguenay River was already the terminus of a trade route reaching to Lake Superior. The Montagnais of the Saguenay Valley (unlike the Micmac/Malecite) combined continued exploitation of St Lawrence food resources with a **middleman position** astride the Saguenay, forbidding interior groups from travelling down to trade directly with Europeans (killing some who tried). Trigger argues this position rested on established Indigenous norms restricting travel across another tribe's territory, reinforced by rumor-spreading about European danger to keep interior groups and Europeans apart.
- Documented resumption of upper-river trade: c. 1580 European travel up the St Lawrence resumed after a gap Trigger attributes to processes still argued over — A.G. Bailey (1933) held that resumption reflected the prior Iroquoian population's annihilation by incoming Algonkians, not (per an alternative, secondhand account via Carleill/Hakluyt 1589) a change in Stadaconan willingness to trade, which Trigger judges unreliable. Jacques Noël reached Montreal Island by c. 1585; François Gravé Du Pont reached Three Rivers in 1599.

### Prices, volumes, and monopoly history

- **Fur prices** (beaver, France): 50 sous (2.5 livres) in 1558; risen to 8.5 livres by 1611; 10 livres by 1618, remaining roughly stable for decades (Lescarbot, via Thwaites).
- **Tadoussac export volumes**: 12,000–15,000 skins annually reported by Charles Lalemant (1626), with as many as 22,000 in an exceptional year; 1627 called the best fur-trade year in a long time; 30,000 skins exported in 1630 (partly a supply backlog). Comparative Dutch figures: New Netherland exported only 4,700 skins in 1624 and 7,685 in 1628, rising to 16,304 by 1635 and 5,000–6,000/year at Fort Orange 1625–1640 — substantially below the Huron-supplied French volumes before 1640, though per-unit Dutch profit may have been higher since more furs were self-trapped rather than middleman-sourced.
- **Huron trade volume estimate** (Trigger/Heidenreich, explicitly inferential): roughly 60 Huron canoes descended the Ottawa River yearly carrying about 200 traders; each canoe carried roughly a hogshead of cargo (~200 skins of about a pound each), implying the Huron supplied on the order of 10,000 beaver skins annually to the French — probably about two-thirds of all furs traded on the St Lawrence at that time.
- **Professional monopoly chronology**: single French ships sent 1581–83 grew to five ships by 1584 and rival-trader conflict on the river by 1587; a twelve-year monopoly (1585, Noël and Chaton de La Jannaye) was cancelled almost immediately under merchant pressure; further monopolies followed (La Roche, Chauvin, 1598–99); the 1613 Condé monopoly named Champlain lieutenant and founded the Compagnie du Canada (25,000+ furs exported 1614); the Compagnie des Cent-Associés received a 15-year and perpetual fur monopoly in 1627, with unmet colonization obligations. The 1627–29 Anglo-French war (Kirke brothers' capture of Tadoussac 1628, Quebec's surrender 1629) disrupted trade until the 1632 Treaty of Saint-Germain-en-Laye restored French control; trade reached a reported ~50,000 skins in 1630 as backlogged Huron furs arrived, then "plummeted" in 1631, a collapse Trigger attributes partly to English failure to arm/protect Algonkian trading partners against the Iroquois. Trigger judges the long-running struggle between free-trade and monopoly-colonization advocates structurally unresolvable: the fur trade needed only a small resident population, but effective defense of trading posts required colonies and naval power that fur profits alone could not fund.

### Huron trade impact, beaver depletion, and the anti-determinism conclusion

- Trigger estimates only about one adult Huron male in six was engaged in external trade in a given year, arguing that trading activity had **less impact on daily Huron life "than is generally imagined"** — Huron men still spent more time on warfare and domestic/subsistence tasks than on trade, 1615–1629 (an explicit continuity argument, contra dependency readings).
- **Beaver depletion**: by c. 1630 beaver had become locally extinct within Huron hunting territories; Sagard (1636) and Paul Le Jeune (1635) both record the Huron exhausting their own beaver and obtaining skins from other Indians instead. Independent faunal evidence (Sidey-Mackay, Maurice, and Robitaille sites) shows beaver-bone percentages dropping from roughly 13% to 7% of identified fauna between earlier and later contact-period assemblages — **documentary and zooarchaeological evidence converge** on this depletion, a case of independent evidence-class agreement.
- Trigger contrasts the Huron (who continued making pottery, birchbark vessels, and stone axes through this period) with the Montagnais/Micmac, who by 1623 could not manage without French peas, corn, and biscuits and had largely abandoned traditional manufactures — evidence, in Trigger's reading, that European goods were absorbed by the Huron without cultural collapse, unlike the coastal Algonkian pattern.
- Trigger's Chapter 13 conclusion (**explicit anti-determinism claim**): no single "logic of the fur trade" existed independent of prevailing custom and intertribal relations; each Iroquoian group's response was shaped by pre-existing culture, not economic determinism — an explicit rejection of Hunt's (1940) thesis that European trade produced instant "social revolution" before Europeans themselves even arrived.
