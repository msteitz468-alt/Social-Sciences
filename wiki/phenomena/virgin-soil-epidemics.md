## Definition and Scope

Epidemics in populations with no prior exposure to a pathogen ("virgin soil"), producing extremely high mortality. In the Americas after 1492, Old World viral diseases (especially smallpox) are the paradigm cases. This page is densified primarily from [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]]'s treatment in *[[trigger-natives-and-newcomers-1985|Natives and Newcomers]]* (1985) of seventeenth-century Eastern Woodlands / [[huron|Huron]] epidemics and the continental demographic debate as of that date — not a global synthesis.

Trigger opens the demographic section by stating (attributed) that diseases hitherto unknown in the New World, against which native populations had no opportunity to acquire immunity, played the major role in post-contact population decline — the classic virgin-soil frame, shared with Borah/Dobyns high-count arguments he surveys.

## Measurement

*(All figures attributed; date-stamp **as of Trigger 1985** unless a nested secondary is named.)*

**High-count / continental baselines (as of Trigger 1985):**

- [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] reports Dobyns (1966) estimated ~**95% population decline** in the first **130 years** (five generations) after initial European contact, after which populations slowly increased; lethal agents included measles, influenza, chicken pox, mumps, rubella, and especially **smallpox**.
- Post-1966 estimates of native population **north of Mexico at contact** (as of Trigger 1985) ranged from Driver’s **3.5 million** to Dobyns’s **9.8 million**, with Ubelaker’s (1976) **4.4 million** from *Handbook* tribal estimates — all far above Mooney’s ~**1.1 million** and Kroeber’s ~**0.9 million**, which rested largely on seventeenth-century or later data. Many demographers held (Denevan) that precise contact totals may never be knowable within more than a broad range.
- Anthropologists (as of Trigger 1985) agreed there was a **major** post-contact decline but **disagreed** on overall magnitude, local variability, dates of population nadirs, and nadir figures; early critiques of Dobyns included Kehoe and Kehoe’s challenge to treating the 1930 census as the North American nadir.

**Huron / Petun local counts (as of Trigger 1985):**

- Jesuit post-smallpox (**1639–40**) total of ~**12,000** for Hurons and Petuns (detailed census lost) — Trigger treats this as probably the **most reliable** population estimate for any eastern North American native group in the first half of the seventeenth century; Heidenreich proportioned ~**9,000** Huron / ~**3,000** Petun; Jesuits thereafter used a round Huron figure of ~**10,000**.
- French pre-epidemic tradition: Champlain’s Huron “thirty thousand souls” (plus 2,000 warriors — Huron-quoted, uncertain method); Sagard’s **30–40k** (1623) likely included Petuns; Jesuits repeated **30,000** in twenty villages. Trigger rejects Popham’s **45–50k** (1950) as archaeologically baseless.
- Trigger’s own (1969) estimate: ~**18,000** Hurons prior to late-1630s epidemics. Reworking the 1640 Jesuit hearth count (32 settlements, 700 longhouses, 2,000 hearths → 4,000 families) yields combined Huron–Petun **20–32k**, median ~**19,500** Hurons in 1639, ~**23,500** before **1634–37** epidemics (**~20%** mortality in those early waves); Heidenreich’s three techniques averaged ~**21,000** pre-epidemic Hurons (median range **16–22.5k**).
- Trigger’s reverse projection from 1640 hearth counts: only **three people per family** surviving after epidemics (vs normal family of 5–8) — supporting catastrophic cumulative mortality across the killing years.

**Neighboring groups (as of Trigger 1985):**

- Neutrals ~**12,000** after 1639, probably proportional decline to Hurons; Champlain and Sagard thought pre-1630s Neutrals larger than Hurons.
- Five Nations: long-standing aboriginal estimates of **10–12k** (from 2,000 warriors in 1668) and Mooney’s reduction to **5,500** post-date severe epidemics. Starna’s Mohawk pre-epidemic **10–17k** corrected for lag in village rebuilding → **6,600–8,300**; Snow’s Mohawk **9–11.3k** implies total Iroquois near Dobyns’s **55k**, Starna-reinterpreted ~**30k**. Trigger argues the Five Nations were **at least as populous as the Hurons** before 1630s epidemics, probably more — a post-epidemic numerical warfare advantage not adequately considered in earlier narratives.

## Patterns and Findings

### Huron killing years, 1634–39 (attributed: Trigger 1985)

| Year | Disease identity (disputed) | Notes (as of Trigger 1985) |
|---|---|---|
| 1634 | Fever, rash, temporary blindness, diarrhoea — “measles or smallpox”; identity disputed | Smallpox (Jackes) vs measles (Duffy/Dobyns) vs influenza; French mild cases favour non-smallpox |
| 1636 | Influenza | High Nipissing winterer mortality; Attignawantan hit hard |
| 1637 | Childhood-type disease (no French illness) | Scarlet fever suggested; via Susquehannock/Wenro |
| 1639 | **Smallpox** from New England via Algonkins | **Many thousands** of Hurons died — far more than the three prior epidemics combined; spread to [[petun]], [[neutral-iroquoians]]; 1640–41 Seneca epidemic |

Trigger concludes (attributed) that over seven years the epidemics killed **more than half** the Huron population (and neighboring populations similarly hard hit). Cumulative, not single-wave, catastrophe is the load-bearing pattern for Ontario Iroquoians.

### Social response among the Hurons (attributed: Trigger 1985)

- Hurons assumed epidemics were **witchcraft** almost from the start: accusations against the ungenerous; torture for confession; killing suspected witches; community curing rituals; shamans fasting/dreaming for new rites. Widespread mortality suggested a powerful foreign enemy; generally Hurons blamed the French (Brûlé murder revenge; Champlain’s death orders), with **Jesuits as agents** — celibacy, health, recovery from influenza as proofs of power.
- Economic dependence on French goods by the 1630s blocked expulsion of Jesuits despite general councils (1637, 1640) considering killing or expelling them. Trigger: no Frenchman murdered as private vengeance is proof of increasing dependence.
- **Religious resilience, not McNeill-style submission.** Trigger contrasts McNeill’s (1976) Mexico model (“stunned acquiescence,” mass conversions) with the Huron case: ritual florescence showed dynamism; each epidemic’s end was credited to concurrent rituals; by smallpox’s end almost all publicly renounced Christianity except a few male heads of trading families and old women supported by Jesuits. Quote (Trigger): Huron religious beliefs were “sufficiently dynamic to emerge with a sense of victory from the epidemics that during seven years had killed more than half the Huron population.”
- Negative effects were worse, Trigger argues, in **economic/political spheres** and conservation of religious knowledge than in ritual practice: half-empty communities relocated; children and elders died (fewer future warriors; elderly-held lore untransmitted); same trader numbers may have maintained or increased fur volume while half the population bore the full transport load — more men in trade, fewer for war/defence; reduced surplus corn buffers raised famine risk.

### Sixteenth-century inland pandemics — open (as of Trigger 1985)

Trigger frames **two positions** for eastern Canada and New England:

1. Massive sixteenth-century epidemics so that seventeenth-century outbreaks continued prior disruption (e.g. Martin 1978).
2. Snow’s view that the **1616–19** coastal New England epidemic was the first major one, so pre-epidemic records can ground late prehistoric demography — archaeology not yet adequate to test (as of 1985).

Cartier’s Stadacona winter epidemic (fifty dead, ~10%) is often assumed European without corroboration. Trigger: **no clear proof** Iroquoian St Lawrence lowlands were substantially reduced by sixteenth-century European disease, but substantial losses **cannot be ruled out**; he will not rule out major southern Ontario epidemics before 1634, but European origin is unproven. He critiques Dobyns’s (1983) claimed 1520–24 pan-North American smallpox pandemic for overinterpreting Chesapeake and Powhatan evidence (Powhatan’s “perish three times” speech referred to warfare, not disease).

## Explanatory Theories

| Framework | Core claim (attributed) | Standing as of Trigger 1985 |
|---|---|---|
| Dobyns high-count / virgin-soil cascade | ~95% decline over five generations; continental pandemics can run far ahead of face-to-face settlement | Major influence; magnitude and sixteenth-century pan-continental claims contested |
| McNeill spiritual-conquest model | Near-immunity of Europeans read as divine → native submission and conversion | Illuminating for Mexico; Trigger finds Huron case does **not** fit |
| Calvin Martin *Keepers of the Game* | Natives blamed animal spirits for epidemics → war of extermination on game → Christianity | Trigger judges supporting scraps “totally insufficient”; Huron beaver extinction by ~1630 **before** 1634–40 epidemics undercuts timing |
| Trigger’s Huron synthesis | Virgin-soil mortality real and catastrophic locally; response via witchcraft etiology and ritual florescence; economic dependence precedes spiritual collapse; more than half dead by 1640 | Primary case study densified here |

## Debates

1. **Magnitude of pre-contact population and post-contact decline** (Dobyns high-counters vs more cautious estimates — as of Trigger 1985). Consensus on major decline; no consensus on overall magnitude, nadirs, or local variability.
2. **Timing of first European epidemics inland** — sixteenth-century pan-continental pandemics (Dobyns 1983 and followers) vs first major northeast shocks in the seventeenth century (Snow and others). Trigger records both; does not adjudicate beyond evidence cautions.
3. **Whether virgin-soil mortality equals cultural/spiritual collapse** — McNeill (Mexico) vs Trigger (Huron religious resilience) vs Martin (animal-spirit war) — positions, not wiki-voice verdict.
4. **Applying Dobyns ratios formulaically** — Trigger’s *Handbook* nadir exercise (~8,000 mid-eighteenth-century Iroquoians → Dobyns-ratio aboriginal ~160,000 vs Clermont’s ~110,000 c. 1600) shows either modest ~30% pre-1600 decline **or** no need for a larger 1600 base; Dobyns himself stressed local variation over formula application.

## The 1976/1987 chronology and argument: *The Children of Aataentsic*

*(All figures and claims below attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]], *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]* — his book-length primary account, densifying and in places refining the 1985 summary above.)*

**Full epidemic sequence, four waves in six years:**

| Wave | Date | Disease identity (Trigger's own position) | Key detail |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Summer–autumn 1634 | **Not smallpox**, and explicitly **not influenza** — Trigger rejects "the oft-repeated claim" (Talbot 1956:131) that it was influenza as "only a layman's guess"; Le Jeune described it as "a sort of measles and an oppression of the stomach," Brébeuf as high fever, rash (likened to but distinct from French measles/smallpox), sometimes temporary blindness, ending in diarrhoea | Spread through all Huron villages; crop/fishing losses from debility; Trigger calls it "the least lethal" of the four waves; no clear evidence of any European-disease epidemic in eastern Canada before this date |
| 2 | Autumn 1636 | **Influenza** | Spread from the St. Lawrence Valley, reached Huron country before mid-September; peaked at Ihonatiria after 10 November, did not fully cease until spring 1637; diffused into northern Ontario via Nipissing traders, whom Huron and other Indians themselves identified as the carriers |
| 3 | Summer 1637 | Fast-course illness, identity undetermined (Trigger's own suggestion: possibly spread north from an unidentified Susquehannock malady of February 1637, rather than from the St. Lawrence) | Broke out after the Huron returned from trading at Three Rivers; many died within two days (far faster course than 1636); no Frenchmen fell ill, which Trigger reads as evidence of a childhood ailment to which adult Frenchmen already had acquired immunity |
| 4 | Autumn 1639–winter 1640 | **Smallpox** | By far the most lethal of the four; Jesuits baptized over 1,000 Huron in danger of death; total mortality estimated in the "several thousand," reducing the Huron population to roughly 9,000 — about half of pre-1634 levels |

**Local mortality data points.** At the Nipissing, who wintered in Huron country and carried away 70 bodies when they departed north in spring 1637, Champlain's population estimate of 700–800 implies roughly a **10% mortality rate** from the 1636 influenza wave alone. Trigger doubled a partial Ossossane count (~50 dead by January 1637) to estimate "about 500 deaths among the Attignawantan" from the same wave, and notes that applying the Nipissing's 10% ratio confederacy-wide would imply "nearly 1,000 Huron died" — a figure he judges probably too high, since Huron diet and housing were better than the Nipissing's.

**Witchcraft interpretation and anti-Jesuit accusations.** The Huron reasoned, in Trigger's words a "remorseless logic" absent modern epidemiological knowledge of differential population susceptibility: because the French rarely fell ill and always recovered while native remedies and rituals failed, the epidemics must be French witchcraft. At least two, possibly three, suspected witches were executed among the Attignawantan during the 1636–37 waves (one woman tortured and killed at Ossossane, April 1637), sanctioned by headmen or war chiefs. Specific anti-Jesuit rumor-narratives Trigger records: a Tionnontate reportedly vomiting a lead pellet; an Arendarhonon account of meeting two Englishwomen in the afterlife who said the Jesuits would kill all the Huron; claims of English pinnaces on the St. Lawrence; a corpse allegedly kept in the Jesuit tabernacle; child-effigy stabbing rituals; malevolent religious paintings; a bewitched cloak buried near Three Rivers. During the 1639–40 smallpox wave, the donné Robert Le Coq became a vector of similar rumor while apparently dying, claiming the Jesuits caused disease via a supernatural serpent (*angont*) or a demon concealed in a gun barrel.

**Demographic-parity argument.** Trigger explicitly rebuts the assumption that the Huron alone were decimated: the Mohawk village of Onekagoncka had lost so many to smallpox by winter 1634–35 that its headman lived apart from it (van den Bogaert); the Susquehannock were stricken in 1636/early 1637; the Wenroronon partly migrated to the Huron in 1638 owing to disease losses; the Neutral suffered heavy population loss from war, famine, and disease by 1641 (one village's size fell to ~300); the Seneca had a serious epidemic, probably smallpox, in winter 1640–41; the Iroquois were afflicted by contagious disease again in 1646–47 (not reaching the Huron). Trigger's conclusion: the Huron and Iroquois were probably roughly equal in numbers before the epidemics and both lost about half their population by 1640 — **no epidemic-driven demographic advantage accrued to either side**, a finding load-bearing for his rejection of Hunt's (1940) fur-trade-driven Iroquois "middleman" thesis (see [[canadian-heroic-age-historiography-debate]]).

**Against McNeill and Calvin Martin, with Krech's independent refutation noted.** As in the 1985 restatement above, Trigger's own Huron research found no evidence that traditional religious faith weakened during the epidemics — instead the 1634–40 period was "a period of great innovation in terms of curing ceremonies," with new rituals credited for ending each wave. He records that documented Huron conversions in the following decade were motivated primarily by economic considerations (French alliance, guns sold only to converts, better trade treatment) rather than religious collapse. Separately, Trigger notes that Calvin Martin's *Keepers of the Game* (1978) thesis — that epidemics were read as caused by animal spirits, provoking a native "war against the animals" that in turn (not desire for European goods) drove the fur trade — was substantially undercut by Shepard Krech III's edited volume *Indians, Animals, and the Fur Trade* (1981), in which a panel of specialists found little evidence for the "war against the animals" claim and Martin's reply was judged weak.

**Age-structure consequences.** Beyond raw mortality, Trigger stresses the loss of elders (holders of ritual and historical knowledge, much of it consequently untransmitted) and of children (the next generation of warriors and workers) as a distinct, compounding harm not captured by aggregate population figures — echoing but sharpening the 1985 summary's point above about conservation of religious/political knowledge.

**Dobyns's pre-1634 decline thesis versus Trigger's stability finding (as of Trigger 1987).** In the 1987 preface to this edition, Trigger states he found no compelling evidence of epidemics among the Huron before 1634, having based his precontact population estimates on Champlain, Sagard, and pre-1634 Jesuit reports; he notes that, as of 1987, "the present archaeological evidence seems to suggest stability in Iroquoian population during the sixteenth century" — a direct, date-stamped rebuttal of Henry Dobyns's (*Their Number Become Thinned*, 1983) argument that dramatic epidemic-driven population loss in North America (including this region) began as early as 1520.
