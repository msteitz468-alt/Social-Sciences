# HIV/AIDS

## Definition and Scope

Acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS), caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV), was first
recognized as a distinct clinical syndrome in the summer of 1981 (among young gay men in California and
New York; a woman was diagnosed within two months). This page treats HIV/AIDS as a *social* phenomenon
as analyzed in [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]]'s *[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and
Inequalities]]* (1999): a pandemic whose contours, Farmer argues (attributed), are "sculpted" by
inequality rather than by "risk groups," culture, or individual attitude. By the end of 1999 an
estimated 34.3 million people were living with HIV; more than 95% of new infections were occurring in the
developing world, and close to 80% of cumulative AIDS deaths to that point had occurred in Africa (as of
the 2000 UNAIDS figures Farmer cites). In Botswana adult prevalence reached ~36% and life expectancy fell
to 47.4 years by 1997.

## Measurement

HIV/AIDS is tracked by seroprevalence surveys, AIDS case surveillance, cohort and case-control studies,
and demographic indicators (life-expectancy decline; AIDS orphans, estimated at 13.2 million cumulatively
by end-1999, over 90% in sub-Saharan Africa). Farmer's methodological warning is the **sampling frame**:
epidemiological studies that draw the "community" boundary narrowly (his "bell jar" critique) exclude the
wealthier communities poor women work in and produce an illusion of equally shared risk.

## Patterns and Findings

- **The inequality gradient.** In the U.S., cumulative AIDS incidence in the 1980s was >11× higher for
  Black than white women; between 1985 and 1994 U.S. AIDS cases among women tripled, 77% of them among
  Black and Hispanic women, mostly poor; 1994 HIV death rates were ~4× higher for Black men and ~9×
  higher for Black women than for their white counterparts. Farmer: "Structural violence — gender
  inequality, racism, and poverty — is at the very heart of these trends."
- **Partner profession over partner number ([[do-kay|rural Haiti]]).** The GESCAP case-control study of
  the first 25 symptomatic HIV-positive women of the Do Kay region vs. 25 seronegative controls found no
  group difference in number of partners (several cases had only one); the discriminating risk factor was
  the partner's *profession* — 19 of 25 cases had a soldier or truck-driver partner. Soldiers and
  truckers functioned as an economic "bridge" from city to countryside, the rural analogue of the North
  American tourist in urban Haiti.
- **The Caribbean political economy.** Farmer demolishes the early "Haiti as AIDS origin" and "voodoo
  transmission" claims: GHESKIO epidemiology traced the epidemic's entry to transnational ties (tourism,
  a documented gay-tourist sex economy, labor migration), concluding HIV "most likely" reached Haiti
  *from* North America/Europe, not the reverse. The five Caribbean-basin nations most economically
  dependent on U.S. exports had the largest AIDS caseloads; Cuba, alone outside the "West Atlantic
  system," had ~0.01% seroprevalence vs. ~9% urban Haitian — a structural, not cultural, pattern
  (recorded as `associated_with`, per Farmer's own caution against over-claiming causation).
- **The "geography of blame."** The 1982 CDC designation of Haitians as an AIDS "risk group" collapsed
  Haitian tourism (from ~75,000 winter visitors to under 10,000) and stigmatized Haitians abroad —
  material harm from an "immodest" causal claim (see [[immodest-claims-of-causality]]).

## Explanatory Theories

Farmer's **[[structural-violence]]** account replaces the "risk group" with the *political economy of
risk*: who is exposed and who is shielded is set by economic dependency, gender inequality, and political
dislocation. His eight-factor model of rural Haitian transmission (attributed) weights: deepening
poverty; gender inequality; political upheaval; traditional and emerging patterns of sexual union;
prevalence of and lack of access to STD treatment; late public-health response; and lack of culturally
appropriate prevention tools. The decisive counter-evidence to biological/cultural-susceptibility
theories is the **access finding** (Chaisson, Keruly, and Moore, cited): when access barriers were
removed in an inner-city AIDS cohort, survival differences by race and sex disappeared.

## Interaction

HIV/AIDS interacts inseparably with [[tuberculosis]] (its leading opportunistic killer among the poor),
with [[concentrated-poverty]] and gender inequality, and — through cost — with [[income-inequality]]:
Farmer notes protease-inhibitor therapy at $12,000–16,000/year (as of 1999) risked producing "an AIDS of
the North and an AIDS of the South," a manageable chronic condition versus a death sentence.

## Detention and the "logic of quarantine" (*Pathologies of Power*, 2003)

In *[[farmer-pathologies-of-power-2003|Pathologies of Power]]* Farmer analyzed the HIV-positive
Haitian refugees detained by the U.S. on its Guantánamo naval base (1991–93): 268 were identified as
HIV-positive out of tens of thousands screened, and some who had already qualified as bona fide
political refugees were held at "Camp Bulkeley" for up to two years solely because of HIV status —
conditions a federal judge (Sterling Johnson, 1993) ruled "cruel and unusual punishment." Farmer
recorded (as documented events, with detainee testimony) the forced injection of Depo-Provera without
consent and hunger-strike beatings. He set this against Cuba's residential AIDS sanatoria, arguing
(attributed) that the U.S. press and even the refugees' own advocates performed "symbolic work" by
treating the two as morally equivalent "quarantine facilities" — a false equivalence that laundered
U.S. abuses. He used a Haiti–Cuba comparison (near-identical geography, divergent health outcomes) to
argue the difference was structural, not cultural. Farmer also read the U.S. HIV-immigration exclusion
(Helms amendment, 1987; retained by a 76–23 Senate vote in 1993) as [[structural-violence|structural
violence]] codified in law.

## Debates

The disputes over whether *culture* or *structure* best explains HIV distribution and outcome — the early
"exotic" AIDS anthropology, the WHO "Pattern II" transmission typology, the placebo-controlled
antiretroviral trials likened to Tuskegee (Farmer treats the 1994–98 Rakai/Uganda serodiscordant-couples
study controversy at length in *Pathologies of Power*) — are recorded at
[[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]].


## Ju/'hoansi and HIV (Lee, as of 2013 textbook)

*(Attributed — [[lee-richard-b|Lee]], *[[lee-dobe-juhoansi-2013|The Dobe Ju/'hoansi]]* 4th ed., change chapters; with Ida Susser collaborative work noted.)* Botswana national adult HIV prevalence was among the world's highest by ~2000 (Lee cites UNAIDS figures in the ~35% range for the country peak period). For Dobe/Nyae Nyae Ju/'hoansi, Lee estimates far lower community rates (order **~3–6%** in the comparisons he reports) and links relative protection partly to **women's sexual decision autonomy** (condom refusal rights) versus neighbors where women reported less leverage — with shebeen drinking and outside partners as local hot spots. Treat as ethnographer's estimate and argument, not a substitute for formal serosurvey publication. Society page: [[juhoansi]].
