# Human Organs Trade

## Definition and Scope

The **human organs trade** names markets and quasi-markets in organs and tissues for transplant — paid living kidney “donation,” brokerage, transplant tourism, contested cadaveric procurement, and commercial tissue banking — as documented especially in late-20th/early-21st-century [[medical-anthropology]]. Primary wiki grounding: *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* ([[cohen-lawrence|Cohen]]; [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] Organs Watch chapter; [[lock-margaret|Lock]] on tissue alienation and gift critique).

## Measurement

No single global measure. Evidence classes in the Organs Watch program (Scheper-Hughes with Cohen, from Nov 1999; Bellagio Task Force 1996 precursor):

| Class | Examples (attributed — Scheper-Hughes 2001/02) |
|---|---|
| Ethnographic | Interviews with sellers, buyers, brokers, clinicians; multi-sited “follow the bodies” |
| Documentary/medical | Records, x-rays, sonograms; Johns Hopkins verification in Goffi case |
| Prices / packages | Chennai women sellers: **32,500 rupees (~$1,200)**; Cuba cornea package **$5,720**; Israeli packages **$180–200k**, donor fee **≤$5,000** |
| Institutional | UNOS foreign-patient thresholds (5% / 15%); THOA India 1994; presumed-consent laws |
| Rumor / panic | Body-parts theft legends as political resistance (distinct from documented sales) |

Bellagio Task Force (Rothman et al. 1997; Scheper-Hughes member) concluded kidney sales were more prevalent than previously suspected, in affluent as well as poor nations (attributed).

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed — volume contributors, principally Scheper-Hughes and Cohen, as of 2001/02.)*

**Organ givers vs receivers.** Scheper-Hughes argues commercialized transplant medicine divides the world into invisible, discredited suppliers of spare parts and cherished patients whose proprietary claims on poor bodies go largely unquestioned.

**Nine Organs Watch issue clusters** (sites: Argentina, Brazil, Cuba, Ecuador, India, Israel, Netherlands, South Africa, Turkey, USA): race/class/gender inequalities in harvest and allocation; widespread violation of anti-sale laws; collapse of religious/cultural sanctions under market pressure; kidney-linked debt peonage; compensated and coerced “gifting”; resistance to presumed consent; morgue violations; organ waste amid public/private competition; medically substantiated “kidney theft” allegations (mostly poor and female patients during routine surgeries).

**Immunopolitics (Cohen).** Mid-century tissue-typing expanded recognition of sameness/difference beyond kin; but **suppression** via cyclosporine (Sandoz/Novartis) made the modern transplant industry by enabling flexible, non-HLA-matched donor subpopulations. India’s blood-brokerage extended to kidneys; THOA 1994 limited but did not end markets; Authorization Committees and “related donor” fictions.

**Gift rhetoric.** Official language of gifts, altruism, scarcities, and life-saving conceals commercial and coercive realities (Scheper-Hughes). Lock critiques animism-policing of recipients and the “gift of life” campaign as mobilization devices. Neo-classical bioethics frames paid donation as autonomy “win-win” (position the volume contests).

**Race and postmortem harvest (South Africa).** Township accounts of eyes/tissues taken without consent (e.g. Andrew Sitsheshe, Guguletu); until early 1990s ~**85%** of SA heart recipients white males; **36%** non-white share noted for 1994 as private-sector tourism expanded (Scheper-Hughes notes).

## Explanatory Theories

- **[[body-commodification]]** and **[[commodity-fetishism]]** applied to the fetishized kidney (Scheper-Hughes): invented scarcities; ultimate fetish is life as endless manipulation.
- **[[ethics-of-parts]]** (Cohen): divisible-body ethics responding to market demand.
- **Gift vs commodity** (Lock via Mauss/Taussig/Weiner): body parts typically inalienable until reified as detachable non-self.
- **[[structural-violence]] / [[everyday-violence]]**: organs traffic as covert violence continuous with hostility to underclass bodies.
- Libertarian bioethics: paid markets as fairness (position recorded, not endorsed).

## Interaction

Tied to genomics/cell-line biopolitics (Lock), disaster/forensic body spectacle (Klinenberg chapter, same volume), reproductive markets’ gift-mask (Tober), and longer corpse-commodification history (Anatomy Act lineage). Continuous with Scheper-Hughes’s earlier organ-theft rumor analysis in *Death Without Weeping* (Bom Jesus).

## Debates

- Legalizing paid kidney markets vs prohibition.
- Status of organ-theft **rumors** vs **documented** trafficking and **medically contested** theft allegations — Scheper-Hughes treats rumors as resistance data without collapsing them into proven crime.
- Presumed-consent laws and popular resistance (Brazil, Mexico — as reported).
- Professional self-surveillance (UNOS, Eurotransplant) vs independent human-rights monitoring (Bellagio recommendation largely unrealized as of the chapter).
