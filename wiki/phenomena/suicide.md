# Suicide (social phenomenon)

## Definition and Scope

As a **social** phenomenon, the object is not only the individual act but the **suicide-rate** of a society or group. [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] (*[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]*, 1897) defined suicide scientifically as all cases of death resulting directly or indirectly from a positive or negative act of the victim himself, **which he knows will produce this result** — including refusal of food and indirect chains (e.g. seeking martyrdom by capital offense), excluding intent as the defining mark. He treated the society’s total as a new fact *sui generis* ([[social-fact]]), not a mere sum of independent private acts.

This page currently rests primarily on Durkheim’s classical analysis, with a pre-statistical methodological precursor in [[martineau-harriet|Martineau]] (1838). Later demography and psychiatry will layer as ingested. Do not read 1897 European rates as current epidemiology.

## Measurement

| Measure | Notes |
|---|---|
| Rate per million (or 100,000) inhabitants | Durkheim’s usual expression of “mortality through suicide” |
| Constancy within society | Year-to-year stability greater than general mortality (as he claims) |
| Inter-society variation | Rates double/triple among “civilized” European peoples while general mortality stays similar |
| Group rates | Confession, marital status × age × sex × children, occupation, military vs civilian, season/day |

Official recording bias, classification of death, and 19th-c. category instability are standing validity threats ([[moral-statistics]]).

## Patterns and Findings

**Wiki voice only for multi-source convergence; below is Durkheim-attributed unless noted.**

- Rate more society-specific than general mortality; relatively stable rank-order among European states in his tables even as absolute rates rise.
- Sex: predominantly male in his European series (~4 male : 1 female average as he states).
- Age: tendency increases from childhood to advanced old age in his tables.
- Seasonal/diurnal: warm half-year and daytime peaks he links to intensity of social life, not heat alone.
- Integration patterns: lower rates where religious/domestic/political groups are more tightly integrated ([[egoistic-suicide]] inverse).
- Military excess over same-age civilians ([[altruistic-suicide]]).
- Crisis and boom both raise rates; divorce parallels suicide ([[anomic-suicide]]).
- 19th-c. large rise (hundreds of percent in some states he cites) — pathological intensity on his diagnosis, while a baseline current is normal.

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Summary |
|---|---|
| **Martineau (1838)** | Traveler should contemplate suicide "in the largest sense" (voluntary surrender of life from any cause); character and number indicate religious sentiment and moral notions; duty-suicide stages, martyrdom, military honour, afterlife beliefs as interpretive frames (*How to Observe*, Religion ch.) — pre-statistical indicator program, not rate theory |
| **Durkheim (1897)** | Social rate fixed by integration and regulation; four types; extra-social factors secondary |
| **Psychopathological** (engaged and rejected as explanation of the *rate*) | Insanity, neurasthenia, alcoholism as primary — Durkheim: field for causes, not the social cause |
| **Tarde / imitation** | Contagion as social principle — Durkheim: insufficient for the rate |
| **Race/heredity, climate** | Period rivals — Durkheim: eliminated for the social rate |
| **Later demography / psychiatry** | Multi-level models, clinical risk — not yet ingested here |

## Interaction

Durkheim Book III: relations to crime and homicide are **type-dependent** (not a single inverse “safety valve”); legal and moral evaluation of suicide shift with the cult of the person. Abnormal modern suicide and general social unrest, he argued, share causes — rates “speak for themselves” as measures of disturbance.

- Classification of military, martyrdom, and assisted death
