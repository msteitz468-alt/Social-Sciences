## Definition and Scope

The Bantu expansion is the spread of an agricultural, iron-using culture and its associated languages from a homeland at the Nigeria-Cameroon border in west-central Africa across most of sub-Saharan Africa, beginning around 4,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). Reich frames it as one of the major demographic transformations documented by his lab's ancient-DNA and genome-wide work, and draws an explicit structural parallel to the farming/steppe-pastoralist expansions that reshaped European and Indian ancestry (see [[ancestral-north-indians]], [[yamnaya-culture]]) — a comparison stated as analogy, not identity, across otherwise distinct regional cases (as of Reich 2018).

## Measurement

Reich's account measures the expansion primarily through genome-wide ancestry proportions in present-day and ancient African genomes, ultra-sensitive relatedness/identity-by-descent methods used to infer migration direction, and cross-checking against the geographic diversity of Bantu languages (diversity is highest where a language family has had the longest time to differentiate, consistent with an origin region) (as of Reich 2018).

## Patterns and Findings

By about 2,500 years ago, Bantu speakers had reached Lake Victoria in eastern Africa and had mastered iron toolmaking; by around 1,700 years ago they had reached southern Africa (as of Reich 2018). Bantu languages are most diverse today in Cameroon, consistent with a Cameroon-region origin for proto-Bantu and the culture that carried it (as of Reich 2018); Bantu is, in wiki voice as a brute taxonomic fact, a subset of the larger Niger-Kordofanian (Niger-Congo) language family.

Genetic data using ultra-sensitive relatedness methods indicate the expansion initially moved south from the Cameroon homeland, with East Africa populated later from a southern staging ground — contradicting an earlier model of direct eastward movement from the homeland (as of Reich 2018).

The sickle-cell mutation, which arose independently at least three times in West, west-central, and central Africa, spread to eastern and southern Africa via the population movements associated with the Bantu expansion (as of Reich 2018).

Present-day central African Pygmy hunter-gatherer populations show marked sex-biased admixture from the expansion: no Pygmy population today speaks a non-Bantu language, and the typical historical pattern, per Reich, was Bantu men mixing with Pygmy women, with children raised within Pygmy communities — a pattern also visible in reduced X-chromosome genetic diversity among Pygmies, paralleling reduced X-chromosome diversity documented in non-African populations generally as a signature of repeated male-biased gene flow (as of Reich 2018). See [[sex-biased-admixture]].

Reich attributes much of present-day Africa's population structure to at least four agricultural/language expansions over the last few thousand years — Bantu, Nilo-Saharan, Afroasiatic, and Khoe-Kwadi — which together "threw a veil" obscuring the continent's much deeper population history, including the earlier, broader range of [[khoe-san|South African Forager-related]] ancestry (as of Reich 2018).

## Explanatory Theories

**Cavalli-Sforza (2000):** treats the Bantu expansion as the “most dramatic demic
expansion” in Africa — from the vicinity of Cameroon ~3,000 years ago or earlier
(late Neolithic), aided by iron ~500 BCE; named from the languages of a successful
branch of Niger-Kordofanian; genetics show Bantus relatively homogeneous and
distinct from other West Africans (closest relatives), mixed with Nilotic speakers
in East Africa and Khoisan speakers in the south; Hiernaux correctly inferred
demic expansion; duration a little more than 3,000 years and ~**1.5× faster** than
the European Neolithic expansion (iron vs stone). Western and eastern streams met
in south-central Africa; Dutch at the Cape (~1650) met Bantu still short of the
Cape along some routes (as of Cavalli-Sforza 2000). **Four-way non-identity:**
“Bantu” is a linguistic label linked by him to a demic genetic package — attributed
association, not automatic identity of language=genes=people.

**Reich (2018):** driven by agricultural subsistence advantage and, from roughly
2,500 years ago, iron toolmaking — a demic-diffusion model in which population
movement, not purely cultural transmission, carried both farming technology and
language southward and eastward; directionality revised (southward-first vs
direct-eastward). See [[demic-diffusion]], [[admixture]],
[[cavalli-sforza-genes-peoples-languages-2000]].

## Interaction

The expansion is presented as structurally analogous to — though geographically and temporally distinct from — the farmer and steppe-pastoralist expansions into Europe and South Asia documented elsewhere in Reich's account: in each case, an agriculturally or technologically advantaged incoming population substantially displaced or absorbed resident hunter-gatherer/forager populations, often with sex-biased admixture patterns (as of Reich 2018). See [[khoe-san]], [[ancestral-north-indians]].

## Debates

No dedicated debate page is warranted from this source alone; the directionality of the expansion (southward-first vs. direct-eastward) was itself revised within the genetics literature Reich cites, and is recorded above as a documented revision rather than a live dispute (as of Reich 2018).

### Phillipson — archaeological–linguistic layer (2005)

*(Attributed — [[phillipson-david-w]], [[phillipson-african-archaeology-2005]], Ch. 7; **as of Phillipson 2005**.)*

Phillipson’s continental synthesis supplies the **archaeological package** that genetic accounts often treat only abstractly:

- **No distinct Bronze Age** in most of sub-Saharan Africa: iron was the first metal widely brought into use south of Egypt/North Africa; farming and iron-working began at approximately the same time in much of the area south of the equator (as of Phillipson 2005).
- The early farming/iron package of eastern–central–southern Africa is framed as the **[[chifumbaze-complex|Chifumbaze complex]]** (with regional facies such as Urewe, Kwale, Nkope/Gokomere) — a **material-culture construct**, not an ethnonym.
- **Bantu comparative linguistics** is treated as a complementary evidence class for dispersal history, not a substitute for archaeology; correlation of Chifumbaze archaeology with early Bantu-speaking communities is an **argued association**, not identity of pots = people = languages (four-way non-identity enforced).
- Mode of dispersal is multi-process (demic movement, local adoption, stream models) rather than a single wave; Madagascar’s settlement is treated as a separate Indian Ocean process (as of Phillipson 2005).
- Second-millennium continuities and style breaks (e.g. Luangwa pottery replacing Chifumbaze facies in much of Zambia/Malawi ~11th century; greater western continuity) caution against reading the entire later Iron Age as one expansion event (as of Phillipson 2005).

Pair with Reich/Cavalli-Sforza genetic layers above; do not silently merge 2005 archaeological chronologies with post-2010 aDNA dates.

### Bellwood — *First Farmers* (2005): farming–language and demic package

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter|Bellwood]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* ch. 5, 10–12; genetics **as of Bellwood 2005** only.)*

Bellwood treats the Bantu case as **“one of the most dramatic examples of language/farming dispersal”** and as the strongest sub-Saharan illustration of his [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]] — with “remarkable agreement” between archaeology and linguistics (as of Bellwood 2005). He does **not** equate pottery styles, genetic clines, or ethnonyms with the language family; associations below are his argued correlations.

**Archaeological frame (as of Bellwood 2005).** By ~1500 BC the southern half of Africa (rainforest and lands south of the equator) remained hunter-gatherer territory until Bantu-associated agricultural spread from a presumed Cameroon homeland around/through the rainforest and down the eastern side of sub-Saharan Africa to the Kalahari and the Cape winter-rainfall limit. By the 17th century agriculture reached the Great Fish River (Xhosa/Bantu frontier). Lake Victoria → Natal (~3,500 km) in under a millennium is, with Ehret (1998) cited, “one of the most rapid spreads of an agricultural complex… with an Iron Age cultural affiliation, on record.”

**Chifumbaze as archaeological face.** Bellwood follows Phillipson (1993, 2003) in treating the [[chifumbaze-complex|Chifumbaze complex]] — early pottery styles west of Lake Victoria from the mid/late 1st millennium BC (Urewe oldest ~500–200 BC, then Kwale, Nkope, Matola, South African EIA mid-1st millennium AD) — as the material face of early Iron Age agro-pastoral expansion `associated_with` (argued) Eastern Bantu. Established package: cattle, sheep, goats, chicken, sorghum, pearl and finger millet; widespread iron (rapid forest clearance); coastal villages vs more pastoral inland south. Main burst may have been only a few centuries rather than a full millennium. By AD 500 agricultural villages reached the summer-rainfall limit in Natal — a limit still held in 1652. Cattle through the tsetse belt appear later (initial appearance below 10°S early 1st millennium AD; Gifford-Gonzalez on bovine disease).

**Western vs Eastern streams (positions Bellwood records).** Phillipson and Ehret (linguistic) emphasize expansion mainly down East Africa with separate Western and Eastern facies. Huffman and Denbow give more weight to a secondary Kalundu / Western Stream from lower Congo–Angola after AD 100 meeting eastern Chifumbaze (Kwale/Nkope) in SE Africa. Bellwood judges mapping of the two archaeological origins onto Western vs Eastern **Bantu language** distribution **unclear** — Western Bantu is not currently spoken where Huffman/Denbow’s western stream would predict in SE Africa, so later language replacement is assumed; he calls this “an area of great uncertainty” (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Khoisan boundary.** Southwest of the Kalahari / coastal SW Africa: pottery ~2000 years ago and sheep herding ~1600 years ago **without** associated agriculture; pointed-based pottery lacks Chifumbaze affinity — indigenous Khoisan adoption via Angola/southern Zambia connections (Sadr; Bousman). Bellwood’s summary sequence: after 1000 BC Bantu-speaking agriculturalists spread partly with iron from near Lake Victoria through eastern and ultimately much of southern Africa; other Bantu through rainforest into Angola; this triggered Khoisan sheep herding ~2000 years ago (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Linguistics (attributed synthesis).** Niger-Congo is the largest family by language count (~1,436 languages, ~300 million speakers per Williamson & Blench 2000 as Bellwood cites); Bantu covers vast sub-Saharan space with almost no substratum survivals except Khoisan in the southwest and Hadza, Sandawe, and South Cushitic in Tanzania. Majority linguistic opinion places greatest Niger-Congo subgroup diversity in West Africa. Blench (1993, 1999): Proto-Niger-Congo vocabulary is probably **pre-agricultural** — the base of the family is not an agriculturalist package; expansion east of the Niger was agriculturally driven. Major agriculturalist Niger-Congo subgroup spreads within ~past 5,000 years; Proto-Benue-Congo has reconstructed agricultural vocabulary; Proto-Bantu time-depth ~3,000–4,000 years archaeologically — terms for oil palm, yams, beans, groundnuts, dog, goat, pottery, pigs/warthogs (Vansina 1990), **without** iron, cattle, cereals, or SE Asian bananas/taro at the homeland stage (these later assisted the Eastern Bantu rapid phase of the 1st millennium AD).

**Origin and split (Bellwood’s synthesis).** Bantu origin in Cameroon, initially grasslands north of the rainforest; early Western vs Eastern split prior to 1000 BC (Holden 2002 tree). Eastern Bantu east along northern rainforest fringe → Lake Victoria ~1000 BC; Western Bantu with pottery, oil palms, and yams into West African rainforest, emerging into Angolan savannas. Asian bananas in the 1st millennium BC (Mindzie et al. 2001 banana phytoliths Cameroon ~500 BC raise earlier Indian Ocean possibilities) stimulated denser rainforest settlement by AD 500–1000. Vansina (1990:257) on Western Bantu mechanism — “emigration preferable to continued life as perpetual junior in overcrowded homeland” — is close to Bellwood’s own **founder rank enhancement** model for Austronesians. Ehret (1997–2000): Eastern Bantu at the Great Rift ~3,000 years ago contacted Cushitic/Nilo-Saharan cattle-herders and millet cultivators plus Khoisan foragers, acquiring sorghum, pearl millet, iron, later cattle/sheep/donkeys, SE Asian crops, and Asian chickens — “one of the most rapid episodes of farming spread in world history”; two southern Eastern Bantu dialect continua (Kaskazi, Kusi) side by side down the Rift, ~700–200 BC toward the Zambezi, >3,000 km Kenya→Mozambique in under a millennium. Vansina (1995) prefers a mesh of expanding related communities rather than streams; Bellwood suggests scale difference (local village mesh vs large-scale streams). Collett (1982) simulation: huge distances + short time depths → occasional large groups leapfrogging. Holden (2002): Eastern Bantu unique common ancestor with little post-settlement borrowing; Western Bantu less clearly monophyletic.

**Genetics (as of Bellwood 2005 only).** Soodyall et al. 1996 — distinctive African mtDNA 9-bp deletion spread during Bantu expansion (also in Pygmies by admixture); absent in other West African Niger-Congo → originated after expansion started. Watson et al. 1996 — farmers/pastoralists (including Bantu) less mtDNA sequence variability than hunter-gatherers → recent population increase. Underhill et al. 2001a — Bantu migration strongly marked by Y haplogroup III; Bantu mtDNA more localized. Poloni et al. — male dispersal farther than female. **Bellwood’s lean:** genetic support for demic diffusion in the Bantu case is “quite firm.” Ch. 12 frames Iron Age eastern/southern Africa as rapid **cross-latitudinal** spread, with iron tools (vs purely Neolithic southern China spreads until Austronesian maritime technology).

**Identity caution:** “Bantu” remains a linguistic label; Chifumbaze a ceramic/Iron Age complex; genetic signals demic correlates **as of 2005**. Do not merge with post-2010 aDNA directionality revisions (Reich southward-first) as if they were Bellwood’s positions.

### Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*) — anti-package critique

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Ch. 8; genetics **(as of Barker 2006)**; not an adjudication against Bellwood/Reich layers above.)*

- **Historiography of package models.** Barker inventories decades of migration explanations: J. D. Clark’s SW Asian farmers into Nile then Sahara/Sahel under desiccation; Iron Age farmers Sahel→Cape with metallurgy from Nile; Greenberg (Nigeria–Cameroon homeland) vs Guthrie (south of forests) vs Oliver’s dual-stream combination; Phillipson proto-Bantu east then two southward streams; Ehret Proto-Cushite/Sudanic/Bantu crop-stock reconstructions. Consensus he critiques: farming as package of new people + new language + pottery (+ iron in the south) + agriculture; Bantu acquired farming from Afro-Asiatic Nile farmers and carried it south (attributed historiography).
- **Core thesis.** Manner of appearance of domestic animals, pottery, crop farming, and iron tools in East, Central, and South Africa is **no longer consistent with the Bantu colonist model**. They did **not** spread together with coherent directional movements. Where good regional archaeologies exist, evidence suggests primarily **existing populations adopting new resources** and integrating them into existing lifeways for subsistence and/or social reasons, rather than arrival of Bantu colonist-farmers (Barker position). Lane (2004 via Barker): demographic, linguistic, economic, and technological change may have operated **independently** of one another.
- **Archaeological texture.** East African Pastoral Neolithic herding widespread 3rd–2nd millennium BC with continued hunting/gathering; Prolonged Drift fauna compatible with mixed-economy seasonal camps that problematize linking assemblage types to ethnic/linguistic groups. Early Iron Age South/Central Africa: herding + hunting widespread early 1st millennium AD; crop remains sparse; whether herding preceded, followed, or developed with mixed farming debated. Cape domestic sheep by end 1st millennium BC (AMS Blombos, Boomplaas, etc.) centuries after pottery; herding+hunting more likely than pure rustling; no coincident human skeletal change (Hausman) (attributed findings as Barker reports).
- **Genetics (as of Barker 2006, cautious).** Microsatellite cattle-breed data suggest cattle spread south down eastern South Africa (Hanotte et al. 2002). mtDNA of 16 Bantu-speaking groups: high homogeneity, complex short-distance movements rather than single migration (Salas et al. 2002). Y-chromosome: relatively recent Bantu lineages + significant Bantu/Khoisan admixture (Cruciani et al. 2002). Jobling et al. 2004 emphasize tentative nature, limited sampling, ambiguous population/ethnic definitions (attributed).
- **Linguistics re-read.** Attempts at tight archaeological–linguistic linkages criticized from both disciplines; Eastern Bantu crop-word stems lack spatial patterning predicted by traditional agricultural-colonist model (Philippson & Bahuchet 1996). Vansina: population explosion and aboriginal extinction not necessary to explain Bantu language spread (attributed).
- **Ethnographic analogy and reflexivity.** Barker: kaleidoscope of recent African forager–herder–farmer relations better than one-way packages. Bantu migration theories fed South African apartheid mythologies and similar Bantu/non-Bantu dichotomies with disastrous consequences (e.g. Rwanda Tutsi/Hutu ethnicizing scholarship) — political misuse is historiographic content, not a verdict on demic models themselves (attributed). Four-way non-identity enforced: language ≠ pottery ≠ genes ≠ people. Cross-links: [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]], [[animal-domestication]], [[chifumbaze-complex]], [[barker-graeme]].
