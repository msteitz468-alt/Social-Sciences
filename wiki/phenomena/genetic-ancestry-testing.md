## Definition and Scope

Genetic ancestry testing is the contemporary consumer and commercial industry that uses DNA analysis — Y-chromosome and mitochondrial-DNA haplogroup assignment, and increasingly genome-wide admixture estimation — to offer individuals claims about their ethnic, geographic, or "tribal" ancestral origins. David Reich, writing as a population geneticist rather than an industry participant, discusses the phenomenon both sympathetically (as a genuine and often meaningful form of identity reclamation) and critically (as prone to overinterpretation of what the underlying data can actually support) (as of Reich 2018).

## Measurement

Early ancestry-testing services, such as African Ancestry (co-founded by geneticist Rick Kittles), used Y-chromosome and mitochondrial-DNA matching to assign customers — particularly African Americans seeking to reconnect with ancestry severed by the Atlantic slave trade — to specific African "tribal" or ethnic origins. Reich reports that such assignments are easily overinterpreted: West African Y-chromosome and mitochondrial-DNA types are often too similar across different ethnic groups to support confident tribal-level assignment (as of Reich 2018).

Consumer genome-wide services such as 23andMe extended testing to estimate proportions of ancestry from broad continental or historical populations, including estimates of Neanderthal-derived ancestry percentage. Reich reports that 23andMe's Neanderthal-ancestry-percentage estimates offered between 2011 and 2015 were highly inaccurate, given that true population variation in Neanderthal ancestry differs only by tenths of a percentage point between individuals — well below what the method could reliably resolve. Reich raised this problem in a 2014 paper, and 23andMe subsequently withdrew the affected statements (as of Reich 2018).

## Patterns and Findings

Reich reports an unpublished 2012 study (with Katarzyna Bryc) of more than 15,000 self-identified African Americans, finding that West African source ancestry has become so genetically homogenized by the demographic history of the slave trade that mainland West African regional or ethnic differences are largely undetectable in most African American genomes. The one documented exception is the Gullah/Geechee Sea Island community, whose ancestry shows a specific, detectable connection to Sierra Leone — consistent with independent linguistic evidence that Gullah grammar preserves specific West African structural features (as of Reich 2018).

## Explanatory Theories

Reich frames the popularity of genetic ancestry testing partly as a response to ruptures in documentary and oral-history record-keeping — most acutely for descendants of enslaved Africans in the Americas, whose genealogical records were deliberately or circumstantially destroyed. He cites Alex Haley's *Roots* (1976) and Henry Louis Gates Jr.'s television series *Finding Your Roots* as cultural precedents and parallel projects using genealogical and genetic evidence to recover ancestry severed by slavery (as of Reich 2018).

## Interaction

Genetic ancestry testing sits adjacent to, but is analytically distinct from, the scientific population-genetics research documented elsewhere in this wiki (e.g. [[admixture]], [[ancient-dna]]): the consumer industry uses similar underlying data and methods but operates at far lower statistical resolution and under commercial rather than peer-reviewed incentives. Reich is notably reticent about consumer testing of his own ancestry, declining to test his own (Ashkenazi Jewish) DNA on the grounds that Ashkenazi populations are already "overstudied," and expressing general suspicion of scientists unusually interested in tracing their own group's genetic history (as of Reich 2018). See also [[race]] for the adjacent debate over genetics and racial categories.
