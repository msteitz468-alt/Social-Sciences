# State Formation

## Definition and Scope

**State formation** is the historical emergence of specialized, enduring political domination — in this wiki’s first systematic treatment, the process by which, according to [[engels-friedrich|Engels]], the gentile constitution gives way to [[the-state|the state]] once [[private-property]] and class antagonisms split the [[gens]]. Other traditions (conquest theories, hydraulic hypotheses, Weberian rationalization, neoevolutionary chiefdom→state, Fried’s stratified→state path) are noted as positions as sources arrive; they are not collapsed into Engels.

**Wittfogel hydraulic path (1957, attributed).** *[[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-1957|Oriental Despotism]]* proposes that large-scale irrigation and flood control generate a managerial **[[hydraulic-society]]** whose state is stronger than society ([[agromanagerial-despotism]] / [[oriental-despotism]]). Contested one-factor model — see [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] and [[wittfogel-oriental-despotism-study]].

**Renfrew Aegean path (1972, attributed).** *[[renfrew-emergence-of-civilisation-1972|The Emergence of Civilisation]]* rejects single-factor paths (including irrigation and simple redistribution) for Minoan–Mycenaean palace “principalities,” favoring [[multiplier-effect]] coupling among subsistence ([[mediterranean-polyculture]]), craft, social ranking, symbolic systems, and trade after an endogenous third millennium — against migration/[[ex-oriente-lux|Oriental transplant]] origins.

**Algaze supraregional path (1993/2005, attributed).** [[algaze-guillermo|Algaze]] ([[algaze-uruk-world-system-2005|The Uruk World System]]) argues, against internalist accounts of Mesopotamian state formation, that the earliest states cannot be explained inside the alluvium alone: the [[uruk-expansion|Uruk expansion]] and its [[core-periphery|core–periphery]]/[[informal-empire|informal-empire]] exchange network were constitutive, not epiphenomenal — a *supraregional* driver of primary state formation. Contested (Stein, Frangipane) as to the degree of core dominance — see [[uruk-world-system-debate]].

## Measurement

No single index. Engels uses historical case narratives (Athens, Rome, Germans). Modern comparative politics and archaeology use settlement hierarchies, administrative artifacts, writing, violence specialists — different evidence class.

## Explanatory Theories

### Elias — monopoly mechanism and courtization (attributed)

[[elias-norbert|Elias]] (*[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]*, 1939) explains European state formation as the outcome of free competition among warrior houses tending toward dual monopolies of physical force and taxation ([[monopoly-mechanism]]), royal balancing of estates ([[royal-mechanism]]), and [[courtization]] of warriors within [[court-society]]. He process-ualizes Weber's [[monopoly-of-violence]] and links monopoly pacification to [[self-constraint]] and manners standards ([[civilizing-process]]). France is weighted; England/German lands/Italy appear comparatively. Evidence class: secondary historical synthesis + process model — not primary archival monograph.

### Mann — social caging, multi-power origins, empires (1986, attributed)

[[mann-michael|Michael Mann]] (*[[mann-sources-of-social-power-vol1-1986|Sources of Social Power, Vol. I]]*, 1986) rejects a *general* prehistoric origin of the state: rank and temporary centralization were widespread, but permanent stratification and the state were rare, local products of [[social-caging]] — fixation into dense cooperative cages (alluvial extremes) that closed exit options people and rival elites had used to evade durable domination. Civilization, stratification, and the state are treated as patterned accidents around multi-power-actor civilizations and empires of domination, not as the next stage of general social evolution. Political power is one of four overlapping sources ([[iemp-model|IEMP]]); formation paths run through interstitial organizational outflanking (military, economic, ideological, and territorial-centralizing), not class motor alone. Empires appear as extensive authoritative organizations with often high [[despotic-power|despotic]] and feeble [[infrastructural-power|infrastructural]] reach (e.g. Persia as ideal-typical patrimonial empire). Complements Engels class-origin and Elias monopoly paths as a multi-causal organizational history; evidence class: secondary comparative-historical synthesis. See [[the-state]], [[organic-national-state]] (early-modern European endpoint of Vol. I's European narrative).

### Tilly — war-driven extraction and the coercion/capital trajectories (1990/1992, attributed)

[[tilly-charles|Tilly]] (*[[tilly-coercion-capital-1992|Coercion, Capital, and European States]]*)
supplies the wiki's charter statement of the [[bellicist-theory-of-state-formation|war-centered
account]] of European state formation, AD 990–1992: "extraction and struggle over the means of
war created the central organizational structures of states," with state structure "chiefly as
a by-product" rather than design. Regional balances of concentrated capital (cities) and
coercion (armed lords) yielded three trajectories — coercion-intensive (Russia, Poland),
capital-intensive (Venice, the Dutch Republic), and [[capitalized-coercion]] (England, France) —
all converging on the [[national-state]] because that form "won at war." Corollaries: the
extraction→bargaining→[[citizenship|rights]] loop; the shift from [[indirect-rule|indirect]] to
[[direct-rule]] (French Revolution paradigm); militarization producing civilianized government;
and the post-1945 divergence — externally supplied coercion without domestic bargaining yields
Third World military rule. Answers his six questions about the concentric geography of European
state formation, representative institutions, and the Mediterranean→Atlantic shift. Complements
Elias's [[monopoly-mechanism]] (whose tax/force "duo" Tilly extends to "a trio" with credit)
and Mann's IEMP reading; evidence class: secondary comparative-historical synthesis + compiled
war datasets. Study + hub: [[tilly-coercion-capital-study]] ·
[[hubs/studies/tilly-coercion-capital-study]].

## Explanatory Theories (continued)

- **Engels / historical materialism:** state arises from need to hold class conflict in check; territorial membership + special public force; typically state of the economically dominant class; exceptional relative independence when classes balance; will [[withering-away-of-the-state|wither]] when classes end.
- **Weberian (already in wiki):** state defined by means (monopoly of legitimate force), not by class origin; formation path not the same problem as definition.
- **State-centered structuralism (Skocpol 1979):** revolutionary *state-building* as a distinct mode of state formation. [[skocpol-theda|Skocpol]] argues (attributed) that social revolutions transform proto-bureaucratic agrarian monarchies into more centralized, bureaucratic, mass-incorporating national states — driven by the tasks of consolidating power under international military pressure, not by revolutionary ideology. The state is theorized as a potentially autonomous organization ([[state-autonomy]]), not merely a class organ. See [[structural-theory-of-revolution]], [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study]], and [[france-russia-china-revolutions]].
- **Fried neo-evolution (1967, attributed):** *[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|EPS]]* — state forms from unstable [[stratified-society|stratified society]] (differential access to basic resources) as power organized superior to kinship; [[pristine-state|pristine]] (autochthonous, political vacuum) vs [[secondary-state|secondary]] (stimuli/models from pre-existing states). War and slavery rejected as *initiators* of stratification; conquest fits after state virtually born. See [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]], [[service-fried-political-typology-debate]].
- **Service neo-evolution (1975, attributed) — integrative/benefit path.** *[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and Civilization]]* argues the state arose from the organizational *benefits* of centralized [[redistribution]], war-leadership, public works, and mediation — "legitimizing itself in maintaining the whole society" — not from class repression; coercion is a symptom of the system's failure, and the observed stratification is political office (governors/governed), not economic ownership. The principal integrative rival to Fried's conflict path and to Wittfogel/Engels; see [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]], [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]], and the [[archaic-state-formation-pathways|comparison of the six archaic civilizations]]. Empirically superseded on many case details (1975 secondary readings). Major archaeological primary-state monographs still pending.
- **Adams multilinear urban careers (1966, attributed).** *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|The Evolution of Urban Society]]* ([[adams-evolution-urban-society-study|study]]) compares early Mesopotamia and central Mexico as independent ([[pristine-state|pristine]]) trajectories sharing one processual pattern: temple-centered theocracy → militaristic differentiation → trade-and-tribute conquest states ([[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]]). State consummated when political power claims a position "above" parties; kingship emergence paralleled across hemisphere and ~4,000 years. Surplus treated as institutionally mobilized gross deployable wealth (Polanyi/Orans), not automatic efficiency. Stratification and polity are analytically independent but intertwined; corporate kin land control becomes encapsulated by elite estates. Wittfogel-style irrigation determinism rejected as independent cause; irrigation can induce stratification as capital investment but is not prime mover. Two-case mid-1960s synthesis — reanalyzed-contested against later multi-pathway primary-state literature.
- **Sanders & Price (1968), attributed — ecological/symbiotic pathway.** *[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Mesoamerica: The Evolution of a Civilization]]* argues pristine state formation is a *local* ecosystem-level process: **population growth → competition → cooperation → surplus/specialization → centralized regulation → stratification**, driven within circumscribed, ecologically diverse zones (Carneiro 1961 circumscription applied). Two ecological engines: **hydraulic agriculture** (converting chiefdoms to states via surplus and management needs — a revised, competition-based version of the [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate|hydraulic hypothesis]], against Wittfogel's scale-determinism and Adams's causal-priority critique) and **[[symbiotic-region|symbiosis]]** (economic complementarity of contrasting zones). Civilization is held near-"inevitable" given microgeographic diversity plus hydraulic potential, so the explanandum becomes "precocity and retardation," not occurrence; diffusion/migration are demoted to accelerants. "Civilizations without cities" (Classic Maya) are Fried's [[secondary-state|secondary states]] lacking local symbiosis; minimum ~10,000 persons proposed for the civilization level. A strongly materialist reading — the conflict/surplus counterpart to [[service-origins-state-1975|Service's]] integrative path (see [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]) and the companion to [[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|Adams 1966]]. 1968-vintage chronology/demography, date-stamped.

### Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) — the Basin of Mexico synthesis, refining Sanders & Price

*[[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|The Basin of Mexico]]* ([[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study|study]]) restates the 1968 program as three explicit "lawlike generalizations" — the **law of biotic potential** (populations tend to increase), the **law of least effort**, and the **law of minimization of risk** — and states a preference for the "coercive" reading of civilizational evolution over the "voluntaristic" one. Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) explicitly adopt [[population-pressure|Boserup's]] (1965) treatment of population growth as an effectively independent variable (against critics including Blanton 1975 and Rappaport 1971, whom they name), and derive their Basin agricultural-intensification model from Boserup. They synthesize this with [[circumscription|Carneiro's]] (1970/1972) circumscription theory: complex societies "should emerge first in smaller, socially or environmentally circumscribed areas," with rough population ceilings by political-organizational level (bands hundreds, tribes thousands, chiefdoms tens of thousands, states millions) — applying the model directly to Basin settlement data (population buildup in the southern Basin as a Carneiro-style social-circumscription "buffer" effect). They explicitly reject explaining Mesoamerican state formation via long-distance trade, citing Howard Odum's (1971) energetics framework (most energy in low-technology systems is produced and consumed locally) and estimating that well under 10,000 Teotihuacanos derived substantial income from long-distance trade at the city's peak.

A load-bearing methodological concession: Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) explicitly acknowledge **bidirectional/feedback causation** between hydraulic agriculture and state centralization at Teotihuacan — "we have strong doubts that this can be done even with ethnographic data" (i.e., that cause and effect can be cleanly sorted) — treating their own hydraulic-agriculture-causes-the-state argument as reversible in the same breath they argue it. They further self-critique Sanders' own earlier [[symbiotic-region|symbiotic-region]] model (Sanders 1959, 1968): it works better for explaining Late Horizon *integration* than for explaining the *origin* of political centralization, since First Intermediate settlement was concentrated in narrow piedmont bands where most resource zones were already locally accessible within single communities.
- **Graeber & Wengrow (2021), attributed — anti-origin thesis.** *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|The Dawn of Everything]]* argues that "the state has no origin": what modern theory bundles as the state is better decomposed into [[three-elementary-forms-of-domination]] (control of violence / information / charisma → sovereignty, bureaucracy, competitive political field) with separate historical origins and no necessary fusion into the nation-state. First- and second-order regimes (Olmec, Chavín, Natchez/Shilluk; Egypt/Inca, Mesopotamia, Classic Maya, etc.) illustrate mixes rather than embryonic modern states. Challenges scale=hierarchy assumptions and single pathways. Contested reception: [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]. Empirical site claims remain G&W's secondary readings, not wiki-voice archaeology.

## Interaction

Tied to [[family]] form (monogamy), [[private-property]], commodity production, and town/country division in Engels’s ch. IX synthesis.


### Adams Heartland / irrigation–state (1981)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] (*[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland]]*; also 1966 comparative lectures) positions large-scale irrigation as **derivative of** prior urban/state organization and argues **limited, loose coupling** of central government and irrigation systems in Mesopotamia — a major empirical POSITION in the [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] (not a wiki-voice law). Prefer cities over potsherd-operationalized "states" as study object (Heartland Conclusion). See [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]].

### Bogucki 1999 — tributary states and chiefdoms in the shadow of states

*(Attributed — [[bogucki-peter|Bogucki]], *[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|The Origins of Human Society]]* 1999, Ch.8. Synthesis; case details as of late 1990s.)*

- **Concept unbundling:** “civilization,” “urbanism,” and “the state” are overlapping but non-identical; Childe’s ten civilization criteria are inadequate as a checklist (many traits predate “civilization”; writing not universal).
- **State definition:** adopts Brumfiel (1994) — powerful institutionalized hierarchies of public decision-making created to implement production relations in stratified societies and mediate conflict among economic interest groups; distinguishes **tributary** states (elites extract goods/services by coercive power) from capitalist states (markets generate wealth; state regulates competition).
- **Early states as tributary:** most early states extend chiefdom political economy by **direct intervention** in household surplus (taxation, corvée) backed by force — the key move is bureaucratic apparatus, not merely scaled-up redistribution.
- **Urbanism:** cities = more than dense agglomerations; territorial role and multi-generational sustainability matter; Bogucki rejects [[cahokia]] as city/state (as of 1999 synthesis).
- **Literacy:** economic roots emphasized for Mesopotamia; independent inventions elsewhere; social accelerant but **not required** for statehood (Andes, early sub-Saharan cases).
- **Shadow-chiefdoms thesis:** adjacency to states can stabilize complex [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]] and prestige commercialism without full state adoption for long periods — a major secondary-complexity pathway.
- **Series division of labor:** detailed rise-of-civilization treatment deferred to Wenke companion volume; Bogucki’s job is situating states in the overall sweep of prehistory.

## Debates

Class-instrument vs autonomous state ([[relative-autonomy-of-the-state-debate]]); unilinear vs multiple pathways; archaeological primary-state formation vs secondary formation — open. Fried’s [[pristine-state]]/[[secondary-state]] vocabulary is the classic anthropological phrasing of the primary/secondary cut ([[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]]). **G&W "no origin" / three-forms decomposition** vs unitary origin stories — open: [[dawn-of-everything-debate]].

**White (1959), attributed.** *[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|The Evolution of Culture]]* Chs. 12–13 treats post–[[agricultural-revolution|Agricultural Revolution]] state formation as qualitative revolution from kinship to property-based civil society, coordinated by the [[state-church]] composite (coercion of surplus, class split, territorial organization). Not a primary archaeological formation study; a universal-evolutionary synthesis. See [[universal-evolution]], [[state-church]], [[the-state]].

## Geopolitical Theory (Collins, attributed)

[[collins-randall|Collins]]'s *[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]* (1975) offers a [[geopolitical-theory-of-the-state|geopolitical theory]] of state formation and disintegration: state size and centralization follow from weapon/supply/administration types and from **heartland** resource base (militarily accessible from a self-sufficient economic center), while **peripheral states** (fewer fronts) accumulate advantage over **interior states** locked in multi-front stalemate. Empire durations range from a few years (tribal conquest coalitions) to centuries (insulated bureaucratic empires); conquest among comparably bureaucratized rivals is least stable — Collins's model of overextension. All claims **attributed** to Collins (1975).

### Childe — temple, king, iron, and empire (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] offers an archaeological pathway (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* — attributed): Copper Age conquest and temple corporations produce stratified societies; Bronze Age metal armament monopolies consolidate state power; divine kingship and residence-based polities; Iron Age cheap weapons wither Mediterranean monarchy into republics/oligarchy/democracy (Athens) while Oriental empires preserve Bronze Age theocracy; Roman Republican exploitation then Principate totalitarianism reverse toward colonate/serfdom. Complementary to Engels gens→state and Elias monopoly paths — different evidence base (material culture + classical texts synthesized), not a replacement.

### Trigger — early-civilization forms (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|Understanding Early Civilizations]]*; study [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]) does not offer a single prime-mover *origin* model; he compares **how** seven early class societies worked. Load-bearing positions for this page: (1) [[early-civilization]] = simplest class-based society; (2) only two viable political forms — [[city-state]] vs [[territorial-state]] — as stable alternatives, not stages ([[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]]); (3) universal sacred [[kingship]]; (4) surplus appropriation (~10 farm families : 1–2 non-producers as synthesis ratio) via tax/rent/corvée/tribute ([[wealth-appropriation]]); (5) irrigation and metallurgy rejected as monocauses; (6) functional constraints + panhuman cognitive/psychological factors + cultural tradition jointly explain regularities. Complements Adams’s two-case urban parallel and Service’s integrative archaic pathways without settling [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]. Evidence horizon ~2001 secondary synthesis — reanalyzed-contested on case detail.

### Yoffee — myths of the archaic state (2005, attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]*) rejects neo-evolutionist stage pathways (chiefdom→large despotic state) as **factoids**. Program: (1) earliest states often **small city-states** within larger **civilizations**, not totalitarian mega-polities; (2) states form when differentiated, stratified groups are **recombined** under new ideologies that naturalize central leadership; (3) **dimensions of power** (economic, social, political) and everyday roles, not trait lists; (4) [[evolution-of-simplicity]] / legibility projects (codes, writing, ritual-metal control) that often fail to erase local authorities; (5) [[societal-collapse]] and non-state trajectories (Chaco, Cahokia) as equal research objects; (6) sequence comparison and tiered [[ethnographic-analogy|ethnographic analogy]] replace “contemporary ancestors.” Mesopotamian evolutionary essay (Ubaid–Uruk phase transition–city-state civilization) is the longest case. Complements Trigger’s two-form typology and Adams’s urban careers; directly challenges Service/Fried operationalizations. Debate: [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

### Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas — collective action and the founding of Monte Albán (2022, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* The authors explicitly frame Monte Albán's founding (~500 BC) and rapid Period I growth through [[collective-action-theory|collective-action theory]] rather than the "neoevolutionist" top-down, chief/ruler-legitimizing model: "the turn to hierarchical political complexity conforms to expectations of collective action theory, taking the form of more faceless polities that did not materially celebrate or legitimize chiefs or rulers." Cooperative labor investment (terracing, retaining walls, canal building, shared household walls) is attributed to defense incentives and a "universalizing ideology" centered on the Cocijo (lightning/rain) deity, which they argue promoted collective goals over the prior factional dual (earth/sky) symbolism of [[san-jose-mogote|San José Mogote]]. They state: "it is hard to see early community foundation processes as having been driven simply by force or coercion since the earliest residents came to the site from different places, opted to invest their labor... and were able to enhance their standard of living."

Four lines of evidence are offered for Monte Albán's status as regional political capital from Period I onward: (1) sudden, environmentally anomalous population appearance on a previously unoccupied, marginal hilltop; (2) inability to feed itself from its own agricultural catchment, implying dependence on external labor/produce supply; (3) faster growth and larger size than any contemporary community; (4) a uniquely large ceremonial concourse and public architecture, including the only known "danzantes" gallery of carved war-captive monuments. They propose defense against raiding as the key factor fostering cooperation, and characterize the site's founding using the Greek-historical analogy of [[synoikism]] (flagged as analogy, not identity) and, separately, [[blanton-richard|Blanton's]] own earlier "**disembedded capital**" hypothesis (Blanton 1978) — a neutral, newly built regional capital disembedded from any single participating polity's territory, comparable (as cited cases) to Washington DC, Ottawa, Canberra, New Delhi, and Brasília. This hypothesis met mixed reception: some support (Adams 1991; Carmack, Gasco, and Gossen 1996; Foster 1997) alongside substantial criticism from neoevolutionist-aligned scholars (Sanders 1979; Sanders and Nichols 1988; Sanders and Santley 1978; Willey 1979) arguing the site was chosen for superior agricultural potential — a claim Blanton et al. 2022 call "clearly inaccurate," citing their own terrace-mapping survey showing "all 2,000 terraces we mapped had been laboriously constructed... only a few were agricultural." [POSITION recorded, not adjudicated.]

**Anti-diffusionism and the Oriental/Occidental critique.** The authors identify at least four independent instances of primary state formation within the broader Old World interaction zone (Nile Valley, Lower Mesopotamia, Indus Valley, North China; after Service 1975), plus Mesoamerican and Andean cases as "clearly distinct" from these and from each other, and state flatly: "Nowhere in the world can the earliest states be shown to have come about by simple diffusion from a single source" — explicitly characterizing diffusionist New World-origin claims (Egypt, Phoenicians, "Lost Tribes of Israel") as pseudoarchaeology tied to racist assumptions that Native Americans could not have built large-scale societies independently (citing Bernal 1980; Feder 1996). They also critique the "Oriental" (autocratic, historically prior) vs. "Occidental" (egalitarian-divergent, post-~1500 CE European) two-path dual model of state formation — associated with Montesquieu, Marx, Engels, and Tilly (1975) and still represented in some contemporary literature (Acemoglu, Johnson, and Robinson 2005; Thapar 1992; Tilly 1990) — as a framework "many archaeologists have come to doubt" (citing their own earlier work, Blanton and Fargher 2008; Fargher and Heredia Espinoza 2016). Cross-links: [[circumscription]], [[population-pressure]] (their coactive critique of population-pressure prime-movers), [[monte-alban]], [[zapotec-writing]].

### Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

[[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski]] supply a mid-2000s **archaeological primary-state** survey (chs. 7–14; attribute as of 2007).

**Definition and six primary areas.** States are stratified societies with centralized political/religious elites, full-time specialists, codified law, armies, taxes/tribute, markets, and settlement-size diversity; Wright and Johnson define the state as a polity with ≥3 decision-making hierarchy levels; early states formed essentially independently in Mesopotamia, Egypt, Indus Valley, China, Mesoamerica, and Peru (attributed). Parallel transitions toward social complexity occurred largely independently in those areas (and a few others); later similar changes also occurred in contact with the earliest civilizations (attributed). Complex societies have existed only five or six millennia yet nearly replaced simpler forms that dominated for a million years or more (attributed). Mesopotamia vignette: ordinary village life c. 6000 BCE vs slave/king/specialists/taxes/war/city scale c. 3000 BCE (attributed). Many early states competed as peer polities until one expanded drastically — first large states toward end of 4th millennium BCE in Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Indus; within ~1000 years in China; Inka and Aztec imperial scale just before 16th-c. European arrival (attributed).

**Chiefdom → state pathway elements.** Earle chiefdoms (regional hierarchy + hereditary inequality) as middle range; band/tribe/chiefdom/state labels treated as static descriptive types poorly suited to origin analysis (McGuire via W&O) (attributed). Webster: warfare breaks chiefdom kinship constraints; Kipp & Schortman: luxury markets destabilize chiefdom personal ties → tyranny/state; Harris: grain stores as "energy gates" in circumscribed farmlands for pristine states (attributed). Feinman corporate vs network political strategies as concurrent options without full egalitarianism (attributed).

**Causal models surveyed (all attributed via W&O).** Leisure-time → civilization **rejected**. Steward/Childe arid–semi-arid intensive agriculture pattern. Athens/Boserup population–food imbalance. Cowgill critique of "strictly from hunger" population determinism. Wittfogel hydraulic hypothesis + empirical problems (simple irrigators without despotism; complex irrigation often not pre-dating monumental urbanism in SW Asia). Carneiro: warfare + population growth + environmental circumscription (Peru sequence: fission → intensification → conquest hierarchy). Contemporary archaeology (as of 2007) widely holds key analyses must be at socioeconomic and political levels, not only demography/environment/assumed warfare. Classical Marxist class-from-land-control → state as elite instrument (limits of archaeological testing noted). Evolutionary cultural theory: group-level selection; punctuated paths. Trigger holistic/synthetic program. Brumfiel & Earle commercial/adaptationist/political models of exchange. Functional explanations heuristic but incomplete (coordination-of-irrigation does not explain why society did not stay egalitarian or take another path). **Ch. 7 conclusion:** no powerful comprehensive explanation yet (attributed).

**Case complications.** Egypt: warfare mechanism not primary cause; Wittfogel poorly fits passive Nile flood; urbanization not indispensable; slow population growth; redistributive almost chiefdom-like Old Kingdom economy vs later Mesopotamia (attributed). Harappan: state vs chiefdom typology disputed (settlement hierarchy supports state; Service-type brevity/weak militarism arguments challenged by newer fortification/writing evidence; Shaffer: urban literate culture without usual hereditary-elite package) (attributed). China: standard early-civilization markers present; less urban early than Sumer; nomad–farmer frontier continual (Lattimore); Chang wealth–power feedback under shamanic religious basis (attributed). Andes: multi-causal — rich maritime + agricultural resources essential; Carneiro circumscription particularly applicable but sustained warfare more important after monumentality, regional economic coordination, craft specialization, and great religions already appeared; geographic isolation and lack of draft animals constrained development (attributed). "Collapse" often decentralization/localization (Kenoyer), not mass extinction (attributed). Slow population growth and weak early agricultural pressure in Egypt and Indus undermine simple population-pressure engines for those cases (attributed). ### Connah — African archaeological synthesis (2016, attributed)

## Flannery & Marcus (2012): kingdoms as forced unifications (attributed)

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]].)* In *The Creation of Inequality*, Flannery and Marcus argue that a **first-generation state (kingdom)** is not a chiefdom that "simply got bigger" — "there is apparently no social steroid that can trigger that kind of growth." In all their cases the first kingdom arose through the **forced unification of competing rank societies**: a leader creates an overarching administrative tier and needs the title "king" because "chief" has become merely a provincial title. They adopt Charles Spencer's model in which a chief whose growth plateaus must step up demand, intensify production, or **expand territory** — and only the third option, pushing administration past what personal rule can manage, tips a chiefdom into a state. Once one polity consolidates, **chain reactions** ripple outward as neighbors nucleate defensively (documented archaeologically for the Zapotec at Monte Albán and in the Maya lowlands). Their comparative cases — Hawai'i (Kamehameha), the [[zulu|Zulu]] (Shaka), Hunza, Merina, the Moche and Zapotec and Maya states, and predynastic Egypt (the "Scorpion King" / Narmer) — share a **usurper-and-conquest** pattern and generate a **new form of inequality**, ethnic second-class citizenship, as kingdoms incorporate foreign labor faster than they tolerate foreign difference. They distinguish first-generation states (from rank societies) from later "generations" — Sargon's Akkadian **empire** being a "macro-state, each province of which had once been a kingdom in its own right." See [[the-state]], [[chiefly-cycling]], [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]], [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]].

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017) reframes earliest state formation as **capture and intensification of a preexisting late-Neolithic grain-and-manpower module**, not invention of sedentism, farming, irrigation, or towns. All claims below are **attributed** to Scott’s synthesis (Mesopotamian alluvium focus, ~4000–2000 BCE, with comparative triangulation). Core concept: [[grain-hypothesis]].

**Neolithic before the state (long lag).** Scott argues first small stratified tax-collecting walled states appear in the Tigris–Euphrates valley only around **3100 BCE** — more than four millennia after crop domestication and sedentism — so any automatic sequence from cultivation to the state fails. Sedentism, the [[domus-complex|domus]], irrigation, and small towns were, he holds, autonomous Neolithic achievements already in place; the embryonic state arises by **harnessing** that module for control and appropriation. Fixed-field grain agriculture is treated as necessary scaffolding, not a switch that turns on states.

**Elective affinity, not cause and effect.** In Weberian terms Scott describes an **elective affinity**: the Neolithic agro-complex made state formation *possible but not certain*; sedentary irrigated alluvial farming without a state was common, yet “there was no such thing as a state that did not rest on an alluvial, grain-farming population.” What is required is not ecological richness per se but **appropriable, measurable dominant grain** plus an administrable cultivating population. Diverse wetlands with many subsistence options are, he argues, **illegible** and poor zones of successful state making — even when they produce wealth and towns (southern alluvium wetlands for more than a millennium before statelets).

**Grain module and state landscaping.** Virtually all classical early states are cast as **grain states** (wheat, barley; millets on the Yellow River; later rice and New World maize; partial Inka potato–maize exception with maize as tax crop). Cereals alone, Scott claims, are “premier political crops” — visible, divisible, assessable, storable, transportable, rationable ([[grain-hypothesis]], [[legibility]]). Once established, the state **amplifies** the agro-ecology it did not invent: canals, captives on arable land, penalizing noncultivators, forbidding swidden/foraging, preventing flight (“state landscaping”). Early states strive for a legible landscape of taxable grain and a population held for corvée, conscription, and grain production — often failing for ecological, epidemiological, and political reasons, but that aim is the “steady glint in its eye.” Agro-geography: “no alluvium, no state”; Scott is “tempted to say ‘no water transport, no state’” as only a slight exaggeration.

**Aridity, concentration, and assembly of subjects.** Following Nissen, Scott presents **~3500–2500 BCE** sea-level and Euphrates-volume decline as a plausible assembly mechanism: population **huddled** on remaining watercourses; salinization cut arable land; irrigation became more labor-intensive; city-states fought over land and water; canals used corvée/slave labor; foraging alternatives diminished — yielding concentration that enabled state domination of grain-and-manpower modules. He rejects the classical Wittfogel-adjacent sequence that arid conditions forced irrigation, which required public authority, which produced the first sedentary communities: earliest large fixed settlements, he argues, sprang up in **wetlands** on wetland resources, not irrigated grain in a desert made to bloom ([[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] adjacency; state extends but does not invent water control).

**Population machine and coercion.** Acquisition and control of population sit at the center of early statecraft: collect people, settle them near the core, hold them, extract surplus beyond their needs. Surplus does not exist until the embryonic state extracts it; under Sahlins’s domestic mode, Chayanovian work effort, and Boserup’s non-monopoly of land, peasantries must be **compelled** (corvée, forced deliveries, debt bondage, serfdom, slavery) — see [[social-surplus]], [[wealth-appropriation]]. Flight is common; replacement via captives, purchase, and forced resettlement is structural. Positionally this is a **conflict/parasitic** reading of origin ([[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]), not an integrative magnet of civil peace.

**Fragility and non-linearity.** Mesopotamian state development was not linear: statelets had short life expectancy; interregna more common than regna; superimposition of tax and warfare added fragility to an already perilous crowded Neolithic complex. Scott believes infectious **diseases of crowding** have been grossly underestimated in early-state demographic fragility. Periodic demise is often **overdetermined** — a delicate balancing act rather than a one-cause death. Collapse and “dark ages” as possible welfare gains for nonelites are treated on [[societal-collapse]].

**Species-historical scale.** First alluvial states were tiny demographically and geographically; on a generous reading definitive state hegemony begins only ~1600 CE — last ~0.2% of species political life — so state formation is a late, local, wobbly process, not the default of human political experience.

### Scott densification (*The Art of Not Being Governed*, 2009)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|The Art of Not Being Governed]]*, 2009; study [[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-study]]) extends the appropriation/grain program from early Mesopotamia to **mainland Southeast Asian valley states** and their upland periphery ([[zomia]]). All claims below are **attributed** to Scott’s anarchist-history synthesis (not wiki-voice archaeological consensus). Complements the 2017 [[grain-hypothesis]] section above; the SE Asia pathway page densifies regional detail: [[southeast-asian-state-formation]].

**State space as space of appropriation.** Scott defines ideal **state space** as the ideal **space of appropriation**: arrangements guaranteeing a substantial, reliable surplus of **manpower and grain** at least cost. The design principle is geographical **concentration** of subjects and fields within easy reach of a court core. Premodern oxcart logistics bound the radius (a team of oxen eats its cartload of grain before ~250 km on flat terrain; Scott cites a Han proverb against grain sale over a thousand *li*). Hard power (extraction of grain and labor) projected only within a small, undependable dry-season radius (~300 km on good flat roads, far less in hills); economic and symbolic reach could travel farther. Navigable water partially nullifies friction of distance; choke points and strategic commodities modify pure grain-core logic but only to a point without manpower.

**Wet-rice core and the padi state.** Irrigated wet rice (*padi*, *sawah*) is Scott’s ultimate SE Asian state-space crop: high return per land unit, dense labor-intensive populations, durable yields, aboveground simultaneous ripening, storable and transportable grain — legible and appropriable in the same sense as the 2017 grain package. Formula (crude, attributed): political/military supremacy → concentrated manpower close at hand → compact sedentary agriculture → irrigated rice (before the twentieth century in SE Asia). Elective affinity, not determinism: wet-rice cores without states and occasional states without wet-rice cores exist; without a padi core, concentration would require slavery, trade tolls, or plunder. Fixed-field cultivators who have sunk generations of labor into bunds and weirs are, Scott argues, the premodern state’s ideal subjects — fixed in space, taxable, conscriptable.

**Manpower, slavery, and cyclical “stateness.”** Concentration of manpower is the first principle of precolonial SE Asian statecraft (people more than territory; wars as captives more than acreage). Classical padi states, Scott holds, were **ingatherings** of previously stateless peoples — trade and status attracting some, captives and slaves the majority. All SE Asian states were **slaving states** without exception for long stretches; paraphrase: no state without concentrated manpower, no concentration without slavery. Precolonial valley states were short-lived beyond a small court radius; interregna often longer than regna; **stateness was often cyclical and reversible**. Physical flight was the principal popular check on state power. Living inside, outside, or between state zones was a revisable choice for much of history.

**Hill–valley coevolution; Zomia as state effect.** Scott’s load-bearing methodological claim: valley states cannot be accounted for without the hills, and vice versa — the **dialectic or coevolution of hill and valley** as antagonistic but deeply connected spaces is the essential point of departure for SE Asian historical change. [[zomia]] (van Schendel’s term; Scott’s working region ~2.5 million km² of mainland massif above ~300 m, ~80–100 million minorities) is cast as the largest remaining relatively **[[nonstate-spaces|nonstate space]]** and as a **region of refuge** for >1.5 millennia: runaways and maroons fleeing valley taxes, corvée, conscription, warfare, epidemics, and slavery — not “living ancestors” left behind by wet-rice civilization. Shatter zones and flight are a direct **state effect** of bondage-based concentration (comparanda: Cossacks, New World maroons). Nonstate space ≠ altitude per se: the key variable is whether concentrated grain production is possible; marshes, mangroves, deserts, and open sea are also nonstate-friendly.

**Escape agriculture and the last enclosure.** Hill stigmata (margins, mobility, [[swidden]], flexible structure, religious heterodoxy, egalitarianism, oral culture) are, on Scott’s long view, **political adaptations** of nonstate peoples to attractive-yet-threatening states ([[state-evasion]], [[escape-agriculture]]) — not evolutionary lag. From the modern developmental state, a “last enclosure” seeks to make the periphery legible, taxable, assessable, and confiscatable (settle swiddeners, close commons, seize timber/minerals, promote cash monocrops) — thematic continuity with [[high-modernism]] / [[legibility]], though *Art* does not reuse the 1998 term as its central label. See also [[ethnogenesis]], [[orality-as-strategy]].

### Roux — Early Dynastic Sumer and the Assyrian imperial accretion (1992, attributed)

[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] contributes two distinct case studies, both attributed.

**Early Dynastic Sumer as inter-city warfare.** Roux frames Early Dynastic Sumerian history overall as "essentially one of wars between city-states and against foreigners," driven substantially by economic causes — land appropriation, control of trade routes and irrigation canals — that are "seldom mentioned" explicitly in the texts themselves, alongside geopolitical/religious rivalry over control of [[kish]] and [[nippur]] specifically. The best-documented case is the Lagash–Umma border war over the Gu-edin field (see [[lagash]]), fought across multiple generations of Lagash *ensi*s (Eannatum, Entemena) and eventually ending in Lagash's destruction by Lugalzagesi of Umma.

**Divine kingship as legitimating ideology.** The doctrine that "the exalted tiara and the throne of kingship" were "lowered from heaven" shortly after mankind's creation is read by Roux as a retrospective theological justification for an already-consolidating monarchical state form, not a description of the state's actual origin (attributed; see [[sumerian-king-list]]).

**"Primitive democracy" vs. monarchy-from-the-start (position recording).** Th. Jacobsen's thesis holds that the *lugal* ("great man"/war-leader) was originally an official elected by a citizen assembly for crisis periods only, later hardening into permanent hereditary monarchy — drawing on the divine-election-of-a-champion scene in the Epic of Creation and on local consultative assemblies of elders (*ukin*) attested in Early Dynastic Sumer. Against this, other Sumerologists argue these assemblies were merely consultative and rarely summoned, making "democracy" a misnomer. Roux states plainly that there is no clear textual evidence of a period of collective rule — as far as texts go back, only rulers and monarchs are seen — but does not resolve the dispute; both positions should be recorded on a debates page (candidate: `debates/sumerian-primitive-democracy-debate`) rather than adjudicated here. The same three-way uncertainty (religious fervor / a primitive-democracy survival / genuine priestly political influence, analogous to Heliopolis in Egypt) is raised by Roux to explain Nippur's peculiar status as Sumer's perpetual religious capital despite never appearing as a ruling dynasty on the [[sumerian-king-list|King List]] — Roux states explicitly that "these have no definite answer."

**The Assyrian empire as accretion, not plan.** For the much later Middle and Neo-Assyrian material, Roux explicitly argues the Assyrian empire was **not** "a planned enterprise" but accreted from interwoven motives: defensive/preventive war to protect the Tigris core and trade routes; economic necessity (replacing lost Cappadocian and "royal trade" revenue after the regional collapse around 1200 BCE, and discovering — per the precedents set by Tukulti-Ninurta I and Tiglathpileser I — that raiding a weakly defended periphery was simply profitable); royal ambition and glory-seeking; and religious ideology (the god Ashur's universal cult, under which enemies of the king became enemies of the god, justifying "brigandry and occasional massacres"). This is Roux's own explanatory synthesis, not a settled consensus claim, but it is a strong empirical basis for an eventual concept page on empire.

Structurally, Roux describes a standard early-imperial trajectory: an annual spring muster "at the command of Ashur"; local chiefs resisting, fleeing, or submitting with tribute; punitive re-conquest (torture, massacre, enslavement, burning of towns/crops/orchards) for broken tribute; and, as campaign distances grew (against Urartu, the Medes, Elam, Egypt), the replacement of native rulers with Assyrian governors and provincial administration — the transition from raiding to direct provincial rule marking "empire" formation proper, per Tiglathpileser III's reforms (see [[assyrians]]).

**Extractive periphery-to-centre structure.** Roux's assessment of imperial economics draws on largely negative evidence — the silence of Nineveh/Nimrud royal correspondence on provincial development, the absence of Assyrian texts in conquered regions, and the poor archaeological "Assyrian level" at excavated peripheral sites — to argue that wealth was transferred "from the periphery to the centre," producing impoverishment or, at best, stagnation in subject territories. Roux frames this extractive structure as containing "the germs of its own destruction" — an explicit, attributed causal claim (`contributed_to`, not `caused_by`) linking imperial political economy to eventual Assyrian collapse, useful alongside this page's other empire-collapse material.

### Postgate — the temple-state debate and the archaeological invisibility of political transitions (1994, attributed)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] adds a documentary institutional perspective on early Mesopotamian state formation and its historiography, distinct from the evolutionary/comparative models surveyed above.

**The temple-state historiographic dispute.** Postgate reports and partly adjudicates a long-running scholarly dispute over how much of the early Sumerian economy the temple institution controlled: the older "temple-city" model (Deimel, and in modified form Falkenstein), built on the Presargonic Bau-temple archive at Girsu, held that the temple owned essentially all land and the whole population were its dependents; the revisionist calibration (Diakonoff, and Postgate himself) treats the temple as one major sector among coexisting private, communal, and palace sectors, and regards the Girsu archive as a partial, unrepresentative record. Nissen's independent skepticism about the antiquity of southern temple dominance is presented by Postgate as a related but unresolved position. See [[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]] for the full position-recording; the dispute bears directly on how "temple-centered" any account of pristine Mesopotamian state formation should be.

**Archaeological invisibility of short-timescale political transitions.** Postgate notes that political collapse and reconstitution in Mesopotamia often operate on timescales that are archaeologically near-invisible: the transition from late Akkad to early Ur III, or from late Ur III to early Isin-Larsa, cannot currently be distinguished by artifact seriation alone — settlement-pattern discontinuity, not ceramic sequence, is the main available evidence for a disruption episode. He treats this as a methodological caution against reading state-formation and state-collapse narratives too confidently off the material record alone, and proposes (as an explicitly untested research program) that centralized rule ("polarization") should produce standardized fine and utilitarian ceramics and homogeneous material culture, while political fragmentation ("diffraction") should produce reversion to local/domestic styles — a correlation he flags as under-researched (as of Postgate 1994; see [[the-state]]).

**Cult transfer as institutional continuity across political rupture.** As a mechanism of cultural survival independent of any particular state's political fortunes, Postgate documents cases of temple-cult transfer: when Eridu was abandoned (no occupation after the Early Dynastic period, despite the shrine's roots reaching back to the Ubaid period), the cult and liturgy of its god Enki/Ea were transferred bodily to Ur, with priests relocated and daily rituals performed in a dedicated shrine within the Nanna temple complex. Parallel transfers include the god Ningublaga moving to Ur from the destroyed city of Kiabrig, and Uruk cults appearing at Kish in the late Old Babylonian period. Postgate interprets these transfers as displacement responses to political and military disruption, and as evidence that religious/institutional continuity could outlast the political order under which a cult was originally established — a continuity mechanism operating independently of, and sometimes cutting against, state-formation and state-collapse cycles (as of Postgate 1994).

## Kemp — Egyptian state formation (2018)

*(Attributed — [[kemp-barry-j]], *[[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]* Ch.2.)*

Kemp’s model (not a single-cause origin story): competitive domains/proto-states in Predynastic Upper Egypt (Nagada, Hierakonpolis) and delta interactions; cognitive/complexity thresholds; “Monopoly” analogy for board-like consolidation; Dynasty 0 elite tombs (e.g. U-j) and Menes as conventional first king of lists. Irrigation is **not** treated as Wittfogel-style hydraulic origin of the state — Nile fertility enables civilization, but ideology and hierarchy drive polity. First Intermediate Period read as possible re-fragmentation / energy dispersal toward local patriarchal centres rather than simple “collapse myth.” Four-way non-identity: archaeological cultures and king names ≠ biological populations.

## Wilkinson — Egyptian narrative arc (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wilkinson-toby]], *[[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]].)*

Predynastic competitive territories → first nation-state → Early Dynastic divine kingship; later “state formation” recurs as reunification after Intermediate Periods (Mentuhotep; Ahmose; Saite Psamtek). Foreign conquests (Hyksos, Kush, Assyria, Persia, Macedon, Rome) show state capacity as reversible. Not a single-origin theory; a long-run political narrative complementary to Kemp’s cognitive/domain model.

## Assmann — Egypt as territorial-state path and its semantics (1996/2002)

*(Attributed — [[assmann-jan]], *[[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|The Mind of Egypt]].)*

Assmann contrasts two earliest textually documented state-formation paths: **federation of city-states** (competitive semantics — Mesopotamia, Greek poleis) versus the **unified territorial state** (integrative/cooperative semantics — Egypt, Confucian China). Egypt's territorial state was imposed against a polycentric system of fortified Naqada II chiefdoms, not built up from them; the "traces" (archaeology suggesting largely peaceful cultural fusion), "messages" (Narmer Palette's violence), and "memories" (Horus-Seth reconciliation myth) tell irreconcilably different unification stories, which Assmann declines to harmonize. He rejects Wittfogel's hydraulic thesis for Egypt (Nile flooding needed only local management — see [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]) and models the Old Kingdom's endogenous collapse as a participation → penetration → distribution crisis between a thin residential elite and a documentarily invisible provincial mass, with the "patron" of vertical solidarity emerging as the First Intermediate Period's new social type (building on Seidlmayer).

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

**Late third-millennium centralization (Akkad, Ur III).** Van De Mieroop frames the Sargonic achievement as unprecedented in scale and permanence of military reach ("never before had Babylonian armies systematically campaigned that far, nor had the political dominance of one city ever been so great"), but qualifies "empire" as extractive/tributary influence rather than uniform territorial control — a heterogeneous patchwork of direct rule (Susa), negotiated alliance (Elam), trading/garrison footholds (Syria), and marriage diplomacy, not one system. Administrative centralization (standardized weights/measures, tablet forms, a state-wide year-name dating system) is dated to Naram-Sin's reign specifically, not Sargon's founding — centralization as a multi-generational, incrementally built project. Ur III then consolidated a more centralized, if geographically more restricted, successor state (see [[the-state]] for the Steinkeller core/periphery model and the "not totalitarian" correction).

**Growth of territorial states in the early second millennium.** Following Ur III's collapse, Van De Mieroop treats Shamshi-Adad's Kingdom of Upper Mesopotamia, Hammurabi's Babylon, and Hattusili I's Anatolian state as a common type: personalist, achievement-based territorial states that were structurally similar to their rivals ("only more successful") and disintegrated rapidly after their founders' deaths, yet left a lasting structural legacy for later territorial-state systems (see [[the-state]]).

**Persia as a "first world empire" — a new political scale (attributed).** Of the Achaemenid empire, Van De Mieroop writes: "In a few decades of the second half of the sixth century, the Persians of southwest Iran created an enormous empire covering the entire Near East and regions beyond from the Indus valley to northern Greece, from Central Asia to southern Egypt... the first in Near Eastern history to acknowledge the diversity of its subjects... the first world empire in history." He frames this explicitly as a genuinely new category, not merely a bigger version of Assyria or Babylon: its scale could not be governed by a single annual "fighting season" muster the way Assyria's could, requiring royal roads and rapid-muster logistics instead; and its ideological program deliberately departed from Assyrian precedent — where Assyrian art emphasized power through depicted violence and coercion, "the Persians portrayed images of peace and order: all subjects benefited from being part of the empire and voluntarily contributed." Van De Mieroop also notes, as a comparative structural observation, that the Achaemenid empire held together as a unified whole for roughly two hundred years without splitting among rival family branches — unlike essentially every later successor empire, starting with the division of Alexander's own conquests among his generals — offering this durability itself as a notable fact worth comparative attention (as of Van De Mieroop 2016).

### Kirch — Hawaiʻi at the chiefdom–stratified/primitive-state boundary (1984, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick V. Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* Kirch treats contact-era Hawaiʻi as the Polynesian **endpoint of chiefdom elaboration** — a stratified, conquest-cycling polity with endogamous chiefly vs commoner classes, territorial land control via *konohiki*, ritualized surplus extraction (*makahiki*), and a multi-tier decision hierarchy — yet **short of a fully transcended kinship/“tribal” structure**. Citing [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]] (1963, 1968, 1972), [[fried-morton-h|Fried]] (1967), and [[service-elman-r|Service]] (1975), he places elaborate Hawaiian chiefdoms at a **critical interface between chiefdom and state** / “boundary of primitive society itself”: elites had not completely severed the ancestral kinship bond with commoners, could not dishonor kinship morality without risk of mass disaffection, and held a majority but **not a monopoly** of force. Position: Hawaiʻi *approached* stratified/primitive-state thresholds; it is not asserted as a pristine archaic state in wiki voice.

**Multi-process, not irrigation-alone.** Kirch rejects Wittfogel-style hydraulic necessity for Hawaiian irrigation (following Earle) and rejects functionalist accounts that invent hierarchy from irrigation management under population pressure or from chiefs as redistributors of dispersed resources (cf. Sahlins 1958). Hierarchical principles (*‘ariki*, *mana*, *tapu*, first-fruits/tribute) were already present in Ancestral Polynesian Society; later elaboration was multi-causal — demography, ecological opportunity hierarchies (wet vs dry production paths; leeward dryfield power bases such as Kohala–Kona), competition/warfare and usurpation, symbolic systems, and political surplus demand — each only partial. Large-scale irrigation was one intensification path among others (intensive dryfield systems were of equal significance; the most competitive Hawaiian chieftainships developed on leeward, non-irrigation sides). Synthesis program: structural + ecological + demographic + political factors as suppletive, not single-paradigm. See [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[hawaiians]], [[chiefly-cycling]].

## Polynesian archaic states (Kirch 2017, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]].)* By 2017 Kirch presents [[tonga|Tonga]] and [[hawaiians|Hawaiʻi]] as the two clear cases of a Polynesian chiefdom-to-[[archaic-state|archaic-state]] transition, reached via markedly **different pathways**: Tonga's route ran through an external "maritime empire" of inter-island conquest and tribute extraction, while Hawaiʻi's ran through internal agricultural intensification coupled with exponential population growth — a trajectory Kirch supports with a population proxy built from roughly 1,036 radiocarbon dates, converging with Hommon's independent house-count demographic model. Both cases materialize political ideology in monumental architecture — Tongan *langi* royal tombs and Hawaiian *luakini heiau* temple platforms — read as the built expression of centralizing chiefly claims rather than incidental construction. See [[chiefdom]], [[agricultural-intensification]].

## Early Chinese state formation (Liu & Chen 2012, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]].)* Liu & Chen adopt a
**social-archaeology** definition of the state — a society with at least two strata (a professional
ruling class over commoners) and typically a four-tier settlement hierarchy (after Wright, Flannery,
Earle) — deliberately setting aside both the historiographical practice of matching sites to
textual dynasties and Su Bingqi's looser "civilization" criterion. On this basis the earliest
demonstrable Chinese state is the [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]] polity (c. 1900–1500 BCE), which they
classify as a **territorial state** (after Trigger 2003). Their central thesis is that early Chinese
states expanded via a **tributary, resource-driven core–periphery system** in which the monopolized
production and distribution of **bronze ritual vessels** — not agricultural management or trade
profit — was the primary mechanism of political legitimacy (epigraph from the *Zuozhuan*: "the great
affairs of a state are sacrifices and war"). The Erlitou→Erligang→middle-Shang sequence displays a
first **cycle of centralization and decentralization** that Liu & Chen note recurred throughout later
Chinese dynastic history. They characterize early Chinese urbanism as a **political/ritual
accomplishment** — hierarchically planned palace–cemetery–workshop complexes — rather than dense
commercial agglomerations, and as culturally continuous with (not differentiated from) the
surrounding countryside, unlike the Greek polis or medieval European city. Sociopolitical evolution
was **multicentric**, driven by interpolity factional competition rather than radiating from a single
nuclear area (see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]], [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]],
[[erligang-culture]], [[shang-civilization]]).

### Chang — Shang state as *tsu*/*yi* network (1980, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Chang, *Shang Civilization*]], 1980.)* Chang
reconstructs the Shang polity as a **king-centered network of lineages (*[[tsu-lineage|tsu]]*) and
towns (*yi*)**, with exterior [[fang-polity|*fang*]] polities, centripetal tribute (including
captives for sacrifice), and elite monopoly of bronze ritual and [[oracle-bone-inscription|oracle]]
communication. He situates Shang among Service-type evolutionary stages while insisting on
middle-level models that articulate An-yang data without forcing universal theory first. Erh-li-kang
[[zhengzhou|Cheng-chou]] is the earlier capital form; [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]] is excluded from
Shang and aligned with Hsia. Comparative epilogue: Shang as an independent Old World case for
urban-society comparison (beyond Adams's Mesopotamia–Mesoamerica pair). Absolute chronology and
empire-scale readings revised by later work; the network/*fang* anatomy remains a standing
reference point for late Shang [[state-formation]] debates.

### Maya Preclassic state-scale urbanism (Coe & Houston 2015)

[[el-mirador]] and the Mirador Basin show capital-scale monumental construction, sacbe networks, and kingship ideology in the **Late Preclassic**, centuries before Classic Long Count florescence — a New World primary case of [[state-formation]] in tropical lowlands as synthesized by [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]]. Olmec Formative models are argued as partial *fons et origo* (contested; see [[olmec-mother-culture-debate]]).

### From Sharer & Traxler 2006 (Maya state formation)

All claims in this subsection attributed to [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Maya-only densification; does not adjudicate non-Maya pathways above.

**Chiefdom vs state archaeological signatures.** Their Table 2.1 hallmarks: **chiefdoms** — monumental temples/assembly areas, **three-tier** site hierarchies, prestige goods, weak integration via warfare and chiefly redistribution, ranking by relation to chief; **states** — monumental architecture including palaces and fortifications, **four-tier** hierarchies, prestige goods and territorial boundaries, strong integration via centralized administration. Multilinear cultural evolution: stages as comparison points, not a deterministic path; every society’s path unique; Maya states diverse and dynamic.

**Sequence and drivers.** General sequence: egalitarian foragers → permanent settlement/agriculture → autonomous communities → achieved then ascribed status and elite endogamy → chiefdoms (multi-community, craft specialization, tribute redistribution, public works) → states (bounded territory, centralized control, four-tier hierarchy). Interwoven economic, sociopolitical, and ideological systems; no privileging of one realm alone. Warfare aims economic (tribute, trade routes), political (boundaries, rivals), and ideological (captives, prestige); escalation (fortifications, specialists, commoner service, centralized command) contributed to preindustrial state origins and maintenance. Political economy (elite-managed prestige goods and some utilitarians) develops alongside social economy (household/community production and markets); most food production stayed outside political economy, though water control could yield power.

**First Maya states and multi-state Classic system.** First states as early as **Late Preclassic** at [[el-mirador]] (earliest lowland preindustrial state in their assessment) and [[kaminaljuyu]] (threshold of highland state); Early Classic is **expansion** of states, not their origin (contra much twentieth-century dating of Maya “civilization birth” to Early Classic and foreign stimulus). Classic lowlands: competing large and small states each ruled from a capital by dynasties of [[kuhul-ajaw|k’uhul ajaw]]; **never unified** under a single emperor — city-state-like system over ~600 BC–AD 1500. Ideology of kingdom continuity constrained conquest empires (contrast Inka): victorious kings often left defeated polities with identity and own kings. Calakmul under Yuknoom the Great (636–686) failed to forge a single unified lowland state despite military apogee.

**Scale estimates (as of 2006).** Emblem glyphs mark seats claiming sovereign power (not all equal). Causeway extents as polity proxies: El Mirador Late Preclassic ~5–6,000 km²; Caracol Late Classic ~5,500; Calakmul ~4,000; Coba peak possibly ~20,000. Chase & Chase ~60 km military control radius (~11,300 km² max); denser LC central lowlands average ~8,000–9,000 km². Trial Tikal polity ~8,500 km² → ~912,000 people in one estimate they report.

**Consolidation vs fragmentation (Marcus).** Fluctuations between fewer larger polities and more smaller ones; synchronized peaks Late Preclassic and Late Classic; declines Terminal Preclassic and Terminal Classic. Northern lowlands grew as southern collapsed, culminating in Chichen Itza.

**Weak-state vs strong-state analogies.** Maya diversity spans theater-state/galactic-polity (weak) and strong-state forms; Calakmul, Tikal, Copan as best Classic strong-state examples — capitals qualitatively different (palaces, tombs, temples, dynastic monuments).

**Internal vs external stimulus.** Reject sole *cultura madre* Olmec or Teotihuacan-as-necessary secondary-state models; continuous multiethnic external interaction **plus** internal processes (population, economy, ideology). No single source for Maya civilization; Gulf Coast, Oaxaca, Basin of Mexico strands interact with indigenous Preclassic foundations. Middle Preclassic chiefdom-level societies (site hierarchies, monumental construction, sacrifice, raiding, feasting, trade) precede Late Preclassic state threshold; public monuments mark beginnings of divine kingship.

**Postclassic reformulation (not Classic divine-king continuity).** After Terminal Classic decline of *k’uhul ajaw*, Postclassic states (drawing on Chichen Itza prototype): seaborne commodity commerce over prestige-goods emphasis; *multepal* / oligarchic power-sharing vs divine kingship; K’uk’ulkan cult; expanded middle class; Mayapan confederacy of elite houses then fragmentation into ~18 northern petty states at Conquest; highland K’iche/Kaqchikel conquest states after ~1200.

## Regional pathway: mainland Southeast Asia

A distinctive pathway runs through ranked chiefdoms into the ritual [[mandala]] polity —
authority defined by circles of allegiance to a divine overlord rather than by fixed
territory, culminating in [[angkor|Angkor]]. Higham (1989) reads it as a largely
indigenous trajectory in which Indian and Chinese contact acted as stimulating variables
rather than as the external cause of the state. See [[southeast-asian-state-formation]] and
the [[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]].

**Scott 2009 (attributed) on the same region, different grain.** Alongside the archaeological
mandala sequence, *[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|Art of Not Being Governed]]*
theorizes classical **padi / manpower states** and their hill–valley coevolution with
[[zomia]] as refuge and [[shatter-zone]] — not an alternative chronology of Angkor but a
political-ecology frame for why wet-rice cores, slavery, and flight structured precolonial

### Island SE Asia — classical *negara* / theatre state (Geertz 1980, attributed)

[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]]’s *[[geertz-negara-1980|Negara]]* treats precolonial Indonesian political development as an “expanding cloud of localized, fragile, loosely interrelated petty principalities,” not monolithic Oriental despotism — continuous formation and dissolution until Dutch conquest. The Balinese case is modeled as a [[theatre-state]] organized around an [[exemplary-center]], with confederate domination (power cumulated from below), people-not-territory politics, and ceremony as primary state business. ## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed handbook synthesis — [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]], mainly Chs. 47–51, 52–61, 62–63; densify from ranges 8–10 claims. Do not promote to wiki-voice law.)*

### Colonial denial, diffusionism, and comparative value (Monroe Ch. 48)

J. Cameron Monroe argues that for much of the 20th century precolonial African states were cast as products of Mediterranean/Near Eastern/European intervention and thus irrelevant to “pristine” cultural evolution—now correctly viewed as **racist colonial ideology** justifying civilizing projects. As archaeology shifted from pristine origins to **political process and variable pathways**, African state histories become ideal comparative cases. Precolonial polities rarely had clear territorial boundaries; maps typically show maximal influence zones.

Colonial archaeology attributed irrigation, metallurgy, and monumental “civilization” trappings to northern influences (Hamitic and related theses). For Swahili and Sahel, Oliver & Fage (1962) called the medieval African state “a superstructure erected over village communities… rather than a society which has grown naturally out of them”—**diffusionism denying African creativity**. Postcolonial archaeology recovered autochthonous iron, urbanism, and farming; mainstream rejected external-only explanations (Monroe 2013).

### Definitions and pathways: hierarchy vs heterarchy

Monroe reviews state definitions from Weber (bureaucracy + monopoly of legitimate force) through Fortes & Evans-Pritchard to specialization/centralization models (Flannery/Eisenstadt; Johnson & Earle). After S.K. McIntosh, complexity is reconceptualized as **horizontal as well as vertical** differentiation—opening **[[heterarchy]]** (Crumley) as alternative pathway. Hierarchy and heterarchy are independent sliding scales. States are not natural evolutionary outcomes; archaeologists must explain **why hierarchy rather than heterarchy** emerged. Political centralization = elite strategies drawing military, ritual, political, economic power; African entrepreneurs mixed local and exotic resources, yielding diverse state forms (Monroe 2013).

**Material signs** recurrently listed: social hierarchy, territorial expansion/integration, economic specialization, labor control, long-distance exchange, state ideologies—underwritten by productive environments, trade opportunities, and indigenous leadership initiative. **In most cases cultural/material gains from long-distance trade especially enabled greater centralization**—trade as elite **opportunity/intensifier**, not sole originator (Monroe’s framing; aligned with Connah’s productive-land package on [[productive-land-hypothesis]]).

### Trade as intensifier, not universal originator (cross-cutting R9-058)

Across Chs. 52–61, long-distance trade repeatedly appears as **intensifier or underwriter** of hierarchy and urban growth already under way from local agricultural, pastoral, or ideological processes—not as sole originator. Explicit handbook cases: Tichitt hierarchy without clear long-distance trade (MacDonald); Jenné-jeno urban centuries before Arab–Berber trade (McIntoshes/MacDonald); Upemba Kisalian hierarchy without Indian Ocean trade (de Maret); Great Lakes without external stimulus (Reid); rainforest Benin/Ife from earthwork agricultural coalescence before Atlantic brass boom (Ogundiran). **Contrasts** where trade is more foundational or nucleating: Begho/Gao/Sijilmasa; Borno slave-trade political economy; Boone et al. post-Idrisid Maghreb **wealth finance** via gold-trade proceeds (Ennahid).

Stump (Ch. 46) challenges Wittfogel hydraulic and simple Boserup population-pressure teleologies for African irrigation cases (Egyptian irrigation long local; Iraqw intensification can *cause* population increase via in-migration). Egyptian state formation is **indigenous** arising from late Predynastic change (Shaw 2013).

### Medieval Nile successors; Maghreb/Sahel/forest notes

Edwards (Ch. 54): [[meroe|Meroitic]] disintegration c. AD 300–350 for unclear reasons; **not** necessarily Nubian invasion/population replacement—medieval Nobadia, Makuria, Alodia sought in local political dynamics. Slave-export “Alodia as primarily slave-trading state” (Adams) challenged as under-evidenced. Phillipson (Ch. 55): pre-Aksumite not a single Sabaean-defined state; Aksum largely self-sufficient with ivory export important. Gronenborn (Ch. 58): central Sahel statehood **not solely** from Islam/trade expansion—internal developments equally significant; mid-1st-millennium BC complexity west of Lake Chad **parallel** to [[nok-culture|Nok]]. Ogundiran (Ch. 59): rainforest towns via population/agriculture + trade-route advantage + sociopolitical expansion; community-building vs later authority-building goals. Pikirayi (Ch. 63): southern Zambezian states **tributary** formations extracting surplus by political/military coercion.

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed throughout — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.]]* 1988. Political-organization arc from first settlements to Akkad and Ur III; settlement-hierarchy measure of centrality. Complements Scott’s Nissen-mediated aridity thesis, Postgate’s temple-state notes, and Van De Mieroop’s late-third-millennium centralization without equating them.)*

**City → regional state arc.** Nissen’s program highlights historical development of early political organization ~9000–2000 B.C., ending not at a hard caesura but where long progress toward more complex organization reaches fixed supra-local states (Akkad Dynasty and Third Dynasty of Ur), after which administration consolidates and true empire formation lies >1000 years later. Working hierarchy: village → town → city → (regional) state. Degree of centrality in a settlement system is his measure of organizational stage; multilevel hierarchy on the Zagros–Susiana–Lower Mesopotamia transect is read as the organizational spectrum from Neolithic settlements through city and state. Dense irrigated packing intensifies conflict that cannot be solved by moving away; need for **rules for living together**, he argues, drives higher civilizational development more than pure water-administration (he rejects early complex feeder-canal organization as the rise of Lower Mesopotamia).

**Akkad / Ur III as first territorial states.** Against ED **rival [[city-state|city-states]]** (many independent centers; particularism of irrigation oases along watercourses), the Akkad Dynasty is framed as a **break**: territorial state with capital Akkad (unlocated) vs many city-states; import monopoly drawing trade to Akkad; ensi-governors from “sons of Akkad” creating direct capital–province links — an innovation Nissen calls key to why Sargon’s centralization succeeded where earlier pan-Babylonian attempts (Eannatum, Lugalzaggesi) collapsed under particularism. He treats the creation of the first **regional state** in the Near East as the answer to problems that could arise in this form only under specific Babylonian conditions. After Akkad’s disintegration into smaller units (externally ≈ ED III pattern again), Ur III under Ur-Nammu/Shulgi re-centralizes with standardized year names, land-register-style administration, ziggurat building program balancing local deities and central control, and — especially under Shulgi from year ~22 — an administrative reform (text explosion; temple accounts) producing transferable civil servants and beginnings of a cursus honorum. Ur III still lives inside the centralism–particularism conflict (Shulgi’s self-deification; Enlil of Nippur as spokesman of local-god interests against the central state), but Nissen holds that administrative forms achieve **general applicability beyond** purely Babylonian problems.

**Organizational-model transfer East → Babylonia → N Mesopotamia (Ch. 7 Prospects).** Nissen’s closing thesis: once Babylonia, newly suitable for settlement and behind its neighbors, obtained ideas and organizational forms from neighbors — above all to the **east** — it developed them further on its own problems and left the original donor lands behind; then Babylonia itself became victim of a similar cycle when those organizational forms became exploitable elsewhere. In the first half of the second millennium B.C., larger regional coalitions become possible on the broader plains of northern Mesopotamia and Syria thanks to models devised (but not brought to maturity) in Babylonia; states such as Hurri-Mitanni and Assyria, with greater reserves of land, raw materials, and people, outweigh Babylonia politically thereafter. If Babylonia still has a role, it is cultural goods and experience, not political instigator. Hammurapi of Babylon names the Old Babylonian turning point in this sketch (not a full Code analysis).

**Settlement hierarchy as measure of centrality.** Surface survey and size classes operationalize political stage: Behbehan simple two-tier; Susiana three-tier; Late Uruk four-tier headed by Uruk; ED urban:rural surface-area reversal; Akkad-period settlement-size distribution continuous with prior urban Babylonia (few rural sites) — though mass-produced pottery so similar across ED III / Akkad / Ur III that unequivocal surface dating of Akkad occupation is rarely possible. Empty settlement strips 12–15 km from Uruk and midway settlements “torn” under competing administrative claims illustrate political/administrative center–hinterland relations. Cultural lead ≠ automatic political leadership of Babylonia over neighbors (POSITION).

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — Oaxaca path

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]]. Short path sketch — full case densification on owned Monte Albán / synoikism / action-theory pages.)*

Marcus & Flannery present the Valley of Oaxaca as a long-sequence laboratory for archaic [[state-formation]]. Path they reconstruct (institutions and transitions, not stage-ladder as motor): Archaic foragers → egalitarian Tierras Largas villages → San José hereditary [[hereditary-rank|rank]] → Rosario **maximal [[chiefdom]]** (three-tier hierarchy, warfare, buffer zone, early writing) → ~500 BC [[synoikism|synoikism]] founding [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] in a former no-man’s-land → Monte Albán I transitional consolidation (piedmont strategy, militaristic propaganda, ethnogenesis of “Zapotec style”) → **Monte Albán II** unambiguous expansionist archaic state (four-tier [[settlement-hierarchy]], grand plaza, palaces, two-room temples, ballcourts, colonization/conquest including [[cuicatlan-canada|Cuicatlán]]).

**Definitional package (attributed).** Archaic states are highly centralized polities whose kings are drawn from hereditary nobles who do not consider themselves closely related to commoners (often descent from supernaturals vs commoners from mud); professional diversification; monopoly of legitimate violence, conscription, taxes, tribute; populations tens of thousands to millions; full-time artisans and priests. Zapotec state is less bureaucratized than Aztec or Tarascan on their reading. Stratified society replaces chiefdom rank continuum via genealogical gap maintained by class endogamy (Hawaiian analogy flagged).

**Theoretical stance.** States form by **takeover of neighboring chiefdoms**, not smooth evolution of one chiefdom. [[action-theory-archaeology|Action theory]] restores ambitious actors who manipulate and transform the system that produced them — complement to ecological-functionalist and selectionist accounts. Dual program: historical depth for a specific civilization **and** comparative regularities across archaic-state sequences.

## Land Behind Baghdad densification (Adams 1965)

*(Attributed — [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-1965]]; study [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-study]].)* The Diyala survey's contribution is negative and sequential: Early Dynastic town formation preceded any irrigation requiring central management (flood/small-ditch irrigation "well within the capabilities of local groups without state intervention"), and no clear dependency of political development on irrigation practice is deducible from town distributions; population pressure is likewise rejected (need ~1,100 sq km vs. ~1,900 available). Political integration in the first irrigation epoch stayed shallow — even [[eshnunna|Eshnunna]] "exercised at best a loose and sporadic control over surrounding towns and villages." State capacity became decisive only in the second epoch: Sasanian city-founding by forced resettlement (70,000 Edessa captives under Shapur I), tax revenue rising ~60% in under a century (214M→340M+ dirhems), and treasury-financed landscape works ([[nahrawan-canal]]) — after which withdrawal of state maintenance capacity, not resource exhaustion, drove the medieval collapse. See [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]].

## Lehner densification (1997)

*(Attributed throughout — [[lehner-mark]], *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* 1997. Pyramid building as state-formation engine. **“First centralized nation state” and empire-path claims are Lehner POSITIONS — never promote to wiki voice.** Complements Kemp, Wilkinson, Assmann Egypt sections above.)*

### Internal colonization thesis

Lehner holds that the beginning of the pyramid age coincided with founding of new villages (*niut*) and estates (*hut*) across Egyptian hinterlands; produce flowed periphery → core to feed pyramids, temples, and élite tombs. Pyramid building thus had a “key role in forging Egypt as the world’s first centralized nation state,” contrasted with Mesopotamian/North Syrian **city-state** patterns. Later he states early OK pyramids helped consolidate a true nation state and set processes culminating in one of the world’s first empires (MK Fayum/frontier → NK empire). **Attribute and do not adjudicate.**

### Evidence sequence (as Lehner presents it)

1. **Archaic precursors:** Qena Bend overlords move administration to Delta-apex “Wall” ([[memphis]]); Abydos royal tombs + valley enclosures; Saqqara niched mastabas of high officials/royalty debate; Djoser stone gigantism.
2. **Peak centralization signal:** three 4th-dynasty generations (Sneferu–Khufu–Khafre) accomplish bulk of stone pyramid mass (~59% of all royal pyramid mass if Sneferu’s three + Khufu + Khafre vs all other royal pyramids combined at ~41% — book figures; treat as order-of-magnitude). Later smaller standardized pyramids “reflect a less centralized society” (caption INTERPRETATION).
3. **Sneferu estate founding:** Palermo Stone 35 estates + 122 farms; estate geography in Middle Egypt/Delta vs 3rd-dynasty monument cluster in Qena Bend.
4. **Centrifugal late OK:** emerging bureaucracy and hereditary temple/estate claims disperse power through colonies meant to feed the core; FIP near-cessation of pyramid building when unified rule yields to municipalities.
5. **MK restart:** mudbrick pyramids; Fayum colonization (Illahun/[[kahun]], Hawara); control-note labour towns still clustered like OK colonization map.
6. **Basin → nome hypothesis (INTERPRETATION):** nomes and late Predynastic proto-kingdoms may have originated in flood basins; sequential filling/discharge communication easier across smaller systems such as the Qena Bend, from which rulers of Egypt emerged more than once.

### Comparative placement

Lehner’s nation-state vs city-state contrast is a **macro-comparative POSITION** for Egypt’s territorial path — file alongside Assmann’s territorial-state semantics and Kemp’s provider-state anatomy; do not collapse into wiki-voice uniqueness claims. ## Matthews densification (2003)

*(Attributed throughout — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]], Ch.4, "States of mind." Reviews and adjudicates among the administrative-technology, organizational-dynamics, power, and cyclical models of Mesopotamian state formation.)*

**Uruk as the region's only pristine case.** Matthews frames [[uruk|Uruk]]-period states as the region's only true "pristine/primary" case — every subsequent Mesopotamian state had an ancestral model to draw on. Henry Wright (1977: 379) is quoted framing state origins as "a fundamental problem which, though it cannot have an ultimate solution, serves as a measure against which to evaluate the effectiveness of new perspectives and new methods."

**Wright & Johnson's administrative-technology approach, and its challenge.** [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews]] surveys the 1970s systems-analysis approach of Henry Wright and Gregory Johnson, who read administrative bureaucracy (seals, sealed bullae) in southwest Iran as evidence of specialization and hierarchical decision-making (Wright 1977; 1978; 1998; Wright and Johnson 1975). He reports a direct empirical challenge: sealed-clay administrative technology also appears at much earlier, non-hierarchical Late Neolithic sites — Sabi Abyad and Tell Boueid in north Syria — in contexts interpreted as securing personal/private property rather than elite redistribution (Akkermans and Duistermaat 1997; Duistermaat 2002). Matthews draws the explicit methodological lesson that administrative-technology evidence does not necessarily imply a hierarchical or stratified social structure, treating this as a `challenges` relation to the Wright–Johnson administrative-technology-as-state-marker inference. [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert Adams]] (1981: 76–78) is cited as an early critic on related grounds — administrative routines cannot represent a polity's full complexity, and such approaches neglect cult/religious organization; Susan Pollock (1992: 319) similarly criticizes the administrative focus for reducing the state to "a managerial entity," and Richard Blanton (1998: 138) faults it for neglecting commerce, households, and urbanism.

**Stein and Rothman's organizational dynamics of complexity.** [[stein-gil|Gil Stein]] and Mitchell Rothman's alternative framework defines complexity as "the degree of functional differentiation among societal units" (households, enterprises, political associations, classes, villages, urban districts), emphasizing economic, political, and ideological integration and centralization dynamics over terminological or stage-typology debate (Rothman 1994; Stein 1994a). Matthews quotes Stein's characterization of states as bounded, imperfect organizations: "instead of viewing states as all-powerful, homogeneous entities, it is probably more accurate to characterize them as organizations operating within a social environment that, for a variety of reasons, they only partially control" (Stein 1994a: 13).

**Earle's tripartite power model.** [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews]] also reports Tim Earle's model of complex stratified societies arising through differential access to and control of three sources of social power — economic, military, and ideological (Earle 1997; cf. Mann 1986) — with Earle locating the ultimate "prime mover" in a biologically rooted individual drive for reproductive success and status (Earle 1997: 2). Earle (1997: 14) is quoted, with Matthews's endorsement, that "the fundamental dynamics of chiefdoms are essentially the same as those of states."

**Marcus's dynamic cycling model and the omitted-climate critique.** From the comparative "archaic states" literature (Feinman and Marcus, eds., 1998), Joyce Marcus's "dynamic model" holds that states cycle through consolidation, expansion, and dissolution (Marcus 1998: 60). Matthews criticizes Marcus's Mesopotamian state-development diagram — which places collapse points around 3,000, 2,200, and 1,400 BC — for omitting climate: these collapse points correspond to attested episodes of climatic aridification (Butzer 1995: 136–138). Per the Evidence-Class Framework, this climate correlation is recorded here as `concurrent_with` rather than `caused_by` or even `contributed_to`: Matthews does not fully argue a causal mechanism, though he implies climate's contribution to the cycling pattern Marcus describes.


## McIntosh 2005 — urbanism without (early) state hierarchy

*(Attributed — [[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005]].)*

Program thesis: Middle Niger urban complexity for over a millennium **without** demonstrated nested coercive state / citadel. Challenges cities-and-states co-occurrence expectation (Adams, Trigger, Fox, etc., as cited by McIntosh). Later hierarchical empires (Ghana/Mali/Songhai) reorganize but may nest residual heterarchy. Connah’s caution on negative evidence remains the principal counter-position in-vault. See [[jenne-jeno]], [[heterarchy]], [[mcintosh-jenne-jeno-study]].

## Pauketat 2007 — definitional survey and the state-as-practice position

*(Attributed — [[pauketat-chiefdoms-delusions-2007]].)* His Ch. 6 survey of rival state-identification frameworks: **(a) population thresholds** — settlements >2,500 fragment without higher-order government (Feinman 1999, after Kosse 1990); states tend to be coterminous with regional populations >10,000; **(b) institutional correlates** — Wright & Johnson's four-tier settlement hierarchy and bureaucracy-isomorphism, or palaces and royal tombs (Flannery 1999); **(c) "effort" definitions** — regal-ritual pageantry (Geertz-line) or Patterson's (1987) labor-control definition ("the power to command labor, allocate labor, or dispose of labor — a spatialized power — was real power"). He quotes **Yoffee, Fish & Milner's (1999) four criteria of early states** (new capitals disembedding power from kinship; ruralization as urbanization's twin — "the countryside is created by, not prior to, state formation"; interplay of competing power sources; ideological power via unifying symbols) and applies them to [[cahokia]]. Even "committed statists" Marcus & Feinman (1998) concede archaic states were "a lot more fragile and internally diverse than the archaeological literature would lead one to believe." Pauketat's own position ([[historical-processualism]]): the state is "less an organizational thing and more a pervasive phenomenon that 'is both everywhere and nowhere'" (A. Smith 2003), "diffuse, heterarchical, and hidden in the practices of people" — echoing Abrams's (1988) "secret of the non-existence of the state." Comparative aside: many eastern North American sites match the average 10-ha Ubaid center, so "it may be more apt to compare a Coles Creek site to an Ubaid town… than to compare Cahokia or Chaco to an ethnographic chiefdom." On Monte Albán he sets the "shopworn" disembedded-capital account (Blanton; Yoffee) against Joyce's and Spencer's readings (old San José Mogote elites; Danzante executions as sacred-covenant ritual drama) — his gloss: "The state did not evolve first and remake identity and community second. Rather, it was a mutual, negotiated, historical process" (cf. [[monte-alban-founding-debate]]).

## Pauketat 2009 — Cahokia as state-in-production

*(Attributed — [[pauketat-cahokia-2009]].)* Working definition offered (attributed by Pauketat to "most anthropological theories"): a state centrally controls labor superseding kinship ties, allocates and stockpiles, "ruralizes" producers, and encourages stratification. Pauketat argues — with the explicit caveat that "not all of these outcomes have been proved beyond a doubt" — that a state government was "at the very least... under production" at [[cahokia]] from 1050 CE, resting on Emerson's replacement hypothesis (abrupt rural reorganization into farmsteads-plus-temples), the sub-Mound 51 mega-feasting record, and Mound 72's staged sacrifice ("church" and "state" as one new order — his formulation). Minimalist counter-positions at [[cahokia-population-urbanism-debate]].
