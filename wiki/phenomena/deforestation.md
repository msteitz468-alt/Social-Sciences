# Deforestation

Deforestation — the human removal of forest cover — is [[roberts-neil|Roberts]]' ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) central example of the growing human "taming of nature" across the [[holocene|Holocene]], from small Mesolithic clearings to the near-total removal of woodland in long-settled landscapes.

## Definition and Scope

The phenomenon spans deliberate clearance for cultivation and pasture, burning to improve grazing or hunting, timber and fuel extraction, and grazing that prevents regeneration. Roberts stresses (attributed) that its onset and permanence must be distinguished from natural forest change, and that early clearance was often localized, leaving most "wildwood" initially intact.

## Measurement

Reconstructed from [[pollen-analysis|pollen]] (declining arboreal:non-arboreal ratios; weed and cereal indicators; the [[blytt-sernander|Grenzhorizont]]), [[mollusc-analysis|land-snail]] shifts from shade to open-country species on calcareous soils, charcoal stratigraphy, and documentary sources such as Domesday.

## Patterns and Findings

In attributed voice, from Roberts (1998):
- Later **Mesolithic** communities used fire to open woodland (southern Pennines, Dartmoor), and elevated hazel may itself be an indirect signature of Mesolithic burning.
- The Neolithic **landnám** cycle (Iversen 1941) records clearance–cultivation–regeneration; ¹⁴C dating later showed cycles lasting 200–600 years, not Iversen's original 50–100 (Smith 1981).
- The mid-Holocene **elm decline** (~5800 Cal. yr BP) is variously attributed to pathogen, human fodder-lopping, competition, or climate (Peglar and Birks 1993); fine-resolution pollen at Diss Mere showed it occurred in as little as six years, favouring disease, with Neolithic activity plausibly deepening it.
- By Domesday (AD 1086) only ~15% of England was wooded (Rackham 1980); most primary clearance predated the Norman Conquest, with the Bronze/Iron Age the most active phase.
- **Island deforestations**: Easter Island's palm forest was destroyed at the height of monument-building (Flenley and King 1984 — see [[easter-island]]); New Zealand lost forest to Polynesian burning from ~1000 years ago; Madeira was burned by 15th-century Portuguese settlers.

## Explanatory Theories

Deforestation is modelled as a threshold-crossing disturbance whose reversibility depends on soil survival: where clearance triggered [[soil-erosion]] or paludification (blanket mire), the change became effectively irreversible (Burren limestone pavement, upland blanket bog).

## Interaction

Drives [[soil-erosion]], blanket-mire and heathland formation, and biodiversity loss; interacts with [[holocene-climate-change|climate]] (beech spread aided by Bronze/Iron Age clearance) and with [[agricultural-revolution|agriculture]].

## Debates

Whether particular vegetation openings are anthropogenic or climatic is the recurring "natural or cultural?" problem; Roberts (1998) closes on the qualified observation that Holocene forests historically reclaimed most cleared land within ~1000 years — "but can we afford to wait that long?"
