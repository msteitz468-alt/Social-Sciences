# Privatization

## Definition and Scope

**Privatization** is the transfer of ownership, management, or control of economic assets and activities from the state to private actors — through sale, restitution, voucher distribution, management buyout, or liquidation of state-owned enterprises (parastatals). As a global phenomenon it accelerated from the 1980s under **[[structural-adjustment|structural adjustment]]** and the diffusion of neo-liberal policy, spanning Latin America, post-socialist Eastern Europe, and Africa. This page treats privatization as a **political** process with political causes and consequences, following [[pitcher-transforming-mozambique-2002|Pitcher (2002)]], not merely a technical divestiture.

## Measurement

See `measured_by`. Measures disagree: counts of enterprises sold flatter programs with many small liquidations; value or GDP-share measures weight the large strategic sales differently; the *method* of divestiture (who is eligible to buy) determines who benefits, a distributional fact the aggregate counts hide.

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed.)* [[pitcher-m-anne|Pitcher]] argues, from the Mozambican case, that comprehensive privatization did **not** produce state withdrawal: state institutions and surviving party elites used it to preserve and redirect power — her concept of **[[transformative-preservation|transformative preservation]]**. International financial institutions rated Mozambique's program "the most successful in Africa," a judgment Pitcher reads as ideological rather than settled.

**Mozambique specifics (attributed to Pitcher, dated to her study).** ~1,000 state companies sold (agriculture/agro-industry 162; industry/commerce/tourism 434; construction 136; transport 64); >90% to nationals but with foreign capital (~$1.2bn of ~$4bn pledged) concentrated in the largest firms. Almost all foreign investment took the **joint-venture** form, with the state or nationals holding equity and board seats — so ownership rarely passed wholly out of national/state hands. Domestic capitalists bought ~90% of firms but supplied only ~9% (~$328m) of pledged investment. Worker surveys undercut the efficiency narrative: state- vs. private-firm failure rates were nearly identical (17% vs. 14%), only ~48% of privatized-firm workers judged privatization "good," and a World Bank/GOM survey claiming 250% productivity gains conflated correlation with causation (ignoring the 1992 peace and credit available only to private firms). Outcomes ranged from genuine successes (Coca-Cola/SABCO, breweries, the $1.3bn Mozal smelter) to deliberate asset-stripping and de-industrialization; ~100,000 workers were dismissed by 1996.

## Explanatory Theories

*(Side by side, attributed.)* **Neo-liberal** accounts explain privatization as a corrective to the inefficiency and rent-seeking of state enterprises, aiming to "push the state back out." **Dependency/globalization critics** read it as externally imposed loss of sovereignty. The **"political underpinnings"** school (Feigenbaum, Henig, Hamnett) and Pitcher stress domestic political coalitions, legitimation, and elite strategy.

## Interaction

Closely coupled with **[[structural-adjustment]]**, **[[post-socialist-transition]]**, and **[[globalization]]**; reshapes the **[[the-state|state]]** as an economic actor.

## Debates

Whether privatization entails state retreat or state redirection is the axis of the Pitcher argument; whether it is domestically driven or externally imposed divides neo-liberals from dependency critics.
