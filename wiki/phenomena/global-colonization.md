# Global Colonization (Human)

## Definition and Scope

**Global colonization** here names the long-run process by which hominins and, later, anatomically modern humans extended their geographical range from African origins to a **near-universal distribution** unmatched by other large mammals — including settlement of high latitudes, deserts, islands, and the Americas and Oceania. The term is used as [[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] uses it in *[[gamble-timewalkers-1994|Timewalkers]]* (1994): as the **primary research object** of a world prehistory, not as a synonym for modern European imperial expansion (though Gamble argues imperial-era expectations shaped why earlier prehistorians ignored the problem).

Distinct from: historical colonial empires; [[austronesian-expansion]] (one late maritime subsystem); language-family spreads treated as identity with people or genes (four-way non-identity).

## Measurement

Operationalized archaeologically by **first-appearance** of hominin/human presence in regions (sites, dated assemblages, fossils), density and continuity of occupation, and environmental envelopes occupied. Absolute dates are method- and era-dependent; Gamble 1994 works largely with then-available radiometric and relative frames — **pre-aDNA** — so timings on this page that rest only on that book stay attributed and provisional. Genetic and isotopic evidence (post-2010) is a separate evidence class; promote only with date-stamped sources.

## Patterns and Findings

As surveyed in *Timewalkers* (attributed, Gamble 1994; **pre-aDNA** chronology):

### Scale terms (Table 1.1)

Gamble distinguishes **migration** (short-timescale directed movement between distinct places), **dispersal** (ecological-time spreading into unoccupied habitat), and **colonization** (larger temporal/geographical extension of range with established occupation of new niches) — the last being the book's object (attributed).

### Colonization guide (Table 1.2 / Fig. 1.1)

| Phase (Gamble labels) | Approx. span (as of 1994) | Geographic envelope (as stated) |
|---|---|---|
| Early hominids | ~5–1 Ma | sub-Saharan Africa (australopithecines, early *Homo*) |
| Ancients | ~1 Ma–200 ka (with later archaic/*sapiens* variants to ~60 ka) | Africa, mid-latitude Asia and Europe; restricted habitats vs later humans |
| Pioneer / transition | ~60–40 ka | continental Eurasia; fuzzy Middle/Upper Palaeolithic boundary |
| Moderns | ~50–10 ka then Holocene | Australia, E Siberia, Pacific margins, Japan, Americas, then Arctic, deep Pacific, deserts, rainforests |
| Historic residue | after A.D. 1500 | a few Atlantic islands; Antarctica still unoccupied |

- Early African hominins assemble bipedalism, tools, and large brains **before** major intercontinental range expansion; Gamble stresses long rootedness in Africa before the Old World track (attributed, ch. 5–7).
- European Age-of-Discovery voyagers **found people almost everywhere** and rarely treated that fact as surprising; Wallace left *Homo* out of *Island Life* (1880) as merely "universally distributed" (attributed, ch. 1).
- **Americas (as of 1994):** no serious Pliocene/Middle Pleistocene support; extensive colonization after ~12 ka undisputed; Beringia occupation argued ~12–11 ka (Hoffecker, Powers, Goebel — Gamble concurs for *significant* colonization); Clovis after ~12 ka; Meadowcroft and Monte Verde as pre-12 ka claims (Monte Verde 13 ka component; 33 ka jury still out in 1994); coastal vs overland open; Martin overkill as POSITION.
- **Sahul:** never a dry crossing (≥65 km open water even at LGM); earliest seagoing craft evidence; accepted Pleistocene ages from Kenniff, Mungo, Huon, etc.; megafauna not simultaneous continent-wide wipeout (contra blitzkrieg).
- **Pacific:** Near Pacific Pleistocene seafaring (~30 ka) then Remote Pacific Lapita/Polynesian triangle; purposive voyaging, not drift (attributed).

Wiki-voice minimal claim only where multi-source consensus already exists on the vault: humans originated in Africa and later occupied all permanently habitable continents (see [[modern-human-origins-debate]], [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]). Fine structure of pulses, routes, and archaic interaction remains attributed; **aDNA has revised many 1994 timings and admixture stories**.

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Claim (attributed) | Carrier |
|---|---|---|
| Progress / hero narrative | Range expansion is a side-plot of gifts (tools, brains) and the march to civilization | 19th–20th c. prehistory (critiqued by Gamble via Landau) |
| Adaptive filling / inevitability | Colonization is selection-shaped design to exploit empty niches; given enough time, humans fill the earth (Quatrefages, Lyell-type views Gamble cites) | ecological–adaptive and Victorian optimist models |
| **Exaptive colonization** | Near-global range is often an **unforeseen consequence** of social/behavioral change; purpose in ecological time without teleological progress; limits to expansion are often **internal to social life** | [[gamble-clive]] 1994; [[exaptation]] |
| Social-scale / alliance models | Larger alliance networks and "storage" via social ties enable marginal and overseas range | Gamble ch. 8–10 (Moderns package) |
| Corridor / climate-gated models | Ice, sea level, and biome shifts enable or block pulses | paleoclimate literature (concurrent with, not identical to, Gamble) |
| Demographic / coastal-route models | Population growth and littoral pathways structure peopling (esp. Americas) | specialized Americas debates |

## Meltzer 2009 — Americas colonization layer

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|First Peoples in a New World]]* 2009. Radiocarbon BP as he uses; genetics as of 2009 only.)*

### Pattern (Americas as near-final major landmass)

- After midlatitude peopling, New and Old World populations lived in isolation for ~a dozen millennia; first post-colonization Old–New World encounter was Norse–American Indian contact in northeast Canada ~AD 1000 (brief); transformative contact followed 1492.
- Ancestors left Asia before animal domestication and epidemic disease evolution; entered via [[beringia]] (possible standstill); south via [[ice-free-corridor]] or [[coastal-adaptation|Pacific coast]]; [[monte-verde]] by ≥**12,500 BP**; Clovis widespread by **11,500 BP**, possibly a second pulse. No co-evolution with Old World crowd diseases—Beringia as **“germ filter”** with long-run consequences for post-1492 disease asymmetry (thesis; Cortés–smallpox case study).
- Travel was **not** an “Oklahoma Land Rush”: hunter-gatherers move fast in familiar homogeneous environments, but each ecological boundary forced new learning of water, food, and stone—process took time (unknown how much).
- Clovis toolkit uniformity historically read as rapid dispersal (possibly ~500 radiocarbon years; longer in calendar time under YD plateaus—still among fastest cultural expansions). Thule later radiated rapidly across high Arctic in more homogeneous niches with watercraft—different problem than Clovis pioneering diverse late Pleistocene North America.
- Interim archaeological conclusion: **at least two** Pleistocene-age migrations (Paleoindian + later Paleoarctic), excluding later Holocene (ancestral Eskimo); more pulses possible.

### Route windows (as of Meltzer 2009 geology)

| Window | Constraint |
|---|---|
| Continuous Siberia→midlatitude | Only after Beringia emerges (~27 ka) and before full ice burial, or after ice retreat before Beringia drowns (~11 ka) |
| Interior corridor | Blocked ~28–12 ka; habitable perhaps only ~12 ka; no pre-Clovis sites in corridor region |
| Pacific coast | Impassable at LGM; clear Alaska–Washington by ~13.4 ka; outer coast ice-free 16–14.5 ka |
| Favored scenarios (Fig. 38) | Earlier coastal entry in time for Monte Verde and/or later corridor entry at Clovis outset |

### Explanatory notes (Meltzer)

- Colonization of empty continents is a first-order research object for American prehistory, not a side-plot of Clovis point typology (stakes include HG landscape learning, demography, genetic/linguistic clocks, post-contact tragedy calibration).
- Counterfactuals Meltzer floats: different departure timing, diseases brought, route, bottleneck, megafaunal survival/domestication—“Lost Tribes” frameworks, disease symmetry—underline contingency (Gould-style).
- Four-way non-identity: archaeological complexes ≠ genetic populations ≠ languages ≠ peoples. aDNA post-2009 revises many molecular timings; keep Meltzer genetics date-stamped.
