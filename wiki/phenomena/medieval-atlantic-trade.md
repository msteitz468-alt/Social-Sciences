# Medieval Atlantic Trade

The commercialized maritime economy of the Atlantic façade c. 1000–1500 CE —
Gascon wine, Breton salt, English cloth, Iceland fish, Iberian fruit and oil —
whose regional specialization and redistribution networks Cunliffe (2001) reads
as the third cycle of the Atlantic [[longue-duree]].

## Definition and Scope

Cunliffe (2001) delimits this phenomenon as the commercialized maritime economy
that reknit the Atlantic façade after c. 1000 CE, once feudal Europe's political
fragmentation (post-Carolingian France; Christian/Muslim Iberia split between
Asturias-Navarra and a Caliphate of Córdoba that broke into 24 taifa states after
1031) gave way to a network of regionally specialized production and long-distance
carriage. It runs from the political re-consolidation of c. 1000 CE to the eve of
transoceanic exploration c. 1500 CE, and is bounded geographically by the Atlantic
seaboard from Scandinavia and the North Sea emporia through the British Isles,
Brittany, Gascony, and Iberia. Cunliffe treats it as the concluding phase of his
"third cycle" of Atlantic interaction (see [[longue-duree]]), distinct from the
earlier Viking raiding/settlement phenomenon ([[viking-age-britain]]) that helped
set its conditions.

## Measurement

Cunliffe's evidence base is customs and toll records (English wine-import figures),
port/shipping registers (e.g., ship-ownership counts at Bordeaux), narrative and
annalistic sources (Icelandic annals recording foreign fishing vessels), and
dendrochronology/archaeological ceramics for shipbuilding and cargo inference. Scale
is macro — regional and interregional commodity flows — not individual transactions.

## Patterns and Findings

Political background: the Angevin "empire" assembled by Henry II of England
(Normandy 1150, Anjou/Touraine/Vendôme 1151, Aquitaine/Gascony via marriage to
Eleanor 1152, English crown 1154) briefly united the Atlantic seaboard "from
Scotland to the Pyrenees" under one crown, but lacked coherence and was reduced by
Philip II of France to Gascony and Béarn; the Hundred Years War (proximate cause a
1337 Gascony dispute between Edward III and Philip VI) ended in 1453 with the
English defeat at Castillon and loss of Normandy and Bordeaux, leaving Calais as
England's last continental holding (Cunliffe 2001).

Gascon wine was the trade's largest single commodity flow: Cunliffe (2001) reports
that under English occupation in 1305–36 wine exports from Gascony to England and
Flanders reached an estimated 750,000 hectolitres a year — "the greatest volume of
any single product traded internationally in western Europe during the medieval
period." By the mid-15th century England still imported about 3 million gallons of
Gascon wine annually, equivalent to nearly one-third of the value of all English
imports; the trade persisted through wartime disruption and resumed after the 1475
Treaty of Picquigny. Bordeaux and Bayonne were the two chief Gascon ports; most
Bordeaux carriers were foreign-owned (only 5 of 200 ships leaving Bordeaux in one
early-14th-century year were locally owned), while Bayonne shippers carried their
own and others' cargoes (Cunliffe 2001).

The Hanseatic League — some 200 towns from the Rhine to the Gulf of Finland,
centered on Lübeck — dominated North Sea and Baltic trade through economic leverage
rather than military coercion, sent annual salt fleets (the *Baienfahrt*, 40–50
vessels) to Brittany and later the Setúbal estuary, and expelled the English from
the Bergen (Norway) fish staple in the early 15th century (Cunliffe 2001). That
exclusion pushed English fishing fleets to Iceland from 1412/1413 (Icelandic annals
record English ships from 1412, 30-plus by 1413); the trade flourished under
fluctuating royal permissions until the Hanse itself moved into Icelandic waters
from the 1470s, prompting English search for new grounds — leading to John Cabot's
1497 voyage from Bristol in the *Matthew* and his landfall at Newfoundland; Cabot
was lost on a 1498 follow-up voyage, and his son Sebastian's 1508–9 expedition
explored the American coast from Labrador to Cuba (Cunliffe 2001).

In Iberia, the Christian Reconquista (Toledo 1085; halted by the Almoravids and
Almohads until the 1212 victory at Las Navas de Tolosa; Córdoba 1236, Jaén 1245,
Sevilla 1246; Granada falling only in 1492 under Ferdinand and Isabella) restructured
the peninsula's political geography alongside Santiago de Compostela's pilgrimage
economy (relics "discovered" 812; shrine sacked by al-Mansur in 997; route a major
unifying and economic factor for the northern kingdoms). Portuguese Atlantic
exploration under Prince Henry (Ceuta 1415, Madeira 1419, past Cape Non 1434,
Sierra Leone by 1460) continued under Fernão Gomes, Diogo Cão, Bartolomeu Dias
(Cape of Good Hope, 1488), and Vasco da Gama (India, 1497–99); Columbus's 1492
voyage was backed by Ferdinand and Isabella immediately after Granada's fall; the
1494 Treaty of Tordesillas partitioned Atlantic and global spheres between Spain
and Portugal (Cunliffe 2001). Navigational advances underwriting this expansion
include the 1406 publication of Ptolemy's *Geography* (reintroducing
latitude/longitude), the caravel (combining square-rig durability with Arab
lateen-rig maneuverability), and Portuguese quadrant/solar-declination tables from
the 1460s–80s (Cunliffe 2001).

## Explanatory Theories

Cunliffe (2001) interprets the medieval Atlantic economy in world-systems-adjacent
terms: regional productive specialization — Castile oil, Portugal fruit, Gascony
wine, Brittany salt, England cloth, Iceland fish, each produced "in quantities far
in excess of local needs" — generated structural interdependency that maritime
carriers and merchants existed to redistribute. He extends this into a
civilizational claim, framing the roughly 120 years between Prince Henry's Ceuta
expedition (1415) and Jacques Cartier's St Lawrence voyage (1534) as the hinge at
which the Mediterranean-centered old order gave way to the Atlantic façade as
Europe's "innovating interface" with the wider world, producing — in his words —
"for the first time, a true world system" (Cunliffe 2001). This is Cunliffe's own
extension of world-systems vocabulary (see [[world-systems-analysis]]) to a
pre-Wallersteinian medieval Atlantic economy, not a claim independently derived
from Wallerstein's model or its core/periphery/semi-periphery apparatus; it is
recorded here as his interpretive framing, attributed, not as wiki-voice
confirmation that Wallerstein's mechanisms operated in this period. Cunliffe also
argues the Viking episode of the 9th–11th centuries (see [[viking-age-britain]])
was a catalyst accelerating trends already under way — state formation in England
and France, and the intensification of Atlantic trade at every level — rather than
an external disruption of an otherwise separate medieval economy.

Cunliffe further reads maritime geography itself as productive of political
identity along this façade ("Remoteness and Identity"): coastal populations
"facing the sea" developed separateness from inland populations, instanced by
Portugal's coastal orientation (formalized in the 1297 Treaty of Alcañices),
distinct Galician/Asturian/Basque regional identities, Breton identity reinforced
by Arthurian legend and ducal chronicle (Le Baud's *Croniques et ystoires*, 1480),
and the comparatively early crystallization of Scottish national identity that
Cunliffe reads into the Declaration of Arbroath (1320) (Cunliffe 2001).

## Interaction

Feeds forward from [[viking-age-britain]] (Norse trade networks, Dorestad-type
emporia, and the North Sea shipping infrastructure the medieval trade inherited)
and from the political aftermath of [[anglo-saxon-migration]] and Anglo-Saxon/
Norman state formation. Connects to [[core-periphery]] via Cunliffe's sea-as-core
inversion thesis (peripheral Atlantic lands using the sea as their connective
"core"). Its closing phase (Iberian and English transatlantic voyaging) sets up
the phenomena treated on [[world-systems-analysis]] proper once European maritime
empires extend beyond the Atlantic façade.

## Debates

No live scholarly dispute is recorded here beyond Cunliffe's own explicit caveat
that his periodization is "a broad generalization" rather than a precise or
uncontested chronology (Cunliffe 2001). His characterization of the medieval
Atlantic economy as "a true world system" is his own extension of Wallersteinian
language and should not be read as an adjudicated claim within the world-systems
literature; if a scholarly critique of this extension is ingested later, it
belongs on [[world-systems-analysis]] or a dedicated debate page.
