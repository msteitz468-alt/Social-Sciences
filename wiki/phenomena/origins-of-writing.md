# Origins of Writing

## Definition and Scope

This page tracks the archaeologically documented sequence from pre-literate token accounting to true cuneiform writing in Mesopotamia, and states explicitly what that sequence does and does not establish about the causes of writing's invention. It is not a general theory of writing systems worldwide; it is scoped to the Mesopotamian case as reconstructed by [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]].

## Measurement

Roux's evidentiary chain, given as a chronological sequence (attributed reconstruction, treated here as a well-evidenced material sequence though the causal narrative around it is Roux's own interpretation):

1. **Proto-accounting tokens/calculi** (from the 7th millennium BCE): small clay balls, cubes, and cones representing quantities of goods, found across Iraq and Iran.
2. **Clay envelopes bearing token-count impressions** (c. 3500 BCE, "bullae"): tokens enclosed in clay envelopes marked on the outside with drawings of their contents — recording quantity without yet recording language.
3. **True pictographic tablets** (c. 3300 BCE, in the archaic temples of E-Anna at [[uruk|Uruk]]) — the first writing proper, still non-linguistic at the level of connected sentences, largely restricted to economic genres (goods lists, workmen lists, receipts).
4. **Gradual phoneticization and abstraction of signs**, through the Jemdat Nasr period, "when pictograms are less numerous, less 'realistic' and often used for their phonetic value alone" (Roux, quoted).
5. **Cuneiform proper** by the mid-3rd millennium BCE — see [[cuneiform]] for the script's subsequent development, decipherment, and use.

## Patterns and Findings

The material sequence above (tokens → bullae → pictographic tablets → phoneticized cuneiform) is well evidenced across multiple excavated sites (Khafaje, Tell Brak, Habuba Kabira, Uruk) and can be stated close to wiki voice as a chronological pattern. What writing was invented *for*, and *why then*, is a separate, attributed causal claim.

## Explanatory Theories

Roux argues (attributed, explicitly his own synthesis, framed as part of a broader and admittedly conjectural account of Sumerian civilization's rise) that writing was invented "purely for accounting purposes" — a direct product of the administrative and economic demands of the emergent Sumerian city-state system: canal-irrigation bureaucracy, concentrated temple-centered wealth and power, and the need for a durable "means of recording transactions." In Roux's fuller (and self-described conjectural) causal chain, population growth and new food-surplus technologies (the plough, sledge, chariot, sail, potter's wheel, copper-alloy casting) interacted with later climatic desiccation to consolidate villages into towns, which in turn required large-scale canal irrigation, concentrated the authority of town chiefs and high priests, and — alongside architectural/artistic advance and armed conflict — produced writing as a recording technology. Roux is explicit that "all such 'explanations' are largely conjectural and we shall probably never know what really happened"; this entire causal architecture should stay attributed to Roux, not asserted in wiki voice.

Roux also reports, as an attributed and explicitly uncertain claim, that "some authorities believe" Sumerian pictograms may antedate and have inspired Egyptian hieroglyphs, based on Jemdat Nasr-style cylinder seals found in late-prehistoric Egyptian (Naqadah) graves, shared artistic motifs, and Mesopotamian-derived architectural features in Egypt's First Dynasty royal tombs — Roux himself notes "it is more difficult to understand through which channel" any such transmission occurred.

## Interaction

Feeds directly into [[cuneiform]] (the mature script), [[uruk-culture]] (the archaeological horizon in which writing first appears), [[city-state]] (the political form Roux links causally to writing's invention), and [[urbanization]] (the village-to-city process Roux frames as writing's proximate context).

## Debates

Whether writing's invention is best explained as a direct product of city-state bureaucratic needs (Roux's position) or reflects other causal pathways is not adjudicated here; Roux's account is recorded as a single, admittedly conjectural, secondary synthesis pending comparison against other ingested sources on the [[urbanization]] and [[state-formation]] pages.

## Postgate (1994) — An Administrative-Technology Account

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] complements Roux's causal synthesis
with a more cautious, evidence-first framing: writing is "probably invented in
Mesopotamia" (a qualified claim, given ongoing debate over Mesopotamia's priority
relative to other pristine writing systems), and its earliest and best-attested use is
narrowly **institutional/temple-administrative accounting**. He reconstructs the same
token → bulla → numeral-tablet → sign-tablet sequence as Roux (drawing on 1970s
excavations at Uruk, Susa, Habuba Kabira, and Godin Tepe), but stresses a sampling
caution Roux's synthesis does not foreground: the earliest attested true writing (the
Eanna IV tablet corpus) is, as of current excavation, found **only** at Uruk, and
"arguments from silence are particularly perilous" given how few contemporary sites
have been excavated. The first evidence of writing *outside* clear institutional or
temple context — small numeral tablets from Jebel Aruda and Habuba Kabira, plausibly
commercial — appears only slightly later, but Postgate cautions there is currently no
positive evidence that writing extended beyond large institutions in this earliest
phase; its absence elsewhere may reflect excavation coverage rather than actual social
restriction.

Postgate frames cross-regional early-writing chronology carefully as a case of
diffusion of an *idea*, not the *form*: the Proto-Elamite script (Iran) succeeds and was
influenced by the Mesopotamian pictographic system; the Indus script developed
independently and "probably rather later," possibly stimulated by Mesopotamian or
Elamite example; and Pre-Dynastic Egyptian material shows traces of Mesopotamian
influence (cylinder seals, Uruk-style pottery/cone mosaics at Buto) even though Egyptian
hieroglyphics lack Mesopotamia's token/bulla precursor stage entirely — an explicit
non-equation of writing systems across regions, consistent with the wiki's four-way
non-identity principle applied to technology diffusion rather than people or language.
(As of [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop (2016)]] qualifies the linear "tokens → bullae → pictographic tablets → phoneticized cuneiform" sequence given above under Measurement. Following Nissen, Damerow, and Englund's "archaic bookkeeping" model (*Archaic Bookkeeping*, 1993), he frames writing's emergence as fundamentally **administrative bookkeeping** — solving the accounting problem of recording a quantity together with the person or office responsible — rather than as a linguistic project from the outset. More importantly, he explicitly **rejects treating the token/bulla/numerical-tablet/proto-cuneiform stages as sequential evolutionary steps**: "There was thus not an evolution of precursors to writing, from bullae with tokens to tablets with signs impressed on them, as is very often suggested. These presumed stages coincided and must be seen as different and competing attempts at conceptualizing the surroundings." This directly qualifies the Measurement section's numbered sequence above (drawn from Roux) — read that sequence as a set of parallel, competing administrative technologies converging on true writing, not as discrete evolutionary stages. See [[cuneiform]] for the fuller treatment. (as of [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]])
