# Origins of Writing

## Definition and Scope

This page tracks the archaeologically documented sequence from pre-literate token accounting to true cuneiform writing in Mesopotamia, and states explicitly what that sequence does and does not establish about the causes of writing's invention. It is not a general theory of writing systems worldwide; it is scoped to the Mesopotamian case as reconstructed by [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]].

## Measurement

Roux's evidentiary chain, given as a chronological sequence (attributed reconstruction, treated here as a well-evidenced material sequence though the causal narrative around it is Roux's own interpretation):

1. **Proto-accounting tokens/calculi** (from the 7th millennium BCE): small clay balls, cubes, and cones representing quantities of goods, found across Iraq and Iran.
2. **Clay envelopes bearing token-count impressions** (c. 3500 BCE, "bullae"): tokens enclosed in clay envelopes marked on the outside with drawings of their contents — recording quantity without yet recording language.
3. **True pictographic tablets** (c. 3300 BCE, in the archaic temples of E-Anna at [[uruk|Uruk]]) — the first writing proper, still non-linguistic at the level of connected sentences, largely restricted to economic genres (goods lists, workmen lists, receipts).
4. **Gradual phoneticization and abstraction of signs**, through the Jemdat Nasr period, "when pictograms are less numerous, less 'realistic' and often used for their phonetic value alone" (Roux, quoted).
5. **Cuneiform proper** by the mid-3rd millennium BCE — see [[cuneiform]] for the script's subsequent development, decipherment, and use.

## Patterns and Findings

The material sequence above (tokens → bullae → pictographic tablets → phoneticized cuneiform) is well evidenced across multiple excavated sites (Khafaje, Tell Brak, Habuba Kabira, Uruk) and can be stated close to wiki voice as a chronological pattern. What writing was invented *for*, and *why then*, is a separate, attributed causal claim.

## Explanatory Theories

Roux argues (attributed, explicitly his own synthesis, framed as part of a broader and admittedly conjectural account of Sumerian civilization's rise) that writing was invented "purely for accounting purposes" — a direct product of the administrative and economic demands of the emergent Sumerian city-state system: canal-irrigation bureaucracy, concentrated temple-centered wealth and power, and the need for a durable "means of recording transactions." In Roux's fuller (and self-described conjectural) causal chain, population growth and new food-surplus technologies (the plough, sledge, chariot, sail, potter's wheel, copper-alloy casting) interacted with later climatic desiccation to consolidate villages into towns, which in turn required large-scale canal irrigation, concentrated the authority of town chiefs and high priests, and — alongside architectural/artistic advance and armed conflict — produced writing as a recording technology. Roux is explicit that "all such 'explanations' are largely conjectural and we shall probably never know what really happened"; this entire causal architecture should stay attributed to Roux, not asserted in wiki voice.

Roux also reports, as an attributed and explicitly uncertain claim, that "some authorities believe" Sumerian pictograms may antedate and have inspired Egyptian hieroglyphs, based on Jemdat Nasr-style cylinder seals found in late-prehistoric Egyptian (Naqadah) graves, shared artistic motifs, and Mesopotamian-derived architectural features in Egypt's First Dynasty royal tombs — Roux himself notes "it is more difficult to understand through which channel" any such transmission occurred.

## Debates

Whether writing's invention is best explained as a direct product of city-state bureaucratic needs (Roux's position) or reflects other causal pathways is not adjudicated here; Roux's account is recorded as a single, admittedly conjectural, secondary synthesis pending comparison against other ingested sources on the [[urbanization]] and [[state-formation]] pages.

## Postgate (1994) — An Administrative-Technology Account

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] complements Roux's causal synthesis
with a more cautious, evidence-first framing: writing is "probably invented in
Mesopotamia" (a qualified claim, given ongoing debate over Mesopotamia's priority
relative to other pristine writing systems), and its earliest and best-attested use is
narrowly **institutional/temple-administrative accounting**. He reconstructs the same
token → bulla → numeral-tablet → sign-tablet sequence as Roux (drawing on 1970s
excavations at Uruk, Susa, Habuba Kabira, and Godin Tepe), but stresses a sampling
caution Roux's synthesis does not foreground: the earliest attested true writing (the
Eanna IV tablet corpus) is, as of current excavation, found **only** at Uruk, and
"arguments from silence are particularly perilous" given how few contemporary sites
have been excavated. The first evidence of writing *outside* clear institutional or
temple context — small numeral tablets from Jebel Aruda and Habuba Kabira, plausibly
commercial — appears only slightly later, but Postgate cautions there is currently no
positive evidence that writing extended beyond large institutions in this earliest
phase; its absence elsewhere may reflect excavation coverage rather than actual social
restriction.

Postgate frames cross-regional early-writing chronology carefully as a case of
diffusion of an *idea*, not the *form*: the Proto-Elamite script (Iran) succeeds and was
influenced by the Mesopotamian pictographic system; the Indus script developed
independently and "probably rather later," possibly stimulated by Mesopotamian or
Elamite example; and Pre-Dynastic Egyptian material shows traces of Mesopotamian
influence (cylinder seals, Uruk-style pottery/cone mosaics at Buto) even though Egyptian
hieroglyphics lack Mesopotamia's token/bulla precursor stage entirely — an explicit
non-equation of writing systems across regions, consistent with the wiki's four-way
non-identity principle applied to technology diffusion rather than people or language.
(As of [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop (2016)]] qualifies the linear "tokens → bullae → pictographic tablets → phoneticized cuneiform" sequence given above under Measurement. Following Nissen, Damerow, and Englund's "archaic bookkeeping" model (*Archaic Bookkeeping*, 1993), he frames writing's emergence as fundamentally **administrative bookkeeping** — solving the accounting problem of recording a quantity together with the person or office responsible — rather than as a linguistic project from the outset. More importantly, he explicitly **rejects treating the token/bulla/numerical-tablet/proto-cuneiform stages as sequential evolutionary steps**: "There was thus not an evolution of precursors to writing, from bullae with tokens to tablets with signs impressed on them, as is very often suggested. These presumed stages coincided and must be seen as different and competing attempts at conceptualizing the surroundings." This directly qualifies the Measurement section's numbered sequence above (drawn from Roux) — read that sequence as a set of parallel, competing administrative technologies converging on true writing, not as discrete evolutionary stages. See [[cuneiform]] for the fuller treatment. (as of [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]])

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed throughout — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.]]* 1988, esp. Chs. 1 and 4. Continuity note: Van De Mieroop 2016 (above) cites Nissen–Damerow–Englund *Archaic Bookkeeping* (1993) for the administrative-bookkeeping model and the non-linear competing-systems reading of precursors; Nissen 1988 is the same author’s earlier synthesis of the administrative package and economic-first use of writing.)*

**Writing ~3100 B.C. is not the start of history.** Nissen argues political-organization development does not start with writing and was not particularly influenced by it; the invention of writing in Babylonia around 3100 B.C. was only one of many significant innovations and cannot ground a prehistory/history split based solely on written sources (POSITION). Overvaluing writing, he holds, split the disciplines: philology claimed universal pronouncements on state, community, economy, religion, and daily life, while “historical” archaeology confined itself largely to art and architecture — whereas preliterate archaeology had to investigate society and economy fully. Consequence: we often know more about basic nutrition and domestic flora/fauna of early periods than of “historical” ones because plant/animal remains were analyzed in prehistoric digs but rarely in historical ones that assumed texts would answer. Archaeology should apply preliterate methods even in “historical” periods; early texts aimed not at exact description for posterity but at fitting a view, trend, or legitimation. Surveys may be more objective than literary evidence for settlement relations that texts (aside from economic texts) rarely cover.

**Mainly economic for centuries.** Writing developed at the end of the fourth millennium B.C. by a mixed language group with Sumerian as apparent main component; for centuries used almost only for recording economic procedures, then short votive inscriptions; only mid-third millennium, after system changes, could writing reproduce complicated texts (~600 years after first writing). Early texts almost exclusively economic; tablet organization shows bookkeeping mentality (entries → partial totals → final total on reverse); content categories include sustenance allotment, sacrifice lists, field partition, herd keeping, metal/textile manufacture. Late Uruk writing appears with cylinder seals, monumental architecture, and large-scale art as characteristic Mesopotamian package; cylinder seals and writing are framed as concrete components of **economic administration**, established by analysis of their use (not primarily art/literature).

**Tokens / bullae / numerical tablets (Ch. 4).** Nissen reconstructs a developmental sequence of administrative recording: (1) tally stones/tokens (number only; clay shapes for units/objects); (2) tokens encased in sealed clay balls/bullae (identity of responsible party + anti-manipulation); (3) oblong numerical impressions on ball exteriors; (4) flat clay slabs with numerical signs often fully covered by cylinder-seal impressions; (5) subdivided tablets recording multiple operations. After many directions of expanding what could be recorded, writing as a universal means of control was an almost logical invention. **Important caveat (do not assert linear evolution as Nissen’s settled law):** he states writing appears well developed from the outset in finds; theories of more primitive *sign* predecessors on less durable materials lead to error — precursors existed but **on a different (token/sealing) level**; tokens are **not** early writing (some forms may have been taken over, e.g. ration container in the “to eat” sign). This sits alongside Van De Mieroop’s later explicit rejection of sequential evolution of precursors (competing concurrent systems) — read Nissen 1988’s “sequence” as his narrative of administrative methods culminating in writing, not as a claim that tokens *are* writing stages. Susiana takes the same control package (tokens, sealed balls, numerical tablets, writing) but with different symbols and most probably a different language (proto-Elamite trajectory; static then ceases while Babylonian writing advances). Habuba Kabira shows writing forerunners (seals, sealed balls, numerical tablets) but **no written signs** — abandoned before Uruk IVa.

**Early complex writing ≠ “primitive civilization.”** Nissen rejects reading clumsy early full-language texts as proof that the whole civilization was only an early stage; organizationally this phase is already a later result of centuries of political/economic/social development — so method of presentation should not flip when first historically interpretable texts appear (POSITION).

## Assmann — Egyptian trajectory (1996/2002)

*(Attributed — [[assmann-jan]], *[[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|The Mind of Egypt]].)* For Egypt, Assmann locates writing's origin in two functional domains — **administration** and **temple cult** — with "literature" (narratives, instructions, laments) emerging only around the turn of the 3rd–2nd millennium BCE amid post-Old-Kingdom reorganization; before that Egypt was a "mnemonic culture" transmitting cultural knowledge orally. He ties Old Kingdom bureaucratization to a "literocracy" (scribal training as the recruitment path displacing kinship), and — at the far end — reads the Late Period explosion of hieroglyphic cryptography (the sign repertoire growing "at least ten times" from Alexander's conquest) as priestly compensation for lost political power. Scribal schooling was apprenticeship and rote copying that internalized normative culture, not technical training alone (all Assmann).

## Potts (1997) — writing as "written resume" of prior complexity

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]], citing Nissen, Damerow and Englund 1990.)* Potts states the earliest written documents in human history are the so-called Archaic texts from [[uruk|Uruk]], the oldest dating to the Late Uruk period (c. 3400–3100 BCE). He cites H. J. Nissen (Nissen, Damerow and Englund 1990:55) as having repeatedly stressed that the elaborate division of labour shown in the Archaic list of titles and professions presumes a level of social complexity that did not appear overnight: "Far from representing the beginning of a phenomenon, these texts constitute a written resume of a process which had reached a point of considerable advancement" — with roots undoubtedly reaching back into the Ubaid period.

On this basis Potts argues that writing, as a tool of bureaucracy and social control, "entered the social arena" at this point and **accelerated, rather than originated**, social, economic, religious, political, and technological elaboration already under way; he explicitly rejects treating the appearance of writing as marking any biological or environmental discontinuity between "prehistory" and "history" — a direct methodological counterpart to Nissen's own 1988 rejection (recorded above) of writing as a start-of-history marker, and to Van De Mieroop's rejection of the linear tokens-to-tablets evolutionary sequence. All three sources converge, from different angles, on treating writing's emergence as continuous with — not a rupture from — the preceding trajectory of Late Uruk social complexity (independent convergence across Nissen 1988, Potts 1997, and Van De Mieroop 2016, though all three ultimately draw on the same underlying Nissen–Damerow–Englund corpus, so this is convergence of interpretation on one evidence base rather than fully independent evidence classes).

The numerical-notation and lexical-tradition evidence supporting this reading (thirteen numerical systems in five categories; the lexical tradition's contemporaneity with earliest economic writing) is detailed on [[cuneiform]].
