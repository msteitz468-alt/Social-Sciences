# Urban Underclass

## Definition and Scope

Late-20th-century construct for populations in highly disadvantaged inner-city (and later some inner-ring suburban) neighborhoods marked by long-term joblessness or weak labor-force attachment, street crime and related behaviors, and long-term poverty/welfare dependency — **collectively different**, Wilson argued, from earlier ghetto lower classes after the exit of stable working- and middle-class residents (*[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|The Truly Disadvantaged]]*, 1987 Ch 1).

Wilson’s definition (attributed): individuals outside the mainstream occupational system — lacking training/skills with long-term unemployment or nonparticipation; engaged in street crime and other aberrant behavior; families with long-term poverty/welfare spells — living and interacting in the same depressed communities, increasingly isolated from mainstream patterns and norms. He defended the term against liberal rejection as a “destructive label,” arguing avoidance obscures class-structure change.

[[massey-douglas-s|Massey]] and [[denton-nancy-a|Denton]] treat the underclass not as a cultural isolate but as a product of [[residential-segregation]] concentrating economic deprivation (*American Apartheid*, 1993).

## Measurement

No single official index. Wilson combined:

- community-area / neighborhood **poverty concentration** (Chicago 1970–1980 maps; high- and extreme-poverty areas);
- **joblessness** and employment-population ratios;
- **family structure** (female headship, nonmarital births) and **[[male-marriageable-pool-index]]**;
- welfare dependency and crime rates.

Massey & Denton emphasize segregation indices and poverty-by-tract interaction. Later work (as Wilson 2012 Afterword reports) includes Jargowsky-type concentrated-poverty series and neighborhood-effects designs.

## Patterns and Findings

**Wilson (1987, attributed):** Despite high mid-century ghetto poverty, rates of joblessness, teenage pregnancy, nonmarital births, female headship, welfare dependency, and serious crime were substantially lower before the 1960s and did not reach “catastrophic” levels until the mid-1970s. Pre-1960 inner-city communities, he argued, retained greater social organization (community identity, role models, informal control). Black female-headed families rose from 22% (1960) to 42% (1983) in series he cites; employment collapse for black men shrank the marriageable pool. Class transformation: nonpoor black exit raised the share of the “truly disadvantaged” in remaining neighborhoods → [[concentrated-poverty]] → [[social-isolation]] and [[concentration-effects]].

**Massey & Denton (1993, attributed):** Segregation multiplies citywide black poverty increases into extreme neighborhood poverty; underclass communities are spatially produced; Hispanic patterns emerge mainly where high segregation and poverty co-occur.

**Wilson (2012 Afterword, attributed):** Basic conditions not qualitatively different after 25 years; 1990s concentrated-poverty declines may have been boom blips; incarceration, immigration, and suburbanization of black poverty are major updates; black/white unemployment ratio remained ~2.0+.

**Anderson (1999, attributed):** Ethnography of Philadelphia’s Germantown Avenue corridor supplies a **cultural-public-order** mechanism under underclass conditions: the [[code-of-the-street]] as informal respect/payback rules where police are distrusted; [[decent-and-street]] plurality (most residents decent or trying); oppositional culture and the code as adaptation to joblessness, discrimination, and [[social-isolation]] — not freestanding [[culture-of-poverty]]. Conclusion frames a vicious cycle (hopelessness → violence → outsider stereotype → further code investment) and living-wage jobs as primary remedy (POSITION). See [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study]]; field society [[philadelphia-inner-city]].

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Core claim (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Wilson | Restructuring + joblessness + class exit → isolation/concentration effects; culture as response to structure |
| Massey & Denton | Segregation is the missing structural link that concentrates poverty |
| Culture of poverty | Autonomous subcultural transmission (Lewis/Banfield variants) |
| Welfare (Murray/Mead) | Policy incentives/permissiveness create underclass behaviors |
| Institutional racism (non-spatial) | Schools/labor markets subordinate without centering housing |
| Segmented assimilation (Portes & Rumbaut) | Some second-generation youth risk joining a multiethnic "rainbow underclass" via downward assimilation — **risk framing** from *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]* / [[segmented-assimilation]], not a completed demographic fact from that volume alone |
