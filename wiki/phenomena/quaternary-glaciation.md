## Definition and Scope

Quaternary glaciation is the recurring cycle of continental ice-sheet expansion and
contraction that has characterized the Pleistocene and Holocene (the [[pleistocene]]),
driven by orbital forcing and expressed globally in sea level, climate, and biome
distribution. Per [[klein-human-career-1999]], deep-sea sediment cores reveal roughly
eight major glaciations in the past 780,000 years, and perhaps fifty over the past 2.5
million years — far more than the four glaciations (Günz, Mindel, Riss, Würm) proposed
by the classic early-20th-century Alpine scheme of Penck and Brückner (1909). Klein
states that scheme "should now be totally abandoned" (as of Klein 1999) even though it
survives in some textbooks.

Cenozoic cooling that set the stage for Quaternary glaciation proceeded in
identifiable steps, per Klein (finding): early Oligocene (~34 mya), mid-Oligocene
(~31 mya), mid-Miocene (~14.5–14 mya, permanent East Antarctic Ice Sheet forms), late
Miocene (6.5–5 mya, permanent West Antarctic Ice Sheet forms, Mediterranean
periodically desiccated), and mid-Pliocene (3.2–2.8 mya, first Northern Hemisphere
glaciation) — culminating in the onset of Northern Hemisphere ice sheets by roughly
2.8 million years ago.

## Measurement

The primary chronological tool is marine [[oxygen-isotope-stratigraphy]], which
resolves individual glacial/interglacial cycles far more finely than land-based
stratigraphy, since glacial advances tend to erase evidence of earlier glaciations on
land. Land-based cross-checks include polar ice cores (Greenland, Antarctica) and long
lake or loess sequences (e.g., Lake Baikal, Lake Biwa, Grande Pile). Paleomagnetic
stratigraphy anchors the largest-scale boundaries (e.g., the Matuyama/Brunhes reversal,
~780 kya).

## Patterns and Findings

Klein reports (finding) that the "astronomical theory," commonly credited to the
Serbian astronomer Milutin Milankovic (developed 1915–1940), attributes the
glacial/interglacial cycling to three orbital parameters affecting high-latitude
summer insolation: precession (~19–23 ky cycle), obliquity (~41 ky cycle), and
eccentricity (~100 ky cycle). Klein states (attributed) that the 41-ky obliquity
cycle dominated the glacial rhythm from roughly 2.8 mya to about 1 mya, after which
the 100-ky eccentricity cycle became dominant, lengthening and intensifying subsequent
glaciations.

At glacial maxima, ice sheets held an estimated 50 million cubic km of water and
covered nearly a third of Earth's surface; sea level dropped 130–160 m, exposing
continental-shelf land and creating land bridges — for example, a Siberia–Alaska
bridge during the Last Glaciation, and a bridge that Klein links to early hominin
occupation of Java between roughly 2 and 1 million years ago.

Glacial climate produced worldwide redistribution of plants and animals: tundra and
steppe replaced forest across mid-latitude Eurasia, and arctic species ranged further
south. Mean annual temperatures fell by as much as ~16°C at high latitudes and ~3°C
near the equator. Precipitation generally declined, especially at lower latitudes
(rain forest gave way to grassland/savanna, savanna to desert), increasing dust
deflation that is recorded in deep-sea sediment cores.

Interglacial peaks comparable in warmth to isotope stage 1 or substage 5e have, within
the past 800,000–900,000 years, lasted only around 10,000 years each, while
intervening cold periods lasted around 100,000 years each — meaning, per Klein, that
hominin populations lived mainly under glacial conditions through most of later
prehistory.

## Explanatory Theories

Milankovitch orbital forcing is the dominant explanatory framework (attributed to
Milankovic, developed 1915–1940) for the pacing of glacial cycles. Klein contrasts this
with an older "pluvial" hypothesis, which held that lower latitudes were wetter during
episodes when high latitudes were glaciated; Klein reports this hypothesis is
contradicted by the evidence — aridity, not pluvial conditions, characterized the
tropics during glaciations, while pluvial (wetter) conditions were mainly an
interglacial phenomenon.

## Interaction

Quaternary glaciation is linked by Klein (attributed, position on tempo) to episodes
of hominin and other African mammal speciation around 5 million and 2.5 million years
ago — connecting climatic forcing to the tempo question raised by
[[punctuated-equilibrium]]. It also frames the chronological backbone (via
[[oxygen-isotope-stratigraphy]]) against which [[encephalization]] and the emergence
of [[behavioral-modernity]] are dated.

## Debates

The abandonment of the four-glaciation Alpine scheme in favor of the deep-sea
isotope record is, per Klein, effectively settled among specialists, though the older
scheme persists in some textbooks — a case of textbook consensus lagging the research
front (see the Textbooks guidance in this wiki's source-handling protocol). The
pluvial hypothesis for low-latitude climate during glaciations is presented by Klein
as a superseded position, contradicted by the aridity evidence.

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]].)*

Roberts frames the [[holocene|Holocene]] as the current interglacial of the ongoing glacial cycle. Deep-sea δ¹⁸O records (see [[oxygen-isotope-stratigraphy]]) show about **eight** ~100,000-year glacial–interglacial cycles in the past 0.8 Myr, correcting the older four-glacial Alpine scheme (Williams et al. 1993). At the Last Glacial Maximum (25,000–18,000 Cal. yr BP) ice sheets exceeded 4 km thick, land temperatures fell up to 20°C, and [[sea-level-change|sea level]] dropped >100 m, exposing land bridges. Deglaciation took ~9,000 years and was unsteady, punctuated by **Heinrich events** — major Laurentide iceberg-discharge episodes into the North Atlantic (Broecker 1994). Ice cores show ~30% less atmospheric CO₂ in glacials; the paradoxically depleted land *and* atmospheric carbon is attributed to enhanced oceanic burial, partly via dust-iron fertilization of marine plankton (Martin and Fitzwater 1988). Driven ultimately by [[milankovitch-cycles|orbital forcing]] amplified by internal feedbacks.
