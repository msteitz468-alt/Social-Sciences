# Immigrant Second Generation

Children of immigrants — typically **U.S.-born** offspring of foreign-born parents, sometimes including the **1.5 generation** (foreign-born, arrived as children). In *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]*, Portes & Rumbaut treat the post-1965 "new second generation" as a transformative population concentrated in gateway metros.

## Definition and Scope

Not a single ethnic group: a **demographic and sociological position** linking parental immigrant incorporation to children's language, identity, schooling, and eventual class location. Definitions vary (strict 2nd vs 1.5+2nd); CILS includes both U.S.-born and early-arriving foreign-born with immigrant parents.

## Measurement

Census/CPS counts of children in immigrant-headed households; longitudinal school panels; language and identity items; educational attainment. Portes & Rumbaut (2001) report: by **1998** ~one-tenth of the U.S. population foreign born; by **1997** foreign-born ~**26.8M (10%)** and foreign stock (1st+2nd gen) ~**54.7M (20.5%)**; first+second generations together ~**55 million** (~one in five Americans) in the preface synthesis. CILS operationalizes eligibility as U.S.-born of foreign parentage **or** foreign-born arrived young (1.5 + 2nd).

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed — Portes & Rumbaut 2001 / CILS unless independently replicated elsewhere.)*

- Rapid **English** dominance among second-generation adolescents (~98% well/very well by mid-1990s follow-up) with only ~¼ **fluent bilinguals**.
- Ethnic self-labels shift **away from plain "American"** toward national-origin and panethnic forms between early and late adolescence in CILS.
- Large **national-origin gaps** in parental SES, family structure, identity, psychosocial climate, and school achievement.
- Sample-average school completion better than native-parentage district peers, with **time-in-U.S.** associated with eroded drive and with high-risk nationalities (e.g. Mexican, Nicaraguan, Cuban public-school) still facing attrition risk.
- Theoretical frame: three-way **[[segmented-assimilation]]** rather than uniform melting.

## Explanatory Theories

- **Classical / straight-line assimilation** — progressive melting over generations (critiqued in *Legacies* as incomplete for today's nonwhite second generation).
- **[[segmented-assimilation]]** — divergent paths conditioned by [[modes-of-incorporation]], acculturation type, barriers (race, hourglass labor market, inner-city counterculture), and family/community social capital.

## Interaction

Tied to urban concentration in gateway metros, racialization, school stratification, and labor-market bifurcation; produces long-run ethnic group formation. Links to [[urban-underclass]] risk framing ("rainbow underclass") are **attributed program claims**, not wiki-voice destiny.

## Debates

[[segmented-assimilation-debate]]; 1.5 vs 2nd generation boundaries; optimism vs pessimism about mobility; whether Mexican-origin size dominates apparent "segment" patterns.
