## Definition and Scope

The Austronesian expansion is the demographic and cultural process by which farming
populations originating in mainland East Asia spread through Taiwan, island Southeast
Asia, and ultimately across the Pacific to remote Polynesia and west to Madagascar,
carrying with them a language family, distinctive material culture, and — as
archaeogenetic evidence now shows — a traceable ancestry component. It sits downstream
of two earlier East Asian farming-population formation events and is itself linked to,
but analytically distinct from, the spread of Austroasiatic languages in mainland
Southeast Asia. All ancestry and population claims below are attributed to Reich
(2018) and date-stamped accordingly; see [[reich-who-we-are-2018]].

## Antecedents: The Yangtze and Yellow River Ghost Populations

Chuanchao Wang, working in Reich's laboratory from 2015, analyzed roughly 400
present-day Chinese individuals across some 40 populations and reconstructed two
unmixed ancestral source populations — [[ghost-population|ghost populations]] not
directly sampled but inferred from admixture modeling of their descendants.

The **Yangtze River Ghost Population** is geographically correlated with the origin of
rice cultivation in the Yangtze River valley roughly 9,000 years ago and contributed
the overwhelming majority of ancestry to present-day Southeast Asian populations and
to Taiwan's indigenous populations (as of Reich 2018). The **Yellow River Ghost
Population** is hypothesized to have developed millet agriculture in northern China
roughly 9,000 years ago near the Yellow River and is associated_with the spread of
Sino-Tibetan languages; the Han and Tibetan populations both derive large ancestry
proportions from it, while it is largely absent from Southeast Asian populations (as
of Reich 2018). Han Chinese ancestry is not consistent with descent from the Yangtze
population alone but includes a substantial Yellow-River-associated component that
increases from south to north, consistent with a historical expansion of
Yellow-River-associated populations southward and admixture with local groups (as of
Reich 2018). Farming reached the eastern Tibetan Plateau from the Yellow River region
beginning roughly 3,600 years ago; Tibetan Plateau populations derive about two-thirds
of their ancestry from the Yellow River Ghost Population and about one-third from an
early-branching East Asian lineage plausibly representing indigenous Tibetan
hunter-gatherers (as of Reich 2018).

**Identity caution**: the association of the Yellow River Ghost Population with
Sino-Tibetan languages, and of the Yangtze Ghost Population with Southeast Asian and
Taiwanese ancestry, are genetic-linguistic correlations argued by Reich and
collaborators, not proof that a genetic population equals a language community —
archaeological culture, language, and genetic population remain analytically distinct
categories throughout this reconstruction.

## Patterns and Findings: The Austronesian Expansion Proper

Combined archaeological, linguistic, and genetic evidence places the origin of the
Austronesian expansion at roughly 5,000 years ago, when mainland East Asian farmers
(carrying substantial Yangtze River Ghost Population ancestry) reached Taiwan — the
island where the deepest-branching Austronesian languages are found today. The
expansion continued to the Philippines by roughly 4,000 years ago, associated with the
invention of outrigger canoe technology, and subsequently gave rise to the Lapita
pottery-making culture from roughly 3,300 years ago, which spread rapidly through the
Pacific — first to Vanuatu, then to Tonga and Samoa — before pausing and later
resuming to reach New Zealand, Hawaii, and Easter Island by roughly 800 years ago.
Moving in the opposite direction, Austronesian speakers reached Madagascar, some 9,000
kilometers to the west, by at least 1,300 years ago (as of Reich 2018).

Mark Lipson (Reich's laboratory) identified a genetic "tracer dye" for this expansion:
nearly all present-day Austronesian-language speakers carry ancestry more closely
related to aboriginal Taiwanese populations than to any mainland East Asian
population, corroborating the Taiwan-origin model on independent genetic grounds (as
of Reich 2018).

### Austroasiatic Languages: A Related but Distinct Expansion

Reich's laboratory extracted ancient DNA (2017) from roughly 4,000-year-old skeletons
at Man Bac, Vietnam, finding a mixture of an early-splitting East Eurasian lineage and
Yangtze River Ghost Population ancestry, in proportions matching present-day
Austroasiatic speakers — supporting the view that Austroasiatic languages spread with
a movement of rice farmers from southern China who interbred with local
hunter-gatherer populations (as of Reich 2018). Lipson further found that in western
Indonesia, where Austronesian languages now predominate, a substantial ancestry share
derives from the same lineage found among mainland Austroasiatic speakers, suggesting
Austroasiatic speakers may have preceded Austronesian speakers in the region — a
finding corroborated by Austroasiatic loanwords documented in Bornean Austronesian
languages by linguists Alexander Adelaar and Roger Blench (as of Reich 2018). This is
recorded here as a related but genetically and linguistically distinct expansion
process, not folded into the Austronesian case.

## Lapita Ancient DNA and the Settlement of the Remote Pacific

Ancient DNA from Lapita-culture individuals in Vanuatu and Tonga, roughly 3,000–2,500
years old (Reich lab, 2016), showed little or no Papuan ancestry — contradicting the
prior view in the genetics literature that the first Pacific settlers were already an
East Asian/Papuan admixed population. This implies the original Lapita-pottery-making
migrants were a nearly unmixed population descended from the Taiwan/Philippines
Austronesian-associated lineage (as of Reich 2018).

A later, major migration bringing substantial Papuan ancestry into the remote Pacific
began by at least 2,400 years ago; Vanuatu samples from that point onward show 90% or
more Papuan ancestry, indicating this later wave comprehensively replaced the
demographic composition of the earlier Lapita-descended population, while the
population apparently retained Austronesian language — a pattern Reich describes as an
unresolved mystery (as of Reich 2018). Reich's laboratory and Johannes Krause's
laboratory independently found that the Papuan ancestry present in Vanuatu is more
closely related to that of the Bismarck Islands than to the geographically closer
Solomon Islands (which lie on the direct sailing route), and that Papuan ancestry in
remote Polynesia derives from yet a different source than in Vanuatu — implying at
least three separate migrations into the open Pacific: the original Lapita-associated
migration and at least two distinct later Papuan-ancestry waves (as of Reich 2018).

This body of work was enabled methodologically by Ron Pinhasi and colleagues'
discovery that the dense petrous bone (the inner-ear region of the skull) preserves
ancient DNA up to 100 times better than other skeletal elements, making ancient DNA
recovery feasible from tropical Pacific remains where preservation conditions are
otherwise poor.

## A Related Case: Japanese Population Formation

Present-day Japanese population formation is recorded here as a structurally
comparable, though separate, admixture case in East Asia. Naruya Saitou and
colleagues modeled the present-day Japanese population as a mixture of Korean-related
agricultural ancestry and Ainu-related hunter-gatherer ancestry (associated with the
Jōmon archaeological culture), estimating roughly 80% farmer and 20%
hunter-gatherer ancestry proportions. Reich and Saitou estimated the average date of
this admixture at roughly 1,600 years ago, corresponding to the Kofun period of
political unification in Japan — notably centuries after agriculture's arrival
(roughly 2,300 years ago), implying a long interval of social separation between
farming and hunter-gatherer populations before genetic mixing occurred (as of Reich
2018).

## Measurement

The expansion is reconstructed through the convergence of three independent evidence
classes: archaeological typology and radiocarbon chronology (Lapita pottery
distribution, outrigger canoe technology), historical linguistics (Austronesian
language-family branching, with Taiwan holding the deepest splits), and ancient/modern
DNA admixture modeling (the Taiwan "tracer dye" signal; Lapita-era genome sequencing).
Reich (2018) treats the convergence of archaeology, linguistics, and genetics on a
Taiwan-origin, sea-borne expansion model as unusually strong, while flagging that the
post-Lapita Papuan-ancestry replacement episodes remain genuinely unexplained.

## Explanatory Theories

The dominant explanatory account is a demic-diffusion model: farming populations
expanded geographically, carrying their language and ancestry together via seaborne
migration enabled by outrigger canoe technology. This is an attributed model, not a
convergent finding independent of the genetic and linguistic evidence that motivates
it (as of Reich 2018); the later Papuan-ancestry replacement waves that retained
Austronesian language show that language and ancestry can decouple, cautioning against
treating "Austronesian" as a single unified population-and-language unit across the
whole expansion.

## Interaction

The Austronesian expansion interacts with, and is genetically downstream of, the
earlier Yangtze and Yellow River farming-population formation events described above,
and runs parallel to (and locally interdigitates with, in western Indonesia) the
Austroasiatic-language expansion. It is comparable in general form, though not in
genetic content, to other well-documented ghost-population-driven demic expansions
recorded elsewhere in the archaeogenetic record (see [[ghost-population]]).

### Gamble — *Timewalkers* (1994): purposive Pacific voyaging

Mid-1990s archaeological synthesis layer (attribute; pre-aDNA chronology). [[gamble-clive|Clive Gamble]] treats Remote Pacific expansion as part of late [[global-colonization]]: Lapita push outside Melanesia ~3500 B.P.; linguists' proto-Austronesian homeland Taiwan ~5500 B.P. with breakup after 4000 B.P. (Terrell caution: linguistic geography ≠ historical geography); Polynesian triangle endpoints Easter Island A.D. 300, Hawai'i A.D. 300–500, New Zealand A.D. 800; Groube: Polynesians "became" in Fiji–Samoa–Tonga rather than simply "came from" elsewhere; Near Pacific Pleistocene seafaring (~30 ka, e.g. New Ireland, Buka) as earlier organizational modern burst (attributed, *[[gamble-timewalkers-1994|Timewalkers]]* ch. 10). Argues against chance/drift (Levison–Ward–Webb simulations) and pure island-biogeography timing: colonizers deliberate, safety-concerned, rational within their worldview; ocean voyaging as **exaptive** cooption of navigation and negotiation, not adaptive design to fill the ocean (attributed). Dates and Lapita models here are **as of 1994** — cross-check against later aDNA and revised chronologies.

### Kirch — *On the Road of the Winds* (2017): the archaeological–linguistic backbone

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]].)* Kirch supplies the archaeological and
historical-linguistic scaffolding that the Reich (2018) genetic account sits alongside. The
**Out-of-Taiwan** model (Bellwood; see [[bellwood-peter]]): ceramic-using peoples appear on Taiwan
~3000 BC (Dabenkeng), descendants expanding south to the Philippines (~2200 BC) and beyond; Robert
Blust's Austronesian phylogeny ("principle of least moves") nests a Taiwan homeland → Malayo-
Polynesian → Central-Eastern MP → Oceanic, with Proto-Oceanic reaching the Bismarcks (the
[[lapita-cultural-complex|Lapita]] signature) by ~3600 BP. Kirch treats the archaeological and
independently-derived linguistic subgroupings as "remarkably consistent" — a case of two evidence
classes converging. Reconstructed Proto-Oceanic vocabulary (crops, pig/dog/chicken, houses,
outrigger-canoe terms) is recovered by comparative-linguistic reconstruction (Pawley, Ross).

Crucially, Kirch keeps the **four-way non-identity** explicit: the Lapita cultural complex is
`associated_with` — not identical to — Oceanic languages and the "Lapita" genetic signature.
Within Lapita/Oceanic formation he adopts [[green-roger|Green]]'s **[[triple-i-model|Triple-I
model]]** (intrusion + local innovation + integration with Papuan-speaking Near Oceanians), and
notes that later Papuan-ancestry replacement in Remote Oceania left Austronesian *language* in
place — language, genes, and culture demonstrably decouple (mtDNA "Polynesian motif" vs. a Near
Oceanic–origin Y-chromosome haplotype). See also [[peopling-of-sahul]] (the much earlier, separate
Pleistocene colonization of Near Oceania) and [[oceanic-voyaging]].
