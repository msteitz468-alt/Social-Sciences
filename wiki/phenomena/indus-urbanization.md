## Definition and Scope

The rapid rise of the Indus (Harappan) cities — Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Dholavira,
Ganweriwala, Rakhigarhi, and numerous smaller towns — out of the preceding, less
differentiated Early Harappan regional communities. Gregory [[possehl-gregory-l|Possehl]]
frames the transition as occurring "so rapidly from relatively undifferentiated Early
Harappan communities" that it constitutes a genuine discontinuity rather than a smooth
extension of prior trends (attributed; his own reading of the pace of change). Of 1,058
known Indus-age settlements, 755 (71%) were founded on virgin soil at or near the
transition, and 324 of 523 known Early Harappan sites (62%) were abandoned before the
Mature Harappan flourished — figures Possehl reads (in attributed voice, via his own
"nihilism" framework) as evidence of a deliberate break with Early Harappan tradition
rather than mere gradual accretion, while noting that subsistence practices (barley/wheat
farming, cattle pastoralism) and some ceramic motifs persisted across the transition.

## Measurement

Measured through systematic settlement survey (site counts and areal extent by phase,
e.g., the Harappa phase sequence and the Cholistan/Sarasvati "breadbasket" settlement
counts) and radiocarbon-anchored phase chronologies (calibration status not stated for
several of these ranges in the source ingested here — flagged as unresolved calibration
provenance, not asserted as calibrated). No demonstrated three- or four-tier settlement
hierarchy has been established across the corpus of known sites — Possehl states plainly
that "no one has successfully demonstrated" such a hierarchical rationalization of Indus
settlement sizes, a finding that bears directly on how "urban" the settlement system as a
whole should be characterized relative to Mesopotamian- or Egyptian-style hierarchies.

## Patterns and Findings

Correlates of Indus urbanization, as documented across excavated sites: architectural
elaboration and civic water-management infrastructure (drains, wells, bathing facilities,
and, at Dholavira, an unusually elaborate system of rock-cut reservoirs); standardization
elements including a widely (though not universally) used brick ratio of 4:2:1, a shared
weight system, and the [[indus-script|Indus script]] itself, which appears even at sites
with otherwise weak "Harappanness" (the Sorath Harappan region used Indus weights and
writing but not stamp seals) — evidence that standardization diffused unevenly rather
than as one indivisible cultural package; occupational specialization inferred from
craft-production evidence (bead-making, seal-cutting, metalworking); and wealth
differentiation visible in hoards and site-size variation. Distinctively, and this is the
empirical basis for [[indus-polity-debate|the debate over Indus political organization]],
these correlates of urbanization occur **without** the monumental religious architecture
(temples, ziggurats) or monumental elite residences (palaces) that mark urbanization and
state formation in contemporary Egypt and Mesopotamia — a documented negative material
fact, not an interpretive claim.

## Explanatory Theories

Possehl's own interpretive device for the civilizational complex underlying this
urbanization is his term "Indus ideology" — an explicitly acknowledged etic proxy
construct (not a recovered emic category) combining four themes: a "nihilistic" break
with Early Harappan tradition, urbanization/city life itself, *wasserluxus* (M. Jansen's
term for the physical and symbolic centrality of water), and sustained technological
innovation. Possehl repeatedly stresses this is his own approximation, never a claim to
have recovered actual Harappan belief, and it must be kept in attributed voice throughout.
Whether this urbanization proceeded under a centralized state or a decentralized,
heterarchical/corporate polity is the subject of [[indus-polity-debate]].

## Kenoyer 1998 — growth, five-city hierarchy, walls as control, infrastructure

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]], *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* 1998, esp. Chs. 1–3.)*

**Gradual growth, not flash foundation.** Kenoyer argues Indus cities were not created in a short time by visionary rulers or architects but **grew out of earlier villages** in the same localities over hundreds of years, becoming the largest regional settlements surrounded by towns and villages. Landowners or entrepreneurial merchants may have been the first administrative leaders, with religious leaders legitimizing power; seasonal ritual and city workshops made urban centers foci for hinterlands. This gradualist framing **contrasts** with Possehl’s stronger discontinuity/“nihilism” reading of the Early–Mature transition on this page’s Definition section — both positions remain attributed; neither is wiki-voice verdict.

**Settlement hierarchy and five major cities.** Of roughly **1,500** settlements in the Indus and Saraswati regions (Kenoyer’s figure), most are small villages/hamlets (<1–10 ha), with fewer towns and small cities (10–50 ha). He identifies **five large cities** as major urban centers: Mohenjo-daro (+200 ha; inhabited area he revises to >250 ha), Harappa (+150 ha), Ganweriwala and Rakhigarhi (+80 ha), and Dholavira (100 ha). The first four form inland centers at roughly equivalent distances in a zigzag across the Indus–Saraswati plain; Dholavira on Kadir island in the Rann of Kutch controlled shipping between Gujarat resources and the Indus core. He estimates each inland city’s hinterland at roughly **100,000–170,000 km²** — magnitudes not reappearing in the northern subcontinent until Early Historic city-states (ca. 600–300 BCE). Cities, in his model, need not have directly controlled all farming, pastoral, fishing, and forager communities in their territories but **did control movement of trade goods** into and out of city markets.

**Harappa as early urban core (Kot Diji / Period 2).** Recent excavations at Harappa (HARP) indicate, per Kenoyer, that the earliest city may have formed **before** the Integration Era during the **Kot Diji Phase (2800–2600 BCE)**, when the settlement grew to ~**25 ha** as a trade node linking Baluchistan/Afghanistan to the southern coast (carnelian, chert, copper, shell into workshops). Within a few hundred years it grew roughly sixfold to **over 150 ha**. Settlement planning (cardinal grid streets) was already established in Period 2 and at peer Early Harappan sites (Kalibangan, Rehman Dheri, Nausharo, Kot Diji) — not a sudden Mature Harappan invention (see [[early-harappan]]).

**Walls and gateways as control (not primarily military defense).** Massive mud-brick walls and brick/stone gateways at Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, and Dholavira attest, in Kenoyer’s reading, to power and wealth. At multi-walled Harappa he **rejects a primary military-defense interpretation**: no battle damage; gateways not planned for frontal military defense (no sharp interior turns typical of later historical fortifications); lack of moat and multi-trace walls (unlike Dholavira’s nested system) suggests gates and walls were **more symbolic and intended to control trade and commerce**. Narrow openings (e.g. ~2.8 m at one Harappa southern gateway — one ox cart) and adjacent open spaces fit market/tax-checkpoint readings. Dholavira’s three nested rectangular walls (outer ~771 × 617 m, ~47 ha) with bastions and formal gateways (including an acropolis north gate with plaza, ramp, and the large script “signboard”) exemplify nested control architecture. Architectural crafts, he argues, let many social and economic classes live in close proximity **within** massive walls while maintaining status differentiation.

**City planning and infrastructure.** Overall layout orients streets and buildings to the **cardinal directions** (grid cultural template Kenoyer links analogically to Mehrgarh compartmented houses and Early Chalcolithic geometric seals — not claimed as direct continuity). Actual street nets are irregular; curving walls show willingness to break directional rules for expedience. Standard **1:2:4** brick proportions for house bricks (~7 × 14 × 28 cm) and wall bricks (~10 × 20 × 40 cm) appear from the Kot Diji Phase and become Integration-era standards; first kiln-baked bricks ca. 2600 BCE. Infrastructure package: wells (tops raised above street; adjacent drains), bathing platforms, house drains to street sewers, corbeled-arch under-street drains (Harappa gateway drain sequences with sequential rebuilds), sump pits and garbage bins requiring municipal cleaning — and, when maintenance failed, street-level rise forcing doorway and house rebuilds that raised entire mounds over centuries. Public architecture includes the Mohenjo-daro Great Bath (Kenoyer: earliest public water tank in the ancient world) and large “granary”/great-hall structures whose storage function remains contested. Multi-story courtyard houses, privacy from main streets, and latrines/drains extend a pattern already emerging at Early Harappan Kalibangan.

**Integration Era package.** In Shaffer/Kenoyer periodization, the Integration Era Harappa Phase (ca. **2600–1900 BCE**) is urbanism with large cities and satellites, [[indus-script|writing]], standardized weights and measures, taxation, hierarchical social order, and unifying symbols on pottery, ornaments, and ritual objects — without Mesopotamian-style palace-temple cores or warfare imagery (see [[indus-polity-debate]]).

## Wright (2010) — five cities, site-count growth, and central-place/city-state modeling

*(Attributed to [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]], *The Ancient Indus* (Cambridge, 2010), Ch. 5.)* Wright reports Possehl's (1990:270) figure of an increase from 463 sites in the Pre-urban period to 976 sites in the Urban period, and identifies five major Indus cities: [[harappa|Harappa]] (Upper Indus), [[mohenjo-daro|Mohenjo-daro]] (Lower Indus), [[dholavira|Dholavira]] (Kutch), Ganweriwala (Cholistan), and Rakhigarhi (Ghaggar-Hakra) — the last discussed only briefly by Wright given the limited published excavation record. Cholistan settlement-count tables (Mughal 1997, per Wright's Table 5.2) show growth from the Hakra phase (37 sites) through the Kot Diji/Early Harappan phase (32 sites) to the Urban period (73 sites, including 8 small towns and one city, Ganweriwala) — with camp-site numbers falling in the Kot Diji phase (read by Mughal as evidence of increasing pastoral sedentarization) and rising again in the Urban period (read as pastoralists being drawn toward, while remaining on the margins of, settled areas).

Wright applies Christaller's ([1933] 1966) central-place theory to this settlement hierarchy, arguing that a primary-center-plus-secondary-centers-plus-periphery structure — with Ganweriwala and Harappa functioning as central places for their Cholistan and Beas hinterlands respectively — fits the Indus evidence, and combines this with the [[city-state|city-state]] concept (following Kenoyer 1997a, Charlton and Nichols 1997) to read the Indus cities as several loosely integrated, culturally unified but politically independent city-states, each substantially agriculturally self-sufficient yet mutually dependent on rural hinterlands for craft goods and food (see [[city-state]] for the full comparative treatment). She reports that Indus public works — civic water-management infrastructure, gateway/entryway monitoring at Harappa, and the possible ceremonial "stadia" at Dholavira — are consistent with, though not proof of, an underlying centralized planning authority and social hierarchy operating within this city-state framework, while explicitly declining to read any of this as evidence for a single unified Indus state.
