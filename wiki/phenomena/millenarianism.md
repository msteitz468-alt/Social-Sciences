# Millenarianism and Movements of Despair

Religious-political movements expecting or enacting a total transformation of the social order — millenarian and Messianic sects, cargo cults, witch-cleansing and nativistic movements, and (in Gluckman's analysis) oath-bound movements like Mau Mau. Seeded from Gluckman's "The Magic of Despair" (BBC broadcast, *The Listener*, 29 April 1954), reprinted in [[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963]].

## Definition and Scope

Gluckman grouped, as responses to colonial-era social strain: intensified [[witchcraft]] fears and anti-witchcraft "cleansing" movements (documented by [[richards-audrey|Audrey Richards]], "A Modern Movement of Witchfinders", 1935); nativistic/revivalist cults (more common, he argued, in North America and Oceania, where societies had crumbled, than in Africa); and separatist African Christian sects — some millenarian or Messianic, envisioning a Heaven with the colour bar reversed — which under restrictions on trade unions and political association were "almost the only forms of organization allowed" to Africans in South Africa (Sundkler, *Bantu Prophets*, 1948).

## Measurement

No standard index; the phenomenon is documented through case ethnographies, colonial administrative records, and comparative historical archives (Cohn's medieval sources). Counting "movements" is frame-dependent — witch-cleansing waves, separatist churches, and cargo cults are grouped or split differently by different authors; treat cross-case tallies as suggestive.

## Patterns and Findings

- **Gluckman's Mau Mau thesis (1954, attributed and self-labelled a hypothesis):** Mau Mau was a product of colonization, not a reversion to indigenous religion — "dominantly a movement of despair" using "a magic of despair": magic mobilized against overwhelming colonial force, its oaths built on fantasies of what secret societies are reputed to do, with obscenity reflecting breakdown of ordinary moral controls. He explicitly rejected the Parliamentary Delegation's and press's primitivist framing ("back to the bush," "30,000 years of stagnation"), arguing nothing in African religions resembled Mau Mau's obscenities. [POSITION — both framings recorded; Gluckman's reply provoked a published rejoinder from Sir Philip Mitchell, former Governor of Kenya.]
- **Intellectual lineage (attributed):** Gluckman built on [[evans-pritchard-e-e|Evans-Pritchard]]'s "Some Collective Expressions of Obscenity in Africa" (1929) and his Azande secret-society analysis (1937) — magic as the "last defence" against a power that can be neither resisted nor avoided; and on the Bambatha Rebellion (Zulu, 1906) bullets-into-water magic.
- **Cross-disciplinary convergence:** Gluckman's 1954 broadcast and Manchester seminars fed exchanges with [[cohn-norman|Norman Cohn]] (*The Pursuit of the Millennium*, 1957, medieval Europe) and Eric Hobsbawm, whose *Primitive Rebels* (1959) was partly stimulated by them; Worsley's *The Trumpet Shall Sound* (1957) developed parallel ideas on Melanesian cargo cults independently.
- **Cohn's modern political-myth extension (1967, attributed):** In *[[cohn-warrant-for-genocide-1967|Warrant for Genocide]]*, Cohn carries the millenarian/demonological toolkit into modern history: Nilus packages the forged *[[protocols-of-the-elders-of-zion|Protocols]]* in Antichrist/end-time frames; Rosenberg's cast of mind is permanently apocalyptic; the [[jewish-world-conspiracy-myth]] functions as a secularized demonology that can warrant [[genocide]]. This is not cargo-cult ethnography — it is the same historian tracking how "subterranean" apocalyptic fantasies capture modern states. *Pursuit* itself remains uningested as full text.

## Explanatory Theories

Deprivation/despair under colonial force (Gluckman); pre-political rebellion (Hobsbawm); proto-nationalist organization where secular channels are blocked (Sundkler/Gluckman on separatist churches). All attributed; no adjudication.

**Wagner's anti-deprivation position (1975, attributed):** [[wagner-roy|Roy Wagner]] (*[[wagner-invention-of-culture-1975|The Invention of Culture]]*) explicitly rejected disturbance/injustice explanations of Melanesian cargo cults as reductive ("explanations in terms of disturbance and injustice belittle human achievements to the level of correctives, and reduce life to an equilibrium model"), reading cargo cult instead as a [[reverse-anthropology]] — "an interpretive counterpart of anthropology itself… a pragmatic sort of anthropology that invents in anticipation of the future." His key case is Yali of Madang (via Peter Lawrence, *Road Belong Cargo*, 1964); cf. Burridge's *Mambu* (1970) Cargo/cargo distinction. [POSITION — stands against the deprivation/despair line above; no adjudication.]

## Interaction

Linked to [[witchcraft]] (accusation surges under strain), [[ritual-of-rebellion]] (contrast: instituted protest *within* an accepted order vs movements against the order itself — Gluckman's rebel/revolutionary line), and colonial political economy ([[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]]).
