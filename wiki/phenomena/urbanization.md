# Urbanization

## Definition and Scope

Growth of cities and of urban social organization. In *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925), [[burgess-ernest-w|Burgess]] calls the growth of great cities "the outstanding fact of modern society," where machine industry's social changes register most obviously (attributed). [[park-robert-e|Park]] defines the city as a state of mind and moral organization as well as a physical and economic unit — urbanization is thus cultural and ecological, not only demographic.

## Measurement

City/metropolitan size and density; natural increase vs migration; mobility series (rides, mail, phones); land-value maps; composition contrasts (sex/age/foreign-born/occupation — Burgess citing Weber, Bücher).

## Patterns and Findings

(Attributed Chicago program unless noted.)

- Radial expansion and [[concentric-zones|zone]] differentiation; succession by invasion.
- Metropolitan area overruns political boundaries; defined by transport facility.
- Secondary replacing primary relations; fashion and public opinion as control.
- High mobility and zone-of-deterioration concentration of pathology rates.
- Newspaper as urban institution paralleling the city (natural history).
- Wirth's bibliography classifies growth, ecology, city–country comparison as standing research domains.
- **Mid-sized industrial city (Lynds, attributed):** *[[lynd-middletown-1929|Middletown]]* (fieldwork 1924–25) documents cultural change in a ~35–38k East-North-Central industrial city — not a metropolis — with machine industry, auto/movie/radio leisure, booster civic ideology, and classed life-chances; selection sought "common features" of American urban life while disclaiming strict typicality (see [[lynd-middletown-study]]).

## Explanatory Theories

[[human-ecology]]; [[social-disorganization-theory]]; later modernization and urban political economy; **[[global-city-theory]]** (Sassen — strategic cities as production sites for specialized services/finance under [[globalization]]; joint dispersal and centralization; [[spatial-polarization]]); **[[network-society-theory]]** (Castells — [[informational-city]] as process under the [[space-of-flows]]; structural schizophrenia with the [[space-of-places]]; mega-cities globally connected / locally disconnected; anti-end-of-cities).

## Interaction

[[juvenile-delinquency]], migration, [[money-economy]], [[secondary-contacts]], immigrant colonies; [[globalization]]; [[producer-services]]; [[residential-segregation]]; [[urban-underclass]]; [[flexible-employment]].

**Late Iron Age temperate Europe — oppida (Cunliffe 1997, attributed).** North of the Alps, the last two centuries BCE see large nucleated fortified centres ([[oppida]]) with craft production, coinage, and multi-function (assembly, trade, religion) roles—Manching-type service centres and Bibracte-class tribal foci—framed by Cunliffe as a periphery response in which **Roman demand for slaves, metals, and markets after c.200 BCE** catalyses (with internal raid-to-commodity social change) urban-like nucleation short of Mediterranean city-states; “Trade preceded the flag.” Distinct evidence class and period from Chicago/Sassen modern urbanization and from Near Eastern Urban Revolution cases above — cross-link via [[oppida]], [[prestige-goods-economy]], [[la-tene-culture]], source [[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]]; do not collapse scales.

**Precolonial African urban diversity (Connah 2016, attributed).** [[connah-graham|Connah]] (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016|African Civilizations]]*) adopts Mabogunje’s simple definition of urbanization as relatively dense aggregations of population, then stresses that African precolonial “cities” were **not one form**: some dense and long-lived (e.g. [[jenne-jeno]], Egyptian and Maghreb stone/earth cities), some mobile or seasonal, some dispersed over large areas, some organic capitals without stone monumental cores (Interlacustrine). He insists **cities and states are not a single process** — not all cities were state capitals; not all states had large static centres. Visibility bias: rainforest timber/earth and Central African organic architecture under-represent urbanism relative to stone ruins. Archaeological cases densified on [[giza]], [[amarna]], [[kerma]], [[meroe]], [[aksum]], [[jenne-jeno]], [[ile-ife]], [[benin-city]], [[kilwa-kisiwani]], [[great-zimbabwe]], [[mapungubwe]].


### Yoffee — earliest cities (2005, attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]* Ch. 3) argues neo-evolutionist archaeology **ignored the meaning of cities**: earliest cities were sites of “urban explosion,” nodes of pilgrimage, exchange, storage, defense, and new citizen identities within [[city-state]] systems (or, for Egypt, specialized royal/ceremonial urbanism in a territorial state). Ruralization co-evolves with urban concentration; cities restructure the countryside. Comparative size table (Table 3.1) and plan figures frame Mesopotamia, Egypt, Teotihuacan, Shang, Indus, Andes, Maya against modern campus/city scales. Complements Adams Heartland and Trigger urbanism-by-polity-type. See [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

### Adams Heartland layer (1981)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] (*[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]*) treats southern Mesopotamia as a long-run **land of cities** under shifting rural–urban balances (study: [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]). Attributed theses: ED I first predominantly urbanized society (~81%/71% settlement area in sites >10 ha); urban proportion never constant enough to suggest pure ecological determination; other side of urban precocity is persistent semisedentary tribalized peasantry; cities preferred analytic object over abstract states. Date-stamp all figures to 1981.

### Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) — Teotihuacan nucleation and ruralization cycles

*[[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|The Basin of Mexico]]* ([[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study|study]]) documents an "extreme nucleation" episode at [[teotihuacan]]: by the end of their First Intermediate Phase Three–Four, 94–97.4% of the Teotihuacan Valley's population resided in the city itself (accepting [[millon-rene|Millon's]] estimate of ~40,000). Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) explicitly revise their own earlier published position (Blanton 1972; Parsons 1968, 1971) that this nucleation occurred during the Middle Horizon via forced removal of population from a wide region — expanded northern-Basin survey after 1972 showed a Middle Horizon population *increase*, not decline, relative to First Intermediate, leading them to reassign peak nucleation to First Intermediate Phase Four and to characterize First Intermediate Five/Middle Horizon instead as a deliberate **ruralization** policy by the Teotihuacan state. They compare the Middle Horizon settlement system to "primate systems" (citing Blanton 1976a) but argue it diverges from modern primate-system theory: rural settlements are read as sociological/economic extensions of the urban center rather than an exploited periphery, and the system endured roughly 500 years without the instability Blanton's model predicts.

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) contrast three Basin urban centers along an explicit evolutionary staging: Teotihuacan as "the beginning of the process," [[tula]] as intermediate, and [[tenochtitlan]] as "the end." At Teotihuacan roughly 40% of all Basin peasants lived physically inside the city (about two-thirds of its population), blurring the urban/rural distinction and — the authors argue — implying a *lower* urbanization index than at Tenochtitlan or Tula despite Teotihuacan's larger absolute population. They also engage [[millon-rene|René Millon's]] (1976) position, itself drawing on Jane Jacobs' *The Economy of Cities* (1969), that cities generate their own developmental dynamic and drive agricultural intensification in their hinterland independent of surrounding ecology; the authors accept this "city as independent driver" claim as supported by their Teotihuacan zone model, but explicitly reject Jacobs' and Binford's (1968) further claim that agriculture itself originated among nucleated ("urban") hunter-gatherer settlements, calling it unsupported by Mesoamerican agricultural-origins data.

## Debates

Whether "urbanism" is a distinct way of life (Wirth 1938 — not yet primary); ecological vs political-economy drivers; [[global-city-model-debate]] (measurement, polarization, state); end-of-cities / electronic-cottage theses vs restructured [[informational-city|informational]] and global cities (Castells, Sassen).

**Deep-time / Graeber & Wengrow (2021), attributed.** *[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Dawn of Everything]]* chs. 8–9 challenge the equation scale → hierarchy: early cities as experiments (Trypillia mega-sites, Mesopotamian assemblies, Indus, Teotihuacan social housing, Tlaxcalan republics) often without kings; monarchy frequently on urban margins ("heroic societies"). Contested: [[dawn-of-everything-debate]]. Links: [[three-elementary-forms-of-domination]], [[state-formation]].

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — Monte Albán as an early small-scale urban revolution

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* The authors situate [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]]'s Period I population (an estimated 3,500–7,000, midpoint ~5,000, in Early I; 17,000 by Late I — Blanton 1978:33–5, 44) as small in absolute terms but historically precocious: for comparison, they note Teotihuacan and Tenochtitlán each reached ~200,000 in the Classic/Late Postclassic (Millon 1973), 1749 New York City had just over 13,000 people (Chandler and Fox 1974:178), and no world city exceeded 1 million until Beijing ca. AD 1800 (Chandler and Fox 1974:323) — framing Monte Albán's 5,000–17,000 Period I population as an early but genuinely urban "revolution" on a small scale. They report Monte Albán's Period I population growth at an estimated **6% per year**, "a rate much higher than that of any of today's developing nations" (Blanton, Kowalewski, Feinman, and Appel 1982:41); physical-anthropological mortality/health data (Hodges 1989) show little change in this period, implying the growth came from fertility increase and substantial in-migration rather than declining mortality — with no clear material-culture or physical-anthropological trace of migrant ethnic identity, suggesting rapid acculturation.

For the broader Late Postclassic Mesoamerican world, they cite Berdan et al. (1996:109) that urbanization rates in core zones (centered on ~200,000-person Tenochtitlán-Tlatelolco) exceeded that of contemporary England — see [[mesoamerica]] for the fuller regional data.

### Deep-time urbanization — Childe (*What Happened in History*, 1942/1954)

Before modern industrial urbanization, [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] treats the first urbanism as the [[urban-revolution|Urban Revolution]] in alluvial Bronze Age valleys: surplus concentration, specialists, writing, class (*[[childe-what-happened-in-history-1942|WHIH]]* — attributed). Secondary centers (Crete, Troy, Helladic, Byblos) expand the urban network by trade and resistance-as-diffusion; Roman Imperial cities later shrink when markets fail to deepen mass demand. Distinct evidence class and period from Chicago/Sassen modern urbanization above — link, do not collapse.

### Deep-time urbanization — Adams (1966, attributed)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] (*[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|EUS]]*) treats early cities as **containers** (Mumford's phrase) for evolving kin, class, and polity institutions rather than as the primary object of generalization. Urban Revolution = city–hinterland network + cumulative social differentiation (ramp) more than style/monumentality plateaus (step). Parallel careers to statehood in Mesopotamia and central Mexico under [[multilinear-evolution]]; towns are "gross outer manifestations" of institutional process. Links: [[urban-revolution]], [[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]].

### Deep-time urbanization — Trigger (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|UEC]]*) argues urban form **tracks polity type**: [[city-state]] systems show higher urban density with farmers often city-resident and multi-function centres; [[territorial-state]] systems show dispersed rural majorities and elite/administrative enclaves (Egypt, Inka). Urbanism is not a single unilinear package (contra Sjoberg preindustrial city — which Trigger’s 1959 undergraduate paper already rejected). Links: [[early-civilizations-city-vs-territorial-states]], [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]], [[early-civilization]].

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017) separates **wetland/proto-urban concentration** from **state-scale grain urbanism** and treats walls as both defense and confinement. All claims **attributed**. Links: [[sedentism]], [[state-formation]], [[city-state]], [[grain-hypothesis]], [[domus-complex]].

**Wetland towns before (and without) grain monopoly.** Scott argues sedentism and the first towns were typically products of **wetland abundance**, not effects of irrigation and states. Southern Mesopotamia shows sedentary populations and towns of up to ~5,000 with little or no cereal agriculture; permanent proto-urban settlements emerge in the southern alluvium wetlands near the Persian Gulf ~6500 BCE — late for year-round settlement and domesticated cereals, yet later hosts of the first statelets (Eridu, Ur, Umma, Uruk). Population concentration must be distinguished from state making: wetlands abundance could yield incipient urbanism and commerce without large-scale grain growing; the state module needs concentrated **fixed-field agricultural** manpower, not merely dense affluent population. Waterborne trade advantage amplifies exchange (Diocletian wheat price doubling after fifty miles; firewood market limit ~15 km) — wetlands may be necessary for early sedentism, but large kingdoms/trading centers depend on positioning for water transport.

**Aridity huddle and state-scale urbanism.** Following Nissen, Scott presents ~3500–2500 BCE aridity (sea-level and Euphrates-volume decline) as driving demographic **concentration** on remaining watercourses, more labor-intensive irrigation, and “urban” huddling that enabled state domination of grain-and-manpower modules. State-scale populations (~4–10× pre-state wetland towns of ~5,000) enlarge disease reservoirs and transmission relative to the Neolithic grain-and-animal complex alone. Uruk as likely largest city of its time (estimates 25,000–50,000; population tripling in ~200 years unlikely from natural growth alone; bas-reliefs of shackled prisoners as population augmentation; walls enclosing ~250 ha) becomes the pioneer form replicated by roughly twenty peer [[city-state|city-states]] on the alluvium.

**Walls.** By mid-third millennium BCE most Mesopotamian alluvium towns were walled. A wall, Scott argues, is an infallible proxy for permanent cultivation and food storage — something valuable protected **or held away** from those outside. Conquerors commonly tore down defeated walls; when walls permanently breached, permanent cultivation likely disappeared. Walls also confine taxpaying cultivators (Lattimore; anti-Amorite walls defining limits of political control) — protection *and* confinement.

**Ruralization after the apex.** Scott reports that ~1800–700 BCE Mesopotamian settlements covered less than a quarter of previous area and urban settlements were only one-sixteenth as frequent as the prior millennium — ruralization rather than urbanization for more than a thousand years after Ur III (pastoralist incursions among debated causes). Complements Adams’s long-run rural–urban balance and Yoffee’s ruralization-with-urbanism without equating urban decline with civilizational death ([[societal-collapse]]).

### Roux — village-to-city transition in Mesopotamian prehistory (1992, attributed)

[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] frames the emergence of Sumerian civilization as a slow, indigenous "mixed product... shaped by the mould into which its components were poured over many years," rather than an import (attributed editorial framing). Two claims are load-bearing for this page:

**Temple-centered nucleation.** Across [[ubaid-culture|Ubaid-period]] sites, temples — not palaces — were consistently the largest and best-built structures, leading Roux to argue that Sumerian cities "grew not around a palace or a castle, but around a shrine," with the temple already functioning as economic and social hub before any city proper existed. Roux explicitly hedges this: "it would be bold... to speak of 'Sumerians'" at this early stage, though he holds there is "strong reason to believe the Ubaid period represents the first stage in the development of Sumerian civilization" (attributed).

**Uruk-period nucleation and a conjectural desiccation model.** By the [[uruk-culture|Uruk period]] (c. 3750–3150 BCE), Roux describes Uruk itself growing into a "huge metropolis" of 400+ hectares with an estimated 40,000–50,000 inhabitants — his own archaeological/demographic estimate, not a census figure. He offers a hedged, explicitly conjectural causal model for the underlying village-to-city consolidation: mid-4th-millennium climatic desiccation (a formerly warmer, more humid Near East growing cooler and drier) pushed dry-farming populations from northern Mesopotamia into the irrigated lower Euphrates valley, detected archaeologically via "a sharp increase in the number of village-size settlements." Roux is explicit that "all such 'explanations' are largely conjectural and we shall probably never know what really happened" — this desiccation-driven consolidation model should be treated strictly as Roux's own attributed hypothesis, not a wiki-voice account of Mesopotamian urbanization's cause, and set alongside Scott's wetland-abundance account and Adams's rural–urban balance model above rather than merged with either.

### Postgate (*Early Mesopotamia*, 1994): house size, craft quarters, and the desert-edge triad

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] adds household-scale and
settlement-pattern evidence to the urbanization picture (attributed throughout).
**House-size shrinkage.** Several "town houses" at Fara and Abu Salabikh (third
millennium BCE) exceed 400 sq m, while average Old Babylonian houses at [[ur]] fall
under 100 sq m — a marked reduction over time whose cause Postgate treats as unclear:
candidates include urban space pressure (though the frequency of "waste plot" sale
documents argues against pure land shortage), increased social stratification shifting
wealth toward the palace sector, or long-run economic impoverishment of the urban
population — though since raw building materials were inexpensive, economic decline
alone does not obviously explain smaller houses. Postgate tentatively favors a shift
in residential/household structure (see [[household]]) as the more likely explanation,
while noting this cannot yet be confirmed archaeologically.

**The craft-quarter question (unresolved).** Whether Mesopotamian cities had discrete
"craft quarters" is poorly evidenced; the intuitive assumption — that craft skill
passed father to son with families staying together spatially — is "natural" but not
well supported. Partial support exists: at Old Babylonian Ur, merchants occupied the
south-east and some temple personnel lived near the main temple; at Nippur, a "scribal
quarter" is inferred from the concentration of literary tablets found there. Clinker
concentrations at Warka (pottery) and Abu Salabikh (a pottery-production zone within
the wall) are cited, but Postgate notes such concentrations do not necessarily prove
institutional (temple or palace) control of craft — private-sector clustering is
equally possible in comparable recent cities.

**Population estimation.** Estimating a Mesopotamian city's population is very
difficult; texts are too vague or sector-specific (military rolls, temple personnel)
to extrapolate citywide totals. The standard top-down method (Adams and others) —
100–400 persons per hectare, derived from recent Near Eastern town statistics — is
flagged by Postgate as methodologically circular and too wide a range to be useful. An
alternative bottom-up approach from house size and density at Abu Salabikh yields, at 5
persons/house × 40 houses/ha over roughly 10 ha walled area, about 2,000 population;
at 10 persons/house, about 4,000; a 10 sq m roofed-space-per-person ethnographic ratio
is also cited but flagged as not uniformly applicable, since larger houses had
proportionally more storage space.

**The city–countryside–desert triad and sedentarization.** Desert nomads — the
Amorites in the early second millennium, preceded and succeeded historically by other
Semitic-speaking tribal groups (Aramaeans emerging c. 1400 BCE, later Arabs) —
maintained an economically dependent but administratively autonomous relationship with
the settled population, described in recent scholarship as "enclosed nomadism."
Nomadic groups sedentarized gradually via intermediate stages rather than a simple
"nomad-to-settled" wave: Mari letters show parts of a single tribe or village fixing a
winter residence and cultivating fields while other members continued seasonal herding
migration along recognized routes (*nawûm* encampments with defined grazing rights).
Amorites integrated into Ur III sedentary society particularly at Lagash and Umma; a
common route to full settlement was granting land plots to soldiers or mercenaries
recruited from nomadic groups. Political disruption from incoming nomadic groups
(Amorites in the late third millennium; Aramaeans roughly a thousand years later) is
associated with widespread abandonment of rural settlements, though each incoming group
was eventually absorbed into, or became indistinguishable from, the existing
population.

## Kemp — Egyptian towns and Amarna (2018)

*(Attributed — [[kemp-barry-j]], *[[kemp-ancient-egypt-2018]]* Chs.5, 8.)*

Contrasts **prescriptive planned communities** ([[kahun|Kahun]] grid, pyramid towns, Nubian forts) with **self-organized urban growth** at [[amarna|Amarna]], where hierarchy is expressed more in house size gradients than in a single imposed residential grid. New Kingdom cities live in the shadow of fortress-walled temple precincts and processional avenues. Amarna population order-of-magnitude in Kemp’s discussion is urban tens of thousands (claims extract ~20k class estimates — treat as attributed approximation). Method: house archaeology and desert-edge plan legibility due to short occupation.

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop (2016)]] frames the Uruk-period first-cities horizon as "the Uruk phenomenon" — the earliest urbanization event anchored archaeologically at [[uruk|Uruk]] itself and textually by the earliest known writing system (see [[uruk-culture]], [[origins-of-writing]]). He explicitly flags the inherited Childean "urban revolution" label as potentially misleading terminology for what was in fact a process spanning roughly a millennium. He records **Debate 2.1 ("Why cities?")** as a live, multi-position scholarly dispute over the drivers of urbanization and state formation, without adjudicating: Adams (1966) on ecological diversity and exchange; Pournelle (2013) on the marshland resource base; Wright & Johnson (1975) on bureaucratic/administrative regulation; Algaze (2005, 2008) on long-distance trade-route control; Pollock (1999) on cities as instruments of elite domination; Charvát (2002) on a more egalitarian counter-reading of rationing as social service; and Yoffee (2005) urging caution against treating urbanism as an inevitable complexity outcome. See [[uruk-culture]] for the fuller treatment of this debate. (as of [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]])


## Muslim cities and settlements (Insoll 1999)

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]*, Ch. 7.)* Orientalist “Islamic city” models (Marçais–von Grunebaum isnad via Abu-Lughod) over-generalized Maghrib/Near Eastern medina packages worldwide. Insoll rejects both uniform stereotypes and pure-diversity denial of common patterns (**mix-and-match**). Islam is not only urban (Lapidus); villages and camps can be Muslim settlements. **Minimum archaeological recognition:** mosque + Muslim burials. Created (*amsar*) vs adapted antiquity cities; Bacharach’s three power-locus phases (mosque/admin core → palace distance → citadel). Qsar es-Seghir Muslim→Portuguese and Ottoman Crete reverse cases show spatial systems are archaeologically distinguishable. Research bias privileges cities over towns/villages/camps. Links [[islamic-archaeology]], [[mosque]], [[household]], [[global-city]].

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed — [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]], mainly Sinclair Ch. 47; MacDonald, Ogundiran, LaViolette, Pikirayi, Reid, Leone & Moussa; ranges 8–10.)*

### Trait lists, indigenous towns, and operational urbanism (Sinclair)

Paul Sinclair argues that uncritical **Near Eastern trait lists** yielded few early “jackpot” African urban examples and enabled derogatory evolutionist comparisons. Fieldwork has established **indigenous African urban origins** at Mali ([[jenne-jeno]]—McIntosh & McIntosh 1980) and the [[swahili-culture|Swahili]] coast, and balanced appreciation of [[zimbabwe-culture|Zimbabwe Culture]]. Rare full continental syntheses include Connah 2001/2005 and Coquery-Vidrovitch 2005.

**Fletcher (1995, 1998)** interaction/communication settlement-size thresholds (as highlighted by Sinclair): key upper limits ~1–2 ha (mobile→sedentary), ~100 ha (agricultural→urban), ~10,000 ha (large agglomerations)—upper limits only; many African agricultural towns were <100 ha.

**Heterarchy and anti-linear evolutionism.** Questioning top-down/bottom-up dichotomies led to hierarchical *and* lateral ([[heterarchy|heterarchical]]) organization—especially Inland Niger Delta (McIntosh). Cities must not be understood only hierarchically (Sinclair; cf. Crumley, Tainter, resilience).

**Operational checklist (Sinclair):** multiscalar socio-environmental integration; cities embedded in countrysides, ecosystems, settlement and trade networks; hierarchical *and* heterarchical forms; not Africa-only isolation.

**Regional criteria differ:** Andah (West Africa: shrine/mosque, palace, market, military/defensive, building styles); Fattovich (NE: size + functional diversity + monumental plan/central authority); de Maret/de Heusch kingship/ritual as population attractors in west-central Africa. Coquery-Vidrovitch’s dense-population + cultural-diffusion definition still misses craft specialization, non-productive elites, and ideological centralizers (Sinclair critique).

### Nile earliest urbanism; Kerma “first sub-Saharan urban settlement”

Nile Valley saw Africa’s earliest urbanism; Sjöberg-type doubts overturned. Southward frame: Qasr Ibrim long duration; **[[kerma|Kerma]]** excavations give “sub-Saharan Africa’s first urban settlement” (Bonnet 1990 via Sinclair); later Napata then [[meroe|Meroe]] as Kushite capitals. Sahel savannahs highly favorable for complex societies (Connah via Sinclair); Jenné-jeno **pre-dates Muslim North African trade** and may have been **non-hierarchical** (McIntosh 1999).

### Regional urban sketches (Sinclair survey + later chapters)

- **Guinean zone:** earthworks (Benin, Old Oyo, Ife); forest–savannah trade towns (Begho, Bono Manso).
- **Ethiopia:** mobile medieval kingship vs under-excavated Islamic towns; [[aksum|Aksum]] urban/palace/stelae complex.
- **Great Lakes/Central Africa:** towns largely independent of transcontinental trade—Ntusi ~100 ha by 15th c. on cattle patronage; Buganda capitals highly mobile; Kongo Mbanza Kongo almost unexplored; **archaeologically nearly invisible** reed/wood capitals (Reid).
- **Swahili:** stone towns once misread as Middle Eastern colonial implants (**now rejected**); bias to elite stone quarters being redressed.
- **Zimbabwe Plateau:** stone-walled elite enclosures—[[great-zimbabwe|Great Zimbabwe]] apex of multi-tier hierarchy with [[mapungubwe|Mapungubwe]] predecessor and Torwa/Mutapa successors at state-level complexity (inter-site spatial analyses).
- **Copperbelt:** heterarchical short-lived chiefly settlements.
- **Rainforest belt (Ogundiran):** delayed nucleation until late 1st millennium AD (soils, clearing costs, malaria); community-building vs authority-building town goals; 18th-c. Bight of Benin/Gold Coast hinterlands “the most urbanized landscape in Africa” (attributed process mix with Atlantic trade as intensifier).
- **North Africa:** Numidian/Mauritanian monumental mausolea and pre-Roman urban planning; Garamantian urban Garama; Roman Maghreb economy; progressive transformation ending in 10th-c. abandonment of almost all ancient cities (Leone & Moussa).
- **Middle Niger types (MacDonald & Camara):** *Fadugu* (state-power, usually transitory) vs *Markadugu* (commercial/spiritual towns like Dia and Jenné-jeno—may endure millennia semi-autonomously).

**Plural urban definitions** (R9-060): rank-size + specialist satellites; settlement hierarchy without craft diversity (Tichitt); multi-ethnic quarters (Begho); divine-kingship town ideology (Ile-Ife); earthwork nucleations; Roman grid vs indigenous irregular plans; invisible Great Lakes capitals. No single trait list travels intact.

Non-elite sectors of major settlements still under-studied (Swahili, Zimbabwe Culture, Tswana towns)—population estimates imprecise; simultaneous/year-round occupation uncertain (Sinclair; Fletcher mobility caveats).

*Source: [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]].*

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed throughout — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.]]* 1988. Settlement geography + regional survey synthesis; not a modern urban-sociology account. Complements Adams Heartland, Scott’s aridity-huddle reading of Nissen, and Van De Mieroop’s Uruk phenomenon without collapsing them.)*

**Village / town / city / regional-state hierarchy.** Nissen maps ordinary English terms onto a [[settlement-hierarchy|settlement-system]] ladder: **village** (lowest organizational level), **town** (middle-level settlement), **city** (level of organization below the state), and **(regional) state** (highest). A local center heads a simple system; a regional center is the focal point of a larger multi-system unit. He prefers the more stable pair **center–surroundings** over village/city/state when describing mutual dependence: surroundings depend on central functions (temples, warehouses, administration/leadership); the center requires tribute/taxes; once surroundings form settlements, the place is a “center of the first order” / simple two-tier system, with spatial limits set by transport and client capacity.

**Multi-tier settlement systems and plain size.** Nissen theorizes a direct relationship between the size of the area available for settlement and organizational level: a simple system that fills a plain cannot expand to higher stages within that plain alone; larger plains allow parallel first-order systems, competition among first-order centers, and second- then third-/fourth-order systems — all of which he holds are attested in the ancient Near East. His fig. 11 typology: isolated settlements in narrow valleys → simple system on a small plain → three-tiered and four-tiered systems on larger plains. Ecological-unit size alone, he notes, is insufficient (northern Mesopotamian/Syrian plains did not simply replicate Susiana’s path).

**From isolated settlement to town (ca. 6000–3200 B.C.).** Chapter 3 frames the path as both densification toward towns and the beginning of regional differentiation. Contiguous settlements on small plains, yield intensification (including irrigation packing), and craft specialization (autonomy of occupations scales with catchment size) set the stage. By Late Susiana, Behbehan-type plains show simple two-tier systems (e.g. Tepe Sohz ~12 ha center with 2–3 ha satellites); Susiana develops multilevel/three-tier systems with Susa-scale centers while contemporary Lower Mesopotamia still shows mainly isolated settlements. Mid-4th-millennium climatic change (cooler/dryer; Gulf sea level ~3 m higher at onset of change, per Nissen’s reading of Meteor/Nützel Gulf sediments) then opens Babylonia to denser settlement once high water recedes.

**Uruk growth and “early high civilization.”** For the Late Uruk period Nissen designates the beginning of early high civilization: in the intensively surveyed Uruk hinterland (drawing on Adams & Nissen, *The Uruk Countryside*, 1972), settlement numbers jump more than tenfold and aggregate settlement area from ~60 to ~440 ha; instead of a three-tier pattern he argues a **four-tier hierarchy headed by Uruk**. Babylonia, he claims, moved with no apparent transition from individual settlements to the most complex organization yet seen. Uruk’s settled area grew into Early Dynastic I, when a city wall enclosed ~5.5 km², with sherd scatter extending 2–3 km beyond the northeastern wall (minimum extents). He compares sizes (attributed figures): classical Athens after Themistocles ~2.5 km² (not half Uruk); Hadrianic Rome only about twice Uruk three thousand years earlier. Between Late Uruk and ED I, absolute settlement numbers fall while average size and overall inhabited area rise — clusters consolidate into larger centers (cluster-to-center mechanism). By later Early Dynastic, in the Uruk hinterland, settlement count falls further (62 → 29 vs ED I) while average size rises to ~38 ha; roughly nine-tenths of population lives in sites >30 ha; small rural settlements almost cease.

**Babylonia as trailblazer (POSITION).** Nissen argues that in the most momentous phase — **from city to regional state** — Babylonia was the trailblazer; to deny this would deny the driving impulse and special peculiarities of ancient Near Eastern history, so Babylonia receives relatively large space in his account. He rejects both the older imbalance that made southern Babylonia seem the natural center of the entire Near East for too long *and* the opposite extreme that all regional developments were equally important for universally valid political forms. High productivity + density under irrigation, he holds, posed problems whose solution became “early high civilization”; conflict-regulation under dense co-residence mattered more than pure water-administration (he rejects the older thesis that early large feeder-canal administration drove the rise of Lower Mesopotamia).

**Neolithic “towns” as non-trajectory outliers.** Nissen treats [[jericho|Jericho]] and (to a limited extent) [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] as early “town-like” extremes with unusual stratification and compact building for the Neolithic — Jericho’s wall requiring collective labor, blocking density relief by expansion, and sharpening insider/outsider difference — but **not** as centers of settlement systems. Jericho was a river oasis without neighboring dependent settlements. Further development toward higher political organization, he argues, did **not** lead via Jericho, Çatalhöyük, or similar extremes; it took the slow route through structured inter-settlement relations and multi-tier settlement systems. After a temporal gap Jericho reverts to ordinary settlement character; later true urban centrality appears only when external “town-like” traits couple with real centrality in a traceable developmental line. Extremes of a spectrum, in his view, rarely drive further development.

## Land Behind Baghdad densification (Adams 1965)

*(Attributed — [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-1965]]; study [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-study]].)* From the Diyala sequence Adams argued: true cities (vs. walled towns of ~30 ha / ~5,000 people) appear only in the Seleucid-Parthian period, associated with Hellenistic city-founding (Artemita ~1.5 sq km, est. 20,000+); Sasanian and Abbasid capitals were "consciously constructed as capitals rather than assuming that status as a result of prior economic or political pre-eminence" — cities as "creatures of state policy"; peak urbanization (metropolitan Baghdad/Samarra) came "not as a concomitant of the greatest intensity of land usage but as the sequel to a decline in provincial settlement" — the capital drawing off the hinterland; and urbanism in the basin was "a cumulative rather than a merely cyclical phenomenon," Baghdad's persistence as civilizational anchor distinguishing the medieval collapse from earlier ones. For the ancient propensity to town-dwelling he offered a defensive hypothesis: much of it "arose from the defensive advantages which larger settlements conferred," inferred from modern dispersal into hamlets under post-WWI pacification.

## Lehner densification (1997)

*(Attributed throughout — [[lehner-mark]], *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* 1997. Pyramid towns and Giza settlement landscape. Complements Kemp Egyptian towns/Amarna section above.)*

### Pyramid town as permanent complex element

Besides pyramid, temples, and causeway designed for eternity, each pyramid had a **pyramid town**, usually at the foot of the plateau, where priests, officials, guards, and support personnel lived while the pyramid functioned as a ritual centre — raising whether this was separate from temporary accommodation for the building workforce. Named examples: Djed Sneferu (Meidum); northern/southern Kha Sneferu (Dahshur double town); at [[giza]], northern **Gerget** (“Settlement”) of Khufu and southern **Tjeniu** (“boundary mark”/“cultivation edge”) of Khafre (possibly south of Khafre’s valley temple and the Wall of the Crow). Administration until 5th dynasty by second-generation princes; later by middle/lower officials with tax-exempt residence.

### Illahun / Kahun (Hetep-Senwosret)

Senwosret II’s pyramid town at Illahun ([[kahun]]): footprint is “one of the basic documents for the study of the history of Egyptian urbanism.” Estimated several thousand inhabitants — 220 small houses west/south; 9–10 substantial urban estates northeast for senior officials. [[kemp-barry-j|Kemp]] compared large households to Meketre tomb models; collectively large granaries could store grain to feed **5,000–9,000** people annually; small-house inhabitants “must have worked for and in the large households in return for their sustenance.” Arnold’s ~5,000 workforce for Amenemhet III Dahshur mudbrick pyramid is the same order of magnitude. Ahmose’s Abydos town shows mirror-image large houses that Lehner compares to Illahun administrative function.

### Giza: from “sacred slum” to “proto-city”

- **Menkaure valley temple:** Reisner’s agglomeration of small houses invading the temple — tax-exempt town expands disorganizedly (“sacred slum”) while cult continues; classic model for OK pyramid towns, though Lehner argues estate processing must have been far larger.
- **Khentkawes town:** among oldest planned urban structures in Egypt — 10 modular houses along causeway; southern L-extension for administration / token royal residence; merges with Menkaure pyramid-town front.
- **AMBRIC / modern-city trenches (late 1980s–1990s):** OK settlement under modern Giza far beyond the plateau — Khufu causeway foundation, basalt pavement (likely valley temple), continuous mudbrick buildings with everyday pottery, bread moulds, bone, grinding stones — perhaps Gerget Khufu. 1994 wall 500 m east of valley temple possibly flood-basin/harbour border. Lehner: more than a planned-house cluster that became a slum — “a sort of **proto-city** — downtown Egypt during Giza’s heyday.”
- **[[heit-el-ghurab]] (Area A):** 1988–89+ excavations of bakeries, galleries, possible *wabet*-related storage; Wall of the Crow as possible necropolis entrance; workforce settlement scale consistent with 20–25k infrastructure totals (Lehner models).
- **Khafre “barracks” galleries:** Petrie (1881) labelled workmen’s barracks; Lehner finds little residential refuse — more like NK temple comb galleries / workshops (Kemp bakery analogy at Amarna).

### Capital mobility and settlement scale

OK “capital” ≈ mobile royal residence near the [[pyramid-complex]] under construction ([[memphis]] zone; Giza/Abusir successive use). Valley-floor living-economy elements of complexes largely lost to mudbrick and modern expansion — recovered remains rewrite the urban map of the pyramid age. Extensive OK settlement at Giza may have covered ~200 ha (494 acres) first revealed when AMBRIC sewage works hit remains (1989).

## McIntosh 2005 — Middle Niger clustered urbanism

*(Attributed — [[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005]].)*

Defines early Middle Niger cities as indigenous, large, craft-specialized, and **heterarchical** — [[urban-cluster|Urban Clusters]] without citadels — emerging by the first millennium BCE–CE via [[self-organizing-landscape|self-organizing]] specialist accommodation ([[pulse-model]]).
