# Structural Adjustment

## Definition and Scope

**Structural adjustment** refers to the packages of macroeconomic and institutional reforms — fiscal austerity, subsidy and price decontrol, trade liberalization, currency devaluation, and state-enterprise divestiture — attached as **conditionality** to World Bank and IMF lending from roughly 1980, especially across the global South and sub-Saharan Africa. In Mozambique the turn came with the mid-1980s adoption of such measures amid command-economy erosion and civil war (see [[pitcher-transforming-mozambique-2002|Pitcher 2002]]).

## Measurement

See `measured_by`. Pitcher (following B. Hibou) treats the **World Bank as an ideal-type** and reads its policy through its own publications; compliance and "success" metrics are, on her account, partly ideological constructions.

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed.)* Neo-liberal literature credits adjustment with correcting parastatal inefficiency; critics document social costs and eroded sovereignty. Pitcher's contribution is to show that adjustment and the **[[privatization]]** it mandated were mediated by domestic political forces and did not simply shrink the **[[the-state|state]]** — see **[[transformative-preservation]]**.

**Mozambique specifics (attributed to Pitcher).** Adjustment arrived as the **Economic Rehabilitation Program (PRE, 1987)**, freeing agricultural prices and encouraging private/family-sector marketing; after inflation of 300–600% and strikes, a social component was added as the **PRES (1989)**. Pitcher's sharpest case is **cashew**: a World-Bank-commissioned study drove raw-cashew export tariffs down (30%→20%→14%, 1995–97) and ended the local-supply-first rule; within two years most processing factories had closed as raw nuts were exported to India, throwing an estimated 3,000–10,000 workers out of work — the World Bank's predicted producer-price gains never materialized because wholesale trade stayed oligopolistic. She calls it "a tragedy and a travesty."

## Explanatory Theories

*(Side by side.)* **Neo-liberal development economics** (state-led models failed; roll back the state); **dependency/globalization critique** (imposed neo-colonial conditionality); **state-in-society / comparative-historical** readings (domestic mediation of external prescriptions).

## Health effects (attributed to Farmer, *Pathologies of Power*, 2003)

[[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]] read adjustment and allied neoliberal reform as a driver of
[[structural-violence|structural violence]] and disease among the poor, across three cases. In
**Chiapas**, he tied the collapse of the peasant economy to the withdrawal of corn and coffee subsidies
and the 1994 NAFTA opening (the [[phenomena/tuberculosis|TB]]-ridden Zapatista base), timing the 1994
uprising to NAFTA's entry into force. In **Russia**, he linked the post-Soviet "shock therapy" sell-off
of state assets (with capital flight he cited at >$130 billion since 1993) to the simultaneous collapse
of the TB-control and health infrastructure and the mass incarceration Loïc Wacquant called "the
police-and-prison management of poverty." In **Haiti**, he treated the 2000–02 U.S.-driven aid embargo
and blocked Inter-American Development Bank loans as sanctions falling on "the most vulnerable
population in the hemisphere." Farmer quoted legal scholars Steiner and Alston that privatization,
deregulation, and adjustment "have had mixed, and sometimes seriously adverse, effects on the enjoyment
of economic and social rights" (attributed).

## Interaction

The immediate driver of much 1980s–90s **[[privatization]]**; entangled with **[[post-socialist-transition]]** in African socialist states and with **[[globalization]]**.

## Debates

Efficacy and distributional justice of adjustment; how much conditionality determines versus merely constrains domestic policy — Pitcher argues for constraint, not determination.
