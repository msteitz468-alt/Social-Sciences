# Post-Socialist Transition

## Definition and Scope

**Post-socialist transition** names the passage of formerly socialist command economies and one-party states toward market economies and (often) electoral democracy, most visibly in Eastern Europe and the former USSR after 1989–91, but also in African socialist states such as **[[mozambique|Mozambique]]** from the mid-1980s. [[pitcher-transforming-mozambique-2002|Pitcher (2002)]] notes that the comparative literature largely privileged Eastern Europe and Latin America and **overlooked** Mozambique and Lusophone Africa.

## Measurement

See `measured_by`. A key methodological dispute (attributed to Stark and Bruszt, adopted by Pitcher) is whether "transition" — implying movement from one determinate order to another — is even the right frame, versus **"transformation"**: rearrangement, reconfiguration, and recombination of existing social logics.

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed.)* Against the "revolutionary break" reading, Pitcher and the recombination theorists emphasize **path dependency** and the persistence of prior structures and networks. Pitcher's **[[transformative-preservation|transformative preservation]]** identifies the case where an incumbent ruling party survives and turns the transition to its own account.

**Mozambique specifics (attributed to Pitcher).** She adopts Ákos Réna-Tas's two-period model — **erosion** of the socialist economy from below by "self-interested individuals pursuing private gain" (Mozambique ~1983–90), then state-driven **transition** to capitalism from above (post-1990) — and stresses **elite recombination**: command-economy elites joined pre-independence elites to form and manage firms, and Frelimo *kept power* through the 1994 and 1999 multiparty elections, unlike the discredited regimes of Eastern Europe (a contrast she likens instead to reforming one-party states in Vietnam and China).

## Explanatory Theories

*(Side by side.)* **Neo-liberal** models expect elite turnover and state contraction; **recombination**/comparative-historical models (Stark, Verdery, Pitcher) expect continuity, hybrid forms, and "somewhere between plan and market" outcomes.

## Interaction

Overlaps **[[privatization]]**, **[[structural-adjustment]]**, and **[[globalization]]**; a special reconfiguration of the **[[the-state|state]]**.

## Debates

"Transition" vs. "transformation"; revolutionary break vs. path-dependent recombination; the neglect of African post-socialist cases in the comparative literature.
