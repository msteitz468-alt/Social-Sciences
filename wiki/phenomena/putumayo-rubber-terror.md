# Putumayo Rubber Terror

## Definition and Scope

The system of forced rubber extraction, torture, and mass killing in the Putumayo region (Colombia–Peru Amazon borderlands) during the Amazon rubber boom, especially under Julio César Arana’s enterprises and the British–Peruvian **Peruvian Amazon Company** (from 1907) / Casa Arana. Stations named in the documentation Taussig synthesizes include El Encanto, Último Retiro, Matanzas, and others along the Igaraparaná, Caraparaná, and related rivers.

[[taussig-michael|Taussig]] (1987) analyzes the episode not only as economic exploitation but as a [[culture-of-terror|culture of terror]] in a [[space-of-death|space of death]], organized through [[epistemic-murk|epistemic murk]] and a colonial [[mimesis|mimesis]] of savagery — the **colonial mirror of production**.

Order-of-magnitude death figures circulated in the scandal literature (including a much-cited coupling of tens of thousands dead with thousands of tons of rubber — treat as **period claim**, not wiki-voice census). Exact demography remains contested in later historiography (not fully ingested here).

## Patterns and Findings

**Wiki-voice (convergent documentary):** A rubber extraction regime in the Putumayo used debt advances, forced delivery, corporal punishment (including stocks), and lethal violence against Indigenous laborers and surrounding populations during the boom years; British parliamentary and consular processes investigated after 1909 press exposure.

**Attributed (Taussig 1987):**

- Terror was mediated by narration: horror of jungle + horror of savagery interlaced.
- Debt-peonage “phraseology of conquest” (*conquistar*, selling of debts as persons) staged capitalist theatricality with slavery’s force.
- *Muchachos* (Indian company police) were central instruments — colonial hierarchy inside the labor of terror.
- Sport torture and exemplary cruelty were not aberrations but ritualized art forms drawing on “civilized” values inverted.
- Colonial employees feared and fictionalized Indian attack, then mirrored that fiction in their own violence (**colonial mirror**).
- Even critical reportage could remain complicit with colonizing imaginative rituals.

## Interaction

- **Memory in healing.** Highland–lowland [[yage|yagé]] song and history-as-sorcery mobilize rubber-era and conquest images ([[implicit-social-knowledge]]).
- **Capuchin missions.** Road-building, exorcism, and “civilizing” projects intertwine with post-rubber frontier (Taussig Part Two).
- **Broader Amazon rubber complex.** Congo analogies (Casement’s prior career; Conrad) structure metropolitan reception.

## Debates

- **Source reliability and representation.** How far Casement/Hardenburg aestheticize or understate; FO editing; “fantastic credibility.”
- **Scale of mortality.** Period figures vs later demographic work (collection gap).
- **Agency.** Company managers (Normand, Fonseca, Loayza, Torres, etc. as named in sources), Barbadian employees, *muchachos*, Colombian vs Peruvian frontier politics.
- **Anthropology’s colonial entanglement.** [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] — Whiffen as ethnologist who omitted terror.
