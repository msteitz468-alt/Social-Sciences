# Emerging Infectious Diseases

## Definition and Scope

"Emerging infectious diseases" (EID) is the analytic category — a rapidly institutionalized field of the
1990s (its own journal, conferences, and funding) — for infections that are genuinely new (AIDS,
Brazilian purpuric fever), old diseases with newly identified agents or dramatic outbreaks (Lyme disease,
Ebola/hemorrhagic fevers), or old diseases changed in pathogenicity or distribution (multidrug-resistant
[[tuberculosis]], invasive Group A strep). This page treats EID as [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]] treats it
in *[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999): as much a *framework* to
be criticized as a set of diseases. Chapter 2 offers the book's central methodological reframe.

## Measurement

The field's signature instrument is the **catalog of "factors of emergence."** The Institute of Medicine's
influential report classifies threats not by agent but by factor: "human demographics and behavior;
technology and industry; economic development and land use; international travel and commerce; microbial
adaptation and change; and breakdown of public health measures." Farmer's critique (attributed) begins
here: **neither poverty nor social inequality appears anywhere in the IOM's list of causes of emergence.**

## Patterns and Findings

Farmer's argument is that dominant EID analysis systematically obscures the social patterning of disease:

- **The nation-state is the wrong unit.** Organisms — dengue, cholera O139, HIV, MDR-TB, hepatitis B —
  "disregard political boundaries"; most nations are 20th-century creations.
- **"Tropical medicine" and "health transitions" mislead.** Malaria was once endemic across the temperate
  U.S. (Farmer cites Drake's 1850 Ohio Valley study; ~1 million Civil War cases); its retreat tracked
  agricultural development and rising living standards, not just antimalarials. The "health transitions"
  model — a tidy shift from infectious to chronic mortality as nations "develop" — masks within-nation
  inequality: in 1990 only 8% of deaths among the world's richest fifth were from infections, versus 56%
  among the poorest fifth; Harlem's age-specific mortality exceeded Bangladesh's.
- **Media consciousness, not epidemiology, sets salience.** The 1976 Zaïre Ebola outbreak (318 cases,
  traced to unsterile syringes and failed contact precautions at Yambuku Mission Hospital) was
  statistically minor within Zaïre's fatal-infection toll but became globally salient — while
  tuberculosis, "the world's leading infectious cause of death," went ignored.

## Explanatory Theories

Farmer calls (attributed) for models of disease emergence that are "dynamic, systemic, and critical" — a
**[[biosocial-analysis]]** that links rapidly changing molecular phenomena to large-scale, often
transnational, social forces, and a "critical epistemology" that asks "what features of disease emergence
are obscured by dominant analytic frameworks." He proposes four corollary research areas: EID and social
inequalities; EID in transnational perspective; EID and the dynamics of change; and EID and critical
epistemology (interrogating who counts as a "risk group" versus an "individual at risk"). He quotes
McMichael's image of an epidemiology "oriented to explaining and quantifying the bobbing of corks on the
surface waters, while largely disregarding the stronger undercurrents."

## Interaction

The EID reframe is the umbrella over Farmer's disease-specific chapters on [[tuberculosis]] and
[[hiv-aids]], and it connects to [[globalization]] and [[world-systems-analysis]] (transnational
transmission) and to [[structural-violence]] (inequality as an uncatalogued "factor of emergence").

## Debates

Whether social inequality belongs in the causal model of disease emergence — and whether the EID
literature's "desocialized" accounts constitute [[immodest-claims-of-causality|immodest claims of
