# The Money Economy

> **Scope note.** This page began as the money economy theorized in [[simmel-georg|Simmel]]'s *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* (1900) — a philosophical-sociological analysis, attributed voice throughout. It is not a treatise on monetary economics (Simmel: "Not a single line of these investigations is meant to be a statement about economics"). A second layer now records [[zelizer-viviana|Zelizer]]'s historical-sociological challenge (*[[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-1994|The Social Meaning of Money]]*, 1994): modern monies as [[multiple-monies|multiple]] and [[earmarking|earmarked]], not a single colorless medium. Marx/*Capital* and further monetary sociology continue to await fuller layering.

## Definition and Scope

The **money economy** is the social order in which [[exchange]] is universally mediated by money, and in which — for [[simmel-georg|Simmel]] — money becomes far more than an economic tool: it is "the reified function of being exchanged," "the autonomous expression" of the exchange relationship, and thereby a force reshaping freedom, personality, culture, and the inner rhythm of modern life. Simmel's premise is that money, though "simply the means to acquire objects," becomes "one of the great cultural elements" whose formal properties — pure quantity, indifference to content, universal convertibility — impress themselves on every domain it touches.

## Measurement

| Measure | Notes (per Simmel) |
|---|---|
| Velocity of circulation | Money's effective quantity is a multiple of its substance set by circulation, not by amount at a single moment |
| Money supply : total saleable goods | Prices reflect the proportion of the two totals; the equation operates as an "unconscious a priori" |
| Functional vs. substantial value | "The functional value of money exceeds its value as a substance the more… rapidly it circulates" |
| Substitution of money payment for obligation-in-kind | Historical index of depersonalization and individual freedom |

Simmel treats money as an "unmoved mover": gold reserves in a vault act through the mere psychological representation of money, not only through circulation.

## Patterns and Findings

**All attributed to Simmel (1900); wiki voice is not used for these interpretive claims.**

- **From substance to function.** Money "passes from the form of directness and substantiality… to the ideal form," exercising its effects "merely as an idea… embodied in a representative symbol." Its development "is a striving towards the ideal of a pure symbol of economic value which is never attained." Salt-coins are money "precisely because they are not used as salt"; money "does not have a function, but is a function." Its value rests increasingly on a guarantee by the central political community — "the core of truth in the theory that money is only a claim upon society" (*non aes sed fides*, "not bronze but trust").
- **Money as absolute means.** In the "sequence of purposes," money is "the purest reification of means" — "an absolute means," teleologically pure and impartial. Precisely because it is tied to no specific end, it "acquires a relation to the totality of purposes," and so its value can exceed that of any single object bought with it.
- **The means that becomes the end.** By a general psychological law ("one cannot promote the final purpose any better than to treat the means as if it were the end itself"), money is elevated "from its intermediary position to absolute importance" — becoming a "psychological value absolute," even, structurally, a stand-in for God as the point where "all the diverse elements and contradictions of the world find their unity" (Hans Sachs: "Money is the secular God of the World").
- **The psychology of money.** Greed and avarice are pathological absolutizations of money (the miser holds money in "timid respect," never spending); extravagance is the reverse fixation on the act of squandering; ascetic poverty (Buddhist, Franciscan — *nihil habentes, omnia possidentes*) negates money but thereby elevates it to the same absolute form. Cynicism and the **[[blase-attitude|blasé attitude]]** are money's levelling of all qualitative [[value|values]].
- **Freedom and depersonalization.** Money is "the ideal representative" of a condition that "makes possible relationships between people but leaves them personally undisturbed." The historical shift from bondage → dues-in-kind → money payment is "a magna charta of personal freedom": it multiplies the number of people one depends on while dissolving dependence on any *particular* person, since "it is not the bond as such, but being bound to a particular individual master that represents the real antipode of freedom." This freedom is largely *negative* ("modern man is free because he can sell everything, and free because he can buy everything"), and its "tax" is the instability of money wages and the rootlessness of those "liberated" by a cash payment.
- **Individualization and its double effect.** Money "atomizes" both society (isolating the differentiated individual, who holds a "latent claim" on others redeemable at will) and the person (freeing inner faculties from one another). "The enlargement of the group goes hand in hand with the individualization… of its individual members," and money is the medium of that enlargement.
- **Calculability and intellectualization.** The money economy breeds "the calculating character of modern times" — conceiving the world "as a huge arithmetical problem," teaching precision "down to the last farthing," privileging intellect as the most valued mental energy, and pushing the reduction of quality to quantity across the sciences. "With reference to money, we do not ask what and how, but how much"; money is "the most terrible destroyer of form."
- **Distance and the pace of life.** Money conquers spatial distance (a German capitalist "involved in a ministerial change in Spain") while interposing new inner and social distance; it accelerates the tempo of life (increases in money supply and, most visibly, paper-money inflation quicken turnover and agitate the lower strata even as nominal incomes rise).
- **Symbol of a relativistic world.** Money is "the historical symbol of the relative character of existence": as a token, "the most ephemeral thing in the external–practical world"; in its content, "the most stable" — embodying both flux (*actus purus*) and constancy. "The more the life of society becomes dominated by monetary relationships, the more the relativistic character of existence finds its expression in conscious life."

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Summary |
|---|---|
| **Simmel (1900)** | Money as reified [[exchange]]; its formal properties (pure quantity, indifference, convertibility) reshape freedom, culture, and inner life — a philosophy "beneath historical materialism" |
| **Marxian (contrast)** | Money and the commodity as historically specific to capitalist relations; Lukács/Mannheim fault Simmel for abstracting "money as such" from its capitalist background — not yet ingested |
| **Weberian rationalization (contrast)** | Simmel's calculability/intellectualization anticipates Weber's rationalization thesis; Weber criticized Simmel for tying the money economy and capitalism "too closely" — not yet ingested |
| **Cultural / relational sociology of money ([[zelizer-viviana|Zelizer]] 1994)** | Money multiplies: people and organizations create [[multiple-monies|multiple monies]] via [[earmarking]]; social relations transform money as money transforms relations; classical "colorless" money is an ideology, not a full description. U.S. domestic, gift, and welfare monies c. 1870–1930 as strategic cases. See [[multiple-monies-approach]]; study [[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]]. |
| **Later cultural money studies** | Dodd (monetary pluralism; foreword to Zelizer 2017); Parry & Bloch and anthropological special-purpose money (as Zelizer engages them) |

## Interaction

The money economy is bound up with the **division of labour** at its roots (specialization requires and enables a universal medium), and with the **centralization of the state** (money taxes make fixed capitals and central administration possible; debasement tracks political disintegration). It drives the [[objective-culture|preponderance of objective over subjective culture]] and the [[tragedy-of-culture|tragedy of culture]], and produces the [[blase-attitude|blasé attitude]]. Its analysis of the reduction of persons to money (wergild, bride-price, slavery, bribery, wages, prostitution) is developed in *[[simmel-philosophy-of-money-1900|The Philosophy of Money]]* ch. 5.

## Debates

- Whether money's structural effects derive from "money as such" (Simmel) or from historically specific capitalist relations (Marx, Lukács, Mannheim) — a genuine dispute; promote to a `debates/` page when *Capital* and the Simmel–Marx secondary literature are ingested.
- Whether Simmel's account is sociology or social philosophy (Durkheim's 1901 review; Weber; Spann).
- **Homogeneous vs. multiple monies:** Simmel (as reconstructed by Zelizer) treats qualitative restrictions as residual atavisms; Zelizer treats [[earmarking]] as central to advanced monetary economies and predicts new earmarks under electronic money. Positions on [[multiple-monies-approach]] and [[zelizer-social-meaning-of-money-study]]; not adjudicated in wiki voice.
- **Hostile worlds:** whether contact between money and moral/intimate domains necessarily corrupts (classical corrosion story; later cultural critics) vs. money's diverse moralities and relational matching (Zelizer afterword).

## Reflexivity / Historiography

This page rests on a single classical text of 1900; its historical vignettes (coinage history, wergild, ethnographic examples) are Simmel's illustrations, marshalled to display a *form*, not independent evidence, and several carry period-typical evolutionist framing ("primitive peoples," children equated with "primitive man"). Simmel's discussion of Jews and trading minorities and money — their "monotheistic schooling" as suiting them to a unifying economic value, their funnelling into money-dealing — is **dated, essentializing early-1900s discourse**, recorded here strictly as Simmel's attributed (1900) position and never adopted in wiki voice. The distinct antisemitism Simmel himself *faced* in the German academy is documented on [[simmel-georg]].

## Patterns — Zelizer layer (attributed)

- Monetization expands commercial reach **and** multiplies social distinctions among monies (U.S. c. 1870–1930 evidence base).
- State standardization of national currency can coincide with proliferation of earmarks (historical paradox).
- Domains classically expected to be leveled (household, gift, charity) show intense [[earmarking]] and contest.
