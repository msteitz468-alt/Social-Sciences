## Definition and Scope

The long transformation of autonomous village communities in mainland Southeast Asia
(the Red, Mekong, and Chao Phraya river systems) into ranked chiefdoms and then into the
early ritual polities Higham calls [[mandala|mandalas]] — culminating in the
[[angkor|Angkorian state]] (802–1431 CE). This page treats the process as reconstructed
from the archaeological and epigraphic record; it is not a claim that these polities were
"states" in the bounded-bureaucratic sense (a framing Higham 1989 explicitly rejects for
the region before the 19th century). The sequence spans roughly a millennium either side
of the first Indian contact.

## Measurement

Higham (1989) and the scholars he synthesises track centralisation through: the
appearance of a **settlement-size hierarchy** with a dominant central place; **moats,
ramparts, and reservoirs** requiring mobilised corvée labour (e.g. Ban Chiang Hian's
earthworks, estimated at ~100,000 m³ of soil, or Muang Fa Daet's at >370,000 m³); the
**epigraphic record** of ranked titles (*pon* → *mratan* → *raja*), vassalage, tribute
missions to China, and dynastic marriage; and the **concentration of prestige goods**
(bronze, iron, glass and carnelian beads, Indian-derived ornaments) in a minority of
burials. Population is crudely estimated at ~50 people per hectare of settlement.

## Patterns and Findings

The following stages are Higham's (1989) synthesis — attributed, single-source, and
based on a 1989-vintage chronology (see [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]]):

- **Emergent ranking without centralisation (c. 3000–500 BCE).** Domestic communities
  ([[non-nok-tha]], [[ban-na-di]], [[ban-chiang]]) show mortuary wealth differentiation
  among kin groups but no regional centre — see [[chiefdom]].
- **Centralised chiefdoms (c. 500 BCE – 500 CE), "General Period C."** Moated central
  places emerge (Non Chai, Ban Chiang Hian in the Mun–Chi basins; the Dong Son polities
  of Bac Bo), each tied to control of a key resource or route — iron ore, rock salt (Bo
  Phan Khan / Non Dua), river nodes, or coastal traffic — coincident with iron.
- **Early mandalas (c. 100 CE onward), "General Period D."** Funan on the lower Mekong
  delta (the port of Oc Eo, with Roman and Indian trade goods); Zhenla in the middle
  Mekong and Tonle Sap (Isanapura / Sambor Prei Kuk); the Mon-speaking Dvaravati centres
  of the Chao Phraya (Nakhon Pathom, U Thong; see [[austroasiatic-languages]]); the local
  Sri Canasa mandala of the upper Mun; the Cham-speaking Champa/Linyi polities of the
  central Vietnamese coast (see [[sa-huynh-culture]], [[austronesian-expansion]]). The
  Red River delta followed a divergent path — direct incorporation into the Han empire
  (commanderies of Jiaozhi, Jiuzhen, Rinan; the Trung sisters' revolt of 40 CE crushed by
  Ma Yuan in 42 CE) rather than mandala formation.
- **The Angkorian mandala (802–1431 CE).** The most durable and monumental realisation of
  the form; see [[angkor]].

## Explanatory Theories

Higham surveys, without endorsing any single one, the standard prime-mover models —
Wittfogel's (1957) hydraulic despotism, Webb's (1975) trade-and-circumscription model,
and Friedman and Rowlands's (1977) prestige-goods/epigenetic model — and argues instead
for **multiple interacting variables** (Renfrew's "multiplier effect"): individual
charisma and claims to divinity, strategic location, agricultural capacity, iron
availability, and access to exotic valuables. On the pivotal "Indianisation" question he
takes an explicitly indigenist position — Indian contact was "one of several variables
with adaptive potential for change in the local system," not the external creator of
civilisation. This dispute is recorded in [[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]]. He
reads the polities through documentary as well as material evidence (invoking Geertz's
1980 Balinese "theatre state" and Leach's 1954 Kachin as comparative models of
loyalty-based, non-territorial polity), stressing continuity: competition, emulation,
ritual, and display run from the Khok Phanom Di burials of c. 2000 BCE through to
Angkorian god-kingship, with rice intensification more often a consequence than a driver.

### Scott — padi state, manpower, and hill–valley coevolution (2009, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-2009|The Art of Not Being Governed]]* 2009; study [[scott-art-of-not-being-governed-study]]. Political-ecology synthesis of precolonial mainland SE Asia and its massif; not a replacement for Higham’s archaeological sequence.)*

**Wet-rice core as state space.** Scott treats classical mainland polities as **padi states**: political form organized around irrigated rice on permanent fields as the most convenient means of concentrating **manpower and foodstuffs** near a court. Ideal **state space** = ideal **space of appropriation** — dense, sedentary, legible cultivators within a small extraction radius (friction of terrain; oxcart grain logistics; hard power often undependable beyond ~160–300 km on flat ground, far less in hills). Wet-rice is electively affine to state-making, not a deterministic cause: states easier around wet-rice cores; wet-rice cores without states and occasional states without wet-rice cores exist. Virtually all premodern SE Asian state cores sat in settings favorable to irrigated rice; large colonial-era padi expanses are distinguished from piecemeal precolonial kinship/hamlet irrigation that often predated and outlasted particular courts.

**Manpower as first principle.** Concentration of manpower, not acreage, is Scott’s mantra of precolonial SE Asian statecraft: “struggle more for men than for land” (Geertz’s Bali formula extended to the mainland). Thai official titles and provincial ranks keyed to muster totals; Siamese custom separated power over individuals from sovereignty over empty land (Thongchai via Scott). Power before the military-technical revolution boiled down to how many men a ruler could address. Agrarian manpower-heavy states (Ava, Ayutthaya, Mataram) tended to prevail over maritime rivals over the long haul (Lieberman via Scott) — a regional echo of Tilly’s coercion-rich landward states.

**Slavery, captives, and the unfree majority.** Against court/schoolbook narratives of peaceful attraction to luminous courts, Scott stresses **war, slave-raiding, and forced resettlement** as essential to creating and replenishing “thickly-settled clumps.” Padi states were ingatherings of previously stateless peoples — some by trade and status, **majority** (he argues) captives and slaves. Paraphrase: no state without concentrated manpower; no concentration without slavery; all such states were slaving states for long stretches. Bondage codes were often non-perpetual (Sangermano via Scott); the manpower imperative was the enemy of hard ethnic exclusion — identity more performance than genealogy at the core.

**State-accessible product vs GDP.** Rulers cared less about total wealth than **state-accessible product** (grain, livestock, and manpower conveniently seized, assessed, and close enough). SAP and overall wealth often diverge; successful state-building maximizes SAP even at subjects’ expense. Single-staple monoculture fosters calendrical and social uniformity that eases monitoring and tax; nonpadi revenues were harder and smaller (colonial Burma still rice-dependent — Furnivall via Scott).

**Hill–valley coevolution and Zomia.** Valley history without the hills is incomplete: [[zomia]] (mainland massif / region of refuge) coevolves with valley cores as antagonistic but connected spaces. Hilly areas above ~300 m were, as a rule of thumb, outside “Burma proper” and analogous flatland irrigation niches; plateau exceptions (Nan Chao, Kengtung, Lan-na, etc.) prove the ecological rule. Hills practiced [[escape-agriculture]] ([[swidden]], roots, dispersal) and escape social structure; flight was the bedrock of popular freedom and the principal check on precolonial power. Postcolonial “last enclosure” — military occupation, anti-swidden campaigns, forced settlement, roads, development schemes — is the late completion of state space. Regional pathway links: [[state-formation]], [[nonstate-spaces]], [[state-evasion]], [[shatter-zone]], [[grain-hypothesis]], [[ethnogenesis]], [[orality-as-strategy]].

**Relation to Higham / mandala.** Scott’s frame is complementary, not chronological: Higham tracks material centralization into [[mandala]] polities; Scott theorizes why wet-rice cores, unfree labor, and peripheral flight structured the *political ecology* of those and later courts. Both reject simple external-creation (Indianisation) stories for local agency — Higham via adaptive potential of contact, Scott via two-way hill–valley ethnogenesis and reversible subjecthood. Do not collapse mandala ritual politics into Scott’s appropriation geometry or vice versa.
