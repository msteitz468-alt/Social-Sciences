# Atlantic Slave Trade

## Definition and Scope

The forced capture, sale, and transatlantic shipment of African captives to the Americas for plantation and related labor, c. 16th–19th centuries. In *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (1982, Ch. 7), [[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] treats the trade as a **joint product** of European demand and African supply-side political economies — not a unilateral European extraction from passive societies.

## Measurement

Wolf synthesizes secondary estimates of volumes, regional supply (West Africa, Central Africa, later East African connections), and African polities' roles (Gold Coast, Oyo, Dahomey, Benin, Niger Delta, Kongo, Imbangala, Luba-Lunda, etc.). Numbers are **attributed to Wolf's sources as of 1982**, not updated wiki-voice demography.

## Patterns and Findings

Attributed to Wolf 1982:

- European slavers typically moved captives from the African coast; **supply inland was largely in African hands**.
- Trade ramified far inland, reorganizing [[kin-ordered-mode-of-production|kin-ordered]] and [[tributary-mode-of-production|tributary]] polities.
- Articulated with American plantation commodity production (sugar, later cotton, etc.) and European mercantile/industrial capital.
- Treating each African group as a tribe "sufficient unto itself" misreads both past and present.
