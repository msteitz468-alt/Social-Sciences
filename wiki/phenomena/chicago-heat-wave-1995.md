# Chicago Heat Wave (1995)

July 1995 extreme heat event in Chicago and the wave of excess mortality that followed. Primary sociological treatment in this wiki: [[klinenberg-heat-wave-study]] / *[[klinenberg-heat-wave-2002|Heat Wave]]*.

## Definition and Scope

A short-duration meteorological extreme that, Klinenberg argues, became a **social disaster** through isolation, place-based vulnerability, and institutional response — not heat alone.

## Measurement

Official heat-related and excess death totals differ by source (city series, ME, CDC); Klinenberg discusses classification disputes. Age-adjusted rates, living-alone prevalence, and community-area maps are central.

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed — Klinenberg, *Heat Wave*.)* Layered mortality: peak daily city deaths **365**; heat-related week/month series **465–521**; **~739 excess** deaths 14–20 July. ~**73%** of heat deaths over 65; elevated Black vs White age-adjusted rates; male excess risk; Latinos underrepresented among dead relative to population share. Spatial concentration on South/West Side high-poverty areas. **North Lawndale** (high mortality, abandoned ecology) vs **Little Village** (low mortality, dense commercial street life) is the book’s key place comparison.

## Explanatory Theories

Social autopsy / urban ecology of support vs pure meteorological or pure individual-risk accounts.

## Dead bodies as spectacle (*Body & Society* / *Commodifying Bodies*)

*(Attributed — [[klinenberg-eric|Eric Klinenberg]], "Bodies that Don't Matter: Death and Dereliction in Chicago," in *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* / *Body & Society* 7.2–3, 2001; related to but distinct from the 2002 book *Heat Wave*.)* Klinenberg argues that forensic and media focus on **dead bodies** as objective evidence of a "natural" disaster **obscured** the everyday structured and political violence of the urban inner city as a zone of abandonment and untimely death. Bodies, he holds, are **not** self-evident speakers of social truth: when fetishized as journalistic commodities and spectacles that sell news, and when centered as scientific/political objects that displace social questions, they distract from the social etiology of the deaths. At the Medical Examiner's office, corpses became "commodified materials for selling stories rather than substantive, human remains" or embodiments of poverty, neglect, and isolation; he reads dead bodies in the news as triply abstracted commodities (bodies sell stories → news products → ads). Title thesis: "Bodies that Don't Matter." See also [[body-commodification]], [[klinenberg-heat-wave-study]], [[social-autopsy]].

## Interaction

Links to [[social-isolation]], [[residential-segregation]], [[urban-underclass]], and later climate-heat mortality debates (2015 preface).

## Debates

Municipal responsibility and PR; validity of heat-death certification; generalizability to other cities and to climate change; whether corporeal evidence clarifies or obscures social structure (Klinenberg's *Commodifying Bodies* caution).
