# Freshwater Acidification

Freshwater acidification — the fall in pH of lakes and streams — is the second of [[roberts-neil|Roberts]]' ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) "natural or cultural?" pollution cases, and the one in which [[diatom-analysis|diatom]] palaeolimnology most decisively settled a contemporary scientific and political dispute.

## Definition and Scope

Acidification is driven by atmospheric deposition of sulphur oxides (chiefly coal-burning) and nitrogen oxides (converted to sulphuric and nitric acids and returned as "acid rain"). Roberts describes it as the opposite trend to [[eutrophication]]: acidified waters become clear but nutrient-poor.

## Measurement

Reconstructed from [[diatom-analysis|diatom]]-inferred pH, sediment metals (lead, zinc) and fly-ash particles as industrial markers, dated by [[lead-210-dating|²¹⁰Pb/¹³⁷Cs]].

## Patterns and Findings

In attributed voice, from Roberts (1998):
- Below pH ~6, biological diversity declines sharply; low pH mobilizes aluminium, toxic to fish — implicated in Scandinavian and Canadian fish-stock losses attributed to transboundary pollution (Almer et al. 1974).
- The **"progressive natural acidification"** hypothesis held that acid Scandinavian/Shield lakes reflected slow Holocene soil leaching. The **Gårdsjön** (Sweden) diatom-pH record refuted it: a gradual ~1.0-unit early-Holocene decline, then an abrupt >1.5-unit fall dated to c. AD 1950 — larger than the entire preceding 10 000-year change.
- The **Galloway lochs** (Scotland) study tested three anthropogenic hypotheses: afforestation, grazing/burning decline, and acid deposition. Afforested and unforested catchments acidified alike, and [[lead-210-dating|²¹⁰Pb]] placed onset before local planting — leaving **atmospheric acid deposition** as the cause (Battarbee, Flower et al. 1985/1989).
- **Recovery**: as emissions were regulated (and leaded petrol phased out — Greenland ice lead fell after 1970), some Scottish and Canadian (Sudbury) lakes show diatom-flora reversal and rising pH — evidence of ecosystem "elasticity."

## Explanatory Theories

The consensus cause is industrial atmospheric acid deposition; trace metals and fly ash are corroborating markers, not themselves the acidifying agents (Battarbee 1994).

## Interaction

A product of industrial atmospheric pollution; the inverse enrichment process is [[eutrophication]]. Roberts also reports the opposite anthropogenic effect — alkaline fly ash (Estonian oil-shale region, Punning 1994) raising bog pH and destroying *Sphagnum* peat.

## Debates

The "natural versus anthropogenic" dispute was resolved by palaeolimnological base-line reconstruction — Roberts' exemplar of proxy science answering a question documentary records could not.
