# Illicit Antiquities Trade

The **illicit antiquities trade** is the commercial flow of archaeological objects that
have been clandestinely excavated ("looted") and/or illegally exported from their
countries of origin, moving through dealers and auction houses to private collectors and
museums. Its defining archaeological feature is that it is **demand-driven destruction**:
the market's appetite for antiquities finances the digging that severs objects from their
[[archaeological-context|context]], and it is that context — not the object's cash or
aesthetic value — that carries archaeological knowledge. The phenomenon is documented
here principally from [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s *[[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Loot,
Legitimacy and Ownership]]* (2000); its central causal claim (that collecting drives
looting) is a **position** in a live dispute — see [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]] —
even where the case facts below are well documented.

## Definition and Scope

The trade turns on the category of the **[[unprovenanced-antiquities|unprovenanced
antiquity]]** — an object without secure documented findspot or collecting history.
Renfrew's working premise (attributed) is that "unprovenanced antiquities are likely to
be looted antiquities," and that objects surfacing on the market after **1970** (the
UNESCO Convention year, his "somewhat arbitrary dividing line") should be presumed
illicit absent documentation.

A distinction Renfrew presses (attributed): **"stolen"** antiquities (removed from a known
owner or museum, hence documented) versus **"looted" / "illicit"** antiquities
(clandestinely dug from the ground, unrecorded). "In the archaeologist's book 'looted' is
worse than 'stolen'," because theft from a storeroom leaves the record intact whereas
looting destroys context and association irretrievably.

## The Chain (attributed — Renfrew 2000)

1. **Diggers** — impoverished local looters (Peruvian *huaqueros*, Greek/Cypriot
   *archaiokapiloi*, British "nighthawks"), sometimes using heavy machinery (Batán Grande,
   Peru; Banteay Chmar, Cambodia).
2. **Middleman dealers** who buy without asking origin, then sell on to
3. **Reputable dealers and auction houses** who market objects as "unprovenanced,"
   invoking "client confidentiality" and "Codes of Practice." London (until Sotheby's
   closed its London antiquities sales in 1997 after the Watson exposé), New York,
   Geneva, and Zurich are named market hubs.
4. **Museums and private collectors** as final demand — the pieces are then
   **legitimated** by public exhibition and lavish scholarly catalogues (Renfrew's
   "antiquity laundering" / "provenance through publication"), which also raise resale
   value and, in the US, underwrite tax-deductible donations.

## Measurement and Case Record

The trade is deliberately opaque, so scale is estimated from downstream traces —
looters' pits, seizures, restitution suits, and site survey. Documented figures (all
attributed to Renfrew 2000 and his cited sources; **date-stamped as of the reporting he
relied on**):

| Case / place | Detail |
|---|---|
| Batán Grande, Peru (Sicán) | ~100,000 looters' pits; a 1965 robbed tomb yielded 40 kg of gold; est. ~90% of "ancient Peruvian gold" is looted (Alva 1999) |
| Sipán, Peru (Moche) | Plundered by the Bernal family 1987; a 1.3 kg gold ornament ($1.6m) smuggled in a diplomatic bag, seized Philadelphia |
| Djenné region, Mali | Toguéré Kaney Boro: ~600 m² pillaged (1989); Gao Sané "a giant piece of Swiss cheese" — "lost forever" (Sidibe 1995) |
| Nineveh, Iraq | Sennacherib Throne Room reliefs recorded by J.M. Russell 1989–90, then cut up for market — "a world heritage disaster of the first magnitude" (Russell 1997) |
| Banteay Chmar, Cambodia | Late 1998 gutted by army units with heavy equipment; 117 sculpture pieces seized in Thailand, valued ~100m baht/$2.6m (Thosarat 1999) |
| Nok culture, Nigeria | Terracotta heads (banned from export since 1943) acquired by the Louvre — "by definition smuggled" (Brinkman/ICOM) |
| Salisbury Hoard, Britain | >500 objects; British Museum bought 22 shields for £55,000 before the looting was uncovered (Stead 1998) |

## Restitution as Response

Restitution (see [[restitution]]) recurs as the trade's chief legal counter-pressure —
though Renfrew (attributed, following Elia) stresses its value is **deterrence of future
looting**, not recovery of lost context. Documented outcomes: the **Lydian
Hoard/Treasure** (looted Uşak, Turkey, mid-1960s; acquired by the Metropolitan
Museum of Art 1966–70; returned to Turkey after 1987 suit and a due-diligence ruling); the
**Aidonia Treasure** (Mycenaean; returned to Greece via a US charity after Ward's 1993
sale attempt); the **Weary Herakles** (Boston MFA/White–Levy upper half vs. Antalya
Museum lower half — casts matched in 1992, restitution refused). See
[[antiquities-market-looting-debate]] for the contested "universal museum" reply.

## Ineffective Safeguards

The principal international instruments (documented on [[cultural-property]] and in the
appendices of Renfrew 2000): the **1970 UNESCO Convention** (framework, non-retroactive,
dependent on ratification — 91 states by 2000, **not** the UK at time of writing) and the
**1995 Unidroit Convention** (Art. 3.2 deems an unlawfully excavated object "stolen").
Renfrew's assessment (position): these "set a framework" but are toothless without
ratification and enforcement, and museum codes (the **ICOM Code of Professional Ethics**,
1989/rev. 1995; the 1970 **Philadelphia Declaration**) are widely read in "the weakest
possible interpretation." Britain's domestic regime — the Treasure Act
1996 and the **Portable Antiquities Scheme** (1997, >13,000 finds recorded in year one)
— he praises for domestic finds while calling UK inaction on the overseas trade
"remarkable and reprehensible."

## Patterns and Findings

Recurring patterns across the case record (attributed — Renfrew 2000; **note: this is one
advocate's reading**, and the causal claim is contested at
[[antiquities-market-looting-debate]]):

- **Source→market geography.** Poor source regions (Andes, West Africa, South/Southeast
  Asia, the Near East) supply; rich market countries (US, UK, Switzerland, Japan) absorb —
  a demand-pull pattern tracking price differentials.
- **Legitimation cycle.** Objects gain value and respectability as they pass up the chain
  and into exhibition and catalogue — "provenance through publication."
- **Legal-patchwork leakage.** Non-ratification and jurisdictional gaps let material transit
  (the Politis Jordan case: intercepted in Britain, released to the US because the UK had
  not ratified the 1970 Convention).
- **Restitution concentrated at the top.** Recovery mostly reaches high-value, well-published
  objects (Lydian Hoard, Aidonia); the bulk of looted material is never traced.
- **Context is unrecoverable.** Even when an object returns, the destroyed
  [[archaeological-context|context]] does not — the finding all sides can agree on.

## Explanatory Theories

Renfrew's account is a **demand-side** theory: looting is a market response to collector
and museum demand, so the ethical and legal leverage lies with the buyers, not only the
diggers (attributed — this is the book's core position, contested by those who locate the
problem in source-country under-policing or who defend a licit market). Structural
conditions he names: price differentials between poor source countries and rich market
countries; the legal patchwork (the US permits unpublished digging on private land; UK
non-ratification made London a "thieves' kitchen"); and the legitimating role of scholarship
and museums.

## Debates

- **Does the market drive the looting?** — [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]] (the

- **Whose heritage?** — cultural-nationalist source-country claims vs.

## Interaction

Bears on [[heritage-management]] (the destructive face management confronts),
