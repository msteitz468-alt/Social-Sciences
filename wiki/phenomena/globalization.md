# Globalization

## Definition and Scope

**Globalization** names intensified cross-border economic, political, and cultural integration — or, in policy discourse, a late-20th-century package of liberalization. World-systems analysts treat the term as **time-bound rhetoric** for a phase of the [[capitalist-world-economy|capitalist world-economy]], not a new unit of analysis.

## Measurement

Trade and capital-flow ratios; global value chains; migration; media. Measures disagree on whether late-20th-century "globalization" is unprecedented or a recurrent expansion phase.

## Patterns and Findings

Wiki voice only for brute descriptive trends where multi-source convergent; not yet established here.

**Attributed positions from *[[wallerstein-modern-world-system-longue-duree-2004|MWS in the Longue Durée]]* (2004):**

- **Wallerstein:** globalization introduced as alternative to developmentalism under TINA ("there is no alternative"), with "governance" as sociopolitical companion term; unhelpful rhetoric for a chaotic transition from a disintegrating [[capitalist-world-economy]]; post-9/11 vocabularies (fundamentalism, terrorism, clash of civilizations) may drown globalization and human-rights languages alike.
- **Amin:** neoliberal globalization organizes "apartheid on a global scale" via five monopolies of the triad; catch-up promises are false under polarizing accumulation.
- **Abu-Lughod:** "global babble" — exponential use, loss of analytic power; like modernization, eviscerated as slogan; better empirical work tracks mechanisms, cities, and historical multi-centered systems.
- **Chase-Dunn:** distinguish **structural globalization** (long-run expansion of exchange networks) from the neoliberal **globalization project**.

**Attributed positions from *[[sassen-global-city-2001|The Global City]]* (Sassen 1991/2001):**

- Economic globalization entails a new organizational structure requiring new conceptual architecture; the [[global-city]] is a strategic scale where global dynamics *territorialize* (endogenize) rather than only cross borders.
- Geographic dispersal of economic activity and simultaneous centralization of specialized [[producer-services|producer services]] and finance are joint outcomes; capabilities for global control must be *produced* ([[practices-of-global-control]]).
- Leading cities form incipient [[transnational-urban-system|transnational urban systems]]; growth can disconnect from national hinterlands; social order shows growth-linked [[spatial-polarization]].

**Attributed positions from *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* (Castells 1996/2010):**

- The [[network-society|network society]] constitutes itself as a global system because networks do not stop at nation-state borders — defining the form of globalization of the Information Age while selectively including/excluding people and territories.
- The new economy is **informational, global, and networked**: core activities organized globally (directly or via linkages); competition played out in networks of business networks; financial globalization as backbone (including the 2010 “global automaton” crisis reading).
- Organizational form: [[network-enterprise]]; spatial form: [[space-of-flows]] articulated with [[space-of-places]]; capital global, labor local.
- Not everything is global: most production/employment remain local/regional; economies are global because they depend on a globalized core.


**Attributed positions from *[[tsing-friction-2005|Friction]]* (Tsing 2005):**

- Globalization-as-frictionless-flow is a 1990s myth; connection works through [[friction]] — awkward engagements that enable *and* particularize movement.
- Global forces are aggregates of local/global interactions, not only pressures that locals accommodate; study via [[ethnography-of-global-connection]] and [[scale-making]].
- Universals that organize capital, science, and politics are [[traveling-universals|engaged universals]] — sticky practical links, not pure truths or pure lies.
- Globalization is not a pizza-whole delivered from centers; it is **fragments**, some of which successfully look like wholes (Coda).

**Attributed positions from *[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|The Consequences of Modernity]]* (Giddens 1990):**

- **Definition:** globalisation as the **intensification of worldwide social relations** linking distant localities so that local happenings are shaped by distant events and vice versa — the "stretching" of connections across the earth’s surface; modernity is **inherently globalising** via [[disembedding]] and reflexivity.
- **Dialectical:** local transformation is as much part of globalisation as lateral extension; outcomes are often mutually opposed tendencies (e.g. Singapore prosperity linked to Pittsburgh impoverishment via global networks); nation-state "too small for the big problems of life, and too big for the small problems of life."
- **Four dimensions** (mapping his four institutional dimensions of modernity): world capitalist economy; nation-state system; world military order; industrialism / global division of labour — plus cultural globalisation and media as an underlying force. Critiques **IR** literature (only one dimension; states-as-actors only fully makes sense for nation-states; weak on crosscutting social relations) and **[[world-systems-analysis|Wallerstein]]** (credits global economic span but rejects single-nexus capitalism-only explanation and core/semi-periphery/periphery as insufficient for political/military power).
- **Sovereignty:** modern state sovereignty formed *in conjunction with* the state system (mutual recognition), not simply lost in a progressive story of decline.
- **Western project?** Institutional *origins* of modernity are Western; globalising *tendencies* are not peculiarly Western once modern institutions are established; the reflexive knowledge dynamic is Western in origin with qualifications.

**Attributed positions from *[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]* (Appadurai 1996):**

- The **cultural dimension** is primary and irreducible to political economy: "the central problem of today's global interactions is the tension between cultural homogenization and cultural heterogenization" — see the [[homogenization-heterogenization-debate]].
- Globalization is **not** homogenization/Americanization; imported forms are **indigenized** at least as fast as they arrive ([[vernacular-globalization]]). "Globalization is itself a deeply historical, uneven, and even localizing process."
- The global cultural economy is a "complex, overlapping, **disjunctive** order" that defeats [[core-periphery|center-periphery]] models; it flows through five [[global-cultural-flows|-scapes]] ([[ethnoscape]], [[mediascape]], [[technoscape]], [[financescape]], [[ideoscape]]) whose relationships are nonisomorphic.
- **Media and migration** jointly make [[imagination-as-social-practice|imagination a collective social practice]]; [[deterritorialization]] delinks people, wealth, and loyalty from territory, straining the nation-state.
- Fear of absorption is scalar and relative ("Indonesianization," "Japanization"), not simply Americanization: "One man's imagined community is another man's political prison."

## Explanatory Theories

- **Neoliberal / TINA discourse:** integration as efficiency and catch-up opportunity (recorded as position).
- **[[world-systems-analysis]]:** current period as crisis/transition of a long historical system; polarization continues.
- **[[modernization-theory]]:** related developmental sequences (competes with world-systems on unit and mechanism).
- **[[global-city-theory]]:** cities as production sites for the infrastructure of global coordination and control (Sassen).
- **[[network-society-theory]]:** globalization as the geographic form of a network society powered by the IT paradigm and informational capitalism (Castells).
- **[[ethnography-of-global-connection]] (Tsing):** globalization as frictional, fragmentary connection; universals and capital studied in sticky encounters rather than as seamless systems (*[[tsing-friction-2005|Friction]]*, 2005).
- **[[global-cultural-flows]] (Appadurai):** globalization as a disjunctive cultural economy of five -scapes, driven by media and migration; heterogenization/indigenization against homogenization (*[[appadurai-modernity-at-large-1996|Modernity at Large]]*, 1996).
- **Salvage / multi-species extension (Tsing):** *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]* (2015) tracks global matsutake supply chains as [[salvage-accumulation]] across pericapitalist patches and multi-species forest histories — capitalism without progress assumptions looks [[patchiness|patchy]] *(Tsing)*.
