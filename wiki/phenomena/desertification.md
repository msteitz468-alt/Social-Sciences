# Desertification

Desertification — the expansion of arid conditions and loss of vegetative productivity — is documented by [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) above all through the rise and fall of the "green Sahara," the clearest [[holocene|Holocene]] case of large-scale, climatically driven biome change with human consequences.

## Definition and Scope

Roberts treats desertification as both a natural, [[holocene-climate-change|climatically]] driven process and, in the later Holocene and today, a partly anthropogenic one — insisting (attributed) that the two must be distinguished rather than conflated, and cautioning against reflexively blaming human "misuse."

## Measurement

Reconstructed from dated lake-level records (Alayne Street-Perrott's global lake-level databank), [[pollen-analysis|pollen]], fossil fauna, and aeolian/lacustrine stratigraphy.

## Patterns and Findings

In attributed voice, from Roberts (1998):
- The **"green Sahara" / African Humid Period**: from before 10 000 to c. 5500 Cal. yr BP, high lake levels and savanna extended deep into the present desert; fossil crocodile and hippopotamus in now-hyperarid northern Mali attest permanent freshwater (Petit-Maire and Riser 1983); the Sahara "effectively did not exist" as desert.
- Cause: [[milankovitch-cycles|orbital]] strengthening of the monsoon amplified by vegetation–albedo feedback (Street-Perrott et al. 1991); the same mechanism operated across Arabia, the Thar, and monsoon Asia.
- **Desiccation** to the modern desert occurred c. 6500–4500 Cal. yr BP, in places abruptly and stepwise (Oyo, eastern Sahara: savanna and deep lake before 9500, dried out by ~5100 Cal. yr BP; Ritchie and Haynes 1987), pointing to monsoon collapse rather than gradual change.
- Human responses included the emergence of **pastoral nomadism** — camel-based in Arabia and the Sahara, cattle-based across sub-Saharan Africa (5000–1000 Cal. yr BP) — and, in African agricultural origins, cultivation *following* desiccation (a better fit to the determinist model than the Near East; see [[agricultural-origins-causation-debate]]).

## Explanatory Theories

The primary driver is orbital-monsoon change with biosphere feedback; [[environmental-determinism|environmental-determinist]] readings (Huntington's desiccation-driven migration) are treated by Roberts as overstated. Later-Holocene and modern desertification adds grazing and land pressure as contributors.

## Interaction

Drives [[soil-erosion]], pastoral adaptation, and population displacement; is one input to [[agricultural-revolution|agricultural origins]] in Africa. Roberts draws an explicit (attributed) analogy between Bronze Age upland abandonment in Britain and modern African desertification — both partly anthropogenic degradation of fragile environments.

## Debates

The balance of climatic versus human causation in modern desertification, and the abruptness of the mid-Holocene Saharan collapse, remain active questions.
