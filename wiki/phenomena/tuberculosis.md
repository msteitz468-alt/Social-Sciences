# Tuberculosis

## Definition and Scope

Tuberculosis (TB) is an infectious disease caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, transmitted by
aerosol and typically attacking the lungs. As a social phenomenon it is, in [[farmer-paul|Paul
Farmer]]'s framing, a paradox: fully curable since effective chemotherapy arrived in the 1950s, yet
"along with AIDS, the leading infectious cause of adult death in the world today." Robert Koch
identified the tubercle bacillus in 1882; the WHO reported roughly **three million** TB deaths in 1996
(as of that report), more than died of HIV that year. Roughly one-third of the world's population is
estimated to carry quiescent (latent) infection. Farmer's central claim (attributed) is that TB is not
"resurgent" or "reemerging" but has never left the poor: "from the vantage of the poor, TB in the 20th
century reemerged *from*, rather than into, the ranks of the poor."

## Measurement

TB is tracked by case-notification rates, tuberculin (PPD) skin-test prevalence (~70% of adults in a
densely populated Haitian slum, as of Farmer's sources), sputum smear and culture, drug-susceptibility
testing, and — for transmission chains — DNA fingerprinting of strains. Farmer stresses that because
poverty is not recorded in national TB data, **race and place function as proxies for it** in the U.S.
statistics. Measurement gaps are themselves findings: the WHO estimated (1991) that only about half of
all TB cases worldwide are ever diagnosed.

## Patterns and Findings

- **Class and race gradient (historical).** English mortuary registers of the 1830s showed consumptive
  rates of 16% / 28% / 30% among gentlemen / tradesmen / laborers. Circa 1900 U.S. TB death rates ran
  ~200/100,000 (white) vs. ~400/100,000 (Black) Americans — a roughly fifty-year lag in the benefits of
  progress (attributed, Rosenkrantz/Farmer). By 1987 the nonwhite:white annual TB-risk ratio in the
  U.S. had risen from 2.9 (1953) to 5.3.
- **The "excess" resurgence.** U.S. TB rates rose in the mid-1980s; central Harlem's rate reached
  222/100,000 by 1992, exceeding many poor countries. In 1989, fewer than half of New York TB patients
  who began treatment were declared cured; in one Harlem Hospital study almost 90% did not complete
  therapy — a completion failure Farmer attributes to structural barriers, not patient character.
- **The [[hiv-aids|HIV]] synergy.** HIV reactivates latent TB as cell-mediated immunity fails; unlike
  most AIDS opportunistic infections, TB is air-transmissible, so HIV-positive people with active
  pulmonary TB expose even immunocompetent bystanders. TB was the most common presenting AIDS illness
  among Haitians (noted as early as 1983).
- **Multidrug-resistant TB (MDR-TB).** Resistance arises from intermittent or poorly designed therapy
  and can then transmit as primary infection. Farmer's "wealth-gradient" model (attributed): in settings
  of *shared wealth* transmission is quickly interrupted; in settings of *shared poverty* (rural Haiti)
  few can access any drugs, so acquired resistance is comparatively less likely; the most resistant
  organisms emerge in settings of *juxtaposed* wealth and poverty (urban Peru), where second-line drugs
  are purchasable but not reliably accessible — producing intermittent, resistance-amplifying treatment.
  In *[[farmer-pathologies-of-power-2003|Pathologies of Power]]* Farmer and colleagues named the
  **"amplifier effect"** (attributed): giving standard first-line short-course chemotherapy to a
  patient already resistant to both isoniazid and rifampin exposes the strain to sub-lethal pressure
  and *worsens* the resistance pattern — so DOTS, which rests on those two drugs, cannot cure MDR-TB
  and can make it worse when applied indiscriminately during a resistant outbreak.

- **Prisons as epidemic amplifiers.** Farmer treated the prison as the paradigmatic site where
  [[structural-violence|structural violence]] concentrates TB risk. In the Russian penal system (as of
  his 1998–2000 fieldwork and cited surveys) an estimated 1 in 10 prisoners had active TB and up to a
  quarter of those MDR-TB; TB accounted for 50–80% of deaths in many former-Soviet prisons. At one
  colony he visited, over 800 of 909 prisoners had active TB, the annual medication budget was ~$2,000,
  and 27 of 30 prisoners due for release that month were still infectious. In Tomsk oblast prison TB
  notification ran forty to fifty times the civilian rate. He read the earlier New York City MDR-TB
  outbreak (1,279 cases 1991–94, ~80% traced to jails and shelters, >$1 billion to control) as the
  same mechanism in a rich city. Farmer's argument (attributed): the only way to halt
  "tuberculosis-as-punishment" is prompt effective treatment of all prisoners, since even amnesty
  releases still-infectious people into dismantled health systems.

## Explanatory Theories

Farmer's **[[biosocial-analysis]]** treats MDR-TB as a "socially produced biological phenomenon" and
argues that "compliance" is an analytically flimsy concept where the poor are "put systematically at
risk for tuberculosis and then denied access to adequate care." Against this stands the biomedical/
behavioral tradition that names **patient noncompliance** "the most serious remaining problem in the
control of tuberculosis" — the dispute recorded on
[[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]]. The historian Randall Packard's political-economy
account (South Africa) locates TB's persistence in the transformations of industrial "racial
capitalism," deeper than apartheid itself.

The load-bearing evidence is Farmer's **[[do-kay|Do Kay / Proje Veye Sante]]** near-experimental
comparison: TB patients given financial and nutritional support plus community-health-worker follow-up
reached a **100% cure rate**, versus at best **48%** for patients receiving the same clinical services
without the support package — while sorcery belief, held equally by both groups, showed *no* relation to
compliance. Farmer's summary rule (attributed): "patient-dependent failure should be a diagnosis of
exclusion."

## Interaction

TB interacts tightly with [[hiv-aids|HIV/AIDS]] (reactivation and transmission), with malnutrition and
[[concentrated-poverty]], and with global political economy — Pierre Chaulet's "the globalization of
currencies... carries with it the globalization of TB transmission," documented by Farmer through
Lima–Boston MDR-TB transmission chains and the transnational case that founded his Peru program.

## Debates

The MDR-TB **cost-effectiveness** dispute is recorded at
[[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]] and treated on [[immodest-claims-of-causality]]: WHO and
allied authorities held (as of the late-1990s "DOTS" era) that treating chronic MDR cases with
second-line drugs should "remain a low priority for national tuberculosis programmes in developing
countries," while Farmer and Partners in Health / Socios en Salud argued — and demonstrated on their
first Lima cohort (>85% culture-negative near end of treatment) — that MDR-TB is curable in poor
settings and that the "not cost-effective" verdict amounts to a differential valuation of human life.
