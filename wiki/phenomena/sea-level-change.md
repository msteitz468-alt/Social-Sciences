# Sea-Level Change

Sea-level change over the last glacial cycle and the [[holocene|Holocene]] reshaped coastlines, opened and closed the land bridges that governed human and faunal dispersal, and drowned the coastal landscapes of the early postglacial. [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) treats it as a first-order control on early-Holocene human adaptation.

## Definition and Scope

Two components combine (attributed): **eustatic** change — global ocean volume, driven by ice-sheet growth and melting — and **glacio-isostatic** change — regional crustal movement under and around ice loads. Their interaction produces regionally very different relative sea-level histories.

## Measurement

Reconstructed from radiometrically dated drowned coral reefs, submerged and raised shorelines, and salt-marsh/estuarine stratigraphy, dated mainly by [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]]. Richard Fairbanks' (1989) dated Barbados reefs are the classic eustatic curve.

## Patterns and Findings

In attributed voice, from Roberts (1998):
- At the last glacial maximum, sea level stood on average >100 m below present, exposing **Beringia** (Asia–Alaska) and the Britain–Europe connection and aiding dispersal (Tooley and Shennan 1987).
- Eustatic sea level rose from about −55 m at 11 500 Cal. yr BP to near-modern levels by c. 6000 years ago; Fairbanks (1989) recorded a marked slow-down in the rate of rise during the [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]] (13 000–11 500 Cal. yr BP).
- **Glacio-isostatic** effects reversed the sign regionally: Scottish late-glacial shorelines are now up to 40 m above sea level (≥70 m of uplift in 15 000 years), while the forebulge along the US eastern seaboard and southern North Sea subsided.
- Regional case histories: the Baltic passed through Yoldia Sea → Ancylus Lake → Littorina Sea stages; Japan's **Jōmon transgression** (c. 8000–7000 years ago) left shell mounds up to 60 km inland; rising seas fragmented the Sunda shelf into the Indonesian archipelago; the isolation of Wrangel Island stranded a dwarf mammoth population that survived until after 4500 years ago (Vartanyan et al. 1993). Roberts reports the contested Ryan et al. (1997) reconstruction of a catastrophic Black Sea flood c. 7500 Cal. yr BP as a single-team model.

## Explanatory Theories

Ice-volume (eustatic) change tracks [[holocene-climate-change|climate]] and [[quaternary-glaciation|glaciation]]; isostatic rebound follows deglaciation with a mantle-flow lag.

## Interaction

Sea-level rise drove coastal-Mesolithic adaptation (see [[mesolithic]]), closed the route back to Asia after the peopling of the Americas (see [[global-colonization]]), and set the alluvial and deltaic stages on which early farming and states developed.

## Debates

The magnitude, abruptness, and human consequences of specific transgressions — the Black Sea flood above all — remain contested reconstructions resting on limited records.
