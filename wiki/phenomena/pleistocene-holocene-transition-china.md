# Pleistocene–Holocene Transition in China

## Definition and Scope

*(Attributed throughout — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]], Ch. 3,
unless otherwise noted.)*

The Pleistocene–Holocene transition in China (ca. 24,000–9,000 cal BP, spanning the Last
Glacial Maximum through the early Holocene) corresponds to major changes in climate,
environment, settlement, technology, subsistence, and social relations: an increase in
sedentary lifestyle, the origins of pottery, the flourishing of microliths, the emergence
of polished stone technology, intensified exploitation of plant foods, and the initial
stages of cereal cultivation. Liu & Chen adopt the term "**Epipaleolithic**" — rather than
"Neolithic" or a fixed trait-list — for the material assemblages of this transition,
reserving "Neolithic" for an economic (domestication-based) definition rather than a
checklist of traits (pottery + polished stone + domesticates). The phenomenon is not
uniform: Epipaleolithic hunting-fishing-gathering persisted well into the Holocene in
arid/semiarid north China, high-altitude Northwest/Southwest China, some South China cave
sites (e.g., Zengpiyan), and tropical southeast China/Taiwan.

## Measurement

Regional pollen sequences anchor the climatic backdrop: at Shizitan (middle Yellow River),
herbaceous taxa predominate 35,000–9,400 BP, with a dry/semiarid steppe phase from 17,000
BP, a cold/dry glacial episode 11,000–10,500 BP, and a warm temperate-steppe early Holocene
phase 10,500–9,400 BP (Xia, Z. et al. 2002, as of Liu & Chen 2012). In the Yangzi region,
pollen data indicate a cool/dry episode 12,900–10,300 cal BP (compared to the Younger
Dryas) followed by warm/wet conditions 10,300–9,000 cal BP; the Yellow River delta shows a
comparable pattern. During the LGM, average annual temperature was ca. 6–9°C lower in
north China and 4–5°C lower in the Yangzi region than present, with sea level ca. 130–155 m
below present (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

Liu & Chen organize the chapter's cultural-ecological variation using **Binford's (1980)
forager-collector model**, treated as a graded continuum rather than a strict dichotomy —
"not two polar types of subsistence-settlement systems, but a graded series from simple to
complex." They also cite Bettinger's (2001) characterization of late-Pleistocene
hunter-gatherers as mobile "niche chasers" responding to climatic variability, versus
early-Holocene groups developing more diverse, specialized, plant-dependent strategies in
more stable resource environments.

## Patterns and Findings

**Earliest pottery in the world.** Pottery appears in China as early as 18,000–20,000 cal
BP (Kuzmin 2003a; Wu, X. and Zhao 2003), among the earliest anywhere, in both south China
and north China during the LGM:

- **Yuchanyan** cave (Daoxian, Hunan): potsherds dated 18,300–15,430 cal BP (Boaretto et
  al. 2009) — "among the earliest pottery vessels in the world." The site's flotation
  recovered 40 plant species (4 edible fruits) and rice phytoliths; wild rice was likely
  collected but probably did not play an important dietary role. One hypothesis under
  consideration (Higham and Lu 1998; Lu, T. 1999) is that cooking of wild rice contributed
  to the origins of pottery.
- **Hutouliang** (Nihewan Basin, Hebei): potsherds (GrA-10460, 16,300–14,700 cal BP) are
  the earliest pottery discovered to date in north China — porous, sand-tempered,
  low-fired, flat-based, unburnt vessels.

Comparably early pottery also appears in the Russian Far East (Gasya, Khummy, lower Amur
River, ca. 16,500–14,500 cal BP) and Japan (Incipient Jōmon, Odai Yamamoto I ca. 16,500
cal BP). Liu & Chen, following Zhushchikhovskaya (2005), note that early East Asian
pottery across this zone commonly imitates basketry form and decoration, leaving open
whether this reflects interregional interaction or independent convergent development.
They review architectural, culinary, and resource-intensification hypotheses for pottery
origins and judge that the Chinese evidence favors the culinary and
resource-intensification explanations.

**Sedentism and settlement.** Sites document a graded shift toward the "collector" end of
Binford's continuum:

- **Xiachuan** (23,900–16,400 BP, Shanxi): tripartite lithic tradition (flaked blades,
  microblades, heavy-duty tools) with some of the earliest grinding slabs/handstones in
  China; use-wear evidence (Lu 1999) suggests seed/grass-panicle processing, though the
  cereal proportion of diet cannot be quantified.
- **Longwangchan** (20,000–15,000 BP, Shaanxi): one of the earliest ground-edge stone
  tools in China and rare Paleolithic-China shell ornaments; interpreted as a mobile
  hunter-gatherer settlement system, likely a seasonal stone-tool-production campsite.
- **Shizitan** (21,000–8,500 cal BP, Shanxi): residue and use-wear analysis of grinding
  stones (Locality 9) indicates processing of acorns, grasses, beans, and tubers ca.
  12,700–11,600 cal BP — plant exploitation that Liu & Chen suggest may include wild
  ancestors of foxtail and broomcorn millet, exploited for millennia before domestication.
- **Donghulin** (11,000–9,000 cal BP, Mentougou): human burials (including secondary
  interments with grave offerings), hearths, grinding stones (acorns and siliceous
  plants), a bone-handled composite knife with an embedded microblade, and pottery —
  read as indicating a new settlement/subsistence strategy: larger site size, longer
  occupation, formal burials, and a broad-spectrum, reduced-mobility subsistence
  consistent with an initial transition toward sedentism.
- **Nanzhuangtou** (ca. 12,408–11,018 cal BP as calibrated, Hebei): the first known
  contemporary north China site on alluvial plains rather than mountainous terrain;
  faunal remains include a domesticated dog (Yuan & Li 2010) — the **earliest evidence of
  animal domestication in China**. Flotation was not used at excavation, so plant remains
  are absent from the record — a methodological/recovery-bias limitation Liu & Chen flag
  explicitly.
- **Zengpiyan** (Guilin, Guangxi): Phases I–IV (12,000–8,000 cal BP) document the
  Paleolithic-to-Neolithic transition in south China; carbonized tuber remains and taro-
  starch residues indicate tubers, not rice, were the dietarily important plant food;
  heavy tooth wear may reflect intensive snail consumption.
- **Shangshan** (ca. 11,000–9,000 cal BP, Puyang River basin, Zhejiang) — the earliest
  known Holocene-China settlement combining dwellings, permanent storage facilities,
  possible burials, pottery, ground stone tools, and substantial plant-food consumption.
  Storage pits, a posthole arrangement read as a pile-dwelling structure, and
  starch/phytolith residues (acorns, Job's tears, tubers) place Shangshan at the
  collector end of the continuum: "Shangshan was a sedentary village, and its people were
  logistic collectors, but we are uncertain whether the occupation was year-round or
  nearly year-round." Both wild and cultivated rice-spikelet-rachis types are present, but
  Liu & Chen judge acorn exploitation, not rice, to have been the economic center of
  gravity — a refinement of Bruce Smith's (1995) prediction that Yangzi rice-farming
  predecessor communities were affluent, wild-rice/aquatic-resource-dependent societies.

Liu & Chen argue three factors jointly enabled the transition: (1) a warm
temperate/subtropical environment with seasonally variable food abundance; (2)
availability of storable foods (nuts, cereals); (3) technological improvement (ceramic
cooking).

## Explanatory Theories

Two attributed, partly competing accounts of what drove increased sedentism appear in the
source: Lu's (1999) more cautious reading of seasonal (not full-year) occupation at cave
sites such as Xianrendong, versus Yasuda's (2002) inference of a broader "sedentary
revolution" in East Asia at the end of the LGM (from ca. 16,500 cal BP), keyed to the
appearance of pottery without specifying degree of sedentism. Liu & Chen themselves
emphasize acorn exploitation — a high-tannin, storable resource requiring complex
processing — as a key driver of sedentism, drawing ethnographic/experimental parallels
(Native American acorn processing and storage; Testart 1982; Soffer 1989 on the general
storage–sedentism linkage).

## Interaction

This phenomenon is the immediate antecedent of Neolithic domestication in China (see
[[rice-domestication]] and [[peiligang-culture]] for the following Peiligang-era
cereal-farming economies) and of the Central-Plain/multiregional origins debate (see
[[nuclear-area]]). Shangshan in particular is treated by Liu & Chen as the transition's
type case for sedentism preceding full agriculture, and Lijiagou (Henan) is noted as
helping establish a temporal — though stylistically discontinuous — link between the
late-Pleistocene microlithic tradition and the early Neolithic Peiligang culture (ca.
9,000/8,500–7,000 cal BP) in the same region.

## Debates

Whether the ca. 12,900–11,600 cal BP Younger-Dryas-equivalent cooling affected the origins
of rice/millet domestication in China "remains to be investigated," per Liu & Chen. The
degree to which basketry-imitation in early East Asian pottery reflects diffusion versus
independent invention is likewise left explicitly unresolved. No dedicated debate page has
been created for either question; both are recorded here pending further ingest.
