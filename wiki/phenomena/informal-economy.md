# Informal Economy

## Definition and Scope

The **informal economy** is the sphere of income-generating activity that operates outside formal registration, licensing, taxation, and labor regulation — from street vending and scavenging to unregistered services and casual labor. The term was coined by the anthropologist **Keith Hart** (from 1970s West African fieldwork) and taken up across development economics and urban sociology. It is a **recurrent** feature of market societies, not a single historical event; its boundary with the formal economy is drawn by law and enforcement, so what counts as "informal" shifts with regulation.

This page treats the informal economy chiefly as documented in [[duneier-sidewalk-study|Duneier's *Sidewalk*]] — the used-book and magazine vending economy of poor men in 1990s New York — and cross-references the wiki's coverage of urban economic life.

## Measurement

Operationalized variously: as the share of employment or output outside registered firms (labor-force and national-accounts estimates), and — in ethnography — as direct documentation of unlicensed trade and its internal rules. Measures disagree sharply because the object is defined by what escapes official record; ethnographic and survey approaches capture different slices (self-report of unregulated work vs. observed transactions).

## Patterns and Findings

As documented by Duneier (attributed, 1999):

- The Sixth Avenue book/magazine trade rested on a legal foothold — **Local Law 33 (1982)**, permitting vending of written matter without a license — showing the informal economy's boundary is set by specific law, not a natural line.
- The economy sustained differentiated roles (vendor, scavenger, place holder, table watcher, mover, storage provider) and an **informal property-rights system** in scarce sidewalk space, enforced by reputation rather than law (see [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors]]).
- Participants drew **moral distinctions** — "an honest living" of vending/scavenging set against theft and drug dealing — staking self-respect on earning money "the right way."
- The trade functioned as a partial route to reintegration for formerly incarcerated and addicted men (Duneier's "fixed windows" argument).

These are single-ethnography findings, attributed; broader informal-economy scholarship (Hart, [[sassen-global-city-study|Sassen]]'s account of global-city informalization, Portes and others) frames the general phenomenon.

As documented by Bourgois (attributed, *[[bourgois-in-search-of-respect-1995|In Search of Respect]]*, mid-1980s–1990s [[el-barrio]]):

- The underground economy is how many East Harlem residents “subsist with the minimal decency” when official poverty and unemployment rates are high; it is gendered (male street dealing vs. often more hidden female strategies).
- Crack retail is a **workplace** (Game Room): piece-rates, lookouts, management–labor conflict, violence as security capital — not only “disorder.”
- Effective dealer wages often approximate legal minimums once risk is counted; the surplus is **autonomy and dignity** relative to office/service “disses.”
- Narcotics street retail is framed as a major equal-opportunity employer for marginalized men; policy must rebalance legal vs. underground rewards (Bourgois’s POSITION in the Conclusion).

## Explanatory Theories

Side by side (attributed): development economics treats informality as a response to labor-market exclusion and regulatory cost; [[sassen-global-city-study|Sassen]] links informalization to the polarized consumption structure of [[global-city|global cities]]; Duneier's ethnography stresses local ecological conditions (a [[sustaining-habitat|sustaining habitat]]) and the informal economy's capacity to generate moral order, against readings ([[broken-windows|broken windows]]) that see street trade as disorder; Bourgois links crack dealing to deindustrialization, cultural-capital barriers in the service sector, and [[street-culture]] as resistance that also ruins participants.

## Interaction

Interacts with [[concentrated-poverty]], incarceration, migration ([[immigrant-second-generation]]), and formal regulation (Business Improvement Districts, quality-of-life policing); the relationship between informal trade and public order is the crux of the [[broken-windows-debate]]. Bourgois’s 2003 follow-up ties indoor dealing and residual street economies to Giuliani-era quality-of-life policing and one-strike public housing (attributed).

## Debates

Whether the informal economy is a survival adaptation to be accommodated or a disorder to be regulated away is the live policy dispute — joined directly in *Sidewalk* against broken-windows enforcement (see [[broken-windows-debate]]). Bourgois’s dual position (decriminalize street-retail profitability **and** rebuild dignified legal work) is a distinct drug-economy variant of the same adaptation-vs-repression problem (POSITION, not wiki verdict).

## Tsing / commercial wild-mushroom foraging (2015)

*[[tsing-mushroom-2015|Tsing]]* treats commercial matsutake picking as exemplifying precarious non-wage livelihood: no companies, wages, or benefits; pickers sell what they find; some years yield nothing. At [[open-ticket-oregon|Open Ticket]], money changes hands without local capital accumulation — a pericapitalist patch feeding [[salvage-accumulation]] downstream *(Tsing 2015)*.
