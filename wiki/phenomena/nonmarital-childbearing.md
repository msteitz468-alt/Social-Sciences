# Nonmarital Childbearing

## Definition and Scope

Births occurring outside legal marriage. Distinct from “single motherhood” as household form: many nonmarital births occur to cohabiting or romantically involved couples who later separate ([[edin-kefalas-promises-i-can-keep-2005|Edin & Kefalas 2005/2011]] stress relationship churning and family complexity).

## Measurement

- U.S. vital statistics: share of all births to unmarried women (Edin & Kefalas 2011 preface: ~1/3 in late 1990s fieldwork window; >4/10 by ~2011).
- Stratified by education/class: sharp class gradient (low rates among college graduates; high among high-school-or-less).
- Not identical to teen birth (most nonmarital births are to adult women; Edin & Kefalas sample oversamples teen first births relative to all unwed mothers).

## Patterns and Findings

**Wiki voice (convergent demography, date-stamped):** Nonmarital shares of U.S. births rose substantially in the late 20th and early 21st centuries, with large class and education gradients. Cross-nationally, nonmarital birth *rates* can be high where cohabitation is marriage-like and stable; the U.S. pattern pairs nonmarital birth with high union instability relative to many European cases (as framed in Edin & Kefalas 2011 preface citing Cherlin, McLanahan, Andersson, Kiernan).

**Attributed (Edin & Kefalas ethnography, *Promises* 2005/2011):**

- Fieldwork-era U.S. nonmarital share ~1/3; by 2011 preface >4/10.
- Philly nonmarital births ~20% (1950) → ~62% (2000) — ~twice national rate; Camden similarly dramatic.
- “Single mother” at birth is often a statistical misnomer: most poor unmarried mothers still romantically involved with the father at birth (national survey figures they cite: 8/10 involved; 4/10 cohabiting); half split by 12 months; two-thirds by child age three.
- Urban children born outside marriage: 78% experience instability and/or family complexity by kindergarten; 18% marriage-like stable; 4% stably solo mother from birth (Fragile Families-type figures via preface).
- White education gradient: nonmarital childbearing rose sharply among dropouts and HS-only whites 1982–~2010 while college graduates stayed ~2% (Wilcox via preface).
- Sample ethnography: semi-planned conception; pregnancy as relationship test; high marriage bar; [[being-there-mothering]]; [[redemptive-motherhood]]; class more than race in comparable poverty contexts ([[edin-kefalas-promises-study]]).

## Interaction

Links to [[concentrated-poverty]], [[marriage]] as institution, multipartner fertility / relationship instability, and welfare-state design. Children’s outcomes depend heavily on **instability and complexity**, not marital status alone (preface framing).

## Debates

Culture-versus-structure explanations of nonmarital fertility among the poor remain live; *Promises* is a major ethnographic position against “don’t value marriage” caricatures while documenting class-specific sequencing.
