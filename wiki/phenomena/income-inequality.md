# Income Inequality

## Definition and Scope

Unequal distribution of **income flows** (labor earnings, capital income, mixed income, transfers) across individuals or tax units. Distinct from [[wealth-inequality]]. Piketty decomposes total income inequality into three terms: inequality of labor income; inequality of capital ownership and its income; and the interaction (correlation) between the two.

Capital income always more concentrated than labor income where data exist. Labor typically accounts for two-thirds to three-quarters of national income, so total-income inequality often tracks labor inequality more closely than wealth inequality—except when capital incomes dominate the extreme top (Belle Époque rentiers).

## Measurement

- Survey Ginis (labor roughly 0.2–0.4; total income 0.3–0.5 in Piketty’s ranges).
- Fiscal [[top-income-shares]] for the upper tail (preferred for century-scale top dynamics).
- Factor shares: capital share α of national income (Britain/France U-curve: ~35–40% late eighteenth–nineteenth centuries → 20–25% mid-twentieth → 25–30% late twentieth/early twenty-first; rich countries 1970 capital income ~15–25% of NI → 25–30% by 2000–2010).
- Top coding and underreporting threaten survey tops; capital gains and tax havens bias fiscal series (direction: usually understate post-1970 rise).

## Patterns and Findings

**Attributed (Piketty 2014)** unless noted:

### Long-run rich-country U-curve (total income)

- **United States top decile of NI:** 45–50% in 1910s–1920s → 30–35% by late 1940s; stable ~1950–1970; rapid rise from 1980s; 2000s–2010s back to ~45–50% (slightly exceeding early-twentieth-century levels). Kuznets’s 1913–48 compression: top decile 45–50% → 30–35%.
- **France top decile:** 45–50% eve of WWI → 30–35% today; compression entirely from collapsed top capital incomes—wage hierarchy long-run stable (top wage decile ~25% of wages first decade of twentieth century and second of twenty-first).
- **Europe vs US ranking flip:** Europe more unequal 1900–1910 (top decile 45–50% vs US ~40%+); mid-century similar ~30–35%; by 2000–2010 US ~45–50% while Europe ranges Britain ~40%, France/Germany ~35%, Sweden <30%.
- **1914–1945:** Piketty holds shocks (wars, depression, inflation, progressive policy) were the only forces since the Industrial Revolution powerful enough to reduce inequality—not Kuznets’s automatic advanced-stage decline.

### Two worlds at the top (Ch.7 typology)

Very high total-income inequality (~50% top decile, ~20% top centile) can arise as:

1. **Hyperpatrimonial / society of rentiers** — extreme inherited wealth (top decile ~90% of wealth); Belle Époque / Ancien Régime Europe.
2. **Hypermeritocratic / society of supermanagers** — peak dominated by very high *labor* incomes; largely US creation of recent decades.

Piketty warns the contrast is overdrawn: types coexist; US currently combines record labor-income inequality with wealth inequality less extreme than Europe 1900–1910—risk of combined extremes ahead.

### Labor-income inequality and institutions

| Case | Top 10% of labor income (approx.) | Bottom 50% | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Scandinavia 1970s–80s | ~20% | ~35% | Most egalitarian labor cases; inclusive education (attributed) |
| Europe ~2010 | 25–30% | ~30% | France, Germany typical |
| US early 2010s | ~35% | ~25% | Most inegalitarian labor observed in his set |
| US 2030? (if trend continues) | ~45% | ~20% | Speculative column |

- Education–technology race (Goldin–Katz) helps some mid-distribution gaps; **fails** for US top 1%/0.1% explosion (discontinuity not matched by continuous skill measures).
- Minimum wages shape bottom: US real peak ~1969 (~$10.10 in 2013 dollars); early 2013 ~$7.25 (~€6), below French ~€9.43; French phases 1950–68 / 1968–83 / 1983–2012 track hierarchy expansion vs compression.
- [[supermanagers]]: Anglo-Saxon phenomenon since 1980; pay for luck (Bertrand–Mullainathan); social norms and top marginal tax rates as protective barrier or incentive to mischief; finance ~20% of top 0.1% but 80% of top not in finance.

### Captured growth (US)

Since 1980 ~15 points of US national income transferred from poorest 90% to richest 10%; 1977–2007 richest 10% took ~3/4 of growth, richest 1% nearly 60%. Piketty links rising inequality partly to financial instability (stagnant middle purchasing power → debt) but denies it as sole cause of 2008—also stresses rising β and international asset positions.

### Global / emerging

2012 PPP: equal-share world income ~€760/month; regional range ~€150–250 (sub-Saharan Africa, India) to ~€2,500–3,000 (Western Europe, North America, Japan). Emerging tax series: top centile historically ~15–25% in high phases; rebound after 1980s. Global income more unequal than output when rich countries own capital abroad (Africa income ~5% below output → ~20% of African capital foreign-owned at α~30%).

## Explanatory Theories

- **[[kuznets-curve]] optimism:** automatic decline of inequality in advanced development—Piketty rejects as description of 1914–45 (shocks) and as prediction after 1970s reverse.
- **[[piketty-capital-dynamics]]:** political history of distribution; capital-share movements with β and r; r > g for wealth; supermanager institutions for top labor.
- **Skill-biased technical change / marginal productivity:** useful for mid-range educational gaps; inadequate for executive pay (near-undefinable individual MPK; compensation committees).
- Bargaining power, unions, minimum wages, progressive tax feedback loops—side by side, attributed.

## Interaction

Feeds [[wealth-inequality]] via saving; shaped by [[progressive-taxation]] and the [[social-state]]; interacts with [[social-mobility]] and education access (US college access strongly stratified by parental income quartile 1970–2010). Factor-share movements connect to [[capital-income-ratio]].
