# Democratization

## Definition and Scope

**Democratization** here names processes by which political regimes become or remain competitive, rights-regarding, and based on peaceful contestation for office — and the reverse risks of breakdown. This page is seeded from [[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]]'s *[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]* (conditions of democratic order) and prior Tocqueville coverage; contemporary democratization research is largely not yet ingested.

## Measurement

Lipset operationalized democratic stability via qualitative/historical classification of regimes correlated with economic and social indicators. Later indices (Freedom House, Polity, V-Dem — not ingested) use different coding rules; do not mix without noting the measure.

## Patterns and Findings

- Lipset (1960, attributed): economic development raises chances of *stable* democracy; legitimacy and effectiveness jointly matter; class structure and extremist social bases condition outcomes ([[economic-development-and-democracy]], [[legitimacy-and-effectiveness]], [[working-class-authoritarianism]]).
- Tocqueville (attributed, prior ingest): democratic social state and equality of conditions as long-run social transformation with political effects.
- Putnam (2000, attributed — [[putnam-bowling-alone-study]]): higher [[civic-social-capital]] associates with better democratic practice (tax compliance, local efficacy, "we" vs "they" politics); U.S. late-20th-c. [[civic-disengagement]] threatens the associational infrastructure of democracy. Causal direction contested ([[bowling-alone-debate]]).

## Explanatory Theories

Side by side: [[modernization-theory]] (Lipset); classical liberal-aristocratic comparison (Tocqueville); [[social-capital-theory]] / civic engagement (Putnam); Marxian and elite theories of the state (see [[political-sociology]], [[the-state]]). No single wiki-voice master explanation.

## Interaction

Links to [[class-voting]] / [[democratic-class-struggle]], [[state-formation]], [[social-mobility]], [[mass-society]], and debates on development and ideology.

**Habermas (STPS, attributed):** liberal democracy’s classical infrastructure includes a [[bourgeois-public-sphere]] of critical publicity; structural transformation (mass media, manufactured publicity, welfare-state service citizenship) undermines that basis while constitutional fictions of [[public-opinion]] persist ([[public-sphere-theory]]).

**Tilly (*[[tilly-durable-inequality-1998|Durable Inequality]]* 1998, attributed):** a polity is democratic to the extent that it institutionalizes broad, relatively equal **citizenship**, protects citizens from arbitrary state action, and gives citizens significant collective control over state personnel and policy. Widespread [[categorical-inequality|categorical inequality]] threatens democratic institutions twice — exclusion incentives for powerful categories, and visible markers for inclusion/exclusion. Politics of inequality is the special case of [[durable-inequality-theory]] when a government is a party ([[the-state]]).

**Tilly (*[[tilly-identities-boundaries-social-ties-2005|Identities, Boundaries, and Social Ties]]* 2005, attributed):** operationalizes democracy as **[[protected-consultation]]** (breadth × equality × binding consultation × protection); effective [[citizenship]] is necessary not sufficient; **[[de-democratization]]** is common and reversible; Ch. 13 mechanism package and inequality conjectures (not findings); trust-network integration into public politics as a democratizing process.


**McAdam, Tarrow & Tilly (*[[mcadam-tarrow-tilly-dynamics-of-contention-2001|Dynamics of Contention]]* 2001, attributed):** Democratization is any net shift toward citizenship and [[protected-consultation]] (breadth × equality × binding consultation × protection) — not A Single Thing but contingent outcome of claims and counterclaims. Two broad paths: **weak-state** (Switzerland 1830–48) and **strong-state** (Mexico since 1968). Contention can produce or detour democratization; democracy results from, mobilizes, and reshapes popular contention. Mechanisms include brokerage, category formation, certification, and related processes (Table 9.1). See [[contentious-politics-theory]].
