## Definition and Scope

The end of the Mature Harappan urban system, c. 1900 BCE: [[mohenjo-daro|Mohenjo-daro]]
and most other large Indus cities (Harappa, Ganweriwala, Dholavira, Rakhigarhi) ceased to
function as urban centers, and numerous smaller settlements across Sindh, the Kulli
Domain, and Cholistan were abandoned or severely depopulated. Gregory
[[possehl-gregory-l|Possehl]] calls the successor material horizon "Posturban Harappan"
and explicitly frames the process throughout as a **transformation**, not a "collapse" or
a conquest — the term "eclipse," used by earlier scholars, is treated by him as a
misleading overstatement of the pattern. Mohenjo-daro itself shows essentially no
habitation between c. 1900 BCE and the Kushan period, but Harappa, though reduced in size
and complexity, was not wholly abandoned; some residents there interred their dead in the
successor "Cemetery H," giving its name to the associated Cemetery H culture (two burial
strata: earth burials, complete in the Eastern Section and fractional/"dismembered" in the
Western; and pot burials, human bone stuffed into pottery vessels, sometimes mixing
several individuals including children, without grave goods). The first radiocarbon date
for the Cemetery H assemblage at Harappa is 1730 BCE (calibration status not stated in the
source excerpt ingested here — flagged as unspecified).

## Measurement

Measured through settlement-count and site-size survey data by phase and by region.
Production of luxury and prestige craft goods (long barrel-cylinder beads, etched
carnelian beads, inscribed stamp seals) ceased; writing (the [[indus-script|Indus
script]]) was no longer practiced; long-distance maritime and overland trade contracted
sharply, though contact with the Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex (BMAC) continued
in the Greater Indus region for a time.

## Patterns and Findings

**This was not uniform depopulation but regional redistribution — explicitly not a
"collapse" in Possehl's own framing.** Sindh, the Kulli Domain, and Cholistan were largely
depopulated of settled farmers, while settlement counts rose in Punjab, Haryana, western
Uttar Pradesh, and northern Rajasthan (the Eastern Domain, where Mature Harappan-to-
Posturban site counts rose from 218 to 853, even as average site size fell sharply from
13.54 to 3.55 hectares — a pattern read as fragmentation into many smaller sites rather
than simple depopulation). Gujarat (the Sorath and Eastern Domains) remained comparatively
stable, and in places — notably [[rojdi|Rojdi]] in Saurashtra — expanded and even rebuilt
at precisely the period Mohenjo-daro and the Sindh/Punjab cities were being abandoned;
Possehl uses this Rojdi pattern explicitly to caution that "the so-called eclipse or end
of the Indus Civilization might well be an overstatement and in need of radical
reexamination." Possehl frames this partial reversion to regional diversity as a return
toward the pre-urban Early Harappan "cultural mosaic" character. Regionally, the
successor sequence continued differently in different places: in Indian Punjab, Posturban
material (per J. P. Joshi's excavations at Bhagwanpura and Dadheri) persists until just
before 1000 BCE, overlapping with the appearance of Painted Gray Ware and the Early Iron
Age; in the Sorath Domain, the Late Harappan tradition (Lustrous Red Ware) ends c. 1500
BCE, followed by an apparent settlement gap until Northern Black Polished Ware appears
in the 2nd–3rd century BCE — a gap Possehl suggests is a fieldwork artifact rather than a
genuine occupation hiatus.

## Explanatory Theories (attributed; causal weight held open per protocol)

Multiple explanations have been proposed and are argued over in detail on
[[indus-transformation-debate]], including Wheeler's now-largely-rejected Aryan-invasion
thesis, river avulsion, the (rebutted) Raikes/Dales dam hypothesis, and G. Singh's
climatic-desiccation hypothesis. Possehl's own preferred explanation locates the primary
cause in an internal, sociocultural/ideological failure — an over-integrated civilizational
system insufficiently able to adapt to changing conditions — while treating tectonically
driven desiccation of the Sarasvati/Ghaggar-Hakra river system as a genuine but
non-sufficient **contributing** factor (`contributed_to`, mechanism: loss of irrigation-
supporting river flow via drainage capture), explicitly never a sole `caused_by`. River
and climate factors are not asserted as sole causes anywhere in this record.

## Kenoyer 1998 — Localization, gradual decline, anti-invasion, legacy

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]], *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* 1998, esp. Chs. 1 and 9.)*

**Gradual disintegration, not mystery disappearance.** Kenoyer frames the end of Indus cities as **gradual** disintegration of economic and political structure into localized states/chiefdoms — not sudden obliteration. Using a pipal-sapling metaphor, he argues the first urban civilization faded as new cultures emerged on eastern and northern edges; it took **over a thousand years** for the political-cultural center of the northern subcontinent to shift from the Indus Valley to the middle Ganga; people living between Indus decline (1900–1300 BCE) and Early Historic cities (800–300 BCE) would likely not have perceived the macro-shift. This converges with Possehl’s “transformation not collapse” language on this page while using the Shaffer/Kenoyer **Localization Era** vocabulary of the [[indus-valley-tradition|Indus Valley Tradition]].

**Anti-invasion position.** Contrary to older Indo-Aryan invasion narratives and uncritical Rigvedic readings of the Mohenjo-daro late skeletons, Kenoyer calls invasion/replacement an “**absurd theory**.” His explicit claim: **no archaeological or biological evidence** for invasions or mass migrations into the Indus Valley between the end of the Harappan Phase ca. 1900 BCE and the Early Historic period ca. 600 BCE. BMAC (ca. 1900–1700 BCE) highland contacts may have involved communities some of whom spoke Indo-Aryan dialects, but skeletal-biology studies he cites find **no new populations** in the northern subcontinent at decline and no major change in long-term highland–Indus contact patterns. Language change and population replacement must not be equated (four-way non-identity). Full dispute lives on [[indus-transformation-debate]] and [[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate]] — this page records Kenoyer’s position only.

**Internal factors and environmental contributors.** In the absence of direct external force, Kenoyer attributes decline primarily to **internal factors** that undermined ruling elites: overextended trade and political-control networks disrupted by river-pattern change, flooding, and crop failure; refugees and overcrowding producing a **legitimacy crisis** for ritual leaders and political rulers, ending in new elites and localized polities around 1900 BCE. Environmental mechanism he states: sedimentation and tectonics capture Saraswati-system water into Sutlej (west) and Yamuna (east); Indus eastward swing floods and silts many settlements; Mohenjo-daro mounds survive on higher land with massive platforms; Harappa continues inhabited but southern long-distance trade infrastructure is damaged as Ghaggar-Hakra-bed settlements are abandoned. River/climate factors are `contributed_to` / concurrent stressors in his synthesis, not sole `caused_by` — parallel to Possehl’s non-sufficient river contribution.

**Localization Era structure (~1900–1300 BCE).** The previously integrated Indus Valley and Gujarat break into **three major localized cultures** (new painted ceramics, geometric seals, new burial customs) for ~600 years until ca. 1300 BCE:
- **Punjab Phase** (north, including Harappa and sites east into northern India) — Cemetery H culture: gradual transition from Harappan burial practice; earlier Cemetery H layout like Harappan coffin burials; later adult cremation and child urn burials with elaborately painted motifs incorporating some Indus symbols (pipal, horned figures) with new rituals; eastern expansion into Ganga-Yamuna Doab with rural dispersal; western highland and coastal trade (lapis, turquoise, marine shell) breaks down; faience refined as substitute.
- **Jhukar Phase** (Sindh and parts of Baluchistan) — continues at Jhukar, Chanhudaro, Mohenjo-daro, Amri into Pirak; **absence of script and Harappan-style animal seals**; circular geometric seals; strong technological continuity with late Indus occupation.
- **Rangpur Phase** (Kutch, Saurashtra, mainland Gujarat) — settlement increase (possible Saraswati-region refugees); Rann siltation reorganizes shipping; diagnostic Harappan package (inscribed seals, weights, perforated vessels, terracotta cakes, pointed goblets) disappears; pottery graffiti may continue after stamp seals fail; north–south trade rupture until Early Historic ca. 600 BCE.

Summer crops (rice, millet, sorghum) and gradual integration of horse, donkey, and Bactrian camel into plains economies enable Localization-era adaptation and eastward expansion (Mehrgarh–Nausharo–Pirak sequence as continuous Kachi Plain transformation; Jarrige’s indigenous intensification argument, as Kenoyer relays it). Seals, writing, and chert weights (possible taxation) are **no longer needed** in the Localization package.

**Legacy / second urbanization.** Kenoyer argues there is **no significant hiatus** between Indus and Early Historic urban civilizations: major subsistence packages already present in Harappan or Localization eras; multicropping surplus supports later cities and armies; trade networks rebuild on and beyond earlier Indus interaction systems. Continuities he stresses include craft technologies (faience → glass ca. 1700 BCE; bead drilling trajectories; shell bangles, trumpets, libation vessels into later Hindu and other practices — continuity of object classes, not identity of religions), settlement planning and water management patterns, and a strong link between Indus and Early Historic **weight systems** — while **rejecting** convincing continuity between Indus script and Brahmi. The Indus Valley Tradition is, in his closing thesis, the first urban state-level society in southern Asia but only the beginning of a longer subcontinental trajectory (Indo-Gangetic Tradition). Living traditional crafts, in his view, preserve optimal adaptive choices rather than cultural “stagnation.”

## Wright (2010) — uneven regional transformation, Jhukar, Sorath continuity, and the household crop-processing shift

*(Attributed to [[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]], *The Ancient Indus* (Cambridge, 2010), Ch. 11.)* Wright frames the process methodologically as a distinction between "general" causes affecting the civilization as a whole (environmental shift, trade disruption, invasion claims) and "local" causes specific to a region or site (e.g., Mohenjo-daro's civic decay). She reports the Aryan-invasion/"massacre" thesis as refuted, following Kennedy (2000) (see [[bioarchaeology-of-the-indus]]; [[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate]]), and cautions explicitly against treating environmental change as a sole or ultimate cause, favoring examination of regional responses instead.

**Regional unevenness.** At Harappa (Upper Indus), continuity is documented across four site areas into the Post-urban/Late Harappan period, including the [[cemetery-h|Cemetery H]] mortuary sequence, in-situ hearths, and a cache of 133 beads showing continuation of Urban-period craft technology — with no evidence of abrupt exodus or new-population intrusion, though encroachment onto streets and public space signals disorder; the site was ultimately abandoned at the end of the Post-urban period. In the Lower Indus, Mohenjo-daro's decline culminated in comparatively rapid abandonment; the "**Jhukar**" pottery style (documented at Jhukar, Mohenjo-daro, and Amri) marks the end of Post-urban Lower Indus occupation with new pigments and motifs, but with enough continuity that Wright reads it as a transformation rather than the sharp break originally proposed by Mackay (1938). At Chanhu-daro, settlement becomes "haphazard and less focused": square stamp seals are replaced by circular seals with new motifs, terracotta female figurines and cubical weights become rare, and Indus script disappears except for incised pottery marks — read by Wright as signaling the end of the administrative system. In the Kutch/Gujarat/Sorath domain, Dholavira and Kuntasi show clear breakdown (erosion, makeshift repairs, disrupted layouts, reduction to a small rural village), but **Sorath Harappan sites show continuity by contrast** — the region having been semi-independent of the Harappan core even during the Urban period (Possehl 2002) — with [[rojdi|Rojdi]] continuously occupied and rebuilt, and 231 Post-urban sites recorded regionally (Mughal 1992a), 96% of which changed occupation status and 93% of which were newly founded (Shaffer and Lichtenstein 1999), read as "considerable settlement mobility and influx of human populations" rather than depopulation.

**Household-level crop processing as political, not environmental, reorganization.** Post-urban seed assemblages at Harappa and Rojdi show high densities of chaff and weed by-products, indicating a shift to household-level (rather than centralized/communal) crop processing (Weber 1999, 2003; Fuller and Madella 2000) — a shift Wright reads as evidence of political and social reorganization "rather than an environmental crisis." She reports a parallel pastoral shift at Post-urban Harappa toward reduced cattle traction/dairying and a reversion toward pre-urban emphasis on sheep and goat (L. Miller 2004), plausibly reflecting reduced need for traction and transport amid deintensified agriculture and contracting exchange networks.

**The continuity debate.** Wright records, without adjudicating in wiki voice, an explicit disagreement with Shaffer and Lichtenstein (1999), who argue that cultural continuity can be traced from the Post-urban period through Painted Gray Ware into the early historic "second urbanism" of the Ganges Valley (c. 800–600 BCE), reading the eastward settlement shift as regional population movement rather than civilizational discontinuity ("It is possible now to discern cultural continuities linking specific social entities in South Asia into one cultural tradition," Shaffer and Lichtenstein 1999:255–6). Wright's own position withholds judgment: she argues that claims of continuity require an unbroken evidentiary chain for the defining Indus traits (planned cities, technical virtuosity, standardized weights, seals and sealings, narrative imagery, the Indus script) — all of which end in the Post-urban period — and that, absent such evidence, city-state leadership and hegemony had been severely diminished by the end of the Post-urban period, with disruptions that were "not wholly ecological." She notes as comparative context that the Indus Civilization was short-lived relative to Mesopotamia and Egypt, and even relative to Maya culture, which is traceable in altered form from as early as 400 BCE to the present (Demarest 2004).
