# Eutrophication

Eutrophication — nutrient enrichment of water bodies — is one of [[roberts-neil|Roberts]]' ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) principal cases of the "natural or cultural?" problem that defines later-[[holocene|Holocene]] environmental history: an outcome that can arise either from slow natural lake ageing or from industrial-era pollution, distinguishable only by dated sediment evidence.

## Definition and Scope

By Liebig's Law of the Minimum (via Hutchinson 1973), aquatic productivity is limited by the least-available nutrient — usually nitrogen or phosphorus; eutrophication is the ecological response to raising that limit. Roberts distinguishes natural eutrophication (lake infilling, reduced hypolimnion volume) from **cultural eutrophication** driven by sewage, fertilizer runoff, and phosphate detergents.

## Measurement

Reconstructed from [[diatom-analysis|diatom]]-inferred phosphorus, sediment phosphorus and organic-matter chemistry, and chironomid and cladoceran remains, dated by [[lead-210-dating|²¹⁰Pb/¹³⁷Cs]] and [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]]. Roberts stresses (attributed) that rising organic matter alone is inconclusive, since it may reflect catchment [[soil-erosion|erosion]] rather than in-lake productivity.

## Patterns and Findings

In attributed voice, from Roberts (1998):
- **Lough Neagh** (Northern Ireland): diatom and chironomid influx rose sharply in the late 19th–20th centuries — tenfold above the pre-AD 1700 baseline — with phosphorus inputs tracking Victorian sewerage, then detergents from the 1950s (Battarbee 1978; Patrick 1988). Roberts notes the correlation is "convincing circumstantial evidence," not proof.
- **Lake Washington** (Seattle): after 1967 diversion of nutrient-rich wastewater, sediment phosphorus fell and the lake recovered — evidence of lake "elasticity" (Edmondson 1974).
- **Lake George** (Uganda): naturally hyper-eutrophic for ≥500 years without pollution — a caution that eutrophic ≠ anthropogenic.

## Explanatory Theories

Cultural eutrophication is specifically a product of industrial/urban society; Roberts (writing 1998, date-stamped) notes it was historically minor outside the industrialized world "although this is changing fast" with urbanization.

## Interaction

Downstream of [[soil-erosion]] (nutrient-bearing sediment) and agricultural intensification; the inverse pollution process is [[freshwater-acidification]].

## Debates

Whether a given lake's enrichment is natural or cultural is resolved empirically case by case through palaeolimnology — the same method that settled the parallel [[freshwater-acidification|acidification]] dispute.
