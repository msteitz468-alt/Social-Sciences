# Flexible Employment

Intensified non-standard, flexible, and contingent forms of work and employment associated (in Castells’s analysis) with the informational paradigm and the [[network-enterprise|network enterprise]] — “flex-timers,” networked labor, and dualized/fragmented labor markets. Primary treatment in this wiki from *[[castells-rise-of-network-society-1996|The Rise of the Network Society]]* Ch. 4 (with G-7 series and Carnoy collaboration noted).

## Definition and Scope

Castells (attributed) treats flexibility along multiple dimensions (working time, job stability, location, social contract) rather than as a single “part-time” residual. The work process is the main lever by which the informational paradigm and [[globalization]] affect society at large. Typology of networkers (networkers, networked, switched-off) maps inclusion in production networks. Related: collapse of separation among working, personal, and family time via multi-tasking — link to [[work-family-conflict]] and [[timeless-time]].

## Measurement

G-7 employment and occupational structure ~1920–2005 (Singelmann-based service/information sectorization; Appendix B methodology); OECD flexibility indicators; country cases (US, Japan dual market, Dutch part-time, Silicon Valley contingent work, Canada). Appendix tables in the source are incomplete in text conversion — prose-cited statistics preferred.

## Patterns and Findings

**Attributed Castells (Ch. 4):**

- Long-run shift toward service and information-handling occupations in G-7, along **two paths**: Anglo-Saxon service-economy model vs Japan/Germany industrial-production model — not a single post-industrial template.
- **No systematic IT → mass unemployment** across advanced economies; EU vs US/Japan job-creation differences are institutional/political as much as technological (jobless-society thesis rejected as main story).
- Growth of temporary, part-time, self-employed, and otherwise contingent work — “flex-timers” as structural feature of informational labor markets.
- Core vs disposable labor; skills-mismatch accounts treated as thin relative to organizational restructuring.
- Deeper diagnosis: dualism as surface of **fragmentation** — labor disaggregated in performance, fragmented in organization, diversified in existence, divided in collective action; capital global, labor local (Conclusion).

Wiki voice not used for mechanism claims; multi-source convergence on rising contingent work is a broader literature (only Castells and Sennett ingested here for this page).

**The personal consequences (attributed [[sennett-richard|Sennett]], *[[sennett-corrosion-of-character-1998|The Corrosion of Character]]* 1998):** Where Castells maps flexible employment at the macro/structural level, Sennett takes the micro/experiential angle — arguing that the same restructuring corrodes [[character]] at the level of the person. He anatomizes [[flexibility]] as a three-element regime of power ("concentration without centralization"), reads the computerized workplace as *illegible* (deskilling that breeds indifference rather than [[alienation]]), treats mandatory risk-taking and age-devaluation as erosions of coherent work-narratives, and diagnoses the new "teamwork" ethic as "power without authority." His evidence is ethnographic vignettes, not statistics — illustrative, not representative. Whether these personal-corrosion claims hold is the substance of [[flexibility-corrosion-debate]].

## Debates

Jobless-society thesis vs restructuring; dualism vs fragmentation; whether flexibility liberates or represses (Castells, citing Mulgan on networks: “to gain position, to outcommunicate”); and — at the level of the person — whether flexible employment corrodes character ([[flexibility-corrosion-debate]]).

## Tsing / precarity beyond the regular job (2015)

*[[tsing-mushroom-2015|Tsing]]* argues modernization promised “standard employment” with stable wages and benefits, but such jobs became rare; supply chains let lead firms abandon commitments to controlling labor, ending progress expectations tied to regularized jobs. Commercial matsutake picking exemplifies livelihood without regular jobs — pickers reject “work” as boss-commanded labor in favor of “searching” *(Tsing 2015)*.
