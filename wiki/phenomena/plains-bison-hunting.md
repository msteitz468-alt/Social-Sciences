# Plains Bison Hunting

## Definition and Scope

**Plains bison hunting** is the long-running economic adaptation of the North American Great
Plains — communal and individual hunting of bison for meat, fat, hides, and bone — from
Paleo-Indian times to the 19th century. As [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991,
after George Frison)]] stresses, the popular image of the horse-mounted Plains warrior froze a
picture that in fact describes only c. 1830–1880; the Folsom-era discoveries of the 1920s proved
the Plains had been **continuously occupied for at least 10,000 years**, refuting the older view
(Lewis Henry Morgan) that Plains life began only with the horse.

## Measurement

The record is dominated by **bison-kill sites**, which Fagan cautions "may give a skewed
impression of Paleo-Indian life." Kills are read through minimum-number-of-individuals counts,
**seasonality** (tooth eruption, fetal bone), and detailed **butchery reconstruction** — Joe Ben
Wheat's Olsen-Chubbuck study estimated ~24,750 kg of meat from ~157 bison.

## Patterns and Findings

- **Communal drives.** From the Paleo-Indian **Olsen-Chubbuck** (c. 6500 BC, bison stampeded into
  a dry gully) and **Casper** (parabolic-dune trap) sites through the Archaic **bison jumps** and
  **corrals**, mass drives required careful orchestration — decoy hunters in bison hides,
  controlled stampede direction. **Head-Smashed-In** (Alberta) was used for **more than 7,000
  years**, its stone-cairn drive lines running up to 8 km.
- **Intensity peaks with the bow.** Communal foot-hunting reached its greatest prehistoric
  intensity after c. AD 550, coinciding with the bow and arrow's arrival on the Plains; Late
  Prehistoric Avonlea and Besant hunters built sophisticated log corrals and may have produced
  surplus for trade with horticultural societies at the Plains margins.
- **Ecology drives the record.** Bonfire Shelter (Texas) shows bison hunting confined to the two
  periods of good grass cover since 10,000 BC; the Vore site records five hunts over ~140 years,
  each following higher-rainfall periods. At Garnsey (c. AD 1550) hunters selectively took fat-rich
  male bison in spring.
- **The horse era and collapse.** Horses reached the northern Plains in the 18th century; differing
  Spanish (restrictive) and French/English (trading) firearms policies created regional
  horse/firearm imbalances (the Blackfoot mounted c. 1740). After Euro-American contact, the fur
  trade, repeating rifles, railroads, and introduced cattle disease drove the bison to
  near-extinction by the late 19th century.

## Explanatory Theories

Fagan records Bamforth's model in which numerical strength from aggregation (achieved through
communal hunts) conferred long-term military advantage, helping explain the northeast-to-southwest
pattern of Plains tribal migration. Shamanistic ritual accompanied large hunts throughout the
sequence (inferred from a possible shaman's structure at the Paleo-Indian Jones-Miller site and
from historic analogy).

## Interaction

Plains bison hunting fed and was fed by relations with neighboring farming societies (surplus
meat/hides traded for maize and goods) and, on the eastern margins, with the Plains Village
horticultural tradition. It is the classic North American case of a **"collector"** subsistence
strategy (in Binford's forager–collector terms).

## Debates

- **Altithermal depopulation.** Some argued mid-Holocene aridity emptied the Plains; Fagan finds
  this "unlikely," citing continued occupation at Hawken and Head-Smashed-In.
- **Skewed sampling.** Kill sites over-represent bison and mass drives; most hunting may have relied
  on individual stalking skill (Frison), so the dramatic kill sites may be atypical.
