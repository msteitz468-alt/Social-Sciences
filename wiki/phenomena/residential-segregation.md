# Residential Segregation

## Definition and Scope

Spatial separation of social groups in residential space. In U.S. urban sociology the load-bearing case is **black–white** segregation: [[massey-douglas-s|Massey]] and [[denton-nancy-a|Denton]] argue it is not analogous to transient immigrant enclaves but a unique, institutionally maintained system they analogize to apartheid (*American Apartheid*, 1993, attributed). [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] (*[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]*, 2012) treats segregation as the **enduring spatial frame** that concentrates multi-strand disadvantage, shapes perceptions of disorder, and structures mobility destinations — not only a compositional background condition.

## Measurement

[[segregation-indices]]: evenness ([[index-of-dissimilarity]]), exposure ([[isolation-index]]), clustering, centralization, concentration; composite [[hypersegregation]] when ≥4 dimensions are high. Sampson/PHDCN also use percent-black continuity, race-stratified mobility tables, and spatial clustering (Moran’s I) of disadvantage, immigration, crime, and [[collective-efficacy]].

## Patterns and Findings

- **Pre-1900:** relatively low black–white segregation in many U.S. cities (Massey & Denton Ch 2, attributed).
- **Ghetto construction:** early–mid 20th century consolidation via violence, restrictive covenants, dual housing markets, FHA/HOLC practices, public housing siting (attributed).
- **Post-1968:** persistence despite Fair Housing Act; 1980 hypersegregation in sixteen major metros (attributed).
- **Group contrast:** black segregation far exceeds Hispanic and Asian averages in the book's 1980 comparisons (attributed).

**Sampson (2012, attributed — GAC ch. 5+):**
- **Asymmetric racial change 1960–2000 (Chicago community areas):** stably black, stably white, white→black / partial→segregated black transitions, and few enduring integrated areas (e.g. Hyde Park, Near West Side); **not one neighborhood transitioned from predominantly black to predominantly white**; above ~**50% black**, neighborhoods maintained or increased black share — durable, not only high, segregation.
- **Percent-black inertia.** Percent black 1990–2000 correlates **0.96** nationally and **0.98** in Chicago; nationwide, thousands of neighborhoods transitioned white→black, but of ~65,000 tracts only about **ten** went from >60% black to ≥60% white (two in Chicago).
- **Race–class ecological non-overlap.** 2000 tracts ≥75% black vs ≥75% white: mean per capita income **$12,276** vs **$42,508**; entire white distribution sits right of the black mean — blacks and whites live in “different social worlds” of work and income; unemployment–economic-dependence coupling strong in minority/mixed areas, flat in white areas.
- **Disadvantage continuity race-contingent.** Concentrated-disadvantage continuity 1990s much higher in segregated black neighborhoods (**r=0.83**) than white (**r=0.24**).
- **Perceived disorder and race.** Sampson–Raudenbush program: racial/ethnic composition shapes **perceptions** of disorder beyond observed SSO disorder — segregation enters cognition of place, not only objective conditions (restated in GAC; see also [[broken-windows-debate]]).
- **MTO and hypersegregation.** Chicago MTO destinations remained ~**90% black** for experimentals and controls; poverty reduced modestly while concentrated-disadvantage and social-process differences stayed marginal within larger structural inequality — mobility experiments do not easily escape the segregated opportunity structure ([[neighborhood-effects-debate]]).
- **Spatial dependence.** Disadvantage, crime, immigration, CE, incarceration show strong spatial autocorrelation — segregation is multi-neighborhood, not tract-bounded.

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Claim (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Massey & Denton | Ongoing discrimination + prejudice + institutional arrangements manufacture and maintain segregation; SES does not explain black isolation |
| Ecological / Chicago tradition | Invasion-succession, natural areas ([[park-burgess-city-study]]) — historical baseline Massey & Denton partly historicize and criticize as incomplete |
| Class / restructuring | Wilson (*[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|TTD]]* 1987): economic change, joblessness, and middle-class exit shape [[concentrated-poverty]] and [[social-isolation]]; ongoing segregation less central than in Massey & Denton (see [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]) |
| Sampson / PHDCN | Segregation magnifies [[concentrated-disadvantage]]; racial inequality concentrates the truly disadvantaged and undermines CE (Sampson–Wilson racial-invariance: community causes same by race, exposure differs by segregation); mobility is **structural sorting** — “neighborhoods choose people” via chainlike destination networks |
