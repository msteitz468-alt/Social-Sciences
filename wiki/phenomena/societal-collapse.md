# Societal Collapse (Ancient States and Civilizations)

## Definition and Scope

**Societal collapse** here means the drastic restructuring or failure of ancient **states** and, sometimes separately, of **civilizations** (shared ideological/cultural spheres). The page privileges archaeological and comparative treatments of early complex societies; modern state failure is adjacent but not the default case.

All major theoretical claims below are **attributed** — principally to [[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] *[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]* (2005, Ch. 6) and the 1988 collapse literature he surveys.

## Measurement

No single index. Indicators used in the literature Yoffee reviews include: capital abandonment; loss of administrative literacy; failure of staple/finance flows; disappearance of elite monumental programs; persistence or extinction of civilizational identity (who still wants to be “Mesopotamian,” “Maya,” etc.). Collapse of **government** need not equal collapse of **civilization**.

## Patterns and Findings

### Yoffee’s program (2005, attributed)

- Collapse is **under-studied** relative to “rise” in neo-evolutionist social evolutionary theory; 1988 was a banner year (Tainter; Yoffee & Cowgill).
- Early states were **not harmonious systems** disrupted only from outside; they generated conflict and bungling as ordinary conditions.
- Prefer **institution-by-institution** analysis over total “house of cards” or hypercoherence short-circuits ([[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]]’s systems pathologies are useful but incomplete).
- Map **social roles and identities** created and relinquished; cleavage planes visible from state formation onward, not late “pathologies.”
- **Regeneration** is common: successor states often consciously model themselves on predecessors (contra pure terminal decline).
- **Civilization vs state:** Mesopotamian **states** rose and fell repeatedly while Mesopotamian **civilization** (shared symbols, writing tradition, identity) persisted until people no longer needed to be Mesopotamian (late first millennium BC / Hellenistic mutation of identity and memory, on Yoffee’s reading).

### Mesopotamian cases (Yoffee’s readings, attributed; secondary chronology)

| Episode | Yoffee’s emphasis |

| Old Akkadian | Political integration projects and their limits; climate-catastrophe monocauses (Weiss/Courty) contested |

### Non-Mesopotamian notes in the same book

Maya collapse is **regionally uneven** (Webster 2002 via Yoffee): southern Classic city-states abandoned while northern trajectories differed. Chaco and Cahokia (Ch. 7) are **not** failed states but alternative “big bang” trajectories that did not evolve the naturalized ideology of rulership of primary states.

### Late Bronze Age Eastern Mediterranean (Cline 2014, attributed)

[[cline-eric-h|Cline]]’s *[[cline-1177-bc-2014|1177 B.C.]]* is the collection’s detailed case study of a **multi-polity international system** collapsing (not a single empire): Aegean palace centers, Hittite imperial capital, Ugarit and other Levantine cities, Cyprus, with Egypt surviving weakened. Full case page: [[late-bronze-age-collapse]]. Cline’s preferred synthesis is a multi-causal **“perfect storm”** (earthquakes, drought/famine, migrations/invasions including [[sea-peoples]], internal troubles) magnified by interdependence and systems cascade — explicitly against Sea Peoples monocausality. He mobilizes [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]’s 1979 systems-collapse features and cites complexity-theory analogies (Bell; Johnson) as promising but under-applied in archaeology (as of 2014).

### Scott densification (*Against the Grain*, 2017)

[[scott-james-c|Scott]] (*[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]*, 2017, esp. Ch. 6) asks us to **normalize** early-state “collapse” and to query whether “dark ages” were tragedies for nonelites. All claims **attributed**; complements Yoffee/Cowgill modularity and Cline multi-causal LBA work without replacing them. Links: [[state-formation]], [[the-state]], [[grain-hypothesis]], [[nonstate-spaces]].

**Fragility as the default.** Scott holds that earliest states’ vulnerability was so great that their **rare appearance and rarer persistence** require explanation — not their collapse. As a subspecies of sedentary grain communities, states shared general perils of sedentary dissolution plus fragilities particular to political entities. Scholarly consensus (as Scott reports: Adams on Ur III’s short stability; Yoffee et al. on fragile power concentrations; Broodbank on foundation/abandonment oscillation) treats first archaic states as fragile and short-lived; causes lack consensus and evidence is rarely dispositive. Periodic demise is often **overdetermined**.

**What “collapse” means / does not mean.** Archaeologically (Ur III ~2000 BCE; Old Kingdom Egypt ~2100 BCE; Minoan palatial Crete ~1450 BCE, as Scott’s examples), collapse means at least abandonment or destruction of the **monumental court center**, typically read as loss of higher-order elites, monumental building, administrative literacy, large-scale trade, and specialist craft. It does **not** necessarily mean regional population decline; decline in health, well-being, or nutrition (may represent improvement); or dissolution of **culture** rather than reformulation and decentralization. Civilizations should not be confused with the states they typically outlast; larger political units should not be unreflectively preferred to smaller ones.

**Collapse as disassembly.** Following Yoffee and Cowgill’s use of Herbert Simon’s modularity / “nearly decomposable” units, Scott treats much that passes as collapse as **disassembly** of larger fragile polities into smaller, often more stable, self-sufficient components. Disappearance of the apical center need not imply disorder or trauma for those units (Nissen caution against equating end of centralization with political trouble). Abandonment of a center often entails little loss of life and is better considered **redistribution** of population. Above all: “the well-being of a population must never be confounded with the power of a court or state center.”

**Intrinsic agro-ecological fault lines.** Beyond contingent invasion, Scott privileges systematic state-linked causes: (1) **disease** from concentrating crops, people, livestock, and pathogens — scale, trade (Algaze/Wengrow “Uruk world system”), and warfare enlarge infection spheres; his “hunch” is that epidemics explain many sudden unexplained abandonments; flight empties the urban node the state depends on without necessarily large total population loss; (2) **deforestation** and upstream siltation/floods from urban wood appetite; (3) **salinization** and yield decline under intensive irrigation (Adams on progressive salinity after ~2400 BCE). “Sudden death” (epidemic, great flood) differs from chronic debilitation (siltation, declining yields) — for which “collapse” is “far too histrionic,” tragic mainly for elites. Grain-complex structural vulnerability: single annual cereal harvest; crowding disease; elite extraction concentrated on the core nearest the center (the lucrative ~48 km overland grain-requisition radius as Achilles’ heel).

**Praising collapse / dark ages as possible welfare gains.** Scott challenges the prejudice that aggregation at state apexes equals civilizational triumph and decentralization equals failure. Why deplore collapse when it often depicts disaggregation of a complex, fragile, oppressive state into smaller decentralized fragments? Subjects may leave agriculture and urban centers to evade taxes, conscription, epidemics, and oppression — apparent “regression” to foraging/pastoralism as **emancipation** (without denying predation outside the state). “Dark age” is often dynastic propaganda; darkness is empirical, not a label. Cases he sketches: post–Ur III Amorite hegemony as more benign than Ur III (abolishing most taxes/forced labor on one reading; Richardson: seven of nine centuries 2500–1600 BCE were division/decentralization without necessary famine/violence); Egypt’s First Intermediate Period as brief decentralization from low Nile, not pure darkness; Greek Dark Age loss of Linear B yet oral epics as a more democratic cultural form than elite literacy. Speculatively, what is read as collapse may often be triggered by **resistance and flight** of desperate core subjects when rulers press the grain core under emergency.

**Source bias.** Melancholic associations of collapse stem from heroic archaeology’s focus on monumental centers as icons of civilization — what was lost were concentrated ruins, written records, and luxuries (the apex of the pyramid), not necessarily the broader social base. The issue of collapse itself is an artifact of mistaking walled monumental centers for civilization.
