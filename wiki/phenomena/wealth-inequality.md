# Wealth Inequality

## Definition and Scope

Unequal distribution of **net assets** (real estate, financial assets, business capital, net of debt) across individuals or households—distinct from [[income-inequality]] though correlated. In all countries and periods with data that Piketty assembles, capital ownership (and capital income) is **always more concentrated** than labor income: top 10% of wealth always owns more than 50% (sometimes ~90%); bottom 50% owns nothing or almost nothing (<10%, generally <5%).

Income inequality can be decomposed into inequality of labor income, inequality of capital ownership/income, and their correlation. Greater positive correlation between high labor and high capital income raises total inequality; traditional rentier societies often showed low or negative correlation (owners need not work).

## Measurement

- Top wealth shares from estate multipliers, tax records, and surveys (surveys undercount tops).
- Aggregate intensity via [[capital-income-ratio]] β and capital share α = r × β ([[first-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]]).
- Inheritance flow as % of national income ($b_y = \mu \times m \times \beta$).
- Composition gradient within the top: near ~€1M, real estate often half or more of wealth; above €5–10M, real estate <20–10%, wealth primarily stock—“true wealth” is financial/business assets.
- Methods: [[historical-wealth-accounts]], [[top-income-shares]] (capital-income side).

**No society**, Piketty reports, has ever shown “mild” capital inequality in his sense (bottom half owning ~1/5–1/4 of wealth). Even Scandinavia 1970s–80s “medium” wealth: top 10% ~50%.

## Patterns and Findings

Wiki voice only for the research object. **Attributed (Piketty 2014)** unless noted:

### Structural regularity and the patrimonial middle class

- Principal twentieth-century structural transformation of wealth distribution: growth of a true [[patrimonial-middle-class]]—middle 40% owning roughly a quarter to a third of national wealth (Europe today ~35% when top 10% hold ~60% and bottom 50% ~5%).
- 1900–1910 Europe (France, Britain, Sweden, etc.): top 10% ~90% of wealth; top 1% >50% (Britain sometimes >60%); middle 40% just over 5%—no wealth middle class. Same order (top 10% 80–90%, top 1% 50–60%) in traditional agrarian societies where sources allow.
- Today: Europe top 10% ~60%, US >70%; bottom half still ~5% as in 1910; middle class collected “crumbs” that were nonetheless historically major. Top centile wealth share Europe: >50% c. 1900 → ~20–25% late twentieth / early twenty-first.

### Country trajectories (capital ownership)

| France postwar–2010 | 60–70% (1950–70) → ~60–65% | 20–30% | Incomplete recovery from 1914–45 |
| Britain Belle Époque | >90% | nearly 70% (1910–20) | Slightly more concentrated than France |
| Britain later | 60–65% (1970s) → ~70% today | ~20% (1970–80) → 25–30% | |
| Sweden 1900–10 / 1970–80 / 2010 | high like FR/UK / ~50% / near France | high / slightly >15% / risen | Not always structurally egalitarian |
| US ~1800 | low (new immigrant society) | — | North low; South closer to Europe |
| US 1910 | ~80% | ~45% | Below Europe |
| US 2010 | >70% | close to 35% | Milder 1910–50 deconcentration than Europe |

Who gained from European deconcentration: almost exclusively the middle 40%, not the poorest half (still ~5%, never >10% even in Sweden).

### Capital intensity (β) and shocks

- Europe private wealth: late nineteenth century ~6–7 years of NI; 2–3 after 1914–45; early twenty-first century approaching 5–6 (Britain, France). Physical destruction only a fraction of 1913–50 collapse; foreign portfolios, low saving, and low postwar asset prices (rent control, regulation) dominate.
- US β more stable (~4–5 years much of the twentieth century); Europe as avant-garde of new [[patrimonial-capitalism]] by early 2000s.
- Global wealth inequality early 2010s ≈ Europe 1900–1910 orders of magnitude: top thousandth ~20%, top centile ~50%, top decile 80–90%, bottom half <5% (highly uncertain).
- Forbes billionaires: ~140 (1987) → >1,400 (2013); share of global private wealth 0.4% → 1.5%; top fractile real returns ~6–7%/year vs world wealth per adult ~2.1%.

### Inheritance and r > g

- French annual inheritance/gift flow: ~20–25% of NI in nineteenth century → ~4–5% mid-twentieth → ~15% by 2010; share of inherited wealth in private capital ~80–90% pre-WWI → just over 40% in 1970s → ~2/3 by 2010.
- Primary mechanism of pre-WWI hyperconcentration: [[r-greater-than-g]] in low-growth societies (e.g. France 1820–1913 r ~5%, g ~1%). Net r fell below g for much of 1913–2012 via destruction, progressive tax, and high postwar growth—first long historical episode of that kind.
- Unequal returns by portfolio size (university endowments 1980–2010: average 8.2%; Harvard/Yale/Princeton 10.2%; small endowments ~6%) amplify divergence at the top.

### Slavery as capital (US)

Slave market value ~1.5 years of US NI late eighteenth–mid-nineteenth century; South 2.5–3 years of southern NI—southern slave owners controlled more wealth than Old World landlords in Piketty’s comparison; North barely ~3 years of northern income. Dual structure feeds later US welfare-state path (attributed interpretation).

## Explanatory Theories

- **[[piketty-capital-dynamics]]:** β = s/g; α = r × β; r > g as powerful force toward inheritance societies and high wealth concentration; twentieth-century compression largely shock- and policy-driven, not natural Kuznets harmony; progressive capital and inheritance taxation reshaped distribution toward the patrimonial middle class without necessarily reducing aggregate accumulation.
- **Life-cycle / Modigliani:** predicts wealth vanishes at death (μ ≈ 0)—Piketty argues it fails: wealth concentration nearly as high *within* age cohorts; annuitized wealth <5% of private wealth in France, at most 15–20% in English-speaking countries.
- **Durable inequality / categorical:** [[durable-inequality-theory]] and related stratification accounts—keep side by side; not Piketty’s frame.
- Classical scarcity and infinite accumulation (Ricardo, Marx) as historical interlocutors Piketty reworks with data.
- **[[ecological-evolutionary-theory|Lenski 1966]] (attributed):** degree of inequality rises with technology and surplus from hunting-gathering through an **agrarian peak** (ruler + governing class often ≥ half of national income in his comparative reading); **industrial societies reverse** the long rise on *income* and political-power measures (mid-20th-c top ~2% ~8–12% personal cash income in democracies he cites), while *property* wealth can remain highly skewed (e.g. USA ~25% of population holding ~80% of private wealth in his sources). Technology + [[laws-of-distribution|power over surplus]] + political-cycle/military-participation secondary factors — a comparative-macrosociological frame, not a r > g capital law. Later wealth data (Piketty) can challenge or re-periodize the industrial “reversal” without erasing Lenski’s agrarian-peak thesis.

## Interaction

Links to [[income-inequality]] (capital income more unequal; feeds saving into wealth); [[social-mobility]] (inherited share of lifetime resources for French cohorts born 1970s+ ~22–24%, near nineteenth-century levels); [[globalization]] (tax havens, SWFs, cross-border assets); [[progressive-taxation]] and [[social-state]] as regulators; [[patrimonial-capitalism]] as regime label.

## Debates

[[piketty-capital-debate]]—measurement of wealth and β; whether r > g is a causal law of concentration; role of elasticity of substitution; global wealth tax feasibility; oligarchic vs international divergence. Secondary critiques not fully ingested.
