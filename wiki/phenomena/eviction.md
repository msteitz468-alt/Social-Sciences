# Eviction

## Definition and Scope

Forced removal of tenants from rental housing — **formal** (court judgment and sheriff execution) and **[[informal-eviction|informal]]** (cash-for-keys, lockouts, door removal, and other shadow-of-law pressure). Broader **involuntary displacement** in Desmond’s Milwaukee measurement also includes landlord foreclosure and building condemnation. In *[[desmond-evicted-2016|Evicted]]* (2016), [[desmond-matthew|Matthew Desmond]] treats eviction as a **common, consequential process** that **causes** poverty and instability rather than only resulting from them: “Eviction is a cause, not just a condition, of poverty” (Epilogue, attributed).

## Measurement

| Source | What it captures |
|---|---|
| Court dockets / sheriff | Formal eviction only — understates total forced moves |
| MARS (2009–11) | Survey of forced moves including informal; folk definitions of “eviction” otherwise miss informal removals |
| Eviction Court Study (2011) | Court attendance, case outcomes, household composition |

Desmond (2016, MARS): between 2009 and 2011 **more than 1 in 8** Milwaukee renters experienced a forced move; composition of forced moves ≈ **48% informal eviction**, 24% formal, 23% landlord foreclosure, 5% condemnation. Formal Milwaukee volume: roughly **16,000** adults and children yearly through court (~16 families/day) in a city with fewer than 105,000 renter households.

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed — Desmond 2016 and related papers cited therein.)*

- Extreme [[housing-cost-burden]] among poor renters (majority >50% of income; many cases far higher).
- Women with children heavily represented among the formally evicted; black women report high lifetime eviction prevalence relative to Hispanic and white women (Milwaukee figures in Epilogue/Notes).
- Consequences: material hardship elevated ~20% in the year after eviction (persisting ≥2 years in related estimates); ~15% higher layoff likelihood; forced movers relocate to poorer/more dangerous neighborhoods; mothers’ depression; children’s school churn; eviction records and debt block public-housing/voucher access.
- Neighborhood spillover: high-eviction areas associated with higher subsequent violent crime rates (Desmond’s analyses cited in Epilogue).
- Cross-city court volumes (Kansas City, New York, Cleveland, Chicago) used to argue Milwaukee is not exceptional.
