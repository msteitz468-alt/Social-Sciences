## Definition and Scope

In Chapter 18 ("Revoking Authority") of *Foundations of Social Theory* (1990), Coleman develops a rational-choice theory of revolution grounded in his broader framework of authority as a consensual right to control another actor's actions, distinct from mere power (the capacity to control, with or without the right). Authorities lose legitimacy either by acting outside the ground rules for which consensus exists, by holding the right but exercising it ineffectively, or through external collapse of the underlying consensus. Coleman defines a genuine change of authority — a revolution — as requiring a **full transfer**: withdrawal of control rights from one set of authorities *and* revesting of those rights in a new set by the population as a whole. He explicitly excludes elite power-struggles and coups d'état from this definition, since such events lack population-wide divesting and revesting. Coleman flags his own account as "a narrow component of a theory of revolutions," limited to individual-level orientation change rather than full multi-institutional structure.

## Measurement

Coleman reports comparative cross-national quantitative research (Sigelman and Simpson 1977; Weede 1981, 1987; Muller 1985) on political violence, income inequality, economic development level, and regime repressiveness. This body of work found that income inequality mostly does not predict political violence — contrary to relative-deprivation theory, with one fragile exception in Muller (1985) disputed by Weede (1986) — and that economic development level shows no consistent relation to violence. Regime repressiveness shows a curvilinear relation, with the highest political violence occurring at intermediate levels of repression; Coleman argues this pattern is not cleanly predicted by either frustration theory or power theory, and cautions that repression itself should not be treated as an exogenous variable.

## Patterns and Findings

Coleman reports, as an empirical regularity drawn from a broad literature he reviews (not solely his own claim), that revolutions are more likely during periods of economic or political improvement than during stable deprivation — the Tocqueville paradox, contrary to common-sense and Marxian expectation. He argues Marx's immiseration thesis — that increasing proletarian impoverishment under capitalism would cause revolt — is empirically falsified both in its premise (workers did not become progressively impoverished) and in its underlying logic, contending that general impoverishment correlates with increased passivity rather than revolt.

Coleman distinguishes two families of prior theory. **Frustration theories** — rising expectations (Brinton), short-term setbacks (Davies), relative deprivation (Runciman, Gurr), status inconsistency (Lenski, Stone) — share a three-link micro-to-macro chain (improvement → expectation gap → frustration → aggregated revolt); Coleman classes these as individualistic and reliant on simple aggregation, criticizing them for failing to explain revolutions' organized, non-spontaneous character. He notes Durkheim's concept of anomic suicide — normlessness producing unbounded expectations and despair — as lending indirect, untested support to rising-expectations theory. Coleman's preferred alternative, **power theory** (Leites and Wolf, Tullock, Laqueur, Oberschall, Tilly, and Coleman's own 1978 work), models revolutionary participation as rational action contingent on the perceived probability of success, not on frustration: improved conditions raise perceived odds of success rather than raising subjective frustration, and frustration observed before revolts is, on this account, an epiphenomenon of rising belief in the movement's power, not a causal driver. Rebellious action is modeled as the joint product of preexisting dissatisfaction and belief that opposing action will succeed, illustrated with the West Bank Palestinian case (1970s–80s) and the French Revolution.

Coleman argues ideology (historically Protestant doctrine; Marxism in the twentieth century) matters principally by providing a utopian vision — an alternative source of legitimacy independent of the state — that delegitimizes existing authority and generates internal, psychic rewards for participants, illustrated with the English Revolution of 1640, the Dutch revolt, the Taiping Rebellion, Sun Yat-sen, and the Columbia University 1968 SDS movement. Revolts, he notes, are typically not mass uprisings but are initiated by a small nucleus of committed activists who have already withdrawn legitimacy from authorities — numerically illustrated by Columbia's SDS core of 50–100 activists among 13,000 students.

## Explanatory Theories

Coleman treats revolution as a public-goods problem subject to the [[free-rider-problem]]: an individual's expected contribution to revolutionary success is typically tiny, so a simple rational cost-benefit calculus normally predicts non-participation (illustrated with a worked numerical example). He distinguishes three categories of benefit — unconditional benefits largely independent of participation, selective incentives contingent on both participation and success (following Olson), and benefits from participation alone (social-capital reciprocity, internalized ideological reward) — and three categories of cost, arguing that mass participation overcomes free-riding especially through zealotry and closure among ideologically homogeneous, tightly knit groups with extensive internal communication.

Divesting of authority is modeled as a rational loss of confidence that vesting authority in existing rulers remains a good investment, driven by external weakness, internal vacillation, status or economic-power shifts unmatched by political inclusion (illustrated with 1970s Lebanon's Muslim population growth against a fixed government allocation, the English Revolution's gentry, and the French Revolution's Third Estate), or ideological delegitimation. Coleman grounds legitimacy and authority in the same rational logic as the vesting of authority in a [[corporate-actor]] discussed earlier in the book: the elimination of Hobbesian negative externalities and the realization of positive externalities from joint action.

## Interaction

Coleman derives paired strategic recommendations directly from the theory — for authorities, maintaining external strength, meeting challenges decisively, undermining alternative ideologies, and preventing closure among opposition; for revolutionaries, promulgating a utopian ideology, demonstrating authorities' weakness, and building closure among supporters. The theory of revolution is presented as continuous with Coleman's broader account of authority systems and their internal morality (Chapter 7) and with his treatment of trust withdrawal and cascading loss of institutional confidence (Chapter 8) — connecting revolt to the same divesting/revesting logic that governs authority change more generally, and echoing the [[anomie]]-adjacent mechanism Coleman attributes to Durkheim in the rising-expectations literature.

**Adjacent peasant-movement theory ([[wolf-eric-r]], *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* 1966):** jacquerie, social banditry (via Hobsbawm), and millenarianism as limited peasant protest; successful modern peasant-aided revolution hypothesized to require war-induced breakdown of traditional leadership plus external professional revolutionary staff (Russia, China, Yugoslavia as attributed cases) — revolution *with* not *for* peasantry. See [[peasant-movements]]. Different evidence class and unit of analysis from Coleman's individual-level rational-choice account.

## Debates

Coleman positions power theory as superior to frustration theory on explanatory grounds (organized versus spontaneous revolt) but does not report a settled resolution between the two research traditions in the wider field; the curvilinear repression-violence relationship is presented as anomalous for both accounts. The Tocqueville paradox and the empirical case against Marx's immiseration thesis are reported as reasonably well-established comparative patterns, while the causal weight of ideology, relative deprivation, and regime repressiveness remain open questions on Coleman's own account.
