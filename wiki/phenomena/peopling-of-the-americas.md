# Peopling of the Americas

The initial human settlement of the Western Hemisphere — the last continental landmasses
(bar Antarctica and remote Oceania) reached by *[[homo-sapiens|Homo sapiens]]*. It is one
of world prehistory's most actively contested problems; this page records the received
account of [[fagan-durrani-people-of-the-earth-2022|Fagan & Durrani (2022)]] (attributed),
which itself flags the topic as fast-moving and likely to be revised. The specific "who was
first" dispute is filed at [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]].

## Definition and Scope

The process runs from an ancestral Northeast Asian population's genetic isolation on the
Bering land bridge, through dispersal into the unglaciated Americas, to the filling of the
two continents and the Arctic. It is a **demographic/migration** phenomenon evidenced by
material sites, genetics, dental morphology, and language — evidence classes that constrain
different parts of the story and sometimes conflict.

## Measurement

- **Sites:** earliest securely-dated, well-excavated occupations set minimum arrival dates
  — [[clovis-culture|Clovis]] (~13,200 BP) as the old floor; Monte Verde, Chile (~14,500 BP)
  and other pre-Clovis sites push it earlier.
- **Genetics:** genome-wide and mtDNA coalescence dates the Siberian/Native American split
  to ~23,000 BP and estimates a small founding population (as of the studies Fagan &
  Durrani cite).
- **Dental morphology:** Christy Turner's **sinodonty** (shovel-shaped incisors, etc.) links
  Native Americans to northern Asian populations.
- **Linguistics:** Joseph Greenberg's tripartite Amerind/Na-Dene/Aleut-Eskimo classification
  — explicitly flagged by the textbook as heavily criticized and unresolved.

## Patterns and Findings

*(attributed — Fagan & Durrani 2022; date-stamped, contested)*

- **Beringian Standstill.** An ancestral population was genetically isolated on Beringia for
  millennia (~23,000–16,000 BP) before dispersing south when climate allowed.
- **Coastal before corridor.** The interior "ice-free corridor" between the Laurentide and
  Cordilleran ice sheets was impassable/unattractive until ~15,000–13,000 BP; the earliest
  dispersal more likely followed a **deglaciated Pacific coast** (Cordilleran retreat by
  ~17,000 BP; Haida Gwaii dates ~12,600 BP).
- **Pre-Clovis established.** Monte Verde, Paisley Caves coprolites (~14,200 BP), Page-Ladson,
  and others are treated as securely pre-Clovis, overturning the old "Clovis-first" model;
  White Sands footprints (~23,000 BP) are reported as credible-but-needing-more-research.
- **Rapid radiation.** After ~13,000 BP, Clovis and successor Paleo-Indian traditions spread
  fast and patchily (a "leapfrog" rather than uniform wave), followed by regional Archaic
  diversification; the Arctic is settled last (Paleo-Arctic → Arctic Small Tool → Thule).
- **Southern Cordillera (as of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]):** colonization of the Andes is treated as early coastal-margin and inland adaptive dispersal; [[monte-verde|Monte Verde]] (excavated by [[dillehay-tom|Dillehay]]) is Moseley's best-cited early southern Chile case. He argues MV plant/zone use "adumbrates many adaptations that would be pursued in the Andes," while stressing that founder chronology remains speculative. Date-stamp Andean peopling claims to the synthesis year; genetics and pre-Clovis debates continue beyond his 2001 horizon (see [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]).

## Explanatory Theories

Settlement timing is debated among a pre-40,000 BP minority view, a ~20,000 BP
late-Wisconsin view (favoured here), and a discredited Solutrean-Atlantic-crossing
hypothesis (dismissed as poorly evidenced). The **route** debate (ice-free corridor vs.
Pacific coast) now favours the coast. The associated **megafaunal extinction** (~11,000 BC,
~38 genera) is explained by competing overkill, climate-change, and seasonality-stress
models; Fagan & Durrani conclude it was **multicausal**, human hunting a plausible final
variable rather than sole cause.

## Interaction

Downstream of the [[upper-paleolithic-revolution|Upper Paleolithic]] settlement of Siberia;
upstream of every pre-Columbian society and of Native American state formation
(Mesoamerica, the Andes) on the [[world-prehistory-timeline]]. Intersects
[[societal-collapse|late-Pleistocene megafaunal extinction]] and, politically, Indigenous
sovereignty over ancestral remains (cf. heritage/[[restitution]] concerns and NAGPRA).

## Debates

- **When and by whom** — [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]] (Clovis-first vs. pre-Clovis).
- **Route** — coastal vs. ice-free-corridor (recorded above; favours coastal).
- **Extinction cause** — overkill vs. climate vs. multicausal.
- **Linguistic classification** — Greenberg's Amerind hypothesis, widely rejected by
  historical linguists.

## Milner 2021 (*The Moundbuilders* Ch. 2) — Eastern Woodlands frame

*(Attributed — [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner 2021]].)* Regional synthesis for eastern North America:

- People south of the ice by roughly **~15,000 years ago** in the broader Americas narrative; Clovis points ≤~**13,400 cal BP** (~11,500 ¹⁴C BP) are the earliest **sure** eastern North American signal.
- Genetic isolation of First American ancestors in NE Asia/**Beringia** for thousands of years before southward spread; early Holocene body-breadth consistent with cold adaptation.
- Land (ice-free corridor) vs coastal routes both discussed; corridor vs coast remains open.
- Pre-Clovis eastern candidates (Florida tortoise ~14k cal BP; [[meadowcroft]]) — ages **disputed**; skepticism remains despite excavation quality at Meadowcroft.
- **Megafauna overkill** for eastern Paleoindians: “resounding absence of support” for specialized big-game hunting (citing Meltzer); opportunistic kills of vulnerable animals; climate cannot be eliminated.
- Paleoindian mobility high; stone from hundreds of miles; population growth ~0.06%/year early — fails to support rapid-expansion overkill models.
- Cross-links: [[clovis-culture]], [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]], [[eastern-woodlands-chronology]].

## Bellwood 2005 — settlement baseline and post-settlement farming–language layer

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter|Bellwood]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* ch. 8, 10–12; **as of Bellwood 2005**. This layer is not a Clovis/pre-Clovis field synthesis; it is Bellwood’s comparative farming–dispersal frame resting on a consensus settlement baseline he cites.)*

**Initial settlement baseline (as he cites).** Documented settlement of the Americas from Siberia ~11,500 BC (Lynch 1999; Fiedel 1999 as of Bellwood 2005). Until ~3000 BC all Americans were essentially hunter-gatherers, with possible but disputed earlier horticultural investment in some tropical regions; dated macroscopic remains of domesticated *staple* food plants only after ~4000 BC, mostly much later (Smith 1995, 2001; Benz 2001; Piperno & Flannery 2001). Exception of unusual precocity in southern Ecuador/northern Peru; virtually the whole tropical American sequence from oldest sedentary agricultural settlements to Late Preclassic urbanism falls between ~2000 and 300 BC — more compacted than SW Asia Jericho→Uruk or Peiligang→Shang. At European contact about half (probably more) of the land area of the Americas was still occupied by hunter-gatherers; many regions suitable for agriculture in western North America from California northward were not reached by farming; northern limit of ~120-day frost-free zone roughly at the US–Canada border / north of Great Lakes. Expansions of agriculture and pottery into the West Indies only during the later first millennium BC (Rouse 1992; Keegan 1994; Callaghan 2001). Athabaskan-speaking Navajo and Apache southward migration into the former Anasazi region after AD 1400 (Matson 2003), after pueblo abandonments.

**Formative homogeneities.** After 10,000 years of prior occupation, Formative homogeneities are **not**, Bellwood argues, the actions of a single ethnolinguistic population inventing a unique agriculture that then spread everywhere — but similarities exceed historical coincidence (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Language families (positions).** Greenberg (1987) Amerind macrofamily (all American families one group except Na-Dene and Eskimo-Aleut as Holocene Siberian hunter-gatherer immigrants): Bellwood notes the linguistic literature still “waxes hot”; he treats Amerind as simply reflecting shared residues from initial settlement ~11,500 BC — **not** a wiki-voice classification. American agriculturalist language families average smaller areas than Old World analogues; N–S continental orientation plus high latitudes/altitudes limited prehistoric agriculture (Diamond 1994 as cited); European language replacement further fragmented distributions. Linguistic time depths of most agriculturalist families ~6,000–3,500 years — spread within agriculturalist time range, **not** since first colonization. Americas settled so recently that major Old World-scale biological differentiation never developed; past movements often mingled already closely related people; more research needed for farming-dispersal genetics (as of Bellwood 2005).

**Identity caution.** Initial peopling (Pleistocene HG dispersal) ≠ later agricultural/language expansions (Formative maize, Eastern Agricultural Complex, Andean farming, etc.). Do not equate Greenberg Amerind, Clovis, or any genetic founder model with a single farming package. See [[maize-domestication]], [[eastern-agricultural-complex]], [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]].

## Coe & Koontz (2013) — Mexican regional evidence

*(Attributed — [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz 2013]].)*

**[POSITION] Short vs. long chronology.** Coe & Koontz record the debate over the date and mode
of entry of the earliest Americans in the terms recorded at
[[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]: a "short chronology" camp holds that migration required an
exposed Bering land bridge (drowned by rising sea levels from roughly 10,000 years ago) and thus
occurred no earlier than 14,000–16,000 years ago; a "long chronology" camp cites older, and
disputed, radiocarbon dates for early human remains in the hemisphere. Coe & Koontz note that
boat travel could have permitted earlier coastal migration, drawing a parallel to human arrival
in Australia — never land-bridge-connected, yet reached by boat by 50,000 years ago. Sea levels
during the late Wisconsin glacial advances were about 200 ft (60 m) lower than present, exposing
a land bridge at least 1,000 miles (1,600 km) wide between Siberia and Alaska, with the bridge
and adjacent Alaska/Yukon remaining ice-free tundra even while much of North America south to the
Great Lakes was ice-covered.

**Mexican and hemispheric sites (as reported by Coe & Koontz, citing Tom Dillehay and others).**
Monte Verde, Chile, provides "compelling evidence" for human occupation at 12,500 BC, and
radiocarbon dating shows people hunting sloth, horse, and guanaco at the Straits of Magellan by
at least 10,000 BC. Clovis fluted points appear by approximately 11,000 BC across much of North
America (Alaska to Panama); Folsom points, a refinement, appear at about 10,300 BC in the
American Midwest/West, supersede Clovis, and are often associated with bison kills. INAH
archaeologists found Clovis points and other stone tools associated with butchered gomphothere
remains at **Fin del Mundo**, Sonora, probably dating to early in the eleventh millennium BC.
Richard S. MacNeish's excavations in Tamaulipas identified the **Diablo Complex** as the earliest
phase of a long cultural sequence — crude bifacial/uniface pebble and flint tools — which
MacNeish proposed as contemporaneous with the Mankato Advance of the Wisconsin, 13,500–12,000 BC.

**Valley of Mexico finds.** "Tepexpan Man" (actually a woman) was found in 1949 by geologist
Helmut de Terra near Tepexpan, Valley of Mexico; per a study by Santiago Genovés, the individual
was a woman of no more than 30 years, about 5 ft 3 in (160 cm) tall. Her stratigraphic position
(Upper Becerra Formation, per de Terra) is contested owing to poor excavation records; fluorine
testing supported contemporaneity with Upper Becerra-age mammalian fossils, though Marie
Wormington argued the individual's flexed burial posture suggests an Archaic-period practice
rather than confirming Pleistocene age (an attributed interpretive caution on the dating). In
1952 and 1954, Mexican prehistorians excavated two mammoth ("imperial mammoth," *Mammuthus
imperator*) kill sites at **Santa Isabel Iztapan** in the Upper Becerra Formation (Valders age),
with associated stone tools (a Scottsbluff point, scraper, and knife, and blades of obsidian and
flint at one; an Angostura point, Lerma point, and bifacial knife at the other). A hearth from a
related mammoth kill in the same formation gave a radiocarbon date of **9200 BC**.

**Weaponry.** Citing experiments by Dennis Stanford (Smithsonian Institution), Coe & Koontz
report the atlatl-hurled spear achieves 15 times the speed and 200 times the impact of a
hand-thrown spear; the atlatl remained the dominant weapon in Mexico until the Spanish Conquest,
and the bow and arrow arrived late and was not adopted everywhere.
