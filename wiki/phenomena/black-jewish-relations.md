# Black–Jewish Relations in the United States

This page records the coalition-and-conflict pattern between African Americans and [[american-jews]] as documented in Dinnerstein (1994), whose Chapter 10 is the most contested part of that book. All interpretive claims here are attributed; the underlying incidents and poll numbers are documentary.

## Definition and Scope

The intergroup relationship — coalition, friction, and mutual perception — between African Americans and American Jews, from documented nineteenth-century attitudes through the civil-rights alliance and its late-twentieth-century ruptures. The page covers group-level patterns; it is not a claim about individuals on either side, and "black antisemitism" here names a documented attitudinal strand, not a group attribute.

## Measurement

Attitude surveys with standard antisemitism indices (1964, 1981, 1992 waves cited by Dinnerstein); organizational funding and membership records (CORE's contribution collapse, 1966); black-press content (1890s–1940s); incident documentation. Measures disagree: index-based surveys capture stereotype assent, not behavior, and Dinnerstein himself notes stereotypes exceed hostile acts.

## Patterns and Findings

**Coalition.** Jews co-founded the NAACP (1909) and supplied three of its presidents (Arthur and Joel Spingarn, Kivie Kaplan); Jews were a plurality of white civil-rights attorneys, over half of the freedom riders, and about two-thirds of 1964 Freedom Summer volunteers. Martin Luther King Jr. (quoted): "It would be impossible to record the contribution that Jewish people have made toward the Negro's struggle for freedom, it has been so great."

**Documented antisemitism within African-American culture.** Dinnerstein reports (attributed throughout) that anti-Jewish teaching reached African Americans through the same Southern Protestant fundamentalist culture that shaped white southerners — slave catechisms and spirituals carried the deicide charge, and Richard Wright recalled being taught in Sunday school that Jews were "Christ killers." Black press of the 1890s–1930s circulated economic stereotypes; DuBois later revised antisemitic passages of *Souls of Black Folk* (1953 edition), admitting he was "not at all sure" the exploiters he had described were Jews. During the Depression some black papers voiced approval of Hitler's targeting of Jews (Chicago's *Dynamite*: "What America needs is a Hitler..."), while others (Philadelphia *Tribune*) condemned Nazi persecution yet repeated press-control tropes. Ralph Bunche and Arthur Huff Fauset criticized the double standard from within.

**Rupture episodes (1966–1990s).** CORE official Clifford Brown's 1966 "Hitler made a mistake" remark and the collapse of Jewish funding to CORE; the 1968 Ocean Hill–Brownsville teachers' strike — per Dinnerstein the most visible Black–Jewish confrontation in US history, with inflammatory rhetoric on both sides (the NYCLU independently condemned union leader Albert Shanker's "half-truths, innuendoes, and outright lies," while the ADL documented threats against Jewish teachers); the WBAI poem and "Harlem on My Mind" catalog incidents (1968–69); the Bakke-era affirmative-action split; the Andrew Young resignation (1979), near-universally attributed by African Americans to Jewish pressure although, Dinnerstein reports, Young privately acknowledged Jews were not responsible; Jesse Jackson's "Hymietown" remark (1984); Louis Farrakhan's Madison Square Garden speech (1985; Julius Lester likened the crowd response to Nuremberg, while Rev. Lawrence Lucas and Alderman William Henry endorsed most of Farrakhan's message — both receptions recorded); the Steve Cokely AIDS-conspiracy affair (1988, linked to the [[jewish-world-conspiracy-myth]]); and Leonard Jeffries's slave-trade claims (1991), rebutted by Henry Louis Gates Jr. with the figure that roughly 2% of the trade involved Jews.

**Survey pattern.** Standard antisemitism indices repeatedly found higher scores among African Americans than whites: 47% vs. 35% (1964), two-in-five vs. one-in-five (1981), 37% vs. 17% "hard-core" (ADL 1992). Gates (1992, attributed) reported younger, more educated Black respondents scoring higher — against the usual education-reduces-prejudice gradient.

## Explanatory Theories (attributed, side by side)

- **Dinnerstein's multi-causal synthesis:** shared Southern Christian teaching as root; scapegoating with low in-group social cost; psychological projection (Furst, Orlansky, the Sobels); real economic friction that *intensified* rather than created prejudice (citing sociologist Richard Simpson's argument that unequal-group contact intensifies pre-existing stereotypes); and elite political competition (Gates's "ethnic isolationism" reading).
- **Baldwin (1967):** "Negroes are anti-Semitic because they're anti-white" — Jewish landlords/merchants stood proxy for white power.
- **Gordis (1967):** scapegoat-seeking, in direct counterpoint to Baldwin in the same *New York Times Magazine* exchange.
- Internal critique (Bunche, Fauset, Barbara Smith's acknowledgment of internalized antisemitism) is part of the record, as are 1990s disavowals by Gates, West, and Jackson.

## Interaction

Interacts with [[antisemitism]] (this is its most-debated late-20th-century American strand), with the civil-rights transformation of the American South (see [[dinnerstein-antisemitism-in-america-study]] Ch. 9 material), and with urban economic stratification.

## Debates

Dinnerstein's framing — treating "black antisemitism" as a distinct, rising strand while white antisemitism declined — is itself the contested move; his own text records the rival readings above. No wiki-voice adjudication; positions stand as recorded.
