# Phoenician Expansion

Westward maritime commercial expansion of the Levantine Phoenician city-states
(Tyre, Sidon) across the Mediterranean to Iberia and through the Strait of
Gibraltar onto the Atlantic, founding Gadir (Cádiz) and articulating the
Atlantic metal trade with Mediterranean demand.

## Definition and Scope

Phoenician expansion names the westward maritime commercial expansion of the Levantine city-states — chiefly Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos — across the Mediterranean and through the Strait of Gibraltar onto the Atlantic façade, culminating in the foundation of Gadir (Cádiz) and a network of coastal colonies from southern Iberia to Atlantic Morocco. Cunliffe (2001) frames the Phoenicians as heirs to Mycenaean-era maritime entrepreneurs: after the collapse of complex eastern Mediterranean networks in the 13th century BCE (amid "sea raiders" recorded in Egyptian texts, the fall of the Hittite empire, and the shrinking of Mycenaean kingdoms), the former Canaanite maritime territory of Syria/Palestine became known, after c. 1200 BCE, as the land of the Phoenicians. The phenomenon is scoped here as the Phoenician (and, after roughly the mid-6th century BCE, Punic/Carthaginian) commercial-colonial system operating between the eastern Mediterranean and the Atlantic, ending conventionally with Rome's capture of Gadir in 208 BCE (Phoenician/Punic phase) though Punic cultural influence in Iberia persisted longer.

## Measurement

Cunliffe (2001) reconstructs the phenomenon from colony-foundation archaeology, shipwreck cargoes, and distribution of diagnostic material culture. Phoenician trading vessels (*gauloi*, "round ships," also called *hippoi* from their horse-head prow) were typically 20–30 m long, 6–7 m broad, drawing about 1.5 m, rigged with a single rectangular sail and a side-mounted steering oar; laden vessels could make 2–3 knots (up to 50 nautical miles/day), while sleeker two-masted, ram-bowed Phoenician warships could reach 5 knots. The Late Bronze Age Ulu Burun shipwreck (off southern Turkey, late 14th century BCE) — carrying 6 tonnes of copper ingots, tin ingots, bronze swords, ostrich eggshell, ivory, blue glass slabs, exotic wood, resin, amber, Syrian/Canaanite goods, and Mycenaean pottery — evidences an already-circular eastern Mediterranean trading route (Cyprus–Levant–North Africa/Nile delta–Crete–Greek mainland–southern Turkey) that Phoenician trade later extended; Cunliffe (2001) notes the ship's tin may have come "from Etruria or possibly from as far afield as Cornwall or Galicia," illustrating Mediterranean–Atlantic interdependence even at this early date. Colony-foundation chronology is itself, in Cunliffe's words, "much debated": archaeological evidence places a Phoenician presence at Nora (south Sardinia) by at least the 9th century BCE and at Gadir by the 8th century BCE, while textual tradition (Velleius Paterculus, citing ancient sources) puts Gadir's founding at roughly 1100 BCE and Carthage's traditional founding date is 814 BCE — a persistent textual/archaeological chronology gap.

## Patterns and Findings

Expansion proceeded in phases. Mycenaean-era Aegean ships had already penetrated the central Mediterranean (Sicily, Malta, Ischia, Sardinia) from the 14th century BCE; early in the 8th century BCE Euboean Greek settlers founded the earliest known Greek colony in the central Mediterranean at Pithecussae on Ischia. Cunliffe (2001) argues Phoenician fleets became essential to supplying home cities and meeting escalating Assyrian tribute demands — Assyria required iron, copper, and especially silver as a medium of exchange from the early 8th century BCE — prompting Phoenician shipmasters to explore the far west, plausibly having heard of Iberia's "silver mountains" via Sardinia-based sailors. Strabo, probably quoting Posidonius (who visited Gadir c. 100 BCE), records an oracle-driven tradition of three attempted Tyrian expeditions to found a settlement at the "Columns of Hercules" before Gadeira (Gadir) was finally settled — read by Cunliffe (2001) as possibly reflecting a long pre-colonial phase of seasonal contact preceding formal colonization. A second phase of denser coastal colonization spread along southern Iberia from the 7th century BCE (earliest examples in the later 8th century), sited at river mouths for access to alluvial land, hinterland silver, and the Atlantic; Toscanos (30 km east of Málaga) is the most extensively studied, with a large warehouse for Phoenician/Greek storage amphorae. Most of these 7th-century colonies were abandoned in the first half of the 6th century BCE; only Malaka and Almuñécar grew into important centres. Phoenician settlement also reached Atlantic Morocco and Portugal: Pliny records Lixus (mouth of the River Loukkos) as the earliest Atlantic African Phoenician establishment, traditionally dated c. 1000 BCE though excavation confirms occupation only to the 7th century BCE; 7th-century Phoenician-carried Attic/Ionian pottery has been found as far south as Mogador island (c. 600 km south of Gadir, the southern limit of the regular coastal trade route), and 8th-century BCE settlements are attested northward along Portugal at Cerro da Rocha Branca (Faro), Abul (Sado estuary), and Santa Olaia (Mondego mouth), with Iberian-Punic pottery reaching Galicia by the 4th–3rd centuries BCE.

The period 600–550 BCE brought major disruption: the Babylonian siege of Tyre (586–573 BCE), depletion of easily worked silver ores as Attic sources came online, and Phocaean Greek colonization at Massalia (c. 600 BCE) and Emporion (whose traders reached Huelva directly). By the mid-6th century Carthage had become master of the western Mediterranean and Punic culture spread across southern Iberia's coastal regions; Carthaginian control of the Mediterranean–Atlantic passage lasted, on Cunliffe's account, until Rome took Gadir in 208 BCE. Further afield, Cunliffe (2001) finds "no reason to doubt" Herodotus's account of a clockwise Phoenician circumnavigation of Africa under Egyptian pharaoh Necho II (c. 600 BCE); the explorer Hanno reached Sierra Leone or possibly Cameroon by the late 5th century BCE; and Herodotus (*Histories* 4.196) describes a "silent trade" system with West Africans — Carthaginians leaving goods ashore and withdrawing to their ships while locals deposited gold in exchange, neither side touching the other's goods until satisfied — which Cunliffe (2001) notes is "of the kind well known to anthropologists," with gold, ivory, skins, and slaves the most sought African products. Diodorus Siculus (possibly via the 4th-century BCE writer Timaeus) recounts a Gadir ship blown off course to a large pleasant island far out in the ocean, likely Madeira; a 1749 hoard of Punic and Cyrenaean coins (4th–3rd century BCE) found in the Azores may represent a genuine ancient deposit, though repeated contact is unconfirmed.

## Explanatory Theories

Cunliffe (2001) advances several attributed interpretive claims rather than settled findings. He argues Phoenician westward expansion was driven primarily by Assyrian tribute pressure for silver from the early 8th century BCE, funneling demand toward Iberia's metal wealth (see [[tartessos]]); this is his causal reading, not a wiki-voice fact. He challenges the traditional historians' assumption that Carthaginian–Greek hostility (550–208 BCE) meant the Straits of Gibraltar were closed to Greek shipping, arguing instead for a "laissez-faire" reality given the blurred political allegiance and ethnic origin of ship crews — a position, not a consensus finding. He also frames the whole Atlantic-facing phase of Phoenician expansion within a longue-durée periodization: a "second cycle" of Atlantic interaction (c. 800 BCE–c. 400 CE) beginning with Gadir's foundation and drawing the older Atlantic gift-exchange system into a Mediterranean-centred economic order, eventually replaced by large-scale/industrial production (mining in Iberia; salt, fish-sauce, and pottery production in Armorica/Britain). On the classical tin-trade sources specifically, Cunliffe (2001) cautions that texts referring to Atlantic tin-producing lands are "fragmentary, ill-understood, and to a large extent contradictory" — Avienus's *Ora Maritima* describes tin- and lead-rich "Oestrymnides" islands whose location Cunliffe holds cannot be securely fixed, and he disputes the common claim that a later reference to Carthaginians "coming to these seas" proves Carthaginian involvement in this specific tin trade. Diodorus Siculus (5.22) describes tin production at Belerion (identified with Cornwall) shipped via the island of Ictis; Pliny separately describes "Mictis," six days' sail from Britain; Cunliffe (2001) suggests Ictis and Mictis were probably two separate islands rather than the same place under textual corruption. He concludes that classical/archaeological evidence points to two principal Mediterranean-known tin sources — north-western Iberia (Galicia, likely a Phoenician/Punic Gadir preserve) and south-western Britain (Cornwall) — with no positive textual evidence yet for early Breton tin exploitation. He also proposes, as a "plausible alternative" to the traditional view that the Massaliot explorer Pytheas (c. 330 BCE) sailed via the Straits of Gibraltar and circumnavigated Iberia, that Pytheas instead crossed southern Gaul overland (Aude–Carcassonne Gap–Garonne) to the Gironde, avoiding Carthaginian-controlled Gibraltar, then used a local Atlantic-familiar vessel northward — a hypothesis Cunliffe supports with archaeological evidence for this route as a major channel of communication and with Strabo's otherwise garbled quotations of Pytheas on Gironde-to-Ushant sailing distances.

## Interaction

Phoenician expansion interacts most directly with [[tartessos]] — Cunliffe (2001) treats the Guadalquivir-region silver wealth as the proximate target of Phoenician westward exploration, and characterizes Gadir/Huelva as "the pin that held" the Atlantic and Mediterranean exchange "systems together." It interacts with [[atlantic-bronze-age]] via Cunliffe's own explicitly hedged and "speculative and difficult to test" hypothesis that growing Phoenician (and later Assyrian-driven Phoenician) demand for bronze and silver stimulated the observed intensification of Atlantic bronze production and circulation in its final centuries. It also intersects the [[castro-culture]] of north-west Iberia, where castro-culture's earliest phase (c. 1000–c. 500 BCE) is explicitly periodized against "the end of the period of widespread maritime contact" as Phoenician/Punic trade contacts emerge, though direct Mediterranean material reaching the castro zone remained slight in volume. Cunliffe (2001) applies Wallerstein-derived core-periphery ([[core-periphery]]/[[world-systems-analysis]]) vocabulary to frame the Mediterranean as an economic "core" and the Atlantic façade as an adjusting "periphery," while explicitly arguing the Gadir–Tartessos relationship specifically was reciprocal "articulation" rather than one-sided colonial exploitation.

## Debates

The chronology of Phoenician westward settlement — whether Gadir's traditional c. 1100 BCE founding date reflects genuine early contact or later legendary inflation against an archaeologically confirmed 8th-century BCE horizon — is, per Cunliffe (2001), "much debated," and is recorded here as an open textual-versus-archaeological dating tension rather than resolved. The extent of "Carthaginian control" of the Straits of Gibraltar (whether it amounted to an exclusionary monopoly against Greek shipping, the traditional view, or a more laissez-faire reality, Cunliffe's position) is likewise unresolved and stated as a position, not a finding. The Celtic-language classification of Atlantic Iberian populations touched by Phoenician contact is part of the broader, contested [[celticity-debate]]. The geographic identity and extent of the classical "Cassiterides" tin-source islands (Oestrymnides, Ictis, Mictis) remains, on Cunliffe's own account, unresolved given contradictory and corrupted classical source texts — no dedicated wiki page for the Cassiterides yet exists; this material is recorded here and on [[tartessos]] pending a possible future concept/comparison page.
