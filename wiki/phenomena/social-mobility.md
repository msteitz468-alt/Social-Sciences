# Social Mobility

Movement of individuals or families between positions in a stratified social structure,
across generations (intergenerational) or within a lifetime (intragenerational). This page
records the phenomenon as it bears on the sources ingested so far, chiefly
[[mills-c-wright|Mills]]'s *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]* (1951), with an earlier
industrial-city snapshot in [[lynd-middletown-study|Lynd & Lynd, *Middletown*]] (promotion
scarcity and classed schooling/college plans, 1924–25), and with [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]'s
network channel for *intragenerational* job mobility (*[[granovetter-getting-a-job-study|Getting a Job]]* 1974;
[[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study|SWT]] 1973).

## Definition and Scope

Social mobility concerns transitions between occupational, class, and status positions.
Mills's treatment centers on the transformation of the American middle class: the historical
"ascent" of roughly 150,000 workers and farmers per year into [[white-collar]] ranks between
1870 and 1930 (Eldridge Sibley's estimate), and the changing *mechanism* of mobility — from
inheritable small property (the [[old-middle-class]] route) to formal education (the
"educational elevator" of the [[new-middle-class]]).

**Piketty bridge (2013/2014, attributed):** *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* treats pure education-driven mobility optimism as incomplete when [[r-greater-than-g]] and high [[capital-income-ratio|β]] restore the weight of inheritance relative to lifetime labor income; the historically novel [[patrimonial-middle-class]] complicates but does not erase top wealth concentration. See [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]] and [[wealth-inequality]].

## Measurement

Mills assembled mobility evidence from multiple sources, flagging their limits. Education-based
mobility: Richard Centers found 46% of better-educated-than-their-fathers sons reached higher
occupational positions, vs. 16% of worse-educated sons. Origin composition: ~4 in 10 lower
white-collar workers had free-enterpriser fathers, ~4 in 10 had urban wage-worker fathers.
Self-recruitment: over one-third of business/managerial/professional people came from the same
categories as their fathers. Blocked mobility: as many as 80% of a clerical sample expected no
promotion; a worker's son had ~6/100 odds of reaching college versus better than even odds for a
professional's son. These are single-study or agency figures, recorded in attributed voice.

**Blau–Duncan OCG (1962/1967, attributed):** *[[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study|The American Occupational Structure]]* operationalizes mobility with continuous [[socioeconomic-index|Duncan SEI]] scores and recursive [[path-analysis|path coefficients]] linking father’s education/occupation, son’s education, first job, and 1962 occupation for ~20,700 U.S. men. Simple correlations with occupational status (approx.): education .60, first job .54, father’s occupation .40. Direct paths to destination: education ~.39, first job ~.28, residual father-occupation path ~.115; R² of the basic model ≈.43. Mobility tables (17 categories) complement SEI: much short-distance movement; two semipermeable class boundaries (white-collar/blue-collar; blue-collar/farm). See [[status-attainment]].

## Patterns and Findings

Mills argued (attributed) that education had "replaced property as the insurance of social
position" for the new middle class, but that the "educational elevator" was itself being
stratified and its payoff eroding: the college-graduate earnings premium fell from ~one-third
above average (c. 1941) to ~one-tenth (c. 1951) as graduate supply outran professional demand.
He proposed a three-stage schema — elite-channel education under liberal capitalism, a
democratized single ladder, then bureaucratic stratification of education via testing and
tracking as educated labor outstripped demand. Life-chance disparities were stark: an unskilled
worker's child faced nearly three times the infant-mortality risk of a white-collar man's child
(Robert Woodbury).

**Middletown (Lynds, attributed, 1924–25):** Within industrial plants, promotion into
supervisory ranks was rare (order-of-magnitude estimates on the order of one in hundreds of
workers); high-school and college plans were strongly classed; business-class youth and
working-class youth faced different occupational horizons even inside the same city school
system. See [[lynd-middletown-study]] and [[business-class-and-working-class]].

## Explanatory Theories

### Blau–Duncan status attainment (1967, attributed)

[[blau-peter-m|Blau]] and [[duncan-otis-dudley|Duncan]] model mobility as a **process of stratification**: [[ascribed-status|ascribed]] origins shape [[achieved-status|achieved]] education and early career, which shape adult occupational status. They argue industrial “universalism” expands achievement criteria relative to particularistic ascription, with **race as the major exception** (net residual white advantage ~9.3 SEI points after origins, education, and first job). Structural mobility (farm contraction, professional expansion) and selective migration generate much gross upward flow; father–son status dependence remains nontrivial (r≈.40). Strong mobility–fertility hypothesis rejected. Program: [[status-attainment]]; study: [[blau-duncan-american-occupational-structure-study]].

Mills read mobility through a Weberian frame (occupation as the new axis of stratification) and
noted the older demographic explanation — upward mobility partly filled by lower fertility among
higher strata — while doubting its future validity. Opportunity ideology itself, in Mills's
account, is partly a legitimating device: vocational guidance and educational tracking manage
mass frustration where ambition outstrips available positions.

[[collins-randall|Collins]] (*[[collins-conflict-sociology-1975|Conflict Sociology]]*, 1975) treats wealth distribution and mobility as products of the **same** conflict model: against the functionalist Davis–Moore market account (which he calls tautological, since a pure market would tend toward wage equality), he argues inequality is produced by **restraints on the market** — tenure, licensing, and educational credentialing that let groups close positions to competitors. He adopts [[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]]'s technology-and-stratification model (*[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]*, 1966; [[ecological-evolutionary-theory]]) as "the only empirically validated" conflict theory of wealth, while noting it cannot explain variation among industrial societies. Collins treats deviant careers as a form of downward mobility governed by the same cumulative processes (attributed, Collins 1975).

### Lenski — mobility by societal type (1966, attributed)

In *Power and Privilege*, Lenski treats **vertical mobility** as a property of [[distributive-process|distributive systems]] that varies by technology and political cycle: high in hunting-gathering (skill-based prestige, little heritable surplus); constrained once hereditary office, property, and slavery appear (advanced horticultural / agrarian); reopened but stratified in industrial systems via education, occupational expansion, and citizenship — while family background remains the strongest counterweight to educational egalitarianism (attributed; mid-20th-c sources). Agrarian chapters stress residual mobility (war, palace politics, commerce, church) against hereditary floors and ceilings.

## The Ethnographic View (Whyte, micro-scale)

Where Mills and [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] treat mobility as a `macro`
stratification variable, [[whyte-street-corner-society-study|Whyte's *Street Corner
Society*]] (fieldwork 1936–40) documents it at the `meso`/`micro` scale of the
street corner — a deliberate scale contrast worth flagging. Whyte's account
(attributed): in [[cornerville|Cornerville]], upward mobility was not a smooth
elevator but a *rupture of local ties*. The [[corner-boys-and-college-boys|corner
boy vs. college boy]] division turned on the incompatibility between gang loyalty
(a [[reciprocal-obligation|reciprocal-obligation]] system rewarding free spending
and presence) and the savings-and-departure discipline of mobility. The settlement
house functioned as a mobility ladder that pulled mobile individuals *out* of the
district, so it "does not win the loyalty of the great majority." Whyte explicitly
rejected a meritocratic reading — "the difference in intelligence and ability does
not explain the different careers of Chick and Doc" — locating the mechanism in
early institutional sorting and group allegiance, and noted reflexively that the
successful mobile individual then reports back to elites that "the corner boys are
lazy," feeding the very opportunity-ideology Mills analyzed at the macro scale.

## Network Information Channels (Granovetter)

[[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]] treats intragenerational **job mobility** as structured by egocentric networks, not only by education or property. *[[granovetter-getting-a-job-study|Getting a Job]]* (1974; Newton, MA male PTM movers, N=282) is the full design: he reported **[[personal-contacts]]** as the predominant finding method (~56%), contact-found jobs associated with higher satisfaction/income/new creation, ~29% nonsearch, work contacts (~69%) over family-social, and — among interview contact-users — weak-frequency skew (often/occasional/rare 16.7/55.6/27.8%, N=54) and short chains (N=64). *[[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-1973|The Strength of Weak Ties]]* (1973) portable-ized the weak-tie half of that evidence. He argued (attributed) weak ties access non-redundant circles — structure over pure motivation — and that job changers also *create* bridging ties between workplace clusters. Single-study, limited class window (male PTM); `replication_status` `partially-supported` (Afterword 1994: prevalence sustained; wage laws conditional). Complements Mills's educational elevator and Whyte's loyalty-rupture ethnography with a **relational** mechanism: see [[strength-of-weak-ties-theory]], [[granovetter-getting-a-job-study]], [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]], [[network-inequality]].

## Interaction

Mobility interacts with the [[status-panic]] (blocked or illusory ascent feeds status anxiety),
with [[rationalization]] (office mechanization shortens white-collar career ladders), and with
[[political-indifference]] (mobility channels, including "tamed" union bureaucracies, drain
potential oppositional leadership). Network structure ([[network-analysis]], bridging [[local-bridge|weak ties]]) interacts with opportunity as *information and influence channels*, distinct from both formal education ladders and corner-group loyalty traps.

## Debates

Mills's claim of a long-run convergence and "status proletarianization" of white-collar workers,
and his forecast of a new social class forming between lower white-collar and wage-worker
positions, connect to the [[new-middle-class-politics-debate]] and remain contested by later
stratification research.

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*,
1959) treats mobility as a structural variable of [[conflict-theory|class conflict]], not only
as individual opportunity: inter- and intrageneration mobility and educational role allocation
diminish the **intensity** and permanence of class conflict and open class boundaries without
eliminating conflict or authority inequality (see [[intensity-and-violence-of-conflict]]).
"Classlessness" by high mobility or alternating chairmanship is for him a limiting case of open
classes, not a Utopian abolition of domination.
