## Definition and Scope

This page covers the initial colonization of Sahul — the Pleistocene landmass uniting Australia and New Guinea — and the adjoining islands of Wallacea and the Bismarck Archipelago, by anatomically modern humans. It is a distinct, much earlier event from the [[austronesian-expansion|Austronesian expansion]] and the later Lapita colonization of Remote Oceania, which occurred tens of thousands of years afterward; the two must not be conflated despite both being maritime colonization events in the same broad region.

## Measurement

Dating rests on several independent methods, each attributed to the relevant absolute chronometric technique used at each site: [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon dating]] (rockshelter deposits, largely reported in the source as uncalibrated conventional dates — flagged, calibration status unstated for most cited figures), uranium-thorium (U/Th) dating (Huon Peninsula reef terraces), thermoluminescence (volcanic ash layers), and deep-sea core plus geomorphological sea-level reconstruction (the Chappell 1993 sea-level curve). U/Th and thermoluminescence dates are uncalibrated absolute chronometric methods distinct from radiocarbon calibration and should not be conflated with cal BP figures.

## Patterns and Findings

**Dating.** Per O'Connell and Allen (2015:80, as of Author Year), 26 archaeological sites or localities offer relatively reliable, well-published evidence of anatomically modern human presence in Sahul, Wallacea, and the Bismarck Archipelago by about 40,000 years ago; O'Connell and Allen conclude that the ancestors of modern Sahul indigenous peoples arrived approximately 47,000 years ago. Kirch (2017) separately states a broader "most likely" colonization window of 60,000–40,000 years ago. **Both figures are preserved here with attribution rather than silently collapsed to one** — this is an actively revised chronology and the two dates appear in the same source without reconciliation.

**Deliberate voyaging.** Colonization required deliberate over-water voyaging even during the lowest Pleistocene sea-level stands (roughly 100–120 m below present, with maximum lowering around 140,000 and 18,000 years ago), because Wallacea always retained open-water gaps between the Sunda Shelf and Sahul. This is the field's central interpretive claim (attributed), and the primary antidote to earlier "accidental drift" hypotheses that arose after Mulvaney's 1962 Kenniff Cave dates first opened the debate.

Key sites establishing purposive voyaging: [[matenkupkum|Matenkupkum]] (New Ireland, Bismarck Archipelago), a rockshelter with an oldest occupation level radiocarbon-dated to 35,410 ± 430 years BP (J. Allen and Gosden 1996:186; flagged as likely conventional, uncalibrated 14C BP, calibration status not stated in the source) — one of the oldest secure Pleistocene sites in Near Oceania, on islands never land-linked to Sahul, and per Kirch "possibly the earliest evidence of marine resource exploitation anywhere in the world" (attributed). Kilu (Buka Island, northern Solomons), first occupied about 29,000 years ago; Kilu is not intervisible from New Britain, and Kirch (with Spriggs) cites this as key evidence against accidental-drift colonization models, part of a broader argument that Pleistocene Near Oceania saw "the earliest purposive voyaging in the history of humankind" (attributed).

**The "voyaging nursery" and settlement thresholds.** Spriggs (1997:29, quoted) states that the Pleistocene settlement of Manus (Pamwak site) represents "a real threshold in voyaging ability as it is the only island settled in the Pleistocene beyond the range of one-way intervisibility" — a blind crossing of 60–90 km within a roughly 200–230 km voyage. Irwin (1991/1992/1993, via Kirch) proposes that the Bismarck–Solomons region functioned as a "voyaging nursery," sheltered from cyclone belts, with seasonal and predictable wind reversals enabling round-trip exploratory voyages — a theoretical model explicitly flagged by Kirch as speculative ("we remain wholly in the realm of theory").

## Four-Way Non-Identity

Kirch explicitly frames the outcome of Sahul's colonization as producing "diverse cultures, languages, and biological populations" — an appropriately non-conflating formulation, attributed. Later "Papuan"/Non-Austronesian-speaking populations should be linked to this colonizing population with `associated_with`, never asserted as an identity relation.

## Explanatory Theories

A separate, related demographic puzzle — why Near Oceania (excluding the malaria-free New Guinea Highlands) never reached high population density despite 40,000-plus years of occupation and subsequent agriculture — is addressed by Groube's (1993a) endemic-malaria hypothesis, endorsed by Kirch as his preferred explanation rather than presented as a live multi-position dispute in this source: *Plasmodium vivax*, *P. malariae*, and later *P. falciparum*, transmitted by *Anopheles* mosquitoes confined to Near Oceania, Reef-Santa Cruz, and Vanuatu, are argued to have suppressed population growth rates sufficiently that even 0.5% case mortality from *P. vivax* alone could produce an 80% reduction in the natural rate of increase (Groube 1993a:175–76, quoted). The New Guinea Highlands are the exception because altitude excludes *Anopheles*.

## Interaction

The peopling of Sahul is temporally and causally prior to, and analytically distinct from, the much later Lapita/Austronesian colonization of the Bismarck Archipelago and Remote Oceania (see [[austronesian-expansion]]); the two events occurred tens of thousands of years apart and involved different source populations, though both took place within greater Near Oceania.

## Debates

The older hypothesis of accidental drift colonization (floating logs, a "single pregnant female") versus the current consensus of purposive, repeated deliberate voyaging is treated by Kirch as a resolved, superseded historiographical episode rather than a live scholarly dispute, and is recorded here as historiographical background rather than promoted to a separate debates/ page.
