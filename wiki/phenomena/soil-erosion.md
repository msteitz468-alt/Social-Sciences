# Soil Erosion

Accelerated soil erosion is, for [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]), the most substantial and often least reversible human impact on the land surface, because — unlike vegetation — soil re-forms too slowly to recover on human timescales.

## Definition and Scope

Erosion has occurred throughout the [[holocene|Holocene]] from climatic change and fire, but human acceleration via [[deforestation|clearance]], cultivation, and overgrazing becomes dominant toward the present (Douglas 1967). Roberts frames severity through "soil life" — the ratio of soil-loss to soil-formation rate — which can fall to as little as ten years on ancient land surfaces yet is effectively infinite on young [[sea-level-change|alluvial]] floodplains.

## Measurement

Reconstructed chiefly from lake-sediment accumulation rates (Technical Box VIII: multiple cross-correlated cores converting sediment yield to catchment erosion rate), dated by [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] and [[lead-210-dating|²¹⁰Pb/¹³⁷Cs]], with mineral-magnetic "signatures" distinguishing topsoil from bedrock sources, plus colluvium and valley alluvium.

## Patterns and Findings

In attributed voice, from Roberts (1998):
- **Mediterranean "Younger Fill"** (Vita-Finzi 1969): historical-age valley alluvium (chiefly late Roman–early medieval) sourced from eroded topsoils; whether climatic or anthropogenic is debated, with pre-3000 Cal. yr BP alluviation in places arguing against a purely climatic cause.
- **North America**: at Frains Lake, Michigan, clearance from AD 1830 raised erosion from ~0.1 to >5 t/ha/yr, settling at ~0.9 (a tenfold rise) (Davis 1976b); eastern-North-American lake sedimentation tripled with European clearance.
- **Sweden**: Håvgårdssjön shows peak 19th-century erosion tracking ploughland and sheep numbers, with elevated rates already from Viking-era clearance (Dearing et al. 1987).
- **Prehispanic Mesoamerica**: Lake Pátzcuaro sediments record erosion episodes as severe as post-Conquest rates, without the plough (O'Hara et al. 1993).
- **Papua New Guinea highlands**: catchment erosion rose from 0.12 to 0.61 t/ha/yr across the later Holocene, doubling since the 1930s introduction of cash cropping (Lake Egari; Oldfield et al. 1985).
- **Irreversible cases**: the Burren (western Ireland) and English chalk downland lost their soils entirely, leaving limestone pavement and thin rendzinas.

## Explanatory Theories

Erosion responds to disturbance of the vegetation–soil system; whether a given episode reflects climate, fire, or human land use is resolved by dated multi-proxy sediment records (see [[environmental-determinism]] for the causal-attribution caution).

## Interaction

Drives valley [[sea-level-change|alluviation]] and delta building, lake sedimentation and [[eutrophication]], and follows from [[deforestation]] and overgrazing.

## Debates

The climatic-versus-anthropogenic attribution of Mediterranean alluviation is a long-running dispute; Andrew Goudie's (1993) caution — that in many cases responsibility cannot be assigned "without risk of contradiction" between people and nature — states the general problem.
