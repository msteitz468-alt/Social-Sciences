# Infant and Child Mortality

## Definition and Scope

Infant and child mortality — the death of children before their first (infant) or fifth (under-five)
birthday — is a demographic phenomenon that, [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Scheper-Hughes]] argues, is neither
natural nor evenly distributed but produced and concentrated by political-economic conditions. This page
treats it as documented in *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]* for
Northeast Brazil and the [[alto-do-cruzeiro|Alto do Cruzeiro]]; the phenomenon is global and recurrent, but
the record here is specific and date-stamped.

## Measurement

Scheper-Hughes shows that measurement is itself a political act: the "discovery" of infant mortality as a
distinct, tabulated problem is recent (an official English infant mortality rate dates only to 1875), and in
Northeast Brazil roughly two-thirds of infant deaths occurred without medical diagnosis. She reconstructs the
Bom Jesus record from the *cartório civil*, hospital ledgers, and even the municipal coffin-maker's inventory,
finding cause of death left blank for the great majority — a "collective invisibility of women and children."
Reported hospital diagnoses were often tautological ("cessation of heart beat and respiration" — "they died of
having lived"), with the actual killers (dehydration, hunger) systematically under-recorded.

## Patterns and Findings

The following are Scheper-Hughes's attributed findings for her sites, with dates:

- Nationally, roughly **1 million Brazilian children under five died yearly (~40/hour, as of the late 1980s)**;
  about a quarter of all Latin American infant deaths occurred in Brazil, and more than half of Brazil's in
  the Nordeste, whose infant mortality rate she cites at **116/1,000** live births.
- On the Alto, **more than 300 of ~500 municipal child deaths in 1965** came from a shantytown of fewer than
  5,000 — a spike she ties to the dismantling of Governor Miguel Arraes's social programs after the 1964 coup.
  (The book carries a minor internal inconsistency in its 1965 figures, giving "more than three hundred" in
  one place and "either 150 or 350" in another — noted here for transparency.)
- Across her 1965/1985/1987 samples, **~83% of child deaths occurred in the first year of life** and ~58% in
  the first six months; by 1989, 96% of child deaths were infants under 12 months — a "modernization of child
  mortality" in which death is standardized into infancy and contained among the poorest.
- Comparative demography (attributed to the authors she cites): after the 1964 coup, income was transferred
  from the poorest 40% to the richest 10% and infant mortality rose sharply in São Paulo and Belo Horizonte
  (Wood 1977); through the 1980s debt crisis, of ten Latin American countries only Cuba showed steady IMR
  decline (Albanez et al. 1989).

## Explanatory Theories

Scheper-Hughes's "**modernization of child mortality**" thesis (attributed, ironic, paralleling the
"feminization of poverty") holds that economic development reorganizes rather than eliminates child death,
concentrating it among the poor and shifting it to ever-younger ages and to new killers (malnutrition,
bottle-feeding-related diarrhea) as older ones (tetanus, infectious disease) are immunized away. This is set
against ecological/climatic, "rampant fertility," and demographic-transition-lag explanations, which she
argues obscure "pernicious class relations." Feeds and is fed by [[mortal-selective-neglect]] and the
[[political-economy-of-the-emotions]].

## Interaction

High child mortality is, in her argument, a *shaper* of culture, not only its outcome: it produces delayed
attachment, the *anjinho* idiom, and selective neglect — a "political economy of the emotions" in which
maternal love and grief are recalibrated to the demographic reality.
