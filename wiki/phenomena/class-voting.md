# Class Voting

## Definition and Scope

**Class voting** is the systematic association between socioeconomic class position and party or left–right electoral choice. [[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] (*[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*, Chs VII–VIII) treats it as the electoral form of the [[democratic-class-struggle]] in Western democracies.

## Measurement

Lipset: cross-tabulations of occupation, income, and related strata with party support across countries; modifiers for religion, region, community. Later indices (Alford, etc.) not yet primary sources here.

## Patterns and Findings

Attributed to Lipset 1960: left voting responds to group needs (insecurity of income, unsatisfying work, status) shaped by channels of communication, belief in individual mobility, and traditionalism; generational and historical patterns produce continuity and change; American and European systems differ in labor-party organization; sectional exceptions (e.g. one-party South) deviate from pure class models.

## Explanatory Theories

Electoral sociology of group needs and [[cross-pressures]]; contrast with extremist non-democratic class politics ([[working-class-authoritarianism]]). Related institutionalization themes in [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]]'s [[institutionalization-of-class-conflict]] (different program).

## Interaction

[[democratization]], party systems, [[social-mobility]], [[end-of-ideology]] (claimed decline of intense class ideology under affluence).

## Debates

Whether class voting has declined since Lipset's mid-century baseline (later literature not ingested); American exceptionalism vs Europe.
