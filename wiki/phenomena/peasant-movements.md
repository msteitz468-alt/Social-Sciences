# Peasant Movements

## Definition and Scope

In *[[wolf-peasants-1966|Peasants]]* (1966, Ch. 4), [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] treats **peasant movements** as open manifestations of the latent opposition between cultivators and those who siphon surplus. Forms include:

- **Jacquerie** — bloody uprising, often short-lived.
- **Bandit-heroes** — Robin Hood, Diego Corrientes, Jánošík, Pancho Villa, Stenka Razin, Chinese peasant-lore bandits — punish rich, aid poor, claim land; per Hobsbawm (quoted), protest *excessive* oppression, not the existence of poverty/oppression; no realistic reconstruction of the social order.
- **Millenarian / chiliastic** movements — golden age past or revolutionary new order; myths unite but do not organize; avalanche that spends itself without external leadership.
- **Modern revolutionary fuel** — when war devastates traditional leadership (Russia WWI; China under Japanese invasion; Yugoslavia under Axis), Communist professional revolutionary staff can supply long-range strategy peasantry itself lacks; otherwise short-term individual interest reasserts quiescence.

## Measurement

Historical-comparative typology; not quantitative indices in Wolf 1966. Adjacent quantitative revolution research lives on [[revolution]] (Coleman et al.) — different evidence class.

## Patterns and Findings

(Attributed to Wolf 1966.)

- Everyday docility coexists with latent hostility; song and story idolize defiant figures (Robin Hood, Diego Corrientes, Jánošík, Pancho Villa, Stenka Razin, Chinese peasant-lore bandits).
- **Jacquerie** as bloody, often short-lived rising; millenarian myths (golden age past or total new order) can mobilize — European chiliastic movements post-11th c.; Spanish anarchists; Taiping — but myths **unite without organizing**.
- **Hobsbawm (via Wolf):** bandit-heroes protest *excessive* oppression, not the existence of poverty/oppression; they right wrongs and invert oppression occasionally, without reconstructing the social order.
- Without outside organizational framework, peasant alignment is unstable (like [[peasant-coalition|coalitions]]). Avalanche spends itself against resistance.
- **State intact** → movements drowned in blood; short-term individual interest reasserts quiescence.
- **War-induced breakdown** of traditional leadership: Russia (WWI → Communist organizational alternatives for chaotic countryside); China (Japanese invasion + compromised leadership → Communist vacuum-fill, citing Chalmers Johnson); Yugoslavia (Axis invasion + leadership failure).
- **Hypothesis:** Communist professional revolutionary staff supplies long-range strategy peasantry itself lacks; only major prolonged disturbance (esp. warfare) likely enables such staff to lead successful peasant-aided revolution.
- **Position:** such revolutions may be made *with* the peasantry's aid but not *for* the peasantry's sake — aims ultimately at "subjugation and transformation of peasantry into a new kind of social grouping."

### From *Peasant Wars* (1969) — attributed densification

*[[wolf-peasant-wars-1969|Peasant Wars]]* historicizes the 1966 sketch across six cases (Mexico, Russia, China, Viet Nam, Algeria, Cuba). Wolf argues (attributed):

- Shared precipitate: North Atlantic capitalism’s conversion of land, labor, and wealth into commodities, cutting the integument of custom (Polanyi/Heilbroner via Wolf).
- **Who moves:** [[middle-peasant|middle peasants]] and free/peripheral poor with [[tactical-mobility]] — not landlord-dependent poor (without external domain-breaking force) and not rich peasants whose power is derivative of external domains.
- **Multipliers:** periphery, mountain redoubts, ethnic/linguistic codes, communal fortresses (mir, free villages).
- **Limits:** peasants in rebellion are “natural anarchists” of the free village; they cannot alone make a societal revolution — army-forged (Mexico, Algeria) or party-engineered (Russia, China, Viet Nam) paths convert rebellion into seizure; Cuba as sierra rebels (~2,000) plus later party grid.
- Case anchors: Zapata/Morelos free villages; Russian Centre communes; CCP northern bastion; Vietnamese middle owners / Nghe An; Algerian middle-peasant organizers / Kabylia; Oriente squatters (not sugar proletariat).

## Explanatory Theories

- Wolf 1966: surplus-siphoning structure + organizational deficit of peasantry + crisis of state/leadership.
- Wolf 1969: capitalist commodity revolution + power-field / tactical-mobility stratification + army/party conversion of rebellion.
- Hobsbawm *Primitive Rebels* (via Wolf): social banditry as limited protest (Cuba sierra as Robin Hood sympathy).
- Contrast Marx [[peasantry-as-class]] (representation problem; "sack of potatoes") and [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|Skocpol]] state-centered structural revolution theory (state breakdown + agrarian structure; different causal cut).
- Adjacent: Coleman et al. on [[revolution]] (rational-choice authority transfer) — different evidence class.

## Debates

- How much agency vs external organization? Wolf stresses organizational deficit and power-field constraints without denying latent opposition.
- Middle-peasant thesis contested (selection on successful zones; operationalization).
- Social banditry romanticization (later critiques of Hobsbawm — not primary here).
- Revolution *with* vs *for* peasantry — 1966 position; 1969 adds natural-anarchist limit and army/party typology.
- Wolf vs Skocpol causal architectures (commodity/stratum leverage vs state autonomy under international pressure) — complementary programs, not wiki-voice adjudication.

## Everyday resistance vs movement-centrism (Scott 1985)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c]], *[[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985|Weapons of the Weak]]*.)* Scott challenged movement- and rebellion-centric maps of peasant politics: archives and social science notice named, organized threats to the state, missing the coral reef of petty insubordination. [[everyday-forms-of-resistance]] is not a substitute claim that movements never matter; it is a claim about the modal historical form of subordinate politics. Sedaka shows failed open campaigns (combines) alongside routine infra-politics. Study: [[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-study]]. Contrasts with revolution-centric [[wolf-peasant-wars-study]].
