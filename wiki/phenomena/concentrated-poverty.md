# Concentrated Poverty

## Definition and Scope

Geographic clustering of poor households into neighborhoods with very high poverty rates — distinct from citywide poverty rates. In Wilson’s Chicago analysis, growth of high- and extreme-poverty community areas epitomizes inner-city class transformation (*[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|The Truly Disadvantaged]]*, 1987).

## Measurement

Community-area or tract poverty shares (Wilson 1970–1980 maps); later national tract analyses (e.g. Jargowsky, cited in Wilson 2012 Afterword — date-stamp). Thresholds vary (20/30/40%).

## Patterns and Findings

Wilson (1987, attributed): black inner-city areas saw sharp increases in concentrated poverty as nonpoor exited and joblessness rose. Massey & Denton (1993, attributed): segregation multiplies citywide black poverty increases into extreme neighborhood poverty — see [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]. Anderson (1999, attributed): Germantown Avenue “hyperghetto” pockets as concentrated ghetto poverty with social isolation and street-code public order ([[philadelphia-inner-city]]; [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study]]). Desmond (2016, attributed): forced moves push families into poorer and more dangerous neighborhoods; high-eviction areas associated with higher subsequent violent crime; landlord screening concentrates deep poverty at the address level ([[eviction]]; [[desmond-evicted-study]]). Small (2009, attributed): high-poverty *neighborhood location* can increase childcare centers' **organizational connectedness** (referrals/collaborations) via state and nonprofit targeting — a buffering channel for enrolled mothers that standard neighborhood-effects tests miss; the unenrolled and the **organizational isolate** remain exposed ([[small-unanticipated-gains-study]]; [[neighborhood-effects-debate]]; [[network-inequality]]).

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Claim (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Wilson | Restructuring + class exit → concentration → isolation/concentration effects |
| Massey & Denton | Segregation is necessary structural link for extreme black poverty concentration |
| Desmond | Eviction and extractive rental markets produce instability and worse-neighborhood sorting within poor areas |
| Small | Organizational ties of local institutions can buffer (or, if scarce, amplify) place disadvantage; organizational isolation deepens Wilson-style isolation |
| Culture/welfare | Secondary or primary rivals — contested |
