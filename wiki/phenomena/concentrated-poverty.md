# Concentrated Poverty

## Definition and Scope

Geographic clustering of poor households into neighborhoods with very high poverty rates — distinct from citywide poverty rates. In Wilson’s Chicago analysis, growth of high- and extreme-poverty community areas epitomizes inner-city class transformation (*[[wilson-the-truly-disadvantaged-1987|The Truly Disadvantaged]]*, 1987). [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] (*[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]*, 2012) treats concentrated poverty as a **durably ranked ecological property** of neighborhoods — stable relative position amid secular growth and high residential turnover — and as a structural input to [[concentrated-disadvantage]] and low [[collective-efficacy]].

## Measurement

Community-area or tract poverty shares (Wilson 1970–1980 maps); later national tract analyses (e.g. Jargowsky, cited in Wilson 2012 Afterword — date-stamp). Thresholds vary (20/30/40%). Sampson/PHDCN embed poverty within a multi-indicator **concentrated-disadvantage PCA** (welfare, poverty, unemployment, female-headed households, percent black, density of children) rather than poverty share alone.

## Patterns and Findings

Wilson (1987, attributed): black inner-city areas saw sharp increases in concentrated poverty as nonpoor exited and joblessness rose. Massey & Denton (1993, attributed): segregation multiplies citywide black poverty increases into extreme neighborhood poverty — see [[underclass-and-segregation-debate]]. Anderson (1999, attributed): Germantown Avenue “hyperghetto” pockets as concentrated ghetto poverty with social isolation and street-code public order ([[philadelphia-inner-city]]; [[anderson-code-of-the-street-study]]). Edin & Kefalas (2005, attributed): eight Philly/Camden neighborhoods selected for ≥20% poverty and high single-parent shares, including rare **white** high-poverty industrial villages (Kensington, PennsPort) that enable class-comparable comparison with African American and Puerto Rican areas; local nonmarital birth rates ~twice the national rate by 2000; sex ratios “grossly out of balance” in minority neighborhoods ([[edin-kefalas-promises-study]]; [[nonmarital-childbearing]]). Desmond (2016, attributed): forced moves push families into poorer and more dangerous neighborhoods; high-eviction areas associated with higher subsequent violent crime; landlord screening concentrates deep poverty at the address level ([[eviction]]; [[desmond-evicted-study]]). Small (2009, attributed): high-poverty *neighborhood location* can increase childcare centers' **organizational connectedness** (referrals/collaborations) via state and nonprofit targeting — a buffering channel for enrolled mothers that standard neighborhood-effects tests miss; the unenrolled and the **organizational isolate** remain exposed ([[small-unanticipated-gains-study]]; [[neighborhood-effects-debate]]; [[network-inequality]]).

**Sampson (2012, attributed — *Great American City* ch. 5+):**
- **Stability amid change.** Morenoff & Sampson: neighborhood poverty 1970 vs 1990 correlates **r=0.87**; ~67% of poverty variance was between neighborhoods rather than within over time; mean neighborhood poverty rose from 11% (1970) to 20% (1990), 75th percentile from 14% to 30% — secular growth with **stable relative rank order** (persistently concentrated while increasingly prevalent). Against pure “social transformation” narratives that imply wholesale reordering of the map.
- **Forty-year forecastability.** At Chicago community-area level, family poverty **1960 vs 2000 correlates r=0.78** (p<0.01); Riverdale and South Side “black belt” areas stand out as long-run “poverty carriers” versus well-off white areas (e.g. Norwood, Edison Park). Knowing a neighborhood’s poverty at one time allows accurate forecasting up to four decades later **despite population turnover**.
- **Welfare concentration.** Percent receiving public assistance 1990–2000 highly stable at neighborhood level in Chicago (N=844) and U.S. (N≈64,902), identical **r=0.85**, despite federal welfare reform.
- **Long reach of early poverty on process.** Concentrated poverty in **1970** has resilient negative association with collective efficacy extending to 2002 — in places stronger than concurrent 1990 poverty — a cumulative “Matthew Effect” of reputation and collective memory (Sampson, attributed).
- **Disorder forecast.** With [[raudenbush-stephen|Raudenbush]], concentrated poverty and mixed land use significantly forecasted **observed** physical and social disorder (SSO); CE predicted lower disorder and violence after controls.
- **MTO design limits.** Applying MTO-equivalent filters to PHDCN yields ~5% of families; Chicago MTO destinations reduced poverty only modestly (e.g. ~42→37% for compliers) while hypersegregation and social-process gaps barely moved — Sampson argues MTO is not a general test of neighborhood effects ([[neighborhood-effects-debate]]; [[sampson-great-american-city-study]]).

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Claim (attributed) |
|---|---|
| Wilson | Restructuring + class exit → concentration → isolation/concentration effects |
| Massey & Denton | Segregation is necessary structural link for extreme black poverty concentration |
| Sampson | Durable ecological inequality + spatial interdependence + structural sorting (“neighborhoods choose people”); poverty as input to multi-indicator disadvantage and low CE; selection is itself a neighborhood process |
| Desmond | Eviction and extractive rental markets produce instability and worse-neighborhood sorting within poor areas |
| Small | Organizational ties of local institutions can buffer (or, if scarce, amplify) place disadvantage; organizational isolation deepens Wilson-style isolation |
| Culture/welfare | Secondary or primary rivals — contested |
