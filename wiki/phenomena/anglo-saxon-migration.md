# Anglo-Saxon Migration

Demographic and cultural transformations in lowland Britain in the mid-first millennium CE conventionally linked to migration and settlement from the North Sea Germanic coast-lands. Primary synthesis: [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]], *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* (2013), especially Ch. 12 and the late Roman lead-in in Ch. 11. Scale of migration versus elite dominance versus continuity is contested — see [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]].

## Definition and Scope

**What is included.** (1) Late Roman Germanization of the army and coasts (*laeti*/*foederati* possibilities; Saxon Shore). (2) Fifth-century settlement and conflict conventionally called the *Adventus Saxonum*. (3) Sixth–seventh-century westward expansion of Germanic material culture and kingdoms to c.650. (4) Parallel “folk wanderings” Cunliffe groups with this horizon: Pictish and Irish pressure, British emigration to Armorica (and some to Galicia), and the consolidation of post-Roman British polities in the west (Cunliffe 2013).

**What is not automatically included.** Later Scandinavian (Viking) settlement — related North Sea mobility but a separate phenomenon page: [[viking-age-britain]]. “Anglo-Saxon” as a later ethnic-political identity is an outcome, not a starting package identical with every cremation urn or brooch type.

**Four-way non-identity (mandatory).** Material-culture package (“Saxon” pottery, jewellery, weapon burials) ≠ West Germanic language ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious “English” peoplehood. Roman “Saxon” was already shorthand for multiple coastal ethnicities; Bede et al. distinguish Frisians, Saxons, Angles, and Jutes from ~900 km of coast (Cunliffe 2013). Later kingdom names are not pure ethnic blocks.

## Patterns and Findings

*(Narrative and figures attributed to Cunliffe 2013 and the sources he cites unless noted.)*

### Late Roman preconditions (third–fourth centuries)

Cunliffe holds that late third-century Britain already heavily used Rhineland and free-German recruits; Carausius allegedly dealt with Frankish pirates; Allectus’s field army was Germanic mercenaries. *Laeti*/*foederati* settlement in Britain is a real possibility analogous to northern Gaul; fourth-century eastern Britain (Thames–Humber) distinctive pottery with German stylistic links **hints** at a Germanic population component from the early fourth century — not “indisputable evidence of extensive settlement” (Cunliffe 2013). *Litus Saxonicum* may mean shore settled by Saxons as much as shore attacked by them (Cunliffe’s position).

Military crises framing mobility: after Constantius’s 296 reconquest, relative peace until the emperor’s midwinter visit of 342 (buffer zone Wall–Forth/Clyde with *areani* scouts vs [[picts]]; Pevensey added to the Saxon Shore). **360:** Picts north of Forth–Clyde and Scots (then NE Ireland) troubled pro-Roman tribes north of the Wall. **365:** Picts, Scots, Attacotti, and Saxons attacked. **Summer 367** *barbarica conspiratio* (Ammianus via Cunliffe): multi-front assault; Hadrian’s Wall overrun and outflanked by sea; chaos to the Thames; Count of the Saxon Shore Nectaridus killed; *dux Britanniarum* Fullofaudes taken prisoner. Count Theodosius restored order with ~2,000 *comitatenses*; *areani* abandoned; new buffer agreements with Votadini (east) and Strathclyde (west); Yorkshire coastal signal stations vs Pictish ships (Cunliffe 2013).

Successive troop stripping (Magnentius 350, Maximus 383, Stilicho 401, Constantine III 407) left little concerted defence. Removal of the last troops in **407** is Cunliffe’s virtual end of [[roman-britain]]; Saxons raided seriously in **408**. Zosimus: Britons expelled Roman governors and set up their own administration (Cunliffe 2013).

### Adventus narrative and Cunliffe’s chronology

**Gildas’s story** (via Cunliffe): post-separation prosperity; renewed Pictish/Scottish raids and plague; Vortigern settled Saxon *foederati* in the east for monthly rations (*epimenia*) and corn (*annona*) against the Picts; more Saxons arrived; rebellion over insufficient food led to pillage and town-sacking westwards; Ambrosius Aurelianus rallied Britons; fighting until British victory at **Mons Badonicus** and a long peace. Nennius/*Historia Brittonum* adds a Kentish tradition: a pro-Roman faction opposed Vortigern’s *foederati* policy, cut supplies, and tried to send settlers home — sparking revolt; Vortigern’s faction then invited still more Saxons (Cunliffe 2013).

**Chronology Cunliffe accepts:** Bede’s 449 initial landings are “based on misunderstandings and… far too late.” The mid-fifth-century Gallic Chronicle under year **442** has Britain “passed under the authority of the Saxons” (confederate rebellion reading). ~446 “groans of the Britons” plea to Aetius (Gildas): barbarians drive to the sea / sea drives back; Aetius too engaged to help. Cunliffe’s outline: major Saxon incursion soon after 407; Britons claim independence and self-defence; 420s–430s more Saxons settle with encouragement of at least one British faction; ~440 immigrants expand beyond proscribed territories; westward advance temporarily halted ~**500** at Mons Badonicus (often identified with Badbury Castle, Dorset). He holds this narrative “conforms well with archaeology,” while ethnicity change needs separate treatment (Cunliffe 2013).

### Causes of folk movement

Principal causes (Cunliffe): **population pressures + rising sea-levels.** The Roman frontier blocked natural movements on the North European Plain; late-third breakthroughs were temporarily restored, then final disintegration a century later. Coastal peoples also lost inhabitable land to sea-level rise and lost Roman-linked economy → pirate raiding (late third) graded into immigration/settlement by invitation or force (Cunliffe 2013). Fourth–fifth-century North Sea mixed traders and freighters/naval escorts for British grain to the Rhine army with “Saxon” pirates and seaborne Picts; Vegetius describes camouflaged sea-green scout ships (*pictae*). After 407 Cunliffe doubts the fleet was seen again; within a year or two settler boat-loads arrived; continued style-tracking of pottery and jewellery implies regular back-and-forth (Cunliffe 2013).

### Archaeological signature of eastern settlement

Cunliffe’s synthesis: the archaeological signature in eastern Britain is **“stark”** — Roman lifestyle material disappears; towns and villas abandoned; centralized production collapses; coin minting ceases; new nucleated wooden settlements on often new sites; alien material culture from Germanic homelands; cemeteries (cremation then inhumation) with ethnicity symbols (pottery, jewellery, weapons). Some occupation of Roman towns (Canterbury, Dorchester-on-Thames sunken-featured buildings) and villa continuity exist, but overall eastern replacement is “rapid and thorough” (Cunliffe 2013). Material-culture package replacement is **not** automatically total population replacement.

**Regional patterns (Cunliffe 2013):**
- **Earliest Germanic settlers:** large cremation cemeteries in East Anglia, Lincolnshire, eastern midlands, into eastern Yorkshire; urn parallels in Anglian region north of the Elbe and Saxon homeland Elbe–Weser; homeland urns datable ~380–420 → earliest British cremation cemeteries may mark mass family movement **408–9** that prompted the provincial plea to Rome.
- **Later fifth century:** East Anglia/east midlands around Wash wetlands link firmly to Anglian and Jutish regions (female-burial brooch distributions). British enclaves likely survived around Lincoln and Verulamium (St Albans) — surrounding areas devoid of early Germanic cemeteries — and other rural Romano-British tracts continued farming.
- **Thames corridor:** Germanic inhumations both banks from estuary to Cotswolds; men with weapons and late Roman military belt fittings; women with Romano-British trinkets + Germanic brooches. Cunliffe tempts reading this as deliberate settlement of Frankish/Saxon *foederati* to protect London approaches and the Thames route west (e.g. Mucking; Dorchester-on-Thames) — a buffer vs further uncontrolled entry and vs eastern Germanic expansion.
- **Kent:** historical tradition of three ship-loads of Jutes under Hengist allowed by Vortigern onto Thanet (via Frisian coast); not prior late-Roman *foederati*; pottery/brooches of Jutish style support the tradition; then strong Frankish trade/intermarriage links; separate Jutish enclave on the Isle of Wight and neighbouring Hampshire.
- **By mid-fifth century:** disparate Germanic groups (invited and not) in the south-east; continuous inflow shown by pottery parallels with continental homelands. Spatial patterning: Anglian communities around Wash wetlands; Saxons in Thames valley, south midlands, Hampshire–Sussex — basis of later Anglian and Saxon kingdoms.

By the sixth century settlements reached the Solent and Cherbourg peninsula; Saxon pottery of Cherbourg clays at Portchester shows cross-Channel contact among immigrant communities. Fifth/early sixth: gangs of young men, families, and small communities in boat-migration; footholds enabled chain migration into decaying Britannia (Cunliffe 2013). Late fifth/early sixth intensified Frankish cross-Channel trade; Kent received Frankish workshop luxuries largely via elite gift exchange, culminating late sixth in Æthelberht’s marriage to Frankish princess Bertha (Cunliffe 2013).

### Westward expansion and British response

Westward spread of Anglo-Saxon culture stalled ~500 by British success at Mons Badonicus (if Gildas is believed); Gildas ~540 wrote of “present security”; within a generation pressure resumed. Battle of **Dyrham** (trad. 577) → fall of Cirencester, Gloucester, Bath and the Cotswolds; ~590 northern Britons defeated at Catterick; by 650 most of Dorset overrun, Somerset within the next decade. Only Dumnonia (south-west), Wales, and parts of the Pennines/north-west remained under British kings (Cunliffe 2013, using Gildas + ASC tradition).

Collapse of Roman order in the north and west left a patchwork of small polities under warlords/“tyrants” (Gildas and Procopius). Gildas names Vortigern (“proud tyrant”) and Ambrosius Aurelianus (descended from Roman aristocracy); both may have been urban elite; towns as rallying centres. Arthur is **not** in Gildas; later Welsh legend may preserve a shadowy Artorius — Noel Myres (quoted by Cunliffe): no borderline figure “has wasted more of the historian’s time” (Cunliffe 2013).

Western Roman towns as resistance centres: ASC names Cirencester, Gloucester, Bath falling after Dyrham, implying other towns survived into the sixth century; Wroxeter baths basilica continued use well into the fifth century if not later. Hill-fort reuse: South Cadbury (Somerset) — Iron Age inner rampart rebuilt timber-and-rubble end of fifth century; large timber buildings; Mediterranean imported pottery → elite coercive power. Also Cadbury Congresbury, Cannington (+ large sub-Roman cemetery). Wansdyke probably a fifth/sixth territorial boundary in Anglo-Saxon vs British conflict. Cunliffe reads reuse as return to tribalism / safe havens under threat (Cunliffe 2013).

Gildas (*De Excidio* 24–25) describes Saxon devastation “from sea to sea,” towns battered by rams, and pre-Mons Badonicus chaos: survivors butchered in mountains; hunger-broken surrender → perpetual slavery or immediate death; sea exodus “scattered… among the heathen”; others held high hills, forests, sea cliffs — **position recording**, not wiki-voice demography (Gildas via Cunliffe 2013).

### British emigration to Armorica and Galicia

Procopius calls Armorica “Britannia”; Sidonius Apollinaris mentions Britanni under king Riothamus on the Loire mid-fifth century; later sources speak of large fighting forces; Council of Tours 567 still mentions Britanni; from the late sixth century the peninsula is widely “Britannia” → Brittany. Breton language was long taken as proof of mass migration from south-west Britain; more recent linguistic work (as Cunliffe reports) questions mass-language-transfer: native Armoricans probably already spoke a Gallic dialect closely akin to western British Celtic; emerging Breton = indigenous Gallic strengthened by immigrant British Celtic, not introduced *de novo* (Cunliffe 2013). Language kinship ≠ scale of migration; migration remains historically attested. Northern Armorican ports (Alet, Le Yaudet) contacted southern British ports from at least the third century; late third/fourth Irish raids and Maximus’s 383 deployments may have encouraged earlier moves; exodus continued mid-sixth (Procopius). Settlers concentrated north/west peninsula → kingdoms Domnonée and Cornouaille reflecting Dumnonia and Cornwall — immigrant numbers/status sufficient for political dominance (Cunliffe 2013). One Atlantic-exodus group reached Galicia (NW Iberia), founding abbey Santa María de Bretoña near Mondoñedo (active mid-sixth century) (Cunliffe 2013).

### How many? Scale estimates (as of Cunliffe 2013)

**Core debate** (Cunliffe’s framing): does stark Germanic cultural takeover mean total eastern replacement (Britons slaughtered/driven west as Gildas) or a small elite (~10,000) whose culture was avidly adopted by numerically dominant Britons? The debate “has relied more on archaeological fashion than on hard facts” (Cunliffe 2013) — see [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]].

**Modern Y-chromosome studies** (as of Cunliffe 2013): inconsistent — some claim near-total eastern male replacement; others estimate Anglo-Saxon male intrusion at most **9–15%** in eastern England and ~**4%** for the British Isles as a whole. mtDNA work suggests female immigration unlikely to exceed **20%**. These are modern-DNA inferences, not culture or language maps.

**Cunliffe’s methodological critique of modern DNA for this question:** (1) immigrant source coast ~900 km, long-mobile — hard to characterize a genetic signature; (2) chronology — millennia of prior eastern Britain–continent exchange, Roman army Germanic from the outset, fourth-century army heavily Germanized with Saxon mercenaries — cannot separate third/fourth-century Y chromosomes from fifth-century settlers; (3) Angles/Jutes from the same Danish territory as later Viking Danes. Modern DNA is “unlikely… ever… [to] give reliable figures”; ancient DNA from early cemeteries may eventually help (Cunliffe 2013).

**Archaeological/skeletal cemetery estimates** (Cunliffe summarizing attempts): immigrant:native ~**1:3 to 1:5** in southern/eastern Anglo-Saxon cemeteries; ~**1:10** in later western/northern expansion zones. If mid-fifth Britain population ~2 million, total immigrants “as many as 250,000” but “likely… significantly less, around **100,000 to 200,000**” — order-of-magnitude only. Both genetic and archaeological approaches, in Cunliffe’s 2013 reading, point to significant south-eastern immigration on the order of **10–20%** in the fifth century — neither total replacement nor negligible elite-only transfer.

### Cross-check against Cunliffe (2001)

Cunliffe's earlier treatment in *Facing the Ocean* (2001) periodizes the same
collapse in two phases — c. 250–450, a "systems collapse" with population mobility
"on a scale probably never before experienced in Europe," and c. 450–800, a period
of "continuous readjustment and consolidation" — and grounds the frontier collapse
in a **demographic-gradient thesis**: the Rhine–Danube frontier, fixed early under
Hadrian, blocked a long-standing north-to-south population flow from high-growth
"barbarian" zones into the low-growth Roman core, building unsustainable pressure
along the frontier as the empire's core population declined (Cunliffe 2001). This
is broadly consistent with, and supplies deeper background for, the 2013 causal
synthesis above (population pressure, sea-level change, frontier collapse). Cunliffe
(2001) also frames the earliest Anglo-Saxon settlement (c. 410–420, East Anglia/east
Midlands/Lincolnshire/east Yorkshire cremation cemeteries) as likely occupying a
landscape already depleted of Romano-British population, and reads the Thames-valley
inhumation group with Roman-derived military belts as possible British-recruited
*foederati* of Saxon/Frankish origin — the same buffer-zone interpretation as the
2013 synthesis. On the Breton-language question, Cunliffe (2001) again favors
indigenous continuity over wholesale replacement, citing an east–west
cultural/linguistic divide in Armorica traceable archaeologically to the 4th century
BCE and persisting to the 4th century CE (place-name distributions matching the
historical KLT/Vannetais dialect split) as evidence reducing the need to posit
large-scale population replacement — see [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]].

### Consolidation by AD 600–700

By ~AD 500 Cunliffe argues the frenetic pace of fifth-century mobility slackened into consolidation of polities and new elite trade rhythms (Atlantic Mediterranean goods; Frankish south coast; Anglian North Sea/Baltic including Sutton Hoo–Vendel links) (Cunliffe 2013). By **AD 600** Germanic settlement of eastern Britain was firmly established with kingdoms crystallizing: Anglians north to Bamburgh/Forth; Mercia toward the Welsh border; West Saxons vs Dumnonia; three polities — Northumbria, Mercia, Wessex — becoming more state-like. By **700** Northumbria stretched from Irish Sea to North Sea, Strathclyde border to Humber–Mersey; Edinburgh Castle rock (Gododdin) taken 638; Rheged, Elmet, Whithorn absorbed (Cunliffe 2013). Mercia’s Welsh border was later defined by Offa’s Dyke; expansion checked when Egbert of Wessex beat Mercia at Elendun **825** as Vikings appeared as a third force (Cunliffe 2013; see [[viking-age-britain]]).

Cunliffe’s Ch. 12 retrospect: a child born in southern Britain’s comparative tranquillity of the 340s would in old age have seen increasing turmoil, total collapse of order/government, and alien incursion on unprecedented scale (Cunliffe 2013). His epilogue frames Germanic fifth-century migrants as motivated by homeland pressures, sea-level rise, and collapse of trading systems plus opportunity; islanders a “mongrel race” enriched by continuous migration (Cunliffe 2013 — interpretive thesis).

## Explanatory Theories

| Position | Core claim | Holders / vehicles in this source |
|---|---|---|
| Total replacement | Eastern Britons slaughtered or driven west; language/culture = population | Gildas’s rhetoric; older invasionist archaeology |
| Elite dominance / culture transfer | Small immigrant elite (~10k); native majority adopts Germanic package | Modern minimalist archaeological fashion (as Cunliffe frames the debate) |
| Significant minority immigration | Order of 10–20% SE fifth-century inflow; culture change faster than genes | Cunliffe’s 2013 balance of cemetery ratios + cautious genetics |
| Late Roman Germanization first | Army, *laeti*, Saxon Shore enclaves pre-structure fifth-century settlement | Cunliffe’s Ch. 11–12 continuity argument |
| Multi-causal folk wandering | Population + sea-level + frontier collapse + invitation + chain migration | Cunliffe’s causal synthesis |

Cunliffe does **not** treat material culture, language, and genes as a single isomorphic “Saxon people.”

## Interaction

- **Preceded / enabled by:** late [[roman-britain]] military mobility, coastal defence, and collapse of central government after 407.
- **Concurrent with:** Pictish and Irish raiding/settlement; British emigration to Armorica; Atlantic monastic and ceramic trade networks in the west (Cunliffe 2013).
- **Followed / overlapped by:** [[viking-age-britain]] (from late eighth century) — Cunliffe warns modern DNA confounds Angles/Jutes with later Danish Vikings.
- **Shapes:** early English [[state-formation]]; English language geography; the standing [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]].
- **Related societies:** [[picts]]; western British polities (Dumnonia, Wales, Strathclyde, Gododdin) as resistance and successor contexts.

## Debates

Primary filing: [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]]. Live axes as of Cunliffe’s 2013 synthesis include:

1. **Scale of fifth-century immigration** (total replacement vs elite vs significant minority).
2. **Whether modern DNA can answer** (Cunliffe’s strong scepticism **as of 2013**).
3. **Bede 449 vs Gallic Chronicle 442 / post-407 chronology.**
4. **Mons Badonicus historicity and location.**
5. **Brittany: mass migration vs language reinforcement of related Gallic dialect.**
6. **Thames corridor *foederati* buffer** as deliberate late Roman/British policy vs uncontrolled settlement.
7. **Relation of “Saxon” ethnonym to multi-ethnic North Sea source populations.**

## Historiography / source notes

Cunliffe treats Gildas as a serious if emotional source with possible family tradition and monastic library texts; other near-contemporaries: Zosimus (late fourth), Procopius and Gallic Chroniclers (~Gildas’s time); later Nennius, Bede, and the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle are retrospective, selective, and agenda-distorted (Cunliffe 2013). Genetic figures and cemetery ratios on this page are **as of Cunliffe 2013** and must not be silently overwritten by later aDNA without the Contradiction Protocol.
