# Green Revolution

> First vault densification from [[scott-james-c|Scott]], *[[scott-weapons-of-the-weak-1985|Weapons of the Weak]]* (1985) — **Muda Irrigation Scheme / Kedah** double-cropping and mechanization case. Not a comprehensive global synthesis; comparative claims remain attributed or pending other sources.

## Definition and Scope

Large-scale postwar transformation of staple agriculture via high-yield varieties, irrigation, chemical inputs, credit packages, and (often) mechanization. In Scott’s Malaysian case, the **Muda Irrigation Project** (begun 1966, full operation by ~1973) enabled double-cropping of wet rice on roughly 260,000 acres, coupled with HYVs, fertilizer, tractors, combines, credit, and milling/marketing infrastructure.

## Measurement

Scott draws on Muda scheme statistics (farm size, tenure, income phases, poverty lines) plus village-level accounts in [[sedaka|Sedaka]] for 1967 vs late-1970s comparisons (Horii baseline). World Bank / official success narratives (e.g. high double-cropping rates by 1974) are reported and then counterposed to distributional and wage outcomes.

## Patterns and Findings

*(Attributed — Scott 1985 on Muda/Sedaka.)*

- **Income phases:** broad but unequal real gains ~1966–74 (owner-tenants far ahead of small tenants); nominal and real retreat 1974–79 for most categories; small tenants often no better — or worse — in real terms than before the scheme period.
- **Poverty:** much 1966–74 poverty-line improvement undone by 1979; Scott reports ≥33,000 officially poor households in Muda in 1979 despite soils, institutions, anti-poverty programs, and subsidies.
- **Mechanization:** combine-harvesters from mid-1970s eliminated a large share of harvest wage opportunities (preface synthesis: ~two-thirds for smallholders and landless in Sedaka’s experience); tractors reduced ploughing labor demand.
- **Tenure:** pressure toward cash/*pajak* rents, landlord reclamation, loss of “living rents”; cultivated acreage among villagers declined as outside owners and larger operators consolidated.
- **Class structure thesis:** shift from **exploitation-with-interdependence** (rich needed local labor/tenants) to **marginalization** (poor “cut adrift” when labor became dispensable) — “freedom of the unemployed.”

## Explanatory Theories

Scott reads Green Revolution change through class power and [[moral-economy]] struggle, not only through aggregate yield success. Ideological conflict over combines, rents, and charity is part of the phenomenon’s local reality, not an epiphenomenon.

In *[[scott-seeing-like-a-state-1998|Seeing Like a State]]* (1998) Scott added an epistemic critique: high-yielding varieties are a [[high-modernism|high-modernist]] package whose genetic uniformity produces a recurring "biological arms race" against pests and durable commercial dependency on seed and chemical suppliers, and whose single yield metric hides the multi-use values (fodder, cooking properties, risk-spreading) that cultivator [[metis]] optimized in landraces and polycropping.
