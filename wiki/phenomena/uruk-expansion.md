# Uruk Expansion

The spread of southern Mesopotamian ([[uruk-culture|Uruk]]) material culture, settlements,
and — on the dominant reading — actual colonists across the Susiana plain, the
Syro-Mesopotamian plains, and the Zagros/Taurus highlands during the second half of the
4th millennium BCE. It is the empirical core of [[algaze-guillermo|Algaze's]]
[[algaze-uruk-world-system-2005|"Uruk world system"]] thesis and the subject of the
[[uruk-world-system-debate]]. This page records the phenomenon; the framework claims
built on it ([[informal-empire]], [[world-systems-analysis|world system]]) are attributed
on their own pages.

## Definition and Scope

The expansion is known from two evidence classes: (a) **settlement patterns** of intrusive
Uruk sites outside the alluvium, and (b) **typical Uruk artifacts** — mass-produced
bevel-rim bowls, four-lugged and spouted jars, cylinder seals, and numerical-notation
tablets — turning up at indigenous peripheral sites. [[algaze-guillermo|Algaze]]
distinguishes two regional modes (attributed): **outright colonization** of the Susiana
plain of Khuzestan (indigenous settlement in centuries-long decline; irrigation
agriculture matching the alluvium), and a thin, strategic network of
[[gateway-community|enclaves, stations, and outposts]] in Syro-Mesopotamia consistent with
[[informal-empire|informal economic control]] exercised through indigenous intermediaries.

Whether "intrusive Uruk material culture" indexes migrating people or diffusing style is
an interpretive claim, not a fact — Algaze argues for population movement/colonization
(the "colonization hypothesis," with Amiet, Nissen, Sürenhagen), against an acculturation
reading (Henry Wright, Gregory Johnson). **Four-way non-identity applies:** Uruk pots are
not automatically Uruk people, a language, or a genetic population.

## Measurement

The expansion is operationalized through three evidence channels, none decisive alone:
(1) the **distribution of intrusive Uruk material culture** (bevel-rim bowls,
four-lugged/spouted jars, cylinder seals, numerical tablets) mapped against indigenous
chaff-tempered assemblages; (2) **regional settlement-pattern survey** distinguishing
intrusive from indigenous sites (of explicitly uneven coverage — some survey regions have
size data for a small fraction of sites); (3) **calibrated radiocarbon** (Wright & Rupley
2001, OxCal). Neutron-activation sourcing (bevel-rim bowls made of local clay) shows the
diagnostic pottery was locally produced, so "Uruk material" measures *contact/emulation or
implantation*, not traded objects — a validity caution the [[uruk-world-system-debate]]
turns on.

## Chronology (as of Wright & Rupley 2001)

The 1st-edition chronology rested on relative glyptic/tablet typology cross-correlated to
the Eanna VI–IV sequence at Warka and the Susa Acropolis I levels. The 2005 edition adopts
**Henry Wright and Eric Rupley's (2001)** compilation and recalibration (OxCal) of all
available radiocarbon dates from southern, intrusive-peripheral, and indigenous Late
Chalcolithic sites: the Middle and Late Uruk phases together "lasted for at least 700
years, and occurred between ca. **3800 and 3100 B.C.**" (cal BCE per the source's
calibration-software framing) — the most comprehensive absolute-chronology basis
available. Algaze stresses **Ubaid-period antecedents** (tallying systems, craft
specialization, small urban centers at Eridu/Ur/Tell el-'Oueili, monumental temple
continuity), reading the expansion as intensification, not rupture.

## Patterns and Findings: Staged Model (attributed — Algaze 2005)

1. Ad hoc colonization of the southwestern Iranian (Susiana) plains.
2. Small settlements plus **takeover of Late Chalcolithic centers** at the apex of
   Syro-Mesopotamian settlement hierarchies (Nineveh, [[tell-brak|Brak]], Carchemish,
   Samsat).
3. Climax with substantial urban **enclaves** ([[habuba-kabira|Habuba]]/Qannas/Aruda in
   the Tabqa reach; Kum Ocağı/Şadi Tepe/Tiladir Tepe near Carchemish).
4. Final stage — isolated highland **outposts** (Godin, Sialk).

The 2005 revision recasts the earliest (Middle Uruk) phase as a few widely separated
"karum-like" outposts (Hacınebi, Tiladir Tepe, Tell Abr, Tell Sheikh Hassan), densifying
into the Late Uruk Euphrates-corridor colonization (now up to 29 Uruk-material sites over
110 km, per Tishreen Dam surveys).

## Explanatory Theories (side by side, attributed)

- **Resource procurement** (Algaze's primary emphasis): a resource-poor core importing
  timber, metals, and stone via [[mesopotamian-trade|long-distance trade]], with textiles
  and processed goods as the return export.
- **Elite ideology / self-aggrandizement** (Conrad & Demarest 1984, cited).
- **Textile-driven pastoral access** (Rita Wright 1989): settlement at watered
  steppe-edges to reach sheep/goat husbandry zones.
- **Population pressure** (Areshian; Lamberg-Karlovsky; Schwartz) — Algaze notes this is
  only weakly supported, since Late Uruk occupied-area estimates barely exceed Early Uruk
  (Adams 1984).
- **Environmental dislocation** — Algaze *raises but does not assert* that Tigris/Euphrates
  channel-shifting may have driven population movement. Per the Evidence-Class Framework,
  an environmental proxy synchronous with social change licenses only `concurrent_with`,
  not `caused_by`.

## Interaction and Aftermath

The network **collapsed abruptly** at the end of the Uruk period (last quarter of the 4th
millennium BCE). Algaze's explicitly speculative dual mechanism: alluvial
socio-environmental strain (political centralization → intensified irrigation →
salinization, tentatively read from a ~3:1 barley:wheat ratio in the Archaic Texts) plus
peripheral strengthening (contact-driven local development making some peripheral polities
expansive enough to threaten southern route control). Aftermaths diverged: parts of the
northern periphery (Ninevite V) reverted to **simpler** forms, while the Proto-Elamite
state (Tal-i Malyan/Anshan) **expanded** to fill and even extend the vacated network — a
genuine complication for any simple collapse narrative, and the empirical basis of the
`informal-empire` fork (collapse vs. strengthening).

## Debates

Whether the expansion was structurally **asymmetric** (Algaze: core dominance, an
[[informal-empire|informal empire]]/[[world-systems-analysis|world system]]) or hosted
**symmetric** [[trade-diaspora|trade-diaspora]] coexistence in far peripheries
([[stein-gil|Stein's]] [[distance-parity|distance-parity model]], from
[[hacinebi|Hacınebi]]; Frangipane's Arslan Tepe local-complexity case) is unresolved — see
[[uruk-world-system-debate]]. [[matthews-roger|Matthews]] (2003), reviewing the dispute,
**sides with Algaze** on aggregate asymmetry while judging both models over-reliant on
untested analogy, and flags Nissen's suggestion that the expansion partly sought to
"reconstitute older, fifth-millennium [[ubaid-culture|Ubaid]] exchange networks."
Stein reads the network as a **distance gradient**: direct
control in the Susiana core, coercive influence at the large fortified Syrian colonies
close to home ([[habuba-kabira|Habuba Kabira]], Tell Sheikh Hassan), and autonomous
enclaves surviving only at local sufferance at the far edge (Hacınebi ~1,300 km upstream,
Godin V in the Zagros).
