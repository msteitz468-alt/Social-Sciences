## What It Does

Experimental voyaging builds and sails replica traditional double-hulled voyaging canoes, navigated without instruments (using star, wave, and wind cues), to test whether purposive two-way voyaging across specific Pacific routes was — and is — feasible. Kirch characterizes it as "a kind of experimental archaeology" (attributed, Kirch 2017).

## Procedure

A replica vessel, built to traditional design specifications, is crewed by navigators trained in non-instrument wayfinding and sailed along a targeted historical route, with the outcome (successful arrival, timing, navigational accuracy) taken as evidence bearing on the practical feasibility of the corresponding prehistoric voyage.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Demonstrating that a voyage is *feasible* by modern replica and trained crew does not itself establish that a *specific* prehistoric voyage occurred, on that route, at that time, by that means — feasibility and historical occurrence are distinct claims. Modern replica vessels and revived navigational training may also imperfectly reconstruct ancient practice.

## History

Introduced by [[finney-ben]] and collaborators through the Polynesian Voyaging Society, beginning with the *Hōkūle'a*'s first successful round-trip Hawai'i–Tahiti voyage in 1976. Subsequent voyages tested other routes: Samoa–central Polynesia, Rarotonga–New Zealand, Hawai'i–central Polynesia, and a 1999 Rapa Nui voyage, with a three-year, 47,000-nautical-mile round-the-world circumnavigation in progress as of Kirch's 2017 revised edition. Finney (1996b, quoted via Kirch) describes the project's dual significance: it recentered voyaging within Polynesian prehistory scholarship and catalyzed an indigenous "voyaging renaissance" — a modern social and political movement distinct from, though inspired by, the ancient practice it investigates.

The method complements, rather than duplicates, computer-simulation approaches to the same question: Levison, Ward, and Webb's (1973) pioneering probability-matrix drift simulation (101,016 simulated drift voyages, using naval hydrographic wind/current data) found that drift accounts well for Pacific Outlier settlement (east-to-west) but that virtually no drift voyages (0 of 16,000 trials) reach Hawai'i from central East Polynesia — concluding that Hawaiian colonization required purposive, intentional sailing "with a motive such as seeking new lands" (quoted, Levison et al. 1973:53). Later refinements (Montenegro, Callaghan, and Fitzpatrick 2016) used higher-resolution wind, current, and precipitation databases to model shortest-hop colonization trajectories, identifying Samoa as the most probable staging area for East Polynesian colonization and Mangareva (rather than the Marquesas) as the likelier source for Rapa Nui's settlement; Goodwin, Browning, and Anderson (2014) modeled the effect of the Medieval Climate Anomaly (AD 800–1300) on the voyaging window to New Zealand. These computational studies are model-dependent probabilistic plausibility arguments, not direct evidence of an actual voyage, and are best treated as convergent but methodologically distinct from replica experimental voyaging.

## Exemplary Applications

The *Hōkūle'a* program's demonstration of two-way Hawai'i–Tahiti sailing directly supported [[irwin-geoffrey]]'s "survival sailing strategy" model of purposive Pacific colonization, and is central to resolving the [[polynesian-voyaging-intentionality-debate|drift-versus-deliberate voyaging debate]] in favor of purposive navigation.
