# Residue Analysis

Laboratory and microscopic identification of chemical and particulate residues adhering to or absorbed into artifacts, vessel fabrics, tools, and soils. Textbook synthesis follows [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Renfrew & Bahn 2012]] (especially Ch. 7 subsistence and Ch. 8 technology). Complements [[use-wear-analysis]], [[paleoethnobotany]], and [[faunal-analysis]].

## What It Does

Residue analysis recovers evidence of what artifacts contacted or contained—foods, adhesives, dyes, blood, and other materials—supporting inferences about tool use, cooking, diet, hafting, and craft. Renfrew & Bahn (2012) position a single clay vessel as capable of multiple inquiries including shape and residue analysis for use and diet. Attention in pottery studies has shifted from pure typology/chronology toward raw-material sources, **residues as diet evidence**, manufacture methods, and vessel use (typology remains important for survey assessment).

It is no longer necessary for actual residues to be visible in a vessel: oils and resins can percolate into the clay fabric and still be characterized. Direct proof of human exploitation of animal resources is also available from tools, vessels, and residues (fats, blood claims, etc.).

**Epistemic rank for consumption:** the only incontrovertible proof that a plant or animal species was actually consumed is presence in stomach contents or desiccated fecal matter; vessel residues and cut/burned bones support consumption by context and condition but are one step more inferential (Renfrew & Bahn 2012). Meals (particular-time evidence) vs diet (long-term pattern) remain distinct; long-term diet leans on bone isotopes and systematic plant/animal remains as well as residues.

## Procedure

### Field and lab precautions

- **Do not over-clean** before specialists assess research questions—food residues in pots and possible blood on stone tools can be destroyed (Renfrew & Bahn field-processing rule). Most artifacts are eventually cleaned enough for sorting after residue decisions.
- Plan sampling and bagging to limit modern contamination (handling, plasticizers, soil transfer).

### Major residue classes (as presented in Renfrew & Bahn 2012)

**Microbotanical residues on tools and in pots**

- **Starch grains** on tools: early root and cereal processing (Kilu taro ~28.7 kya—world’s oldest evidence for root-vegetable consumption in the textbook account; Aguadulce manioc ~5000 BC; Ohalo II grindstone ~23 kya; MSA Mozambique grass seeds ≥105 kya).
- **Phytoliths:** plant part, harvesting method, wild/domestic distinctions, taxa that preserve poorly (bananas; rice in late Jomon pottery walls).

**Chemical / molecular vessel and sediment residues**

- **Lipid chemistry, chromatography, and DNA** identify vessel contents (oils, wine, beer, cabbage, possible opium).
- **Isotopic partitioning of plant residues** (N/C ratios) into legumes, non-leguminous terrestrial plants, and marine plants; Hastorf & DeNiro used isotopes plus SEM on burnt Upper Mantaro pot residues (200 BC–AD 1000) to identify boiled/mashed tubers (including potatoes) on plain domestic pots when morphology failed.
- **Animal fats** in sediments and pots: horse, reindeer, fish, seal, milk, etc.
- **Organic residue chemistry** of elite feasting: King Midas’s funerary feast and mixed fermented drinks as textbook examples.
- **Dairy fats** in early Neolithic pots (LBK Europe ~5400 BC; Anatolia 7th millennium; British Neolithic already dairy when farming arrived in the 5th millennium)—residue evidence revising Sherratt’s Secondary Products Revolution timeline toward earlier dairying as intensification of an existing practice.

**Hafting and craft residues**

- **Mastics:** heated bitumen on Middle Paleolithic tools from Umm el-Tlel (Syria); complex birch pitch from Germany (~80,000 ya)—evidence for hafting of stone blades to wooden shafts.
- **Dye chemistry** on textiles (Viking York; Kahun) identifying plant dyes (also linked to textile [[use-wear-analysis]]).

**Blood residues (contested)**

- Loy’s hemoglobin-crystal method claims species identification and long survival on stone tools; Renfrew & Bahn report it as **controversial**.

**Soil and site residues related to husbandry**

- Phosphates, dung spherulites/mites, and stanols (manuring detection; Butser experiment applied to Minoan Pseira)—overlap with geochemical survey ([[geophysical-survey]] complements) and [[experimental-archaeology]].

**Individual meal residues**

- Stomach contents (bog bodies), desiccated feces, latrine/sewer chemistry—high-resolution short-term diet, not lifetime diet.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Contamination and laboratory false positives** — modern handling, burial environment, and method-specific artifacts.
- **Survival bias** — lipids, starches, and proteins preserve unevenly by climate, matrix, and vessel porosity; absence of residue ≠ absence of use.
- **Interpretive leap** — residue of milk fats indicates dairy product presence/processing, not automatically herd management intensity or recipe; multi-use vessels mix signals.
- **Controversial blood methods** — species-level blood residue claims are not textbook-endorsed as settled.
- **Not whole diet** — vessel and tool residues sample contact events; integrate with isotopes, macroremains, and fauna.
- **Destructive sampling** — many chemical techniques consume sample fabric.
- **Cleaning conflict** — museological/display cleaning vs residue research design must be resolved before processing.

## History

- Pottery’s long role as chronological/typological workhorse preceded late 20th-c. shift toward manufacture, use, and residues (Renfrew & Bahn synthesis).
- Microbotanical starch/phytolith programs expanded plant-use evidence beyond macrocharred remains.
- Lipid residue chemistry became central to early dairying and feasting debates (Secondary Products revision).
- Middle Paleolithic mastic finds pushed hafting evidence deep into the Paleolithic.
- Blood-residue claims (Loy) sparked high-profile debate over protein survival and method reliability.
- Experimental and ethnoarchaeological crop-processing and manuring studies (Butser; “external evidence” for processing stages) supply middle-range baselines for residue interpretation.

## Exemplary Applications

As reported in Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

- **Kilu Cave starch** — taro; oldest root-vegetable consumption evidence in textbook account.
- **Ohalo II grindstone starch** (~23 kya); **MSA Mozambique** grass-seed starches (≥105 kya).
- **Aguadulce manioc starch** (~5000 BC).
- **Upper Mantaro pot residues (Hastorf & DeNiro)** — isotopic + SEM identification of boiled/mashed tubers including potatoes.
- **Early Neolithic dairy lipids** — LBK, Anatolia, British Neolithic pots.
- **Umm el-Tlel bitumen mastic** and **German birch pitch (~80 kya)** — hafting.
- **King Midas feast chemistry** — mixed fermented drinks / funerary menu reconstruction.
- **Textile dye chemistry** — Viking York; Kahun plant dyes.
- **Butser-calibrated manuring stanols** at Minoan Pseira.
- **Pedagogical multi-inquiry pot** — residues as one of several parallel analyses (dating, clay source, typology, belief iconography, use/diet).

## Debates

- **Reliability of ancient blood residues** (Loy method and successors).
- **Secondary Products Revolution timing** — residue dairying evidence pulls milk use earlier than classic mid/late 4th-millennium models of [[sherratt-andrew|Sherratt]]’s **[[secondary-products-revolution|SPR]]** (milk/wool/traction package as of 1981–97 essays); “revolution” often reframed as intensification or multi-tempo process. Sherratt’s own volume already splits many liquid vessels toward **alcohol** rather than milk (1997 notes; Ch.15–16) — residue work on both dairy and fermented drinks is the empirical arena. See also [[prehistoric-intoxicants]].
- **Residue vs morphology** when identifying processed foods (Mantaro tubers unreadable morphologically).
- **Meal vs diet** over-interpretation of single vessels or fecal samples.
- **Field cleaning policies** — tension between traditional finds processing and residue science (must assess research questions before cleaning).
- **Relation to use-wear** — residues show contacted materials; wear shows motion and worked-material polish categories; best practice combines both with experimental baselines.
