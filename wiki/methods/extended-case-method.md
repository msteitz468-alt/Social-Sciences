# Extended Case Method

## What It Does

The **extended case method** (ECM) builds theory by **extending** situational fieldwork in time and space and — in Michael Burawoy’s sociological formulation — by **reconstructing preexisting theory** in light of anomalies rather than discovering theory from a blank slate.

As reconstructed by Tavory and Timmermans (*[[tavory-timmermans-abductive-analysis-2014|Abductive Analysis]]*, 2014, Ch. 1) — **attributed to their synthesis**, pending primary ingest of Burawoy and Manchester texts:

### Manchester anthropology

Emerged mid-1960s as an alternative to Malinowski/Radcliffe-Brown **structural functionalism** for studying conflicts. Gluckman and van Velsen proposed studying actors through particular **incidents**, then linking incidents as constitutive of extended processes in time and space; document disputes, ruptures, and norm conflicts; attend to how individuals exploit norms. van Velsen: theory should guide but not determine fieldwork. Exemplar they cite: **J. Clyde Mitchell** on witchcraft accusations in a Yao village over eight years — not isolated sorcery but fragile village politics culminating in village split; bitter quarrelling as inherent in village life cycle.

#### Gluckman’s primary statement (*Politics, Law and Ritual*, 1965)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], *[[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965|Politics, Law and Ritual in Tribal Society]]*, Ch. VI “the ‘extended-case’ method.”)* Gluckman names the method against **“apt and isolated illustrations”**: instead of single anecdotes chosen to fit a norm, the analyst follows relations of persons and groups **through a fair period of time**, showing how people **manipulate** the very customs and beliefs that constrain them. He ties the method to cumulative science: later workers build on predecessors; anthropology is a science, not an art (Introduction; Ch. VII). Exemplars he deploys:

- **J. Clyde Mitchell, *The Yao Village*** — successive witchcraft accusations and segment oppositions over years, ending in village fission; quarrelling as inherent in the village developmental cycle, not mere pathology.
- **[[turner-victor|Victor Turner]], *Schism and Continuity in an African Society* (1957)** — Ndembu village politics as **[[social-drama]]**: breach → crisis → redress (judicial *or* ritual) → reintegration or recognition of schism. Gluckman treats Turner as showing that judicial and ritual redress are alternative paths within one process of social adjustment.

Ch. V’s methodological critique of dispute ethnography (shortage of full **trouble-cases**, neglect of legislative/administrative aftermath) is the complementary negative program: without extended cases, legal anthropology remains morphology-plus-illustration.

### Burawoy’s sociology

Burawoy encountered Manchester anthropologists while fieldworking in former Northern Rhodesia. His ECM “applies reflexive science to ethnography in order to extract the general from the unique, to move from the ‘micro’ to the ‘macro,’ and to connect the present to the past in anticipation of the future, all by building on preexisting theory.” Four movements: (1) localized interventions (obs/interviews — interventions because data-gathering intervenes in lives); (2) wider analysis of regimes of power in participants’ space-time; (3) broader structuring external social forces; (4) reconstructions of existing theory.

Signature contrast with GT (as they quote Burawoy): begin with favorite theory seeking **refutations** that deepen it — “Instead of discovering grounded theory we elaborate existing theory.”

### Earlier statement of the shift (*Order and Rebellion in Tribal Africa*, 1963, Autobiographical Introduction)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], Introduction, 1962/63.)* Before the 1965 textbook statement, Gluckman had already described the Manchester circle's move away from "apt illustration" — using ethnographic data merely to illustrate general points — toward analysis of a **"series of cases"**, tracing a succession of events affecting the same group or individuals, in his Foreword to [[turner-victor|Turner]]'s *Schism and Continuity in an African Society* (1957). He credits [[barnes-john|J. A. Barnes]] with independently noting the same generational shift in Barnes's Sydney Inaugural Lecture (1958). He distinguishes **"structural time"** — analysis proceeding as if a full cyclical process were being completed — from actual historical/real time, citing [[mitchell-j-clyde|J. Clyde Mitchell]]'s *The Yao Village* (1956) as exemplary of analysis conducted in structural time.

## Procedure

Summary (Burawoy via T&T):

1. Situational interventions.
2. Map social processes in local space-time.
3. Articulate external social forces (often not fully apparent on site — **theory-dependent**).
4. Reconstruct/extend the starting theory.

T&T note that ECM, like their own program, can aim at **double-fitting** theory and anomalies; they judge Burawoy et al. somewhat narrow in preferring to begin from a single favorite theory rather than multiple perspectives.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Tavory & Timmermans argue (attributed):

- ECM is mostly compatible with theories that tie field observations to **unobservable** control patterns and macrostructures of domination (Gramsci, neo-Marxist state/labor, later globalization theories) — theories answering “What does the world look like?” that supply the denouement of the empirical narrative.
- The third step toward underlying structures modified by broader forces requires macro-structure theory because those forces often “lie outside the realm of investigation” and outside participants’ consciousness — risk of psychoanalytic-style imputation and moving “from observation to predetermined theorization all too quickly.”
- Comparative designs encouraged early **tether** researchers to the initial theoretical frame; midstream reframing becomes nearly impossible to justify.
- Therefore ECM does **not** solve the inductive dilemma of [[grounded-theory]]; it sacrifices qualitative **flexibility**.

**Coverage note:** Gluckman’s *Politics, Law and Ritual* (1965) now primary-ingested for the Manchester coinage and exemplars; *Manufacturing Consent* (1979) primary for Burawoy’s sociological ECM; van Velsen monographs and Mitchell’s *Yao Village* still pending as freestanding sources (cited via Gluckman 1965 and T&T).

## History

Manchester/Rhodes-Livingstone anthropology, mid-century; Gluckman publicizes the method in *Politics, Law and Ritual* (1965) after Mitchell and Turner monographs; major sociological interlocutor Burawoy. Positioned against structural-functionalism’s apt-illustration style and later against GT inductivism. Foil for [[abductive-analysis]].

## Exemplary Applications

Mitchell Yao village (as cited by T&T). **Burawoy, *Manufacturing Consent* (1979) — now primary-ingested** ([[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-study]]; [[burawoy-manufacturing-consent-1979]]): the seedbed of his later codified ECM. Design facts from the primary: Burawoy's Zambian copper studies (1968–72) under **Jaap van Velsen** tie him directly to the Manchester line; the 1974–75 Allied fieldwork begins from Marxist theory (Gramsci's "hegemony is born in the factory") and reconstructs it against shop-floor anomalies (consent where coercion was predicted); he defends single-case generalization by two explicit logics — "the part as an expression of the totality" and "generalization by extension from the part to the whole" (case situated among family, school, state, union, other firms); and he declines a "ritual methodological appendix" while making the Roy restudy comparison auditable by extensive quotation. The Zambian appendix (Gluckman's "an African miner is a miner"; Kapferer's Kabwe factory) applies situational analysis against imported-culture explanations.
**[[turner-victor|Victor Turner]]'s Ndembu work** is a primary anthropological exemplar now in
the wiki: his [[social-drama]] analyses treat particular ritual performances — a 1953 Mukanda
circumcision read as village succession politics; the Ihembi cure read as therapy for a
corporate group's tensions ([[turner-forest-of-symbols-study]]) — as extended cases rather than
normative composites. Turner developed the method within Gluckman's [[manchester-school|Manchester
School]] (*Schism and Continuity*, 1957), the same milieu from which Burawoy later drew it.

## Debates

ECM vs [[grounded-theory]] vs [[abductive-analysis]] as solutions to the theory–data relation; how far unobservables may be imported via theory.
