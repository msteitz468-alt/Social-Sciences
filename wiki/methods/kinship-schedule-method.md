# Kinship Schedule Method

## What It Does

The **kinship schedule method**, as developed by [[morgan-lewis-henry|Lewis Henry Morgan]] for *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems of Consanguinity]]* (1871), is a **standardized questionnaire** of kin-type descriptions (on the order of two hundred items) used to elicit the full **system of relationship** of a language/community — not merely the English eight primary terms. Schedules were filled by Morgan in the field and by missionaries and correspondents worldwide under Smithsonian auspices.

## Procedure

Summary (as described by Morgan and by Tooker’s 1997 introduction):

1. Design a printed schedule of questions describing persons by genealogical position (father’s brother’s son, etc.).
2. Circulate with explanatory letter (to Indian agents, missionaries, foreign correspondents).
3. Elicit native terms for each kin type; note dialect and filler.
4. Tabulate systems side by side; check internal consistency (schedule “self-corrective” for reciprocal terms).
5. Compare systems for shared plan (descriptive vs classificatory subtypes).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Assumes kin types are cross-culturally translatable via the schedule’s genealogical English.
- Correspondent quality uneven; incomplete schedules common.
- Collection embedded in settler-colonial and missionary infrastructures.
- Comparison unit (“nation,” “family” of languages) is Morgan’s analytic construct.

## History

Morgan began after noticing Seneca terminology differed in plan from European systems; printed schedules from late 1850s; Joseph Henry/Smithsonian sponsorship from 1860; global returns through the 1860s; published 1871. Later anthropology retained schedule/genealogical method variants (Rivers’s genealogical method is a related but distinct refinement — not yet ingested).

## Exemplary Applications

*[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-study|Systems]]* tables themselves; Seneca-Iroquois type case.
