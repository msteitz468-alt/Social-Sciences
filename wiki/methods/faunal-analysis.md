# Faunal Analysis (Zooarchaeology)

Analysis of animal remains from archaeological (and ethnoarchaeological) contexts to infer subsistence, butchery, transport, and site function — tightly linked to [[taphonomy]] and [[utility-index|utility indices]] in [[binford-lewis|Binford]]'s program.

## What It Does

Identifies taxa and skeletal parts; measures frequencies and modifications; models what hunters keep, transport, or abandon; uses bone distributions as **site-structure** evidence (drop/toss zones, door dumps, processing hearths).

## Procedure

Element identification; quantification (NISP, MNI, etc.); modification recording; comparison to utility models and to natural bone accumulations. In *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* (1983), Binford stresses that early-man textbooks treating bones+tools as self-evident meals ([[hunting-hypothesis]]) skip [[taphonomy|taphonomic]] and formation controls (Dart vs Brain cases; waterhole death assemblages).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Requires control of taphonomic pathways; ethnoarchaeological indices transfer imperfectly; post-discard clean-up and dog feeding reshape patterns.

## History

Long comparative-anatomy tradition; New Archaeology and [[ethnoarchaeology]] made faunal patterning central to testing hunting vs scavenging and site-function hypotheses. The full utility-index apparatus is developed in *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* (1978), where Binford also argued conventional **MNI** calculation (side/age-based counting) inflates counts and proposed a fractional-MNI standardization; *In Pursuit of the Past* (1983) popularizes the critique and the Nunamiut bone-layout models.

## Exemplary Applications

Early-hominin bone assemblages (Dart / Leakey / Brain cases in *Pursuit*); Nunamiut caribou processing, marrow hearths, exterior bone dumps; site-structure diagnosis (indoor vs outdoor activity).

## Debates

Hunting vs scavenging; cultural vs functional assemblage variability; schlepp effect and transport models; relation of bone patterning to lithic facies debates ([[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]).

## From Renfrew & Bahn (2012)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]* 6th ed., Ch. 6–7.)* Renfrew & Bahn treat faunal analysis as the core of **[[zooarchaeology]]** — the study of animal remains for subsistence, domestication, butchery, seasonality, and (secondarily) environment. Post-WWII zooarchaeology asks first whether bones result from human agency rather than natural death, carnivores, owls, or burrowers, and whether use was dietary or for skins/tools. Microfauna are preferred over large mammals as climate indicators; macrofaunal presence proves tolerance ranges more than precise climate. Identification is against reference collections (collagen protein analysis can separate even sheep vs goat fragments). Quantified assemblage work (NISP, MNI, age/sex profiles, butchery marks) and ethnoarchaeological criteria (e.g. Binford’s articular-end vs cylinder bone-breakage patterns) bridge residues to behavior; single proxies remain partial, so multi-proxy sampling is required (attributed). ## Insoll 1999 — Muslim diet and faunal recognition

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]*, Ch. 4.)* Islamic archaeology under-uses fauna relative to prestige pottery. Insoll poses four recognition questions: (1) absence of prohibited species (pigs, dogs); (2) whether a halal diet is visible via special butchery; (3) whether slaughter patterns differ in Muslim-owned herds; (4) whether “Muslim herd” composition differs. Assemblages are death assemblages, not living herds; Jordanian Christian→Muslim transitions show limited change in major food animals; camel may signal dietary shift in some contexts. Exceptions (alcohol, residual pig) are expected. Links [[zooarchaeology]], [[islamic-archaeology]].
