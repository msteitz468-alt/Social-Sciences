## What It Does

A breaching experiment (or "demonstration," in Garfinkel's own preferred term) deliberately disrupts a taken-for-granted feature of an ordinary social scene in order to make visible the [[background-expectancies]] that ordinarily govern it. As Garfinkel put the procedural strategy, "it is my preference to start with familiar scenes and ask what can be done to make trouble" — multiplying senseless features of a situation to produce bewilderment, anxiety, shame, or indignation, and thereby reveal how everyday order is ordinarily produced and maintained.

## Procedure

Garfinkel's *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (1967) reports numerous such demonstrations, each run on small convenience samples of undergraduate students and their acquaintances or family. Three canonical examples:

- **Boarders in their own homes.** Thirty-three students spent 15 minutes to an hour viewing their own household's activities while silently assuming, without acting on it, that they were boarders; a further 49 students *acted out* the boarder role (circumspect, formal, speaking only when spoken to). In roughly four-fifths of the acting-out cases, family members were "stupefied" and worked actively to restore normal appearances, producing astonishment, anger, and demands for explanation.
- **"Please clarify what you mean" conversations.** Twenty-three students engaged an acquaintance in ordinary talk and, without signaling anything unusual, insisted the other party clarify the sense of commonplace remarks. Reactions across the reported cases ranged from stunned silence to open hostility ("Drop dead!").
- **Ticktacktoe with moved marks.** In 247 trials, an experimenter erased a subject's first move, relocated it, and made his own mark without acknowledging anything unusual; in roughly half the trials subjects read the altered mark as a meaningful hidden gesture (e.g., a slur or a sexual pass) rather than as mere game deviation.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Garfinkel explicitly disclaimed that these studies were experiments in the controlled sense: "my studies are not properly speaking experimental. They are demonstrations, designed, in Herbert Spiegelberg's phrase, as 'aids to a sluggish imagination.'" There were no control groups, samples were small and drawn from convenience populations (mostly students and their families), and outcomes were reported qualitatively rather than measured against a baseline. The method also raises acknowledged ethical concerns — subjects' family members were deceived and distressed without informed consent, prompting reactions such as one participant's plea, "Please, no more of these experiments. We're not rats, you know."

## History

Garfinkel developed the demonstrations through the 1950s–60s in connection with his broader ethnomethodological program and the Langley Porter/UCLA research milieu; the theoretical rationale for the "distrust" variant was worked out separately in his 1963 paper "A Conception of and Experiments with 'Trust' as a Condition of Stable Concerted Actions." The demonstrations were collected and given their fullest statement in Chapter 2 of *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (1967).

## Exemplary Applications

- [[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967]] — the demonstrations of Chapter 2 (boarder-in-own-home, clarification conversations, ticktacktoe, bargaining, the medical-school "fake applicant" interview) are the method's canonical exemplars.
- [[background-expectancies]] — the concept the demonstrations are designed to expose.

## Debates

Whether breaching demonstrations *establish* anything is itself contested. Because they lack
controls and rest on small convenience samples, critics treat them as evocative illustrations
rather than evidence — a charge Garfinkel partly pre-empted by refusing the label
"experiment." The deception of unwitting subjects also makes the method an early touchstone in
research-ethics discussion. These objections are part of the wider dispute over whether
ethnomethodology supplements or subverts conventional method — see
[[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate]].
