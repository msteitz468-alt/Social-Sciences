## What It Does

Box-grid excavation — the **Wheeler grid** — excavates an area as a checkerboard of square
units separated by standing walls of unexcavated soil called **baulks**, which preserve
continuous vertical **sections** on all four sides of each square until the final phase of
work. It is [[wheeler-mortimer|Mortimer Wheeler]]'s signature operationalization of
**[[stratigraphic-excavation]]** for open areas, published in
*[[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Archaeology from the Earth]]* (1954).

## Procedure

Wheeler derived the layout from six stated pre-requisites an area excavation must satisfy:
subdivisibility for record and control; easy progressive expansion without disturbing
datum-lines; preservation of complete vertical sections at maximum points until the final
phase; eventual integration into one continuous exposure; accessibility for spoil removal;
and sufficient light and openness to inspect the sections. He held that only one layout —
a grid of squares with a baulk left standing between each adjacent pair — meets all six
(attributed, Wheeler 1954). Practical parameters: the horizontal dimension of a square
should roughly equal its anticipated maximum depth (a 20-ft-deep site wants ~20-ft squares),
with a practical minimum of about 10 ft; baulks are standardly 3 ft wide (2 ft on 10-ft
squares), so a 20-ft pegged square yields a ~17-ft cutting. Squares are labelled by letters
on one axis and numbers on the other (A1, A2, B1…), each corner peg bearing the designations
of all four squares it adjoins. A small **control-pit** ("a principle of universal
application") is dug ahead of the main gang, 1–2 ft below the working level, so the
supervisor can anticipate underlying layers — expressing Wheeler's rule of "vertical digging
first, horizontal digging afterwards."

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method's chief merit, in Wheeler's terms, is that it "localizes both control and record"
— each supervisor owns a bounded stratigraphic and recording responsibility. Its chief cost,
pressed by later critics, is that the **baulks fragment the horizontal plan**: features,
floors, and structures are interrupted at every square edge and can be hard to trace across
the grid, and the standing sections are only removed late, so the full plan of a phase is
never seen whole until the end. From the 1960s **open-area (single-context) excavation** —
removing baulks and recording each stratigraphic context across the whole exposure —
displaced the grid as the default for many site types, arguing that continuous horizontal
relationships matter as much as the vertical section. The grid remains appropriate where
deep stratigraphy and tight control are paramount; it is one option among several, not the
universal method Wheeler presented.

## History

Wheeler developed and refined the system across his interwar excavations (he contrasts the
1935 trenching and 1936 square-method work at Maiden Castle) and codified it in 1954. It
built directly on [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]]'s principle of total
three-dimensional recording and on the geological **[[law-of-superposition]]**. It became
the dominant British and Commonwealth field method into the 1960s and was carried abroad
through Wheeler's Indian directorship and his many trained students.

## Exemplary Applications

Verulamium and Maiden Castle (England); Arikamedu, Brahmagiri, Harappa, and Taxila (South
Asia), where Wheeler used the grid to build regional culture-sequences.

## Debates

Grid-with-baulks versus open-area excavation is a standing methodological dispute about
whether the preserved section or the continuous plan is the primary stratigraphic document;
see [[stratigraphic-excavation]].

## From Renfrew & Bahn (2012)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]* 6th ed., Ch. 3.)* Renfrew & Bahn describe the Wheeler box-grid as descending from the Pitt-Rivers tradition: intact **balks** between grid squares preserve vertical profiles so layers can be correlated across the site; once extent and layout are known, balks may be removed to open features of special interest (e.g. mosaic floors). They note the system is **still widely used in parts of South Asia** (where Wheeler introduced it in the 1940s) because many untrained workers in boxes are easily supervised by few staff. Against this they set the open-area critique (balks wrongly placed; blocked horizontal patterning) and observe that rigid box-grids rarely suit very deep tells — alternatives include step-trenching and cofferdams (attributed). See also [[open-area-excavation]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]].
