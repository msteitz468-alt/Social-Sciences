## What It Does

Stratigraphic excavation recovers the human past by identifying, recording, and correlating
the **successive layers** (strata) of construction, occupation, destruction, and deposition
that accumulate on a site, so that the relative time-order of deposits — and of the finds
and structures within them — can be read from the earth itself. It applies the geological
**[[law-of-superposition]]** to human deposits: lower layers were, as a rule, laid down
before upper ones. [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]] framed its primary product as securing "the
orderly succession of the vestiges," and its evidentiary surface as the **vertical section**
— "the digger must learn to read his sections, or he should be constrained from digging"
(attributed, Wheeler 1954).

## Procedure

Strata are exposed and read in a cut vertical face, each layer labelled *in situ* (Wheeler's
practice: a numbered peg driven into the section as digging proceeds), numbered top-down as
finds emerge but assigned to cultural phases bottom-up (earliest = Culture I). Every
significant find is fixed by **three-dimensional recording** — longitudinal and lateral
measurement from a pegged datum plus a vertical measurement — and cross-referenced to its
layer. Structures are dated by three classes of stratigraphic evidence: material predating
construction and material postdating it *bracket* the structure, while material contemporary
with construction *defines the point within* (attributed, Wheeler 1954). Walls are
cross-sectioned at intervals rather than cleared continuously, since continuous clearance
destroys the wall's relationship to adjacent layers. For area work the method is
operationalized through **[[box-grid-excavation]]** (or, later, open-area excavation).

## Assumptions and Limitations

The governing caution is Wheeler's: "the first rule about stratification is that there is no
invariable rule." Not all visible layering is chronological (a day's spoil-dump may be
finely layered yet time-meaningless); occupation gaps must be detected from soil character
at a junction (wind-blown sand, turf, water-lain deposit) or from the statistical
("actuarial") overlap of finds across a boundary; objects migrate between layers
(intrusion, residuality), so a single find does not date a layer. **No sequence should be
established on one section** — extending the cut can reveal an apparent succession as
simultaneous deposits. Wheeler condemned the older **bench-level / datum-line** method
(treating all finds at one absolute elevation as contemporary), used at Mohenjo-daro
(1927–31), as "the very parody of scientific method," since an occupation mound is never
level. Above all, excavation is **destructive and unrepeatable** — "at the best, excavation
is destruction" — so the method's results are only as good as the recording, and
"unrecorded excavation is the unforgivable destruction of evidence" (attributed, Wheeler
1954). Stratigraphy establishes *relative* order only; absolute dates require
[[radiocarbon-dating]], dendrochronology, or datable imports.

## History

Wheeler traces stratigraphic observation in archaeology to Thomas Jefferson's 1784
excavation of a Virginia burial mound and to Captain Meadows Taylor's section-recording of
Indian megalithic tombs from 1851, but locates "the first substantive advance in the
technique of excavation and recording" in [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]]'s Cranborne
Chase work of the 1880s–90s, with its trained staff and total three-dimensional recording
(attributed, Wheeler 1954). Heinrich Schliemann's 1870s digs at Troy and Mycenae are
Wheeler's cautionary counter-case — a "splendid book" of stratified evidence torn apart in
the finding. The method was codified for a generation in Wheeler's
*[[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Archaeology from the Earth]]* (1954). Kathleen
Kenyon, one of Wheeler's collaborators, later carried section-based stratigraphy to Jericho
and Jerusalem.

**Critical history ([[lucas-gavin|Lucas]] 2002).** [[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]] adds a
non-progressive account (attributed). In North America a distinct **"stratigraphic
revolution"** (Willey & Sabloff's term) ran through Manuel Gamio (Valley of Mexico,
1911–12, under Boas), Nels Nelson (1914–16) and A. V. Kidder (Pecos, from 1915) — but often
meant *metrical* excavation by arbitrary spits with awareness of stratification (Nelson)
rather than digging by natural strata (Kidder), the "arbitrary versus stratigraphic" or
"metrical versus natural" distinction. Lucas separates **"stratigraphic excavation"** proper
from mere **"excavation which is aware of stratigraphy"** (e.g. Putnam's 1880s slice method)
— a distinction he says underlies much confused priority-claiming. He also historicizes the
sectional bias: Wheeler and Kenyon modelled the stratigraphic unit on the *layer* (Kenyon's
1950s correlation-table "matrix" was "almost a visual analogue of the section itself"), and,
following Edward C. Harris (1989), Lucas argues they did **not** treat the interface/cut as a
numbered unit in its own right — the recognition of the negative unit was Harris's own
contribution (see [[harris-matrix]], [[single-context-recording]]).

## Exemplary Applications

Wheeler's own deep marginal section (50 ft, unstepped) through the highest tell at Harappa;
his staged Verulamium campaign; the Arikamedu/Brahmagiri South Indian chronology. The
robbed-out "ghost walls" of the Verulamium north-west gateway were recovered stratigraphically
by Kathleen Kenyon (1931) while preserving stratified floors intact (finding, Wheeler 1954).

## Archaeological vs. geological stratigraphy (Clark 1957)

[[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] (*[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and
Society]]*, 1957) stresses that archaeological stratigraphy departs increasingly from the
geological model as a society's economic sophistication rises: people raze, redig, and raise
floors, so "the more advanced the technology, the more people interfere with prior strata,"
and mechanical "levelling" without understanding how a deposit formed can yield sequences
"the very opposite of the historical truth" (attributed). He also cautions that a sound
stratigraphic sequence proves succession *only at that site*: cultures are like "irregular
lenses with frilled and undulating edges" that overlap neighbours differently at different
points, so stratigraphy must be read jointly with geographic distribution before broad
conclusions are drawn.

## The Mesopotamian Tradition and Its Adaptation

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]].)* Matthews locates
the first true stratigraphic sounding in Mesopotamian archaeology in Walter Andrae's
excavation of the Ištar Temple at Aššur (1902–1914), conducted as Robert Koldewey's
assistant; Koldewey himself, excavating Babylon for the German Oriental Society from 1899,
had developed a **mud-brick tracing technique** (from 1887 fieldwork at Surghul/al-Hiba) to
distinguish successive mud-brick walls and floors — a soil-reading skill distinct from the
British sectional tradition. Touring Near Eastern excavations in 1936 ahead of directing the
new Institute of Archaeology in London, [[wheeler-mortimer|Mortimer Wheeler]] found regional
stratigraphic recording standards far below British norms of the time: "the scientific
analysis of stratification, upon which modern excavation is largely based, was almost
non-existent" (Wheeler 1956: 110). Matthews argues that **single-context planning**
(developed in Western European urban archaeology, recording a discrete context in its
entirety and reconstructing deposit relationships via a Harris matrix) requires modification
for Southwest Asian mud-brick tell sites, since it does not systematically retain sections
and risks losing finely layered floor/occupation sequences distinctive of tells. **Soil
micromorphology** — removal of intact deposit blocks (e.g. floor-plaster sequences),
resin-impregnated and thin-sectioned for microscopic analysis — was pioneered as a
systematic settlement-wide technique at Çatalhöyük (W. Matthews et al. 1997), revealing
traces of food preparation, trampling, and burning otherwise archaeologically invisible, and
was also applied at Abu Salabikh; excavation sampling more broadly there includes flotation
for carbonised plant remains and sieving for small artefacts and faunal remains.

## Debates

Whether the standing baulks of Wheeler's grid system serve or fragment stratigraphic reading
is contested by advocates of open-area excavation (see [[box-grid-excavation]],
[[open-area-excavation]]); whether the primary stratigraphic document is the preserved
section or the analytically-built [[harris-matrix|matrix]], and whether the model of
stratigraphy should be geological or archaeological, is recorded on
[[stratigraphic-recording-debate]]. The wider question of whether excavation technique is the
whole of archaeology or the servant of a humane interpretive aim is recorded on
[[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]].

## From Renfrew & Bahn (2012)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]* 6th ed., esp. Ch. 3–4.)* Renfrew & Bahn present excavation techniques as combining **vertical** emphasis (deep cuts for stratification) and **horizontal** emphasis (large exposures for spatial relations) after survey and grid layout from a datum for 3D mapping. For relative dating they restate the [[law-of-superposition]]: the underlying layer was deposited first; good stratigraphic excavation must detect later pits, burrows, and other disturbances, since only carefully observed stratigraphy yields a reliable relative sequence. Interconnecting stratigraphic sequences with absolute methods “provides the most reliable basis for dating archaeological sites and their contents.” Wheeler’s Indus Valley section (pit disturbance vs a sealed Harappan seal in layer 8) is their teaching example of association and residuality.

Operationally they contrast the **[[box-grid-excavation|Wheeler box-grid]]** (intact balks preserve vertical profiles; balks may later be removed for special features; still used in parts of South Asia for workforce supervision) with **[[open-area-excavation]]** (Philip Barker’s critique: balks are often wrongly placed and block large-area patterning; open large areas and cut “running sections” only where needed; now the norm in much British archaeology, especially shallow single-period sites and salvage). Rigid box-grids rarely suit very deep tells; alternatives include step-trenching (e.g. Koster, Illinois) and cofferdams. Methods should follow natural geological or cultural layers rather than arbitrary spits; dig quality equals recovery/recording quality because excavation destroys much evidence and is unrepeatable. Hybrid practice (e.g. Jamestown Rediscovery: initial grid through post-fort plow layers, then feature-based open-area at the 17th-c. level) is presented as standard professional compromise (attributed).
