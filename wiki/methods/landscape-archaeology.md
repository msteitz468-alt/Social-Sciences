# Landscape Archaeology

## What It Does

**Landscape archaeology** studies past human life at the scale of regions, viewsheds, land use, and relations among places — integrating survey, environmental data, monument distributions, and (in interpretive variants) embodied experience. It bridges site excavation and grand culture-historical maps.

## Procedure

Typical toolkit: regional survey ([[archaeological-survey]], [[aerial-archaeology]], [[geophysical-survey]]); GIS and viewshed analysis; environmental proxies; monument biography and sequence; in interpretive work, walking and phenomenological recording ([[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]]). [[bradley-richard|Bradley]]’s *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998) exemplifies a **monument-biography / long-term landscape** synthesis without being a methods manual: sequences of construction, reuse, and agricultural overprinting across Europe and especially Britain/Ireland.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes meaningful spatial structure beyond site boundaries; modern terrain and vegetation may mislead; "landscape" can smuggle aesthetic or nationalist frames. Experiential (phenomenological) sub-methods face the critique that modern bodies ≠ prehistoric subjects.

## History

Roots in British field archaeology, US settlement-pattern work ([[settlement-pattern-archaeology]]), and historical geography; Critical Turn expansions via meaning, memory, and power ([[post-processual-archaeology]]). Related vault pages: [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]], [[place-as-lived]], [[ritual-landscape]].

## Exemplary Applications

- Bradley 1998: monument-biography / long-term landscape synthesis (Orkney; Cranborne Chase; house life-cycles — [[death-of-the-house]]); adjacent to [[phenomenological-landscape-archaeology]] without full Tilley program.
- Tilley 1994 (Welsh/English monument phenomenology); Cranborne Chase (Barrett, Bradley, Green); settlement-pattern classics (Basin of Mexico, etc.).
- **US Southwest CRM landscape program:** [[whittlesey-stephanie-m|Whittlesey]] in *[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River]]* (1997) organizes the [[lower-verde-archaeological-project-study|Lower Verde Archaeological Project]] around landscape theory drawing on cultural geography (J.B. Jackson), landscape ecology, interpretive/post-processual space-as-medium (Tilley contrast with processual container-space), and Zedeno et al. (1997) **formal / historical / relational** dimensions — dwelling, food-production, procurement, ritual, and communication spaces with sedimented life histories. Ch. 19 reconstructs three regimes along the Verde: prehistoric riverine farming landscapes; [[western-apache|Western Apache]] mountain and [[yavapai|Yavapai]] high-desert landscapes; Euroamerican hydraulic/ranching landscapes after beaver loss, grazing, and dams (Ch. 3).

## Debates

Processual vs interpretive landscape; phenomenology vs GIS formalism; ritual vs economic landscapes (Bradley’s continuity thesis).

## A comparative middle-range alternative (Moore 2005, attributed)

[[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Jerry Moore's *Cultural Landscapes in the Ancient Andes*]] (2005) proposes a **comparative, cross-culturally documented alternative** to intuitive phenomenology of the [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]] type. Rather than reading past experience directly off a modern body's walk through a site, Moore builds middle-range experiential variables from ethnographically and physically documented human universals — [[soundscape|speech-intelligibility distances]] (acoustic thresholds for whisper vs. normal-voice communication, calibrated against decibel/distance physics and ethnographic camp-spacing data), categories of visual accessibility (public-near to public-distant visibility), and the design logics of [[procession|processional]] movement (ceremonial calendar anchoring, place-linkage, centrality of sound, material props, multiple participant roles) — and applies these as constrained analogy to prehistoric built environments (Formative-period Peruvian ceremonial architecture, Chan Chan's ciudadelas). Moore states his explicit methodological commitment is to be "transparent in its assumptions, clear in its hypotheses, and explicit in analysis" in direct response to the verification problem raised against phenomenological archaeology (see [[phenomenological-archaeology]]). This positions the method as falsifiable and cross-culturally grounded, rather than resting on a single modern observer's embodied impressions.

## Memory and Ideology in Landscape (Historical Archaeology)

Holtorf & Williams and De Cunzo & Ernstein, in adjacent chapters of the *Cambridge Companion*, extend landscape archaeology's remit into social memory and ideology ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], chs.13–14). Holtorf & Williams distinguish **accumulative landscapes** (traces of past action forming a focus of retrospective memory) from **created landscapes** (built to hold prospective memory, i.e. what their makers intend to be remembered), and describe landscape more broadly as a **palimpsest** (attributed to Muir 2000) — a sequence of traces overwritten across decades to millennia. W.G. Hoskins's characterization of landscape as "the richest historical record that we possess" is quoted and then critiqued by Holtorf & Williams as attending mainly to the landscapes of the living and rarely to the dead or the past's own experience of history.

Case studies of landscapes of memory include: the **District Six** area of Cape Town, forcibly cleared under apartheid from 1966 and later reclaimed through the District Six Museum from 1997 (attributed to M. Hall 2000); British commemoration of the 1857 Delhi revolt, which suppressed Indian-uprising sites before independence and later reversed to foreground them (attributed to Lahiri 2003); and the **Ulanendenkmal** war memorial in Demmin, Germany, whose meaning shifted from Weimar-era nationalist symbol, to Third Reich appropriation, to postwar embarrassment and dilapidation, to post-1989 restoration — read as a case of prospective memory being overwritten by later retrospective reinterpretation. Michael Dietler's study of how French national monuments (Alésia, Gergovia, Bibracte) recast Vercingetorix's resistance to Julius Caesar, particularly under excavations commissioned by Napoleon III, is cited as a further case of landscape mobilized for national commemoration.

Raphael Samuel's concept of **"theatres of memory"** is applied by the chapter to explicitly commercial memory landscapes — Caesars Palace and the Luxor on the Las Vegas Strip — and, via Holtorf, to zoos, which commemorate individuals, collective pasts, and extinct species; the chapter notes that Carl Hagenbeck's *Völkerschauen* (exhibiting "exotic" people alongside animals) left a legacy still visible in some contemporary zoo displays.

De Cunzo & Ernstein trace a distinct **ideology strand** running from Deetz's "Georgian Order" thesis through the Archaeology in Annapolis programme: Robert Paynter's study of spatial inequality in early-19th-century western Massachusetts; Mark Leone's reading of the William Paca garden as deploying a "naturalising ideology" that masked unequal social relations as timeless and natural; James Delle's analysis of 19th-century Jamaican coffee plantations as sites of "spatial discipline" restricting enslaved people's movement and surveillance; and Terrence Epperson's application of Foucauldian panopticism to Gunston Hall and Monticello. They also trace a parallel **experiential/phenomenological strand** — Tim Ingold's concept of the [[taskscape]] as landscape enacted through rhythmic, temporally embedded activity; Lu Ann De Cunzo's study of the Magdalen Society Asylum in Philadelphia, where spatial hierarchy and daily routine enacted a moral-reform ideology that inmates also contested; and Eleanor Casella's study of Tasmania's female factories, where convict women's movement was "meticulously choreographed" yet also subverted via an alternative, semi-subterranean geography. De Cunzo & Ernstein conclude by arguing for "genuine pluralism" among these approaches over calls for theoretical or methodological unity in landscape archaeology.
