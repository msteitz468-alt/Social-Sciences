## What It Does

The Harris matrix diagrams a site's **stratigraphic sequence** as a network of relative
temporal relations among stratigraphic units. Its basis (Harris 1989): any unit can have
only three possible *direct* relationships to another — **none, earlier/later than, or
equivalent to**. The diagram fixes the ordinal sequence of contexts independently of any
standing section, which is what makes total plan-based [[single-context-recording|single-context
recording]] and baulk-free open-area excavation workable. The account here follows
[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]]'s critical treatment (*Critical Approaches to Fieldwork*, 2002),
which distinguishes matrix inventor **Edward C. Harris** from the unrelated anthropologist
Marvin Harris.

## Procedure

Harris defines a stratigraphic unit as a **topological entity** — specified by its spatial
extension and by the quality of its interface/edge/surface, independent of whether the
producing event was natural or cultural and independent of the deposit's material
constituents. This lets the system recognize **negative units** ("feature interfaces" —
cuts) alongside "layer interfaces" (deposits, which have both surface and depth) — units
that content-focused geological schemes tend to miss. *Indirect* relationships (whether two
units are contemporary) cannot be settled stratigraphically; they require grouping the
matrix into broad, approximately contemporary **phases**, ideally at major horizon breaks
and with independent dating (pottery, coins). Harris himself described the method as a form
of **critical path analysis**, borrowed from quantitative geography (Cole & King 1968) —
part of the 1960s statistical/quantitative "revolution" in the social sciences.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Lucas argues (attributed) the matrix's deepest limitation is that its units have **no
temporality of their own** — only a place in a sequence — because it treats them as
spatially-bounded objects rather than durational events; a ditch cut's years-long
silting-up "use phase," for instance, is not represented. Harris (1991) proposed the matrix
"can be lengthened, shortened or otherwise reordered to give some indication of duration"
(similarly Dalland 1984, box-depth); Lucas builds on this with a supplementary
duration/"longevity" chart (illustrated on a Çatalhöyük building sequence) meant to
*supplement*, not replace, the matrix — converging independently with Carver's (1990)
alternative. A further flaw (per Barham 1995) is that the system does not distinguish, at
the moment of recording, properties **inherited** from earlier deposits, properties acquired
**at deposition**, and properties acquired **post-depositionally** (e.g. bioturbation),
leading to misidentified or "invisible" boundaries.

## History

Edward C. Harris formulated the matrix in 1973 while doing post-excavation work for the
Winchester Research Unit, to accommodate the **multi-linear** site development that Biddle's
site-wide "phase" digging could not (see [[single-context-recording]]); he set it out fully
in *Principles of Archaeological Stratigraphy* (1979). Adopted by the London Department of
Urban Archaeology from the mid-1970s and, by the 1980s, taken up in North American practice
alongside the shift to excavating by natural stratigraphy. Lucas notes the ironic echo of
[[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]'s follower **Kathleen Kenyon**, whose 1950s **correlation table**
(squares in columns, layers in rows) was also called a "matrix" — but in the mathematical
sense, "almost a visual analogue of the section itself," modelling a simpler unilinear
layering, quite unlike Harris's topological system.

## Exemplary Applications

Deeply stratified urban excavation under [[single-context-recording|single-context
recording]] (Winchester; the City of London). Applied and critiqued at
[[hodder-ian|Hodder]]'s Çatalhöyük, where Lucas develops his duration-chart supplement.

## Debates

Whether the matrix's reduction of a site to a diagram of relative sequence adequately
represents archaeological *time*, or flattens the material and temporal character of
deposits ([[lucas-gavin|Lucas 2002]]); and the **geological vs. archaeological** model of
stratigraphy — the geoarchaeological/soil-science school (Stein: "culture is only one of
the many… agents of transport and deposition") against Harris's topological "form" school.
See [[archaeological-record]], [[stratigraphic-recording-debate]], [[stratigraphic-excavation]].
