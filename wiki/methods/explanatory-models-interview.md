# The Mini-Ethnography and Explanatory-Model Interview

A clinical method proposed by [[kleinman-arthur|Arthur Kleinman]] for the care of the chronically
ill, set out in "A Method for the Care of the Chronically Ill" (chapter 15 of
*[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]*, 1988). It imports the
[[participant-observation|ethnographer's]] craft into the clinic to recover the
[[illness-disease-distinction|illness experience]] that biomedical practice discards.

## What It Does

Kleinman's stated essence: "empathic listening, translation, and interpretation… the craft of
the clinician who treats illness, not just disease." The method is meant "to complement and
balance, not replace, the standard biomedical approach," and is explicitly *not* a panacea. Its
goal reframes chronic care: since chronic disease "by definition cannot be cured" and "the quest
for cure is a dangerous myth," the aim is "reduction of disablement in the ongoing experience of
an illness." Kleinman insists "uncertainty must be as central to the experience of the practitioner
as it is to the patient," warning against "complete psychosocial systems of care" as "a dangerous
hubris."

## Procedure

**The mini-ethnography** (clinician as qualified analog to the ethnographer — "I would not defend
this likeness as complete"):

1. **Empathic witnessing.** Place oneself "in the lived experience of the patient's illness,"
   including from family members' standpoints — "the great leap into the world of the patient"
   from which the other steps flow.
2. **Reconstruct and interpret the [[illness-narrative]]** across its four meanings — symptom
   symbols, culturally marked disorder, personal/interpersonal significance, and
   [[explanatory-models|explanatory models]] — attending to the account's plot and rhetoric.
3. **Record the illness's social impact** (family, work, finances), probing "menacing life changes
   or breakdowns in the social support network." Adjunct scales are permitted but subordinate:
   "our model is ethnography, not survey research."
4. **Compile an illness problem list** parallel to the biomedical problem list (marital, work,
   financial, school, daily-living, and psychological problems).
5. **Record interventions and their effects** alongside it, tracked longitudinally like drug
   effects.

Adjuncts: a **brief life history** ("as broad and unrestricted by apparent relevance as the
clinician can make it" — it both prevents reductive care and "remoralizes the physician"), and
routine **screening for anxiety and depression**.

**The explanatory-model elicitation and negotiation sub-method:**

1. **Elicitation.** Contracted form (verbatim): **"What do you think is wrong? What caused it?
   What do you want me to do?"** Expanded form adds onset timing, lay causal understanding,
   expected course and seriousness, plus: **"What is the chief way this illness (or treatment) has
   affected your life? What do you fear most about this illness (or treatment)?"** Because EMs are
   "inchoate," re-elicit at different points.
2. **Presentation** of the practitioner's biomedical model "as an act of translation," using the
   patient's own metaphors ("No doctor is taught how to explain the biomedical account to
   patients").
3. **Negotiation** — "an explicit comparison between the lay model and the professional biomedical
   model," with the clinician disclosing uncertainty and biases and prepared to change; outcomes
   are a compromise toward either party or "a joint lesson in demystifying professional and public
   discourse." If none is possible, refer on.
4. **Clinician self-reflection** — using the EM framework to examine "the interests, biases, and
   emotions that underlie his own model" (with Balint groups and ethics rounds as institutional
   analogs).

A manualized **grief-work protocol** (5–6 weekly 30–40-minute sessions moving from recognizing
losses, through guided grieving, to "restitution") is offered as a sub-technique of "medical
psychotherapy."

## Assumptions and Limitations

Kleinman is explicit about the assumptions: adequate **longitudinal time** with the patient (the
method "can be fitted into the routine of care" over many visits, unlike acute care); interpretive
and communicative **training most clinicians lack** ("most are not trained and some have no
interest"); and shared acceptance that **disablement-reduction, not cure**, is the goal. He
flags that the collaborative style is not universal — "a severe acute exacerbation is often better
dealt with by an authoritative style; patients from traditional ethnic groups may misunderstand
an egalitarian style." The method is self-described as non-exhaustive and warns against being
systematized into "a rigid, falsely complete formula."

## History

The explanatory-model interview derives from Kleinman, Eisenberg & Good (1978); its original
elicitation schedule appears in *[[kleinman-patients-and-healers-1980|Patients and Healers]]* (1980,
p. 106) — the **eight questions** later widely cited as "the Kleinman questions" (verbatim):

1. What do you call your problem? What name does it have?
2. What do you think has caused your problem?
3. Why do you think it started when it did?
4. What does your sickness do to you? How does it work?
5. How severe is it? Will it have a short or long course?
6. What do you fear most about your sickness?
7. What are the chief problems your sickness has caused for you?
8. What kind of treatment do you think you should receive? What are the most important results you hope
   to receive from the treatment?

Kleinman also there operationalized the practitioner–patient EM transaction as measurable "cognitive
distance" (Distance A/B/C, scored across the five EM domains). *[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The
Illness Narratives]]* (1988) later folded this elicitation into the fuller **mini-ethnography** and the
medical-education program of [[meaning-centered-medical-anthropology]]. The EM interview subsequently
entered medical-education curricula and cross-cultural clinical guidelines (e.g., cultural-formulation
frameworks), often in simplified form.

## Exemplary Applications

Kleinman applies it retrospectively across the book's cases (Alice Alcott's fear of inexorable
decline; the pain-center conference on Helen Bell, where the family and social network — the very
data a mini-ethnography would gather — were excluded). See
*[[kleinman-illness-narratives-1988|The Illness Narratives]]*.

## Debates

The method sits inside the standing tension in [[medical-anthropology]] between an interpretive/
clinical program that reforms biomedicine from within and a [[critical-medical-anthropology|critical]]
program that regards such collaboration as co-optation of anthropology by medicine.
