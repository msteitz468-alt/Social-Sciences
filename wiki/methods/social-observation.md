# Social observation (morals and manners)

## What It Does

As systematized by [[martineau-harriet|Harriet Martineau]] in *[[martineau-how-to-observe-1838|How to Observe Morals and Manners]]* (1838), **social observation** is the trained study of a people's **morals and manners**. Martineau argued that the "observer of Men and Manners" needs intellectual preparation equal to any physical-science student: truth is as easy to miss as to find even when materials are present to the senses; powers of observation must be trained and habits of method acquired (*How to Observe*, Introduction).

She treated the science of morals as among the least cultivated and most difficult of application, and held that classifying a society's moral manifestations should eventually be confided to "philosophers" standing to the science of society as Herschel to astronomy — founding-era professionalization language concurrent with, not identical to, [[comte-auguste|Comte]]'s program.

The aim is what is **fixed and essential** in a people — approximated across many observers over time — not peremptory national-character sketches from a few conversations.

## Procedure

Summary from Parts I–III:

### Philosophical requisites (Part I, Ch. I)

1. **Defined aim** — know what is to be known; in morals, agents are known in the gross; what must be ascertained is their **proportions in combination**.
2. **Happiness criterion** — subordinate national and philosophical prejudice to the **relative amount of human happiness** as the only general test; do not judge one people by another’s practice (forks, doors, marriage forms).
3. **Principles as rallying point** — principles that test and order facts; trained judgment outweighs intact senses without philosophy (Holman the blind traveller; the Deaf Traveller as examples).
4. **Origin of right and wrong** — reject innate universal Moral Sense; conscience **grows from influences**; expect a universal core (e.g. torment without reason is wrong; making others happy is right) plus infinite secondary variation.
5. **Circumstances form prevalent virtue and vice** — every prevalent virtue or vice is the result of particular circumstances; do not impute national faults to individuals as if they lived under the observer’s influences.

### Moral requisites (Part I, Ch. II)

6. **Untrammelled sympathy** — without sympathy the observer cannot reach hearts and minds; openness meets openness; hearts of like quality understand like. Mechanical and intellectual aids fail if "his heart be idle."

### Mechanical requisites and recording (Part I, Ch. III; Part III)

7. **Access** — mode of travel that multiplies varied contact (Martineau preferred the pedestrian over carriage-and-courier); language competence; avoid single-class informant circles (e.g. merchants only).
8. **Query lists** — pre-built, **country-specific**, expandable catechism of factual classes; not brandished in conversation; glance in the morning; enlarge as new subjects arise.
9. **Journal** — primary function is to reflect the traveller’s mind day by day (not a pre-classified statistical form); record what is most likely to slip; **defer generalization** until home; keep inferences **conjectural** (queries, not dogmas); daily entries (morning often best).
10. **Note-book** — separate, always at hand for transient wayside groups and scenes that cannot be recalled with precision; not for ostentatious note-taking mid-conversation.

### Evidence priority and departments (Part II–III)

11. **[[things-before-discourse]]** — begin with institutions and records; use discourse of persons as commentary.
12. **Five departments** — Religion; General Moral Notions; Domestic State; Idea of Liberty; Progress — plus Discourse as a commentary class and Mechanical Methods as apparatus.
13. **Cumulative observation** — one traveller need not settle every point; many observers supply materials for a cautious philosopher; no fact is without its use.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Morals and manners are **knowable** through trained observation of things, records, and varied discourse.
- **Line-of-road sampling**: the tourist pursues a line, not the expanse; at best a sample whose representativeness remains unverifiable; conversation with perhaps one in ten thousand (Introduction).
- **Happiness** as evaluative center is a utilitarian/providential commitment, not a neutral metric.
- Comparative illustrations draw on travel literature and second-hand reports — evidence class varies; Martineau herself records a correspondent’s position that "No books are so little to be trusted as travels."
- Hasty national generalizations are socially sticky — may take a century to reverse — so method is also an ethics of reporting.
- Distinct from later [[moral-statistics]] (rates of suicide, crime) and from [[positive-method|Comtean positive method]], though historically adjacent as founding-era social methodology. Family resemblance to later [[social-fact]] thinking (objectified products over subjective report) is suggestive and must not be equated without attribution.

## History

Travel observation is ancient; Martineau’s move is to treat it as a **science of morals** requiring training and method, in Charles Knight’s popular instructional series parallel to geology (De la Bèche, 1835). Written after her 1834–36 American tour and *Society in America* (1837). Contemporaneous with early Comte and with [[tocqueville-alexis-de|Tocqueville]]’s American-tour literature; themes of majority opinion and democratic manners appear in her US illustrations without Tocqueville’s conceptual apparatus.

## Exemplary Applications

- Martineau’s own *Society in America* (not yet ingested) is the companion empirical application.
- This wiki’s [[tocqueville-democracy-study|Tocqueville study]] is a parallel founding-era travel-based comparative sociology with different design and aims.
- Within *How to Observe*, worked examples include suicide as religious-sentiment indicator (see [[suicide]]), epitaphs/cemeteries, police origin, press freedom, charity hierarchy, and servant relations as structural Things.
- [[du-bois-w-e-b|Du Bois]] *[[du-bois-essays|The Black North]]* (early 1900s): census-informed urban [[black-urban-north|Black population]] study — ward geography, sex ratios, occupational percentages, business capital inventories, historical legal/labor episodes (truncated before homes/public institutions in the ingested reprint). Distinct from Martineau’s traveler catechism but continuous with observational sociology of group conditions.
