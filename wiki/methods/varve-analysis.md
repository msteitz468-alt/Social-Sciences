## What It Does

Varve analysis dates glacial and post-glacial sediments by counting **varves** — the paired annual layers (a thick pale summer band and a thin dark winter band) deposited each year in lakes fed by glacial meltwater. Each couplet is one year, so counting them gives an absolute, annually-resolved chronology by direct count. The method was pioneered by Baron Gerard de Geer on the sediments of the retreating Scandinavian ice-sheet (per Clark 1957).

## Procedure

Varve sequences are measured from a fixed reference horizon and counted forward. De Geer used the thick "zero" varve marking the ice-sheet's north–south bipartition and counted subsequent varves along the Ångerman River, dating the bipartition to c. 6800 BCE (by count), the final-phase Fenno-Scandian moraine to c. 8200 BCE, and the ice-sheet's presence in extreme southern Sweden to c. 12000 BCE. Varve thickness (roughly 0.2–3.0 cm) de Geer ascribed to minor solar-radiation fluctuations.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Varve chronology requires undisturbed annually-layered glacial-lake sediment, so as a regional absolute clock it was applicable, in Clark's day, **only in northern Europe**. M. Sauramo's Finnish sequence confirmed de Geer's Swedish results, but attempted transatlantic **"teleconnexions"** (matching Baltic sequences to American ones) "were not generally accepted" — the same caution Clark applied to long-range [[dendrochronology|tree-ring]] correlations (attributed, Clark 1957).

## History

De Geer's counts, built up from the 1880s onward, gave prehistory its first absolute calendar for the close of the Ice Age — a generation before [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] provided a globally applicable alternative. Clark (1957) noted radiocarbon already reached roughly four times the chronological range of the varve method.

## Exemplary Applications

- Dating of the Scandinavian ice-sheet's retreat phases (bipartition c. 6800 BCE; Fenno-Scandian moraine c. 8200 BCE), tied to the late-glacial and Mesolithic archaeological sequence of northern Europe.

## Debates

Varve analysis, like [[dendrochronology]], is an absolute method resting on a direct annual count, and Clark ranked both above the astronomical (Milanković–Zeuner) and sedimentation-rate chronologies. Its regional restriction is precisely what radiocarbon later overcame.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook synthesis (2012)

[[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]] and [[bahn-paul|Paul Bahn]] (*[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]*, 6th ed. 2012, Ch. 4) treat annual lake-bed sediment deposits (**varves**) from glacial melt—especially in Scandinavia—as sequences that, when linked, stretch from the present back to the start of ice-sheet retreat ~**13,000 years ago**. The method first allowed a fairly reliable date for the **end of the last Ice Age**, contributing to chronology beyond Scandinavia (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

They frame varves under the general requirement that absolute methods depend on a **regular, time-dependent process** (here the earth’s annual solar cycle expressed in meltwater sedimentation). Before radioactive methods, **varves and dendrochronology** were the most accurate scientific absolute clocks; today they rank [[radiocarbon-dating]] as the single most useful method, and treat varves as remaining of **restricted use relative to tree-rings** (attributed positions). Long-distance varve “teleconnections” (like long-range tree-ring matches) must be treated with caution (attributed). Varved marine sediments also contribute to extended radiocarbon calibration beyond tree-ring masters (INTCAL09 to 50,000 Cal BP as of their 2012 edition—see [[radiocarbon-dating]]).

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]].)*

Roberts distinguishes clastic, biogenic, and chemical varves and notes their multiple formation mechanisms: Baron de Geer's late-19th-century Scandinavian clastic varves gave one of the first quantitative estimates of Holocene duration; winter-ice lakes accumulate organic then meltwater then summer algal/diatom laminae; alkaline lakes (Zürichsee) precipitate a summer carbonate "white-out." Varve counts have been used to time the [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]] and, at Lake Gościąż (Poland), Holzmaar (Germany), and Soppensee (Switzerland), to independently calibrate the ¹⁴C timescale near the Holocene onset (see [[holocene]], [[radiocarbon-dating]]).
