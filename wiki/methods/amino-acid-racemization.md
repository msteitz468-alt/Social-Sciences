## What It Does

Amino acid racemization (AAR) dating measures the ratio of D- to L-amino acids preserved in fossil shell or bone. Only L-forms occur in living tissue; after death, amino acids convert to D-forms at a rate specific to each amino acid and dependent on temperature, moisture, and pH, so the D/L ratio is a measure of time since death.

## Procedure

A sample's D-form to L-form ratio for a chosen amino acid is measured and compared against a racemization-rate curve calibrated for local conditions (chiefly temperature history). Because the rate is temperature-dependent, AAR reaches further back in cooler regions — up to roughly 1 million years — than in the tropics, where higher temperatures accelerate racemization and the effective range is closer to 200,000 years.

## Assumptions and Limitations

AAR dating of bone was popular in the 1970s but was largely abandoned after a well-known misapplication to prehistoric human skeletons from California: AAR suggested ages of 40,000–70,000 years, but independent uranium-series and radiocarbon dates on the same skeletons showed they were no more than 11,000 years old. The error was attributed to incorrect racemization-rate estimates for old, amino-acid-depleted bone. Mollusk shell and ostrich eggshell (OES) retain organic material better than bone and are considered more reliable AAR substrates, often agreeing closely with associated radiocarbon dates.

## History

The California skeleton episode remains the field's cautionary case for the method, shifting practice away from bone toward shell and eggshell substrates.

## Exemplary Applications

AAR on ostrich eggshell was used at Border Cave, South Africa, bracketing the Middle Stone Age sequence there between 145,000 and 70,000 years ago, and has been proposed as a cross-check candidate for the roughly 92,000-year thermoluminescence date at Qafzeh Cave, Israel (see [[thermoluminescence-dating]], [[qafzeh]]). Discussed in Klein, *The Human Career* ([[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Debates

The California skeletons episode is Klein's (1999) central cautionary example: it demonstrates that AAR dates on bone can be badly wrong (by tens of thousands of years) when racemization-rate assumptions for degraded, old bone are incorrect, and it underlies the general preference for shell/eggshell substrates over bone in current practice.
