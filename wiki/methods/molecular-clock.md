## What It Does

The molecular (biomolecular) clock method assumes that DNA or protein differences between taxa accumulate at a more or less constant, linear rate. Calibrated against one or more divergences whose dates are independently established from the fossil record, the resulting rate is then applied to estimate divergence dates for taxa or lineages with poorer fossil records — most consequentially, the human/African ape split.

## Procedure

A genetic or protein distance measure is taken between living taxa (for example, DNA-DNA hybridization distance, or amino acid sequence difference in a protein such as globin). The measure is calibrated to absolute time using an independently fossil-dated divergence elsewhere in the phylogeny (e.g., the Old World monkey/ape split, or the catarrhine/platyrrhine split), yielding a rate of molecular change per unit time that is then extrapolated to date other splits in the same tree.

Using DNA-DNA hybridization data calibrated against a 25-million-year fossil date for the Old World monkey/ape divergence, estimated splits are: human-chimpanzee around 5.5 million years ago, the gorilla lineage around 7.7 million years ago, the orangutan lineage around 12.2 million years ago, and the gibbon lineage around 16.4 million years ago (as of Klein 1999). Globin-gene differentiation data, calibrated against a 30-million-year catarrhine/platyrrhine split and assuming a rate slowdown, instead suggest a human-chimpanzee divergence around 6.5 million years ago, with gorilla, orangutan, and Old World monkey lines splitting at roughly 6.8, 12.3, and 21.5 million years ago respectively (as of Klein 1999).

## Assumptions and Limitations

The molecular clock has been controversial since it was first applied to human evolution, because its estimates often contravene fossil-based dates. Klein (1999) reports evidence that the mutation rate in protein-coding DNA has slowed progressively from more primitive to more advanced primates — meaning a clock calibrated on one lineage's divergence will systematically mis-estimate divergence times elsewhere in the tree unless recalibrated lineage by lineage. DNA and protein divergence rates are consequently not assumed constant across taxa or genome regions without qualification.

## History

Early molecular-clock estimates for the human/African ape split (12–14 million years ago, based on jaw fossils then assigned to Ramapithecus/Sivapithecus, see [[ramapithecus]]) were overturned once more complete fossil material and reanalysis showed those jaws instead documented the orangutan lineage's divergence, not the hominid lineage's. As of Klein (1999), "virtually all paleontologists now agree that the human/African ape split occurred between 8 and 5 my ago as indicated by the molecular studies," reflecting the molecular clock's eventual — if contested — convergence with revised fossil interpretation.

## Exemplary Applications

Applied to estimate the human-chimpanzee-gorilla-orangutan-gibbon divergence sequence and, in Klein (1999), to argue for a human/African ape split between 8 and 5 million years ago, discussed alongside the fossil evidence bearing on [[ramapithecus]] and Sivapithecus. Discussed in Klein, *The Human Career* ([[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Debates

[POSITION, as of Klein 1999]: molecular data are ambiguous on whether chimpanzee-human or chimpanzee-gorilla share the more recent common ancestor. Most protein and DNA analyses favor a chimpanzee-human clade, but a minority of studies argue chimpanzee-gorilla are closer; rapid successive branching within 1–3 million years, or an ancestral polymorphic population, could make the trichotomy genuinely unresolvable by molecular data alone. This dispute, and the broader molecular-clock-versus-morphology tension over the Ramapithecus/Sivapithecus reassignment, bear on the [[modern-human-origins-debate]] evidentiary landscape more generally, though Klein (1999) treats the ape-divergence dating question as distinct from that debate.

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Source: [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]]; as of Stringer 2012 / Endicott–Ho–Metspalu collaboration.*

**Calibration problem.** Standard mtDNA calibrations often assume human–chimp split ~6 Ma; short-term human mtDNA substitution rates (islands, family studies, mitochondrial disease pedigrees) are much faster than long-term chimp–human rates — purifying selection offered as explanation for the discrepancy. Chimpanzee–human genetic similarity ~2% (depending on counting); fossil+genetic calibration suggests lineage split ~6 Ma; Allan Wilson and Vince Sarich albumin work helped establish closer African-ape than orangutan affinity.

**Recalibrated recent-human clock.** Stringer with Phillip Endicott, Simon Ho, and Mait Metspalu compared calibrations using newly calculated rates **not** based on the ~6 Ma chimp–human split. New rates gave **younger** ages consistent (per Stringer) with latest fossil/archaeological data: African Eve ~**135 ka** (vs ~200 ka), exit from Africa ~**55 ka**, Americas arrival ~**14 ka**. If correct, these imply younger Neanderthal–modern mtDNA divergence, a multi-millennial gap between first modern fossils in Africa and Eve, and cast doubt on early exits toward China and Australia — geneticists should reconsider relying on human–chimp divergence to calibrate recent events (as of Stringer 2012). Recalibrated work also places L3 origin/expansion ~60 ka. Keinan–Reich X-chromosome drift analyses of Out of Africa male/female founder ratios and haplotype-map SNP ages from recombination breakdown are cited as complementary molecular-timing tools.

**Body-louse clocks.** Stoneking; Toups et al.: body-louse origin ~80–170 ka taken as proxy for regular clothing/bedding use in Africa before the diaspora (as of Stringer 2012).
