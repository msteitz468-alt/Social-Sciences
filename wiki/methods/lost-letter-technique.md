# Lost-Letter Technique

## What It Does

Stamped, addressed letters are "lost" in public places; **return rates** index helping behavior / social altruism. [[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] deploys variants across Chicago neighborhoods in *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* (ch. 9; 2010 replication in ch. 16) as a **behavioral ecometric** complement to survey measures of the "good community."

## Procedure

### PHDCN 2002 (Sampson 2012)

Second community-survey wave included a Milgram-adapted lost-letter experiment: project staff unobtrusively dropped two preaddressed stamped envelopes on opposite random corners of each home block of randomly selected study participants; conditions recorded; addresses "Mary Jones" vs "Universal Services" to ISR Michigan PO boxes, with a fictional Chicago return address. **~3,300 letters dropped; just over a third mailed back** (as Sampson reports).

### 2010 replication (ch. 16)

Streamlined design: **325 letters** over 3 days in **9 theoretically chosen communities** (Loop, Near North, Irving Park, Avalon Park, Roseland, Grand Boulevard, Lower West Side/Pilsen, Uptown, Hyde Park), ~36 drops per community for ecometric reliability.

Compare rates to survey altruism, [[collective-efficacy]], disadvantage, organizational density, and [[legal-cynicism|moral/legal cynicism]].

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes return primarily reflects local helping norms rather than only mail density or composition of passersby; interpretation is comparative across places. Small N in the 2010 nine-community revisit limits power; Grand Boulevard was an outlier Sampson links possibly to unsettled norms after housing demolitions.

## History

Milgram's classic technique; urban/community applications including Sampson's Chicago program.

## Exemplary Applications

### Sampson / PHDCN findings (attributed, 2012)

- **Ecometric reliability:** significant neighborhood variation in letter returns; reliability highest at community-area level (**0.75**).
- **Structure:** severely disadvantaged (poor and segregated) communities return at significantly lower rates; heterogeneity made no difference; population size *positively* linked to returns (Wirth would not have anticipated this, Sampson notes); nonprofit density a large direct associate of returns.
- **CE and trust:** mean 1995–2002 [[collective-efficacy]] correlates with later letter returns **0.41** at community-area level; trust in neighbors **0.60**, shared expectations for control **0.32**.
- **Cynicism:** high moral/legal cynicism communities lower returns (r≈–0.46); association largely explained by disadvantage once stability/diversity adjusted (2002 wave).
- **Stickiness (2010):** community-level letter-return correlation over time **R=0.74** (p<0.05; 0.78 without Grand Boulevard) — "substantial stickiness" of altruistic neighborhood character. Citywide return ~31% in 2010 vs ~37% nearly a decade earlier in the same communities — not a "hunkering down" collapse. Loop and Near North among the most other-regarding (challenge to Wirth anonymity).
- **2010 correlates:** returns higher with organizational density (R≈0.58); lower with moral/legal cynicism (R≈–0.82); prior cynicism still predicts lower 2010 returns controlling baseline return propensity and concurrent poverty.
- Combined with bystander CPR rates, letter returns form a behavioral altruism marker that, with CE and moral cynicism, predicts later homicide and teen births net of composition (as Sampson reports, ch. 9).
