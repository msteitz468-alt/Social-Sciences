# Cultural Resource Management

**Cultural Resource Management (CRM)** — also called applied or contract archaeology — is the practice of identifying, evaluating, conserving, and (when necessary) excavating archaeological and historic resources threatened by development, agriculture, conflict, or neglect. Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (Ch. 15) treat CRM as the institutional response of “public archaeology” once heritage is valued by the state: **survey → conservation → mitigation**, embedded in national law and increasingly landscape-scale management. Closely linked to the concept [[heritage-management]] and the engagement method [[public-archaeology]].

## What It Does

- Locates and evaluates archaeological resources before construction, mining, roads, dams, and land conversion.
- Weighs options under “preservation” statutes that often **do not guarantee** in-situ preservation — they mandate process (identification, consultation, mitigation).
- Implements **avoidance/redesign**, protective agreements (e.g. no-plow), or **data-recovery** excavation when loss is unavoidable.
- Produces management plans (e.g. US DoD ICRMPs; BLM IRRAMPs) that increasingly favor remote sensing and minimal excavation.
- Handles portable heritage reporting regimes (UK Portable Antiquities Scheme) and site-purchase conservation (Archaeological Conservancy model).

## Procedure

(As synthesized in Renfrew & Bahn 2012, attributed.)

1. **Pre-development environmental / cultural assessment.** Inventory archaeological resources, historic properties, traditional cultural properties, species, and landscapes using satellite imagery, aerial photography, GIS, and fieldwalking (“ground truthing”).
2. **Significance evaluation.** US path (Section 106 of NHPA): identification → National Register eligibility → consultation (SHPO; Advisory Council on Historic Preservation if disputed) → mitigation decision.
3. **Mitigation hierarchy.** Prefer **avoidance** and redesign; if damage is inevitable, **salvage/rescue** excavation and sampling; rarely, major unexpected finds halt development (Mexico City Great Temple; London Rose theatre context).
4. **Research prioritization.** Growing presumption: do not excavate unthreatened sites when threatened sites can answer comparable questions; assign significant research budgets to mitigation projects.
5. **Documentation and curation.** Record, report, and store collections — the textbook flags failures of publication and long-term storage as structural problems.
6. **Standards.** Professional registers (e.g. Register of Professional Archaeologists) and agency credential requirements aim to raise fieldwork quality.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Significance filters** mean most sites are documented then destroyed in construction.
- Private firms conduct ~**≥90% of US field archaeology** (textbook figure) — quality, research linkage, and grey-literature access vary.
- Up to ~**60% of modern excavations** may remain unpublished after 10 years (textbook summary figure); duty to publish follows from excavation as destruction.
- US private-land law can permit owner-authorized destruction (Mimbres looting case); CRM cannot alone stop the illicit market.
- International instruments (UNESCO 1970; World Heritage 1972) depend on national law; effectiveness limited.

## History

| Milestone | Role (per Renfrew & Bahn 2012) |
|---|---|
| England Ancient Monuments Act **1882** | First schedule; Inspector (Pitt-Rivers); guardianship |
| US Antiquities Act **1906** | Federal-land protection; permits; presidential landmarks |
| NHPA **1966**, NEPA **1969**, ARPA **1979**, Shipwrecks **1987**, NAGPRA **1990** | Core US federal archaeology stack |
| 1970s–80s large mitigations | e.g. Tennessee-Tombigbee: 682 sites identified, 27 affected; Lubbub Creek |
| Shift toward remote sensing & management plans | Minimize excavation |
| UK Treasure Act **1996** + **PAS** | Voluntary reporting culture for detector finds |
| UNESCO WHC **1972**; 1970 illicit-traffic Convention | International incentive frameworks |

Main destruction agencies the textbook names: **construction**, **agricultural intensification** (wider/slower), **armed conflict**, plus tourism pressure and looting.

## Exemplary Applications

- **Mexico City Great Temple (1978–):** Urban salvage from Coyolxauhqui stone discovery → successive temple phases, offerings, permanent museum/national monument.
- **US CRM industry:** Section 106 pipeline; private firms dominate fieldwork; quality/publication/storage recurring problems.
- **Mimbres Foundation → Archaeological Conservancy:** Purchase and protect looted Southwestern sites after irreversible early destruction.
- **Portable Antiquities Scheme (UK):** >650,000 reported objects; Roman site counts up >30% in some counties; Frome hoard (52,500 coins) left for excavation after detectorist notification.
- **York Archaeological Trust:** Urban salvage under UK planning (PPG 16 era) fused with public engagement (Jorvik).

## Debates

- Academic vs contract archaeology split; research value of compliance-driven digs.
- Whether “preservation” law produces conservation or bureaucratic clearance for destruction.
- Looting and collectors (“collectors are the real looters”) vs private property rights.
- Indigenous consultation and repatriation constraints on CRM collections (NAGPRA).
- Tourism funding vs “Disneyfication” of heritage presentation.
