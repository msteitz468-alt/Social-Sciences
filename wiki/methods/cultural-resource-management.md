# Cultural Resource Management

**Cultural Resource Management (CRM)** — also called applied or contract archaeology — is the practice of identifying, evaluating, conserving, and (when necessary) excavating archaeological and historic resources threatened by development, agriculture, conflict, or neglect. Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (Ch. 15) treat CRM as the institutional response of “public archaeology” once heritage is valued by the state: **survey → conservation → mitigation**, embedded in national law and increasingly landscape-scale management. Closely linked to the concept [[heritage-management]] and the engagement method [[public-archaeology]].

## What It Does

- Locates and evaluates archaeological resources before construction, mining, roads, dams, and land conversion.
- Weighs options under “preservation” statutes that often **do not guarantee** in-situ preservation — they mandate process (identification, consultation, mitigation).
- Implements **avoidance/redesign**, protective agreements (e.g. no-plow), or **data-recovery** excavation when loss is unavoidable.
- Produces management plans (e.g. US DoD ICRMPs; BLM IRRAMPs) that increasingly favor remote sensing and minimal excavation.
- Handles portable heritage reporting regimes (UK Portable Antiquities Scheme) and site-purchase conservation (Archaeological Conservancy model).

## Procedure

(As synthesized in Renfrew & Bahn 2012, attributed.)

1. **Pre-development environmental / cultural assessment.** Inventory archaeological resources, historic properties, traditional cultural properties, species, and landscapes using satellite imagery, aerial photography, GIS, and fieldwalking (“ground truthing”).
2. **Significance evaluation.** US path (Section 106 of NHPA): identification → National Register eligibility → consultation (SHPO; Advisory Council on Historic Preservation if disputed) → mitigation decision.
3. **Mitigation hierarchy.** Prefer **avoidance** and redesign; if damage is inevitable, **salvage/rescue** excavation and sampling; rarely, major unexpected finds halt development (Mexico City Great Temple; London Rose theatre context).
4. **Research prioritization.** Growing presumption: do not excavate unthreatened sites when threatened sites can answer comparable questions; assign significant research budgets to mitigation projects.
5. **Documentation and curation.** Record, report, and store collections — the textbook flags failures of publication and long-term storage as structural problems.
6. **Standards.** Professional registers (e.g. Register of Professional Archaeologists) and agency credential requirements aim to raise fieldwork quality.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Significance filters** mean most sites are documented then destroyed in construction.
- Private firms conduct ~**≥90% of US field archaeology** (textbook figure) — quality, research linkage, and grey-literature access vary.
- Up to ~**60% of modern excavations** may remain unpublished after 10 years (textbook summary figure); duty to publish follows from excavation as destruction.
- US private-land law can permit owner-authorized destruction (Mimbres looting case); CRM cannot alone stop the illicit market.
- International instruments (UNESCO 1970; World Heritage 1972) depend on national law; effectiveness limited.

## Exemplary Applications

- **Mexico City Great Temple (1978–):** Urban salvage from Coyolxauhqui stone discovery → successive temple phases, offerings, permanent museum/national monument.
- **US CRM industry:** Section 106 pipeline; private firms dominate fieldwork; quality/publication/storage recurring problems.
- **[[lower-verde-archaeological-project-study|Lower Verde Archaeological Project]] (LVAP, 1990s; *[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River]]* 1997):** Bureau of Reclamation Safety of Dams / reservoir-area mitigation by Statistical Research, Inc. under Tonto National Forest permit — a “big CRM project” case ([[altschul-jeffrey-h|Altschul]] Ch. 17). APE-limited sample; construction schedule under FAR; 23→26 sites; contract modified 14 times; cost ~$1.45M→>$2.6M over ~4→6 years; print synthesis plus CD-ROM specialist volumes. Shows how sponsor theory frames (here [[hohokam-core-periphery-model|Hohokam core–periphery]]) structure research design, and how landscape theory can unify multi-specialist CRM products. CAP-era survey bias toward reservoir locales is explicit.
- **Mimbres Foundation → Archaeological Conservancy:** Purchase and protect looted Southwestern sites after irreversible early destruction.
- **Portable Antiquities Scheme (UK):** >650,000 reported objects; Roman site counts up >30% in some counties; Frome hoard (52,500 coins) left for excavation after detectorist notification.
- **York Archaeological Trust:** Urban salvage under UK planning (PPG 16 era) fused with public engagement (Jorvik).

## Debates

- Academic vs contract archaeology split; research value of compliance-driven digs.
- Whether “preservation” law produces conservation or bureaucratic clearance for destruction.
- Looting and collectors (“collectors are the real looters”) vs private property rights.
- Indigenous consultation and repatriation constraints on CRM collections (NAGPRA).
- Tourism funding vs “Disneyfication” of heritage presentation.

## African CRM and salvage (*Oxford Handbook*, 2013)

*(Attributed — heritage management and practice-history chapters in *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013|Mitchell & Lane 2013]]*; as of ~2012.)*

- **Traditional custodianship vs colonial law:** Pre-colonial site survival often via sacred-place management and specialist custodians (Great Zimbabwe *mijejeje*; Kasubi tombs clan maintenance); colonial legal systems centralized control and often barred local access while prioritizing monumental aesthetics (heritage chapter).
- **Dam salvage record (Connah Ch. 2):** 1960s Aswan High Dam salvage outstanding internationalization of African archaeology (many nations; thousands of publications), extending Egyptological attraction to Nubia; contemporary Volta Basin Project produced many small excavations but little publication; Kainji Dam Project in Nigeria was “a virtual failure.”
- **Development and capacity:** Metolong Dam (Lesotho) CRM funding (Mitchell & Arthur 2010) illustrates shift toward mitigation survey/excavation **and** national capacity building. International (EU–ACP; World Bank) funding still often privileges monumentality/aesthetics over integrated community rights; Munjeri calls for harmonizing domestic law, community traditional systems, and international treaties.
- **Institutional gap:** Kusimba & Klehm urge CRM programmes **within** African university/museum archaeology departments — almost absent outside southern Africa; development projects often skip CRM surveys or hire non-African expertise; nations should criminalize illegal excavation/export.
- **Looting market:** Connah documents intensified plundering driven by developed-world demand; blame on purchasers and international dealers; Western legislative reluctance except e.g. 1993 Mali–USA Bilateral Accord.
- **Post-conflict and indigenous leadership:** Ethical stakes intensify in reconstruction contexts (Giblin on Rwanda); Mire (2011) Somaliland model of trained local groups leading research and heritage management under decolonization goals.

## UK vs US Frameworks and Recent-Past Management (Schofield & Johnson 2006)

Schofield & Johnson give a fuller UK/US institutional contrast ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch.7). In England, cultural resources are managed through legislation covering ancient monuments, historic buildings, historic/conservation areas, and marine archaeology (wrecks), enforced mainly by the Department of Culture, Media and Sport with English Heritage advising on protection decisions; there is **no "fifty-year rule"** — sites of any date can receive statutory protection. In the United States, historic preservation is organized state-by-state through State Historic Preservation Offices (SHPOs) reporting to the National Park Service, typically triggered by federal-agency undertakings under Section 106; a fifty-year rule applies to National Register eligibility but can be set aside for properties of "exceptional importance" (in 1994, 2,025 National Register properties had achieved significance within the prior 50 years). The Advisory Council on Historic Preservation's estimate that roughly 90% of US archaeology is CRM-driven appears in this chapter as well as in King's household-archaeology chapter (see [[household-archaeology]]).

Recency-based designation decisions are contested on both sides of the Atlantic: England's Greenham Common Cold War missile-alert site (GAMA) was scheduled less than ten years after decommissioning; a Jimi Hendrix "blue plaque" survived challenge; a 1990s roads-protest monolith on Twyford Down was refused protection despite an English Heritage recommendation.

Schofield & Johnson introduce **"informed conservation"** (attributed to Clark 2001) — "the research, analysis, survey and investigation necessary to understand the significance of a building and its landscape and to inform decisions about repair, alteration, use and management" — applied to decisions from national Cold War-site triage down to paint-conservation choices on individual structures. **Historic Landscape Characterisation (HLC)** emerged in the 1990s partly as a reaction against the limits of site-by-site designation, which suits individual buildings and monuments poorly at the scale of whole landscapes; it maps attributes (field morphology, place names, boundary loss, land-use data) in a GIS to inform rather than block development and agri-environment policy, and was extended to the recent past by the *Change and Creation: Historic Landscape Character 1950–2000* project.

Denis Byrne's **social-significance approach** (Byrne et al. 2001, Australia) holds that "everywhere has the potential to hold value for somebody" and that heritage designation frameworks systematically miss value held by non-elite or minority communities. Schofield & Johnson apply this critique to Tower Hamlets, London, where a large Bangladeshi community's significant buildings (mosques, community centres) lacked protection because they did not meet national designation criteria, while "listed" buildings meant little to residents — characterizing government-led English designation as recognizing value in "a restricted way, a white, middle-class way" (attributed to Gard'ner 2001). Public-opinion polling cited in support of preservation funding: 87% of English respondents (English Heritage's *Power of Place*, 2000) and 99% of US respondents (Ramos and Duganne 2000, on archaeological sites' educational/scientific value).

Findings on WWII-site survival in England (English Heritage documentary and aerial-photograph assessment, Anderton and Schofield 1999): only 14 of 242 wartime radar stations and 10 of 981 heavy anti-aircraft sites survived in recognizable form within 60 years, most losses attributed to postwar suburban expansion.
