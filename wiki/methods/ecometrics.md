# Ecometrics

## What It Does

**Ecometrics** is the methodology of assessing **ecological settings** (neighborhoods, communities) with the same seriousness psychometrics brings to individual measurement: multi-level models, item reliability, and aggregation rules that distinguish **true neighborhood variation** from sampling noise and individual composition. Developed in the PHDCN / Sampson–Raudenbush program and used throughout *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* (2012).

## Procedure

*(Summary — Sampson 2012 analytic frame.)* Define ecological units; sample individuals (or observed street segments) within units; model item responses with multi-level structure; estimate neighborhood-level latent traits (e.g. [[collective-efficacy]], observed disorder via [[systematic-social-observation]], letter-return rates via [[lost-letter-technique]]); use those scores as predictors/outcomes of community processes.

### Sampson / Raudenbush rationale (attributed)

Calling ecological measures' properties merely "psychometric" reveals individualistic bias: **ecological constructs are not merely aggregates of individual characteristics** — hence "ecometric" rather than psychometric properties of social-ecological measures. Without a coherent strategy for evaluating ecological assessments, multilevel studies overemphasize the individual component. Sampson treats ecometrics as "just as important" as psychometrics and calls for scaling it nationally/internationally (CPS-style civic items; organizational contexts such as schools).

### Constructs measured ecometrically in GAC

| Construct | Data mode | Notes (as Sampson reports) |
|---|---|---|
| Collective efficacy | Community survey vignettes + cohesion/trust | 1995 CS N≈8,782 in 343 NCs; informal-control vignettes because capacity is unobservable when crime is low |
| Observed disorder | SSO video/logs | Independent of survey perception |
| Perceived disorder | Survey "problem" items | Sticky shared judgments; distinct from SSO |
| Letter-return altruism | Lost-letter drops | Community-area reliability ~0.75 (2002) |
| Organizational density / civic events | Survey + event catalogs | Multi-item org resources; nonprofit density |

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes ecological units are meaningful relative to the theory; that survey/observational items load on shared neighborhood properties; that multi-level decomposition separates composition from context sufficiently for the claim at hand. Does not by itself identify causality against selection (Sampson addresses selection as social process separately). Multiple scales (face-block → NC → community area) are legitimate; no single "correct" neighborhood operationalization.

## History

Emerges from hierarchical linear modeling / item-response work applied to neighborhoods in the 1990s PHDCN design; named and systematized as "ecometrics" in the Raudenbush–Sampson literature. Wilson's foreword to *Great American City* flags ecometrics as a roadmap for studying context. Continuity with [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] ecological mapping, revised for multi-level inference and mechanism measurement.

## Exemplary Applications

[[collective-efficacy]] measurement; [[systematic-social-observation]] of disorder; [[lost-letter-technique|lost-letter]] behavioral altruism; community-process chapters of [[sampson-great-american-city-study]]; spatial lag / neighbors-of-neighbors models; inter-neighborhood mobility matrices as valued networks.

## Debates

Tied to [[neighborhood-effects-debate]] (what community constructs mean and whether they cause individual outcomes) and [[broken-windows-debate]] (observed vs perceived disorder measurement).
