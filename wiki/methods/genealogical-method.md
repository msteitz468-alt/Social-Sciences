# Genealogical Method

## What It Does

The genealogical method is [[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]]' technique (attributed) for
collecting social-organisation data by first recording exhaustive concrete **pedigrees** —
named individuals, their marriages, and their descendants across several generations — and
then deriving the kinship terminology, marriage regulations, descent, inheritance,
succession, and property rules from the genealogical network itself, rather than from
abstract questions about "what do you call your father's brother?" Rivers held that
pedigrees are "bodies of dry fact" whose accuracy is "about as incapable of being
influenced by bias, conscious or unconscious, as any subject that can be imagined"
(attributed), and that the method was what made survey ethnography possible at all: "It is
certain that without the genealogical method this book could never have been written"
(attributed, of *[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]*).

## Procedure

- **Collect pedigrees from named informants**, recording each person's kin by concrete
  reference (this actual man, his actual wife, their actual children), not by category.
- **Derive terminology from the pedigree**: ask what term ego uses for each concretely
  identified relative, so that a term's full range of application emerges from real cases.
- **Disambiguate double relationships**: where intermarriage makes two people related in
  more than one way (common in small endogamous islands), multiple pedigrees are needed to
  fix which relationship a term encodes.
- **Read social facts off the network**: land tenure, inheritance, adoption, and
  succession are traced through the recorded descent lines (Rivers' Mota "Takwale's land"
  case is his worked example).
- **Grade evidence by the linguistic medium and informant**, region by region — Rivers
  rated Mota-via-mission-interpreter work most reliable and Eastern-Solomons pidgin-English
  work least.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Rivers explicitly flagged the method's constraints. Melanesian pedigrees were typically
shallow (3–4 collateral generations, occasionally 8–9 in the direct line in Fiji), limiting
deep reconstruction. Acculturated informants tended to supply "the terms they were
accustomed to use in talking to Europeans," missing the structurally significant native
usage — Rivers reported "completely miss[ing]" key features of Fijian systems on first
attempt for exactly this reason. The method's evidential value was hostage to informant
quality: much of his Banks Islands data came from a single man (John Pantutun), and his
Tikopia data from a single non-native castaway (John Maresere), later checked against W. J.
Durrad's independent fieldwork. The method's most contested extension is the use of
kinship terms as **[[survivals]]** ("fossils") to reconstruct extinct marriage customs —
an inference the functionalists rejected as conjectural (see [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]).

## History

Rivers developed the method on the 1898 Cambridge Anthropological Expedition to the Torres
Strait, gave it its first monographic use in *The Todas* (1906), codified it in the
*Sociological Review* (1910), and deployed it at scale in
*[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]* (1914) and *Kinship
and Social Organisation* (1914). It distinguished his approach from
[[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]]'s earlier schedule method (which collected terms directly
against a fixed list of relationships) by grounding every term in a concrete, checkable
pedigree. The pedigree-collection core of the method — separating recorded fact from
interpretation, verifying terms against real cases — outlived Rivers' diffusionist theory
and became standard equipment for the fieldwork generation.

## Exemplary Applications

- **Rivers, *[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|The History of Melanesian Society]]*** (1914):
  the full Oceanic survey; the Mota "Takwale's land" tenure case; the Pentecost
  daughter's-daughter-marriage reconstruction (a theoretical term-set built from a
  hypothesized marriage, then matched against recorded terms).
- **Rivers, *The Todas*** (1906): first monographic deployment.
- **Chagnon, *[[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-1968|Yanomamö: The Fierce People]]* (1968):** multi-village genealogies under severe **name taboos** and systematic informant deception; public elicitation failed (invented names, absurd marriages); private cross-checks, enemy-village verification, and headman Kaobawa's year-one collaboration produced ~2,000 names across four villages — a classic case of the method under hostile elicitation conditions. Study: [[chagnon-yanomamo-fierce-people-study]].

## Debates

The method's descriptive core is uncontested; its use to infer unobservable past
institutions from present terminology is the live issue — see
[[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]] and, on the underlying logic, [[survivals]] and the
[[comparative-method]].

### Sahlins 2013 — method vs definition of kinship

*(Attributed — [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]], *[[sahlins-what-kinship-is-2013|What Kinship Is—And Is Not]]* 2013.)* The genealogical method as a data technique is not denied; what is attacked is treating **genealogical connection as the definition of "kinship proper"** (Scheffler–Lounsbury; Davis–Warner; Gellner; the Morgan premise). Sahlins: pedigrees and birth chains do not make kinship biogenetic *by definition*; performative mutuality, name-sharing, and ancestral co-generation show genealogical idiom can be a folk-biological **metaphor for social positions** (McKinley via Sahlins). Viveiros (as quoted): gift-economy animistic worlds are "utterly beyond the grasp of the genealogical method." See [[kinship-biology-vs-culture-debate]] · [[mutuality-of-being]].

## From Kuper, *Invention of Primitive Society* (1988)

*(Attributed — Kuper 1988, Ch. 8.)* Rivers invented the genealogical method on the Torres Straits expedition: systematically collected pedigrees to derive kinship terms, marriage rules, and social organisation — making ethnology “a science” (Haddon’s phrase for Rivers, as Kuper reports). Method underwrote both Morganian and later diffusionist phases of Rivers’s work. Source: [[kuper-invention-of-primitive-society-1988]].

## From Stocking, *After Tylor* (1995)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking]].)* Stocking traces the method's origin to the 1898 [[torres-strait-expedition-study|Torres Strait Expedition]]: Rivers, after consulting Francis Galton before departure, originally intended the collection of genealogies to test whether closely related individuals resembled each other in psychological/physiological test results. On finding informants' genealogical memory extended 3–5 generations, Rivers began — with [[haddon-a-c|Haddon]]'s encouragement — systematically collecting genealogies for sociological data (kinship terms, residence, totem/clan membership), using minimal English kin-terms in pidgin, cross-checked against multiple informants for consistency. Rivers argued the method was self-correcting because overlapping genealogies collected from different informants (or observers) could be checked against one another, and claimed it let trained but linguistically unprepared observers "with no knowledge of the language and very inferior interpreters" uncover, in comparatively short time, indigenous social structure that had eluded long-term resident Europeans — enabling the anthropologist "to formulate laws regulating the lives of the people which they have probably never formulated themselves." Stocking flags the "evolutionary and methodological hubris" implicit in this framing.

By 1912 (in the *Notes and Queries on Anthropology* 4th edition, which Rivers helped write, and a companion 1913 essay), Rivers elaborated a substantially revised and more linguistically and epistemologically cautious conception of fieldwork built around the genealogical core: the investigator's "first duty" was to learn the local language as fully as possible; direct questions were flagged as liable to import "civilised categories" onto informants' thought, so volunteered information and witnessed events (especially broken social rules) were prioritized as more reliable than direct interrogation; corroboration from "two or more independent witnesses" and cultivated "sympathy and tact" were required. Stocking treats this 1912–13 formulation — and Rivers's companion 1913 argument that ethnographic labor should not be divided across a multidisciplinary team but carried out by a single "private worker" living alone among a community of some 400–500 people for a year or more — as the direct conceptual bridge to what [[malinowski-bronislaw|Malinowski]] later publicized as [[participant-observation]] in the Trobriands, contra the mythologized "invention" narrative. Rivers nonetheless still held that the ultimate "aim of anthropology" was historical, situating even his refined method within a broadly diffusionist/historical theoretical frame, not a synchronic-functionalist one.
