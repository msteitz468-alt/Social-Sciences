# Symbolic Exegesis (Three Levels of Meaning)

## What It Does

[[turner-victor|Victor Turner]]'s method for interpreting ritual symbols, stated in "Symbols
in Ndembu Ritual" (*[[turner-forest-of-symbols-1967|The Forest of Symbols]]*, 1967) and
applied throughout *[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* (1969). It separates a
symbol's meaning into three distinct **levels** or **fields**, so that agreement and — more
tellingly — *disagreement* between them can be analyzed rather than smoothed over.

## Procedure

Turner's three levels (attributed):

1. **Exegetical meaning** — the indigenous interpretation elicited by questioning informants.
   Turner distinguishes esoteric (specialist) from exoteric (lay) explanations, and personal
   idiosyncratic from representative views; he treats even folk ("fictitious") etymologies as
   valid data because "for the people themselves it constitutes part of the 'explanation'."
2. **Operational meaning** — the symbol's meaning equated with its *use*: what Ndembu actually
   do with it, who handles it, the composition and affective tone of the group acting, who is
   excluded and why. This level "has the most bearing on problems of social dynamics."
3. **Positional meaning** — the symbol's meaning derived from its relations to *other* symbols
   within the total system (a Gestalt); tied directly to multivocality, since context
   determines which of a [[dominant-symbol|dominant symbol]]'s many senses becomes paramount
   (a symbol multivocal in isolation becomes univocal within a single binary opposition).

Turner insists on **keeping observation and interpretation methodologically distinct**, and on
the analyst's licence to go *beyond* native exegesis: contextual analysis of behavior can
reveal meanings and conflicts that informants cannot or will not verbalize — most famously the
Ndembu **milk tree** (*mudyi*), whose *operational* meaning (women mobilized against men, kin
against rival villages) contradicts its *exegetical* meaning of matrilineal harmony and unity.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- The **positional** level is, in Turner's words, "largely worked out by the anthropologist" —
  the most interpretation-laden and least checkable step.
- **Multivocality** is analytically powerful but makes readings hard to falsify.
- The method presupposes a culture rich in verbalized exegesis; Turner notes the Ndembu have
  "a paucity of myths," which is *why* he works up from symbols rather than down from cosmology.
- Turner marks a disciplinary limit: the analyst may interpret the ideological pole and social
  behavior, but not "the precise sources of unconscious feeling" — that he leaves to
  psychology, treating the sensory pole as a constant and the social meanings as variables.

## History

Turner credits [[wilson-monica|Monica Wilson]]'s principle that "any analysis not based on
some translation of the symbols used by people of that culture is open to suspicion" as
prompting his systematic collection of exegetical texts. He develops the method *against*
Nadel and Wilson (who restricted analysis to conscious native interpretation) and *against*
psychoanalytic interpreters (who dismissed native exegesis as rationalization) — arguing both
are one-sided. The whole approach is framed within Lewin's field theory (the symbol as a force
in an activity field) and the [[manchester-school|Manchester School]]'s processualism.

## Exemplary Applications

- The Ndembu color triad (white/red/black) and the *mudyi* milk tree ([[dominant-symbol]]).
- The Isoma and Wubwang'u rituals ([[ndembu]]); the Ihembi cure read as [[social-drama]].

## Debates

- Turner's exegetical–operational gap (native says harmony; behavior shows conflict) is a
  standing methodological argument for treating what people say and what they do as different
  data — an anthropological parallel to the survey "self-report ≠ behavior" caution.
