## What It Does

The typological method classifies artifacts into **types** — recurrent forms grouped by
shared features — and arranges those types into developmental series, giving material
culture diagnostic and chronological value and making assemblages comparable across sites.
Borrowed from biological taxonomy, it underpins **[[seriation]]** (which orders assemblages
by the changing frequencies of types). [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]] credits its systematic
introduction into excavation to [[petrie-flinders|Petrie]] (*Diospolis Parva*, 1901), noting
that typology "scarcely entered into our discipline until Petrie" (attributed, Wheeler
1954); [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt Rivers]] had earlier arranged museum collections on
typological-developmental principles.

## Procedure

Diagnostic classes of material — above all **pottery**, "the alphabet of archaeology"
(attributed, Wheeler 1954), and coins as precise "date-indicators" — are sorted by form,
fabric, and manufacture into types, which are then compared within and across sites and, for
dating, ordered developmentally. In practice this is combined with **corpus / index-series**
publication (Petrie's method of standardizing recurrent types into referenceable catalogue
numbers) so that assemblages can be cited rather than redrawn in full.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Wheeler flags two characteristic failure modes. First, **independent recurrence**:
over-crediting diagnostic significance to a form that arises independently in unrelated times
and places. Second, **superficial matching** — reducing comparative typology to
"philately"-like pattern-matching that attends to outline while ignoring paste, manufacture,
and firing technique; he cited a Tell en-Nasbeh study whose mechanical pot-type statistics
absurdly spread one tomb across the 17th–2nd centuries BCE. He added that **architectural
typology** (moulding styles, brick thickness, masonry) is "more often correct than not, if
cautiously used," but that its values are "liable to be local rather than universal" —
rusticated masonry dated to the 1st c. CE in Rome occurs as early as the 9th c. BCE in
Palestine (attributed, Wheeler 1954). Typologies are analyst-imposed constructs; they order
form, not necessarily time, and require stratigraphic or absolute anchoring.

## History

Rooted in 19th-century evolutionary museum arrangement ([[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt
Rivers]]) and systematized as an excavation dating tool by [[petrie-flinders|Petrie]] (1901),
typology became central to **[[culture-historical-archaeology]]**'s definition of
[[archaeological-culture|archaeological cultures]] by recurrent type-assemblages. The rise of
[[radiocarbon-dating]] displaced typology as the primary *absolute* chronometer while leaving
it central to classification and comparison.

## Exemplary Applications

Petrie's Egyptian ceramic series; the Mattingly–Sydenham Roman coin corpus, which let one
report compress 1,668 coins into 11 pages where a pre-corpus report needed 80 pages for 1,000
(finding, Wheeler 1954); Wheeler's rouletted-ware typology linking South Indian sites.

## Evolutionary Typology and the Concept of the Type (Lucas 2002)

[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]] (*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches
to Fieldwork]]*, 2002) treats typology historically and critically (attributed). He traces
the **material basis** of classification to Ole Worm's 17th-century Cabinet of Curiosities
(objects grouped by material — clay, amber, stone, metal — collapsing the artificialia/
naturalia line), carried into Christian Thomsen's **Three Age System** (objects grouped by
function, then seriated). **Evolutionary typology** peaked with [[pitt-rivers-augustus|Pitt
Rivers]] (explicit analogies to biological genera/species/varieties) and its "master
synthesiser" Oscar Montelius (a six-period Bronze Age, 1885), against Sophus Müller's
counter-argument that typology could not be prior to contextual find-association. Lucas's key
point: evolutionary typology was **explanatory, not merely chronological** — it carried a
whole philosophy of prehistory — which is why context was subordinated to it even as the
discipline claimed context as its distinguishing concern.

Lucas frames the mid-20th-century **typological debates** as being, at bottom, about the
emic/etic status of the type:

- **Culture-history (Childe):** types are objective, socially shared mental templates — "in
  the heads of a society," not the maker's alone.
- **Taylor (1948) / Steward & Setzler (1938):** types should match the actual (emic)
  cultural categories of the society studied. *[[taylor-study-of-archeology-1948|A Study of Archeology]]* (primary-ingested) elaborates: **empirical types** (descriptive groupings of forms) are not automatically **cultural types**; type vs class as primary vs secondary grouping; "more interpretation not less"; quantification can reverse presence/absence similarity judgments (textile industries example). See [[conjunctive-approach]], [[cultural-context-archaeology]].
- **The Ford–Spaulding debate:** James Ford held statistically-derived types are "accidents
  of sampling" reflecting the archaeologist's abstractions; Albert Spaulding (1953) used
  chi-squared testing of attribute combinations to show non-random (culturally meaningful)
  patterning within a single assemblage — with the consequence that multiple valid
  typologies may exist for one community. (Lucas: "time has sided with Spaulding.")
- **The function/style debate:** New Archaeology treated function as adaptive/central and
  style as evolutionarily neutral, residual variability; Sackett's **isochrestic** style
  ("function writ small") vs. Wiessner's active/communicative (iconological) style; Hodder
  (1985) later reframed function/style as a **dialectic** read through context.

On the statistical methods themselves, Lucas argues **attribute-association** techniques
(monothetic, struggling with residuals) and **object-clustering** techniques (polythetic,
unable to fix where clustering is significant) share an **essentialist tendency** that
collapses the individual/type distinction; and that a typology's success depends on the
**degree of standardisation in production** — highly standardised traditions (Scandinavian
Bronze Age metalwork) yield stable typologies, while context-specific ones (British Beaker
pottery, where David Clarke's 1970 statistical typology failed against radiocarbon) resist
classification regardless of method. He also notes the French **chaîne opératoire**
(operational-chain) tradition (Leroi-Gourhan, Lemonnier) as an alternative that reconstructs
technical choices in production rather than classifying finished form.

## Evolution vs. Devolution (Clark 1957)

[[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] (*[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and
Society]]*, 1957) stresses that typological series can run by **degeneration** as well as
elaboration. Sir John Evans traced native British and Gaulish coinage *degenerating* from
Philip II of Macedon's stater — a naturalistic head, chariot, and horse progressively
abstracted into dots and strokes — and noted he had applied "the principles of 'evolution'
and 'natural selection' to numismatic inquiries" about a decade before *Origin of Species*
(attributed). [[petrie-flinders|Petrie]]'s predynastic **Sequence Dates** used the same
degeneration principle. Clark also warns that *direction of development is itself
contestable*: the western-European megalithic tomb series, long read as indigenous
elaboration, is one where "modern opinion is almost unanimous in reversing this apparent
sequence" — a worked case of "basing broad conclusions on local observations." His rule of
thumb: traits expressing choice and style are more reliable cultural indicators than those
controlled by ecological or economic function.

## Debates

Whether type-frequency methods can bear chronological weight without attention to fabric and
manufacture is the recurring critique above; whether types are emic cultural categories or
etic analyst constructs is the Ford–Spaulding / function–style debate (Lucas 2002); the
reliability of typology-based cultural identities is treated under [[archaeological-culture]].

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook synthesis (2012)

[[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]] and [[bahn-paul|Paul Bahn]] (*[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]*, 6th ed. 2012, Ch. 4) treat typology as a core **[[relative-dating]]** tool alongside stratigraphy of sealed deposits. In their chapter summary, placing things into sequence is the first and often most important step; typological sequencing rests on recognizable style and gradual evolutionary change (attributed positions).

### Definition and two underpinning ideas

They define **form** by attributes of material, shape, and decoration; several pots with the same attributes constitute a pot type, and **typology groups artifacts into such types** for relative dating (attributed). Underlying typological relative dating are two ideas: (1) products of a given period and place have a **recognizable style** characteristic of the producing society; (2) change in style (shape and decoration) is often **gradual or evolutionary**—an idea drawn from Darwinian evolution and used by 19th-century archaeologists with the rule **“like goes with like”** (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

When studying a series of artifacts of unknown date (e.g. daggers), it is logical first to arrange them so that the most closely similar sit beside each other—likely the true chronological sequence because it best reflects that rule; **rate of change still requires absolute methods** (attributed).

### Practice: ceramic backbones and differential rates of change

For many purposes the best relative date for an artifact is to match it to one already recognized in a well-established typological system. **Pottery typologies usually form the backbone of regional chronological systems** (e.g. Hohokam bowl styles, American Southwest, ~500-year sequence with phase dates as they report) (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

Different attributes and artifact classes change at different rates: with pottery, **surface decoration** often changes most rapidly (often over periods of just a few decades) and is therefore the best attribute for a typological sequence, while vessel shape may be constrained by function for centuries; metal weapons/tools may change rapidly and be useful indicators, whereas stone tools such as hand-axes change slowly and rarely make useful short-term chronological indicators (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

### Relation to seriation, cross-dating, and absolute methods

[[seriation]] extends typology by ordering *assemblages* via type frequencies (battleship curves). Outside historic and literate lands, Renfrew & Bahn state that cross-dating and broad typological comparisons have been **almost entirely superseded** by scientifically based absolute methods for assigning absolute dates—though typology remains essential for relative order, classification, and linking finds to regional sequences (attributed position, 2012).
