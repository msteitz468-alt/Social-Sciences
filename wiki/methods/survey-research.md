# Survey Research

Standardized questioning of samples (or panels) for attitudes and reported behaviors. In *[[putnam-bowling-alone-2000|Bowling Alone]]*, Putnam relies heavily on repeated cross-sections (Roper Social and Political Trends, GSS, DDB Needham Life Style, and others) as one evidence class among membership and time-use series.

## What It Does

Produces comparable indicators across persons and (when repeated) over time.

## Procedure

Sampling → instrument → fielding → weighting → analysis. Detail varies by archive. Causal analysis of survey (and other observational) data often proceeds by multivariate [[control-variables|controls]] meant to simulate experimental isolation — the practice [[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]] (*[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]*, 1985) called molding nonexperimental data into a **mock experiment**.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Self-report gap; mode effects; civic "good citizen" response bias. When survey results are used for causal inference without random assignment, [[selectivity]] (nonrandom sorting into conditions) and unmeasured selectivity *within* control categories threaten the experimental analogue (Lieberson, attributed). Availability of survey measurements is itself socially conditioned — Lieberson's "social theory of data" (e.g., sexual-mores surveys more feasible when mores are looser).

## History

Gallup, Roper, academic omnibus surveys; postwar expansion. Mid-1980s internal critique of quasi-experimental analysis of survey/observational data: *[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]*.

## Exemplary Applications

[[putnam-bowling-alone-study]]; earlier political sociology (e.g. [[lipset-political-man-study]]); [[bourdieu-distinction-study]] (class-fraction oversample + multi-domain lifestyle questionnaire + secondary national surveys); [[portes-rumbaut-legacies-study]] / [[children-of-immigrants-longitudinal-study|CILS]] (school-based longitudinal panel of second-generation youth + parental survey).
