## What It Does

Uranium-series (U-series) dating comprises a family of methods based on the decay of the parent isotopes 238U, 235U, and 232Th through intermediate daughters toward stable lead isotopes. Dating exploits the point at which a sample is transferred from one geochemical system to another in a way that "zeroes" the decay clock.

## Procedure

Uranium is highly soluble in water, while its decay products 230Th and 231Pa are not; the daughter isotopes precipitate out of solution into sediments as uranium-bearing water evaporates or reacts. This makes the method applicable to sea- and lake-floor sediments, corals, shells, and inorganic carbonate deposits such as speleothems and travertines, where the initial precipitation event resets the clock. The 231Pa/235U ratio permits dating to roughly 150,000 years and the 230Th/234U ratio to roughly 350,000 years, together filling the range beyond radiocarbon's practical limit and up near radiopotassium's practical lower limit.

## Assumptions and Limitations

U-series dating of bone is unreliable: fresh bone contains only about 0.1 ppm uranium, while fossil bone can contain 10–1,000 ppm, showing that bone absorbs uranium unevenly from its burial environment over time rather than in a single dateable event. This produces discordant U-series ages among bones from the same layer, and such dates should be treated critically. The method works best on materials — corals, speleothems, some shells — where uranium incorporation happened essentially once, at formation.

## History

Fossil corals have given concordant U-series dates of 125 ± 10 ky for a high sea level correlated with the Last Interglacial. High-precision U-series dates on coral also showed that conventional radiocarbon dates systematically underestimate true calendar age, enabling calibration of the radiocarbon timescale out to 22,000 years and beyond (see [[radiocarbon-dating]]).

## Exemplary Applications

U-series dating of the 17-meter travertine sequence at Abric Romani (Barcelona) yielded more than 20 stratigraphically concordant dates ranging from 70,000 years at the base to 40,000 years at the top, placing the earliest Upper Paleolithic there at roughly 43,000 years ago — older than AMS radiocarbon dates (39,000–37,000 years) from other Spanish sites, illustrating radiocarbon's likely underestimation in the 40,000–35,000-year range. Discussed in Klein, *The Human Career* ([[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Debates

The Abric Romani discrepancy is cited by Klein (1999) as evidence that AMS radiocarbon dates in the 35,000–40,000-year range likely understate true calendar age by 3,000 years or more, a caution relevant to any Middle-to-Upper-Paleolithic transition chronology built on radiocarbon alone.

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Source: [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]]; as of Stringer 2012.*

**Principle (as Stringer presents).** Radioactive decay of uranium forms; daughter products measured in **stalagmites and corals** (sealed systems) — useful in caves and for sea-level/coral–radiocarbon cross-checks. **Bone U-series is difficult**: bone remains open to U gain/loss, so its physical clock can run erratically — progress ongoing. Coupling ESR with U-series on the same tooth enamel addresses uranium-uptake unknowns in ESR dose models.

**Applications he reports.** Coral terraces dated by both radiocarbon and U-series as independent cross-checks. Moroccan/Algerian MP *Nassarius* bead sites dated by luminescence or U-series to ~80–100 ka. **Iwo Eleru** (Nigeria) skeleton: direct U-series age estimate unlikely older than ~20 ka, yet cranial shape closest to late archaic Africans (Ngaloba, Irhoud, Omo 2) — archaic-looking people after modern morphology appeared (Stringer et al. as of 2012). New ESR/U-series work on **Broken Hill** undercuts the classic ~500 ka age (see [[electron-spin-resonance-dating]], [[homo-heidelbergensis]]).
