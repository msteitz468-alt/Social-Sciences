# Bioarchaeology

Study of **human remains from archaeological sites** — skeletons, mummies, bog bodies, and associated biomolecules — to reconstruct individual lives and population patterns. As presented in [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Renfrew & Bahn 2012]] (Ch. 11), the modern archaeologist’s role is not to replace the specialist osteologist or geneticist but to **integrate** specialist biological data into archaeological interpretation of past lives.

## What It Does

Bioarchaeology builds a **biological profile** (primarily age, sex, stature, and ancestry) and extends it with evidence of health, trauma, activity, nutrition, relatedness, mobility, and (where soft tissue or DNA survive) appearance, pathogens, and genetic history. Soft-tissue bodies (mummified, desiccated, freeze-dried, peat-preserved) can preserve hair, skin color, tattoos, scars, and even fingerprints that skeletons cannot. The field also underwrites **paleopathology**, **paleodemography**, **forensic archaeology** of mass graves and crime scenes, and joint work with [[ancient-dna]] and [[isotope-analysis]].

## Procedure

Per Renfrew & Bahn 2012 (attributed textbook synthesis):

1. **Context and recovery.** Record mortuary context, associations, and taphonomy before and during lift; forensic-style protocols apply where recent death or legal issues are possible.
2. **Inventory and osteology.** Identify elements; assess completeness, preservation, and non-metric traits.
3. **Sex estimation.** Best skeletal indicator is **pelvic shape** (dimorphism varies by population); males tend more robust with cranial differences; single measures capture size more than shape; multivariate analyses ~85% accuracy on modern blind tests. Subadults are hard to sex osteologically; **DNA** can sex fragmentary or infant remains (e.g. Ashkelon neonate femurs: 14 male, 5 female of 19 successful tests, as reported by the textbook).
4. **Age at death.** Usually only **biological age categories** (young, adult, old) are secure, not exact years. Indicators: dental calcification/eruption/wear, epiphyseal fusion, cranial sutures (unreliable alone), bone microstructure (osteons), aspartic-acid racemization in dentine. Spitalfields crypt tests (known-age burials) showed traditional methods **underestimate** ages of the old and **overestimate** the young.
5. **Stature.** Long-bone regression equations; **population-specific** formulas because body proportions differ (e.g. Aboriginal/African legs ~54% of stature vs some Asian ~45%). When source population is unknown, a mean femoral equation is often adequate to within ~5 cm for archaeological purposes (per textbook).
6. **Paleopathology and trauma.** Record lesions, healed fractures, intentional modification (cranial shaping, scalping cutmarks); population-level injury patterns matter more than single dramatic wounds. Soft tissue, parasites, and pathogen DNA address infections that leave no bone trace.
7. **Imaging.** Non-destructive methods: X-ray, xeroradiography, CT/helical CT (wrapped mummies), limited MRI on desiccated tissue, fiber-optic endoscopy for sampling.
8. **Molecular and chemical extensions.** [[ancient-dna]] for kinship, sex, pathogens, and population history; [[isotope-analysis]] (C, N, Sr, O) for diet and mobility; facial reconstruction and photo-superimposition as interpretive aids, not proofs.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Cemetery samples ≠ living populations.** Renfrew & Bahn warn that life-expectancy figures from excavated cemeteries require unrealistic assumptions (everyone buried there regardless of age/sex/status; nobody died elsewhere; no reuse). Treat published paleodemographic rates critically.
- **Death rarely readable from bone.** Most fatal afflictions leave no skeletal signature; paleopathology “tells us far more about life than about death” (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, attributed).
- **Race and ancestry.** Postwar reluctance to integrate archaeology and biological anthropology reflected misuse of skeletal typology to “prove” white superiority and deny indigenous monument-building (eastern US mounds; 1970s Rhodesian claims about Great Zimbabwe). Today’s biological anthropologists, per the textbook, are less willing to recognize populations from few skeletal measurements without robust statistics. Identity is social; ethnicity is not reducible to haplogroups (Ch. 11 “Questions of Identity”).
- **Cannibalism and sensational claims.** Following better taphonomy and funerary knowledge (and Arens’s ethnographic critique), most prehistoric cannibalism claims are ambiguous; cutmarks may reflect secondary burial (e.g. Krapina reanalyzed as cleaning for reburial). Starvation cannibalism may occur; “custom cannibalism” is hard to prove.
- **Ethics and repatriation.** Analysis can conflict with descendant obligations; NAGPRA and related regimes constrain retention and study (see [[heritage-management]], [[public-archaeology]]).

## History

- **Term.** “Bioarchaeology” was first coined in the 1970s by [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] for the study of **animal bones**; it has since been adopted for **human remains** from sites (though in the Old World the term still sometimes covers other organic material) — Renfrew & Bahn 2012.
- **Postwar fracture.** Race controversies delayed archaeology–biological anthropology integration after WWII.
- **aDNA era.** Svante Pääbo’s 1985 extraction of mtDNA from a 2400-year-old Egyptian mummy, PCR from bone/tooth DNA, Neanderthal mtDNA (1997), and the ~2010 draft Neanderthal genome open a “new era in biological anthropology” (textbook narrative).
- **Forensic archaeology** emerges as a sub-discipline for murder victims and mass burials (e.g. Rwanda, former Yugoslavia).

## Exemplary Applications

(As reported/synthesized in Renfrew & Bahn 2012 unless noted.)

| Case | Leverage |
|---|---|
| **Eulau** (Corded Ware, Germany, ~4600 BP) | aDNA: father, mother, two sons (earliest genetic nuclear family); Sr isotopes: local men/children, non-local women (exogamous/patrilocal) |
| **Lindow Man** (Iron Age/Roman Cheshire) | Multi-method bog body: age, diet, parasites, violent death sequence |
| **Georgia coast** (Larsen) | Hunter-gatherer vs maize agricultural skeletons: dental decline, stress lesions, greater female impacts |
| **Spitalfields crypt** | Known-age validation of aging methods |
| **Laetoli footprints** + Lucy debates | Bipedalism from prints, postcrania, inner-ear morphology |
| **Peruvian mummy TB DNA** (Aufderheide et al.) | Pathogen aDNA: TB pre-dates European contact in Americas |
| **Shanidar Neanderthal** | Survival with severe injury → possible community care |
| **Trepanation corpus** | >1000 cases worldwide; many healed; medical skill deep in prehistory |

## Debates

- **Out of Africa vs multiregional** modern-human origins (mtDNA “Eve,” Y-chromosome, Neanderthal admixture 1–4% into non-Africans as of 2010 draft genome).
- **Gene–language correlations** (Cavalli-Sforza et al.; macrofamilies such as Amerind/Nostratic treated with caution by the textbook).
- **Speech capacity of Neanderthals** (vocal tract, hyoid, FOXP2, hypoglossal canal — unresolved).
- **Repatriation vs research** (NAGPRA, Kennewick Man, Australian deep-time reburial) — ethical, not merely technical.
- **Cannibalism vs funerary processing** (Gran Dolina vs Sima de los Huesos / secondary burial readings).
