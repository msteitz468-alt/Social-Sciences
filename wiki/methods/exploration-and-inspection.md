# Exploration and Inspection

## What It Does

[[blumer-herbert|Herbert Blumer]]'s pair of procedures for the **direct naturalistic examination** of the empirical social world, set out in *[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Symbolic Interactionism]]* (1969), Ch. 1. Together they define the methodological stance of [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]]: inquiry must respect the nature of human group life as ongoing, interpretive interaction rather than forcing it into pre-mapped variable protocols.

- **Exploration** — flexible inquiry that simultaneously (1) builds close, comprehensive acquaintance with a sphere of social life and (2) develops and sharpens the research problem, data, relations, and concepts from that sphere.
- **Inspection** — intensive, focused examination of the empirical instances of the analytic elements the scholar has identified, to clarify their nature and relations.

## Procedure

Summary resolution (wiki, not manual), attributed to Blumer 1969:

### Exploration

1. Begin with a broad focus; allow problem, relevant data, and conceptual tools to sharpen as acquaintance grows.
2. Use any ethically allowable procedure that clarifies what is going on: observation, interviewing, listening to conversations, life histories, letters and diaries, public records, group discussions, counts when useful.
3. Seek **acute, well-informed participants**; Blumer argues a small number of such informants (especially as a discussion/resource group probing disagreements) outweighs a large unobservant sample.
4. Continuously test and revise images and conceptions of the area under study.

### Inspection

1. Take the analytic elements emerging from exploration.
2. Examine empirical instances of each element intensively and comparatively.
3. Clarify the nature of the element and how it operates in the process under study — not merely to classify cases under fixed codes.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Assumes** that the empirical social world is known through respectful close contact, not solely through remote indicators or pre-fixed variables.
- **Assumes** that meaning and process are central; designs that bypass actors' meanings falsify the object of study (Blumer's standing charge against much variable analysis and polling).
- **Limitations:** hard to standardize; weak fit with protocols that require fixed problems and techniques in advance; vulnerable to charges of soft method; depends on researcher judgment and informant quality.
- Related but distinct: [[participant-observation]] (role-based presence); [[personal-documents-method]] (documentary arm of Chicago inquiry).

## History

Blumer presents exploration/inspection as the methodological counterpart of the SI perspective, continuous with Chicago naturalistic study and opposed to mid-century "current scientific protocol" that demands prestructured problems and techniques. The 1969 essay is the clear published statement; earlier Chicago practice is the living antecedent.

## Exemplary Applications

- Programmatic statement: *[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Blumer 1969]]* Ch. 1.
- Downstream ethnography in the SI/Chicago lineage (e.g. [[goffman-asylums-study|Goffman *Asylums*]]) embodies naturalistic close examination though not under Blumer's exact labels — treat as related practice, not as Blumer's own applications.

## Debates

- SI naturalistic method vs. variable analysis / survey protocol (Blumer essays on the "variable," social theory, and public-opinion polling).
- Soft-method charge vs. Blumer's claim that exploration/inspection are rigorously demanding.
- Relation to later grounded theory and qualitative methodology (not yet ingested as primary sources).
