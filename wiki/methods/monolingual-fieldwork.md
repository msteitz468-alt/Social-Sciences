# Monolingual fieldwork

**Monolingual fieldwork** is the description of a language carried out when the researcher and the speakers **share no common contact language**. The analyst cannot ask for a translation or an explanation in any bridge language; meaning must be built up from ostension, context, and immediate testing against other speakers. It is closely associated with the Summer Institute of Linguistics descriptive tradition and, in the ingested source, with [[everett-daniel|Everett]]'s work on [[piraha|Pirahã]] — "very rare among the languages of the world," in his phrase.

> Note: a commonly-assumed link to Kenneth Pike's "monolingual demonstration" is **not** documented in the ingested source and is not asserted here; Everett names only generic SIL field-linguistics training.

## What It Does

It provides a route into an unwritten, previously undescribed language in a genuinely monolingual setting — where bilingual elicitation is impossible — yielding phonology, lexicon, and grammar from first contact onward. It is the extreme case of descriptive field linguistics and overlaps heavily with anthropological [[participant-observation]]: long residence and immersion are prerequisites, not optional.

## Procedure (as practiced by Everett)

- **Ostension and immediate cross-speaker testing.** Point at objects/actions, elicit the native response, then test that response with other speakers before trusting it.
- **IPA transcription from the first encounter**, building a phonemic analysis inductively (Everett built on Heinrichs/Sheldon's prior finding of ~11 phonemes and OV order).
- **Index-card vocabulary system.** Phonetic transcription + context + likely meaning on hole-punched cards on a belt-loop ring, target ~10 new items/day, organized by semantic field.
- **Discovery by contrast.** Grammatical categories (e.g. an **evidentiality** marker distinguishing eyewitness from non-eyewitness assertion in otherwise-identical sentences) surface from minimal-pair comparison rather than from informants' metalinguistic explanation.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method's great hazard is **imposing the analyst's categories**: Everett repeatedly warns that his English glosses can add structure (e.g. relative-clause embedding, [[recursion]]) that the original lacks — a caution that is itself central to the [[piraha-recursion-debate]]. Working alone in a monolingual setting produces **single-observer corpora** that are difficult to audit or replicate; results depend on rapport and on a handful of skilled language teachers (for Everett, chiefly Kóhoi). Tone, tiny phoneme inventories, and unusual features compound the difficulty.

## History

Everett notes methods for monolingual work "were mostly around long before I came on the scene," in the SIL/descriptivist tradition; he says he "helped develop some of those methods as a result of my ordeal" with Pirahã. The approach sits in the inductive, field-based North American lineage ([[boas-franz|Boas]], [[sapir-edward|Sapir]]) that Everett contrasts with [[chomsky-noam|Chomsky]]'s deductive, non-fieldwork method.
