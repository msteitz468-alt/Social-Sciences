# Militant Anthropology (engaged / companheira ethnography)

## What It Does

Militant anthropology is [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]]'s name for an explicitly engaged,
politically-committed practice of ethnography — the anthropologist as *companheira* ("comrade") and witness
rather than detached observer. In *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death Without Weeping]]* she
calls it *antropologia-pé-no-chão* ("anthropology with one's feet on the ground") and frames anthropology as
simultaneously "a field of knowledge (a disciplinary field)" and "a field of action (a force field)," with
ethnographic writing itself "a site of resistance." Its programmatic statement is her manifesto "The Primacy
of the Ethical: Propositions for a Militant Anthropology" (*Current Anthropology*, 1995).

## Procedure

The practice combines conventional [[participant-observation]] with active partisanship: living within and
"accompanying" the community's *luta* (struggle), serving practical roles (Scheper-Hughes acted as the
[[alto-do-cruzeiro|Alto]]'s *despachante* and helped run its base community), and developing analysis
collaboratively through Freire's *conscientização*. The ethnographer writes herself into the text with
declared moral and political sympathies, refusing the "invisible, objective ethnographer-narrator" of classic
ethnography. Scheper-Hughes grounds the stance in Levinas's ethics (ethics as prior to culture) and models it
on [[taussig-michael|Taussig]]'s "writing against terror," Basaglia's "negative worker," and de Certeau's
*la perruque*.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method assumes that taking sides yields access and moral insight unavailable to the detached observer —
and that neutrality is itself a political choice, usually on the side of the powerful. Its characteristic
risks are the mirror image: loss of analytic distance and comparability, advocacy overriding evidence, and
the difficulty of holding informants accountable while championing them (Scheper-Hughes insists "with agency
begin responsibility and accountability," refusing to reduce the poor to pure victims). She positions her own
practice as a "middle ground" between Freire's "culture of silence" and Fanon's optimism, and between Carol
Stack's "survival strategies" and Oscar Lewis's "culture of poverty."

## History

The stance crystallized out of the reflexive, post–*Writing Culture* moment and Scheper-Hughes's own biography
(she came to the Alto first as a Peace Corps activist, not a scholar). Its 1995 *Current Anthropology*
formulation provoked the discipline's defining exchange on advocacy vs. objectivity.

## Exemplary Applications

*[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-study]]* is the method's exemplar; her later Organs Watch investigation
of the global organ trade extends it into activist research.

**Organs Watch as hybrid ethnography / surveillance / human rights.** In "Commodity Fetishism in Organs
Trafficking" (*[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]*, 2001/2002),
Scheper-Hughes states (attributed) that the Organs Watch project blends ethnography, fact-finding,
documentation/surveillance, and human-rights work — transgressing boundaries among anthropology, political
journalism, scientific report, moral philosophy, and advocacy. Newer ethnographic engagements, she argues,
require spaces where nothing can be taken for granted and a **"hermeneutics of suspicion"** replaces earlier
modes of bracketing, moral relativism, and suspension of disbelief. The program aims to pierce gift/altruism
rhetoric around the [[human-organs-trade]] and to establish a social-justice "ethical blueprint" for
anthropology and medicine (including the demand that the "gift of life" not become a "theft of life"). See
also [[multi-sited-ethnography]], [[body-commodification]], [[critical-medical-anthropology]].

## Debates

The method is the subject of a standing dispute over whether anthropology should be a moral-political or a
scientific-objective enterprise — see [[militant-anthropology-debate]].
