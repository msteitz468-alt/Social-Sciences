# Numerical Taxonomy

Quantitative classification methods (similarity measures, clustering, phenograms) developed in biology and imported into archaeology during the New Archaeology. [[clarke-david-l|Clarke]]'s *[[clarke-analytical-archaeology-1978|Analytical Archaeology]]* is a principal archaeological vehicle: polythetic taxa at every hierarchical level, phenon lines for rank comparison, and explicit affinity thresholds for type/culture/technocomplex talk.

## What It Does

Replaces (or disciplines) intuitive typology with measured overall similarity among entities described by multi-state attributes. Supports **phenetic** grouping (overall affinity) while remaining silent, by itself, on phylogenetic interpretation.

## Procedure

*(Summary resolution.)*

1. Define operational attributes/characters and states for the population (artefacts, assemblages, etc.).
2. Code a data matrix (entities × characters).
3. Compute pairwise similarities/distances with a chosen coefficient.
4. Cluster (hierarchical or other) and cut phenon lines for rank comparison (type group / type / subtype, etc.).
5. Interpret clusters against time, space, and independent controls — do **not** equate high affinity with descent automatically.

Clarke stressed comparison via numerical taxonomy at all hierarchy levels, and warned that cultural phylogeny includes branch convergence and fusion, so affinity ≠ simple ancestry (fig. 28 framing).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Attributes are observer-selected; "objective" matrices inherit those choices.
- Polythetic ideal is approximate (functional nuclei create near-universal attributes).
- High affinity + shared idiosyncratic correlations strengthen transformation hypotheses but do not prove them.
- Computer output can create false confidence without research design (Clarke's broader polemic against technique-without-theory).

## History

- **Biology:** Sokal & Sneath *Principles of Numerical Taxonomy* (1963) — mono-/polythetic aggregates, phenetics.
- **Archaeology:** 1960s–70s uptake with computers; Clarke 1968 as theoretical charter; Doran & Hodson 1975 as methods handbook (Chapman cites as superseding first-edition methods chapters).
- **Relation to seriation and multivariate methods:** adjacent quantitative toolkit of [[processual-archaeology]].

## Exemplary Applications

- [[clarke-analytical-archaeology-study|Analytical Archaeology]] illustrations (Beaker attributes, Hallstatt swords, Acheulean, etc.).
- Broader New Archaeology clustering of ceramics, assemblages, and site data (*[[binford-binford-new-perspectives-1968|New Perspectives]]* generation).

## Debates

- Phenetic vs phyletic interpretation of clusters.
- Whether classification-first programs under-serve hypothesis-testing process archaeology (Binford-side tensions).
- Post-processual suspicion of quantification as false neutrality — method remains useful when attributes and questions are explicit.
