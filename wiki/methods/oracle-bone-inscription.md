# Oracle-Bone Inscription

## What It Does

**Oracle-bone inscription** (*chia-ku-wen*) is the practice of writing divinatory charges, prognoses, and verifications on prepared animal bones and turtle shells used in Shang royal scapulimancy/plastromancy. As reconstructed by [[chang-kwang-chih|K. C. Chang]] (*[[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Shang Civilization]]*, 1980), the corpus is the primary **internal** textual door into late Shang political order — complementary to archaeology, later classical texts, and bronze inscriptions.

## Procedure

Bones (usually cattle scapulae) or turtle plastrons were cleaned, hollowed, heated until cracks formed, and inscribed. Diviners (*chen-jen*) conducted the rite under royal authority; inscriptions record day-signs, the charge (what was asked), sometimes the king's prognosis, and occasionally a later verification. Modern study recovers pieces from looted market contexts (from 1899) and from stratified excavation at [[anyang|Anyang (Yinxu)]], notably pit H-127 and other storage/dump contexts.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Elite filter.** Almost all surviving charges are royal; commoner and non-Shang perspectives are largely absent.
- **Not a complete archive.** Survival is patchy; publication and periodization (Tung Tso-pin's five periods; disputes over Period I vs IV *chen-jen* assignments) shape what can be counted.
- **Language ≠ people.** Chang (1980) treats the oracle language as the earliest stage of Chinese writing/speech continuous with later Chinese, while keeping skeletal/physical typology and polity identity as separate evidence classes (four-way non-identity).
- **Chronology.** Absolute calendar anchors (e.g. Wu Ting lunar eclipses) depend on day-boundary assumptions and remain disputed "(as of Chang 1980)."

## History

Antiquarian recognition of inscribed bones dates to 1899; the Institute of History and Philology ([[li-chi|Li Chi]], Tung Tso-pin, and colleagues) recovered ~100,000 pieces in fifteen seasons at Yin hsü (1928–37). Chang (1980) treats oracle bones as one of his **Five Doors** to Shang, alongside traditional historiography, bronze studies, field archaeology, and theoretical models.

## Exemplary Applications

- Reconstructing the royal genealogy and succession rules at late Shang [[anyang|Anyang]]
- Mapping *fang* polities, hunting grounds, and tribute flows ([[fang-polity]], [[shang-civilization]])
- Relative ceramic/stratigraphic correlation with [[erligang-culture|Erligang]] / Cheng-chou sequences

## Debates

Periodization of *chen-jen* groups; Old School vs New School succession models; whether eclipse records fix absolute dates; the scale of the Shang state inferred from place-name "state scores" (Keightley, as summarized by Chang 1980).
