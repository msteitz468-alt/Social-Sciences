# Hermeneutics

The **science of interpretation** — originally the discipline of recovering the real but hidden meanings of sacred texts, generalised in the 19th–20th centuries (Dilthey, Schleiermacher, Heidegger, Gadamer, Ricoeur) into a method for understanding any object of human thought and action. In the social sciences it is the methodological core of interpretive traditions, and in archaeology it is the explicit foundation of [[contextual-archaeology|contextual]] and [[post-processual-archaeology|post-processual]] archaeology, as set out in *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]]* (Hodder & Hutson, ch. 8).

## What It Does

Hermeneutics treats the world "as an object of human thought and action, rather than a physical system," and so pursues **understanding (Verstehen)** rather than covering-law **explanation**. Its object is meaning — for someone — and its product is a *hybridised* understanding that fuses the horizons of interpreter and subject, identical to neither (Gadamer's "fusion of horizons"). Hodder & Hutson argue that even ostensibly scientific archaeology "follows simple hermeneutic procedures within a stream of changing interpretation," since data are perceived within theory and pattern always requires interpretation.

## Procedure

- **The hermeneutic circle.** "We must understand any detail such as an object or word in terms of the whole, and the whole in terms of the detail" (Gadamer). The interpreter plays back and forth between part (an object; an actor's plan) and whole (the wider context of historical meanings) until they cohere.
- **A second circle** runs between the interpreter's own social/theoretical horizon and the subject's — "we think ourselves into the past," critically and self-awarely (not by empathy or "getting inside another's head").
- **Question and answer** ([[collingwood-r-g|Collingwood]]). "Nothing has meaning except in relation to a question"; questions must be definite ("are these loose stones a ruined wall?") not vague ("let us see what is here"). Answer reshapes the question in an "endless spiral."
- **Wylie's "tacking"** — moving vertically (particular↔general) within both "our" and "their" context and horizontally across the two (the "fourfold hermeneutic," Shanks & Tilley).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Validation rests on two criteria, not testing: **coherence** (the internal coherence of the argument and its fit with the interpreter's picture of the world) and **correspondence** (fit between understanding and the tangible, constraining evidence). Hodder stresses "there is no test for correspondence: it is instead a matter of fitting"; as fit tightens across more cases, "hermeneutic circles become spirals." The method is charged with vicious circularity, with sliding into empathy ("it makes sense to me"), and with opening "the floodgates of relativism"; its defenders answer that data are "real" and constraining (Shanks & Tilley's "networks of resistances"; Latour's "sturdy relativism"), so some interpretations demonstrably fit better than others.

## History

Nineteenth-century biblical and philological hermeneutics (Schleiermacher) was generalised by Dilthey into the method of the human sciences, then ontologised by Heidegger and Gadamer (understanding as a mode of being-in-the-world) and extended to action and text by Ricoeur ([[ricoeur-paul]]). [[weber-max|Weber]]'s *Verstehen* and [[interpretive-anthropology|Geertz's interpretive anthropology]] are cognate. Archaeology imported it in the 1980s through [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]'s contextual programme; Giddens's [[double-hermeneutic|double hermeneutic]] names its distinctive social-scientific twist (interpreters interpreting already-interpreting subjects).

## Exemplary Applications

Collingwood's reconstruction of *why* the Antonine Wall was built cheaply (question-and-answer from the evidence of turf ramparts and timber gates); Hodder's reading of Neolithic tombs as "houses"; the interpretive readings surveyed throughout *[[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]]*.

## Debates

Whether hermeneutic interpretation can be "scientific" is the crux of the [[processual-postprocessual-debate|processual–post-processual debate]]: processualists (Binford) demanded universal, context-independent instruments of measurement; interpretive archaeologists reply that no such instruments exist for cultural processes, and that coherence-and-correspondence fitting is the honest description of what archaeologists actually do.
