# Ethnoarchaeology

Archaeological method that studies **living peoples' material behavior** to build bridging knowledge for interpreting the [[archaeological-record]] — a core strategy of [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|archaeological middle-range theory]].

## What It Does

Documents how activities produce and organize material residues (site structure, discard, butchery, land use) so similar patterns in the archaeological record can be given warranted, testable meanings. Distinct from using ethnography as untested [[ethnographic-analogy|analogy]].

## Procedure

*(As illustrated in *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* 1983 from Nunamiut work.)*

Long-term observation of contemporary communities; mapping of residential and special-purpose sites, activity areas, hearths, and bone/artifact distributions; comparison of site complexes at multiple scales (region → residential core → site complex → site → activity). Example patterns (attributed, Nunamiut mid-20th-c. documentation):

- **Drop zone / toss zone** around hearths (small chips at feet; large refuse tossed behind).
- **Inside vs outside** hearths and houses (size-biased interior drop; exterior door dumps; absence of indoor toss onto beds).
- **Site complexes** for single activities across multiple loci (e.g. Tulugak Lake drives, blinds, caches).
- **Clean-up** strategies when sites will be reused vs abandoned; bulk-processing effects; heat and light constraints on layout.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes some uniformitarian continuity in **process types**, not cultural content. Risk of projecting modern foragers onto Pleistocene hominins. Binford insists Nunamiut lessons do **not** directly prove his Mousterian functional model — they show that traditional assemblage-as-culture conventions cannot cope with documented land-use diversity.

## History

Developed as a New Archaeology response to the analogy and “facts speak” problems; [[binford-lewis]]'s Nunamiut work (fieldwork 1969–1973; *[[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-1978|Nunamiut Ethnoarchaeology]]* 1978; synthesized in *Pursuit* 1983) is a flagship program — see the study page [[binford-nunamiut-ethnoarchaeology-study]]. Related student and comparative work on other foragers appears as control cases in *Pursuit* (e.g. Yellen !Kung ring model contrasted with Nunamiut layout).

**Schiffer / behavioral archaeology.** *[[schiffer-formation-processes-1987|Formation Processes]]* (1987) treats ethnoarchaeology as a primary laboratory for **c-transforms**: Tucson reuse/conservatory collecting (Schiffer, Downing, McCarthy); McKellar residual primary refuse on campus surfaces; Hayden & Cannon Coxoh Maya waste streams; Murray cross-cultural secondary refuse; Stevenson Yukon abandonment modes; Longacre Kalinga breakage/uselife — all cited as principles sources for [[refuse-types]] and discard models (attributed). Same method family as Binford’s middle-range ethnoarchaeology; different theoretical packaging ([[behavioral-archaeology]]).

## Exemplary Applications

[[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] Anavik Springs and Tulugak Lake; Palangana’s house; Mask site men’s camp; early-man waterhole and bone-site actualism; challenges to Mousterian ethnic readings ([[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]]).

## Debates

Whether ethnoarchaeology solves or relocates the analogy problem; relation to [[formation-processes|Schiffer clean-up / Pompeii]] debates; post-processual critiques of positivist bridging ([[processual-postprocessual-debate]]).

## Ethnoarchaeological Case Studies of Meaning (Johnson 2011, attributed)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson]] reports two studies illustrating that
material patterning requires cultural, not just processual, knowledge to interpret:

- **Henrietta Moore — Marakwet gendered space.** Moore's ethnoarchaeological study of the
  Marakwet (East Africa) found that household/compound spatial layout reflected cultural
  gender ideology — specific areas of domestic compounds were culturally coded "male" or
  "female," shaping residence and compound organization (attributed single-study finding).
- **Ian Hodder — Nuba pig-bone distribution.** Hodder found markedly different quantities
  of pig bones across compounds of different Nuba groups, attributed to differing beliefs
  about female "pollution" (women were associated with pig-keeping; where beliefs in
  female pollution were strong, compounds were kept clear of pig remains; where weak,
  refuse was left in place). Hodder argued an archaeologist would need ethnographic
  knowledge of Nuba belief systems to correctly interpret the faunal remains (attributed
  single-study finding).

Johnson notes ethnoarchaeological and experimental work has concentrated especially on
bone [[taphonomy]], stone-tool production, plant remains, and site economy, with a
disciplinary bias toward Palaeolithic and early-farming-period questions and toward
subsistence economy specifically (wiki-voice descriptive claim about disciplinary scope,
attributed to Johnson's synthesis).

Johnson also traces the historical roots of ethnographic analogy in archaeology to
nineteenth-century social-evolutionary thought, which held all societies pass through the
same developmental stages, licensing "primitive" contemporary societies as analogies for
prehistoric ones — citing John Lubbock's 1865 *Prehistoric Times* (subtitled "As
illustrated by ancient remains, and the manners and customs of modern savages") as an
early instance (attributed historiographical claim).

## From Renfrew & Bahn (2012)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]* 6th ed.)* Renfrew & Bahn define ethnoarchaeology as archaeologists living among contemporary communities specifically to understand how societies use material culture and how it becomes incorporated into the [[archaeological-record]] — a New Archaeology response to [[formation-processes]] that made ethnoarchaeology central (e.g. Binford’s Nunamiut butchery studies). Fully fledged ethnoarchaeology is largely a late-20th-century development distinguished by **archaeologists themselves** (not ethnographers) doing the research; crude 19th/early 20th-century “ethnographic parallels” tended to stifle thought.

Their textbook cases include [[binford-lewis|Binford]]’s Nunamiut hearth **drop zone / toss zone** bone patterns (treated as seating-procedure regularities usable for group-size and duration inference, and for reinterpreting Pincevent hearths) and [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]’s Lake Baringo regional work (Tugen, Njemps, Pokot female ear decorations asserting tribal distinctiveness) showing that other material-culture classes need not map the same “ethnic” units — so assemblages cannot unthinkingly be grouped into regional “cultures” as social units. Across environment and subsistence chapters they authorize ethnoarchaeology and [[experimental-archaeology]] as middle-range bridges for crop-processing residues, butchery marks, storage, and site structure, while warning that modern analogy remains an assumption (attributed).


## Insoll 1999 — living Islam as ethnohistoric privilege

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999|The Archaeology of Islam]]*.)* Archaeologists of Islam enjoy multi-disciplinary and **living-religion** advantages (core doctrines relatively stable; anthropology/history/sociology available) but must treat ethnography as hypothesis-generating analogy, not proof of past meanings. Insoll critiques ethnoarchaeological discussions of Muslim nomads that treat architecture without Islam as a structuring principle. Contemporary mosques and domestic adaptations (Cambridge conversions; Bradford terraces) are valid comparative material-culture cases, not anachronistic contaminants.
