# Ethnographic Analogy

Evidence-class Rule 5: **flagged, never smuggled** — hypothesis-generator only, with an explicit bridging argument. Cross-links: [[comparative-method]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]], [[actualistic-studies]].

## What It Does

**Ethnographic analogy** infers properties of past societies from observations of living (or historically documented) societies — technologies, site formation, social organization, or processes of change. Foundational to [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]’s *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* strong form (living “lower tribes” as approximations of earlier stages, warranted by [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]). Distinct from [[ethnoarchaeology]], which studies modern material culture systematically to build middle-range links between dynamics and statics rather than importing culture-content replicas.

## Procedure

A legitimate analogy states: (1) the **source** (documented society), (2) the **subject** (past/archaeological case), and (3) the **bridging relation** (shared environment, technology, subsistence, or demonstrable descent) — and marks the conclusion as a **hypothesis**, not a finding.

Yoffee 2005 (attributed) tiers use by theory level (BLT / MLT / HLT): prefer basic-level technological/formation analogies; reject stage-type social-change analogies.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Living peoples are not timeless relics (ethnographic-present trap).
- Form does not transfer meaning without argument.
- Stage ladders that treat “traditional” societies as **contemporary ancestors** of prehistoric stages are circular ([[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]]; Sahlins 1960 taxonomic program as cited by Yoffee).
- Verification of past dynamics remains archaeological (Binford); analogy inspires, does not prove.

## History

### Classical evolutionist form

Tylor / Lubbock strong form: living “lower” societies *are* the human past wholesale — discredited with unilinear [[social-evolutionism]]. See [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

### *Man the Hunter* (1968) reckoning

Part VI staged positions *(attributed)*: Isaac (calibration); Freeman (minimize); Lewis [[binford-lewis|Binford]] (inspire, never verify); Sally Binford (pre-*sapiens* limits); J. D. Clark (systematic ethnoarchaeology); Deetz (visibility threshold); Howell (skepticism). Seeded processual archaeology and [[ethnoarchaeology]]. See [[hunter-gatherer]], [[kalahari-debate]].

### Grahame Clark (1957)

“Grave error” to equate modern foragers with the human past; ethnography “suggest[s] what to look for” — hypotheses to narrow against specimens (*[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and Society]]*, attributed).

### Formal vs relational (Wylie/Hodder via Johnson 2011)

Formal analogies rest on similarity points; **relational** analogies rest on argued cultural/natural connection (e.g. direct historical method). Attributed via [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].

### Ethnoarchaeology program (Binford 1983)

*[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* operationalizes analogy critique as ethnoarchaeology and middle-range theory: observe **dynamics leaving statics** (e.g. [[nunamiut]]), not culture-content fossils.

### Yoffee 2005 — new rules of the game (attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]* Ch. 8):

- Analogy is inevitable but dangerous; many MLT/HLT social-organization analogies have been “spectacularly unsuccessful.”
- **Comparison ≠ analogy**: developmental-sequence comparison is legitimate world history; stage fossils are not.
- Adjudicate rival models by **parsimony and coverage** of published data (Monte Albán disembedded-capital case).
- Ethnoarchaeology (David & Kramer 2001 as cited) rests on analogical principles — useful for material process, not state-origin stage labels.

## Exemplary Applications

- Nunamiut site structure → middle-range models ([[binford-lewis]]).
- Renfrew megalith/Polynesian monument formal analogies (flagged).
- Yoffee secondary-state analogies (David’s Jerusalem) vs ethnographic stage labels for Monte Albán.
