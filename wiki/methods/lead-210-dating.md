# Lead-210 and Caesium-137 Dating

Short-lived radioisotope dating fixes the chronology of recent (roughly the last 150 years) lake, peat, and coastal sediments — the window in which [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] is unreliable because of the Suess and bomb effects. [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]], Technical Box X) presents it as the dating backbone for the study of industrial-era [[freshwater-acidification|acidification]], [[eutrophication]], [[soil-erosion]], and pollution.

## What It Does

- **Lead-210 (²¹⁰Pb)**, half-life ~22 years, is part of the ²³⁸U decay series. "Unsupported" ²¹⁰Pb, derived from atmospheric decay of radon-222 and deposited by rainfall and dry fall-out, decays in the sediment column and yields ages over ~100–200 years (Appleby and Oldfield 1978).
- **Caesium-137 (¹³⁷Cs)** is an artificial isotope released by atmospheric nuclear-weapons testing. Deposition began in 1954 and peaked in 1963; the 1986 Chernobyl accident added a second, chemically distinguishable pulse (via ¹³⁴Cs). It provides dated marker horizons and traces sediment/soil movement.

## Procedure

Sediment cores are sub-sampled at fine intervals and their ²¹⁰Pb and ¹³⁷Cs activities measured by gamma spectrometry. ²¹⁰Pb age–depth models (constant-rate-of-supply or constant-initial-concentration) are cross-checked against the 1954/1963 ¹³⁷Cs horizons.

## Assumptions and Limitations

²¹⁰Pb models assume a constant supply rate; changing lake hydrology or catchment [[soil-erosion|erosion]] alters the flux and complicates dating (Oldfield and Appleby 1984). ¹³⁷Cs peaks can migrate or blur through mixing and bioturbation. The methods reach back only ~150 years.

## History

Developed in the 1970s (notably by Appleby and Oldfield for lake sediments), the pair became standard in palaeolimnology precisely because they date the industrial period that documentary records cover unevenly and radiocarbon cannot.

## Exemplary Applications

Roberts (1998) reports ²¹⁰Pb/¹³⁷Cs chronologies dating the onset of Galloway loch acidification to the late 19th/early 20th century — before local afforestation — and the acceleration of diatom influx and sedimentation in Lough Neagh; ¹³⁷Cs is also used as a tracer of agricultural soil loss (Walling and Quine 1990).

## Debates

By dating recent sediments precisely, the method arbitrated whether 20th-century [[freshwater-acidification|acidification]] and [[eutrophication]] were natural or anthropogenic — see [[diatom-analysis]].
