# Regional Survey (Settlement-Pattern Survey)

## What It Does

Regional survey is the systematic investigation of whole landscapes to map and date sites
and land use, principally from **surface pottery** used as chronological "index fossils," in
order to build a diachronic picture of settlement patterns — site numbers, sizes,
hierarchies, and their change over time. In [[mesopotamian-archaeology|Mesopotamian
archaeology]] it is the signature method for questions that operate above the scale of the
single site: the growth of cities, the emergence of settlement hierarchies, and the
relationship of urban centers to their rural hinterlands.

## Procedure

Surveyors traverse a region recording sites, collecting diagnostic surface sherds, and
assigning occupation periods from ceramic typology, increasingly aided by aerial photography,
satellite imagery (LANDSAT), soil maps, and GIS. [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert Adams's]] Diyala
survey (1957–8, building on Jacobsen) covered ~8,000 km² and assigned 867 sites to 16
historical periods; his Uruk-countryside survey with Hans Nissen (1972) added
nearest-neighbour locational analysis, and his Euphrates floodplain survey (published 1981)
added rank-size analysis and satellite mapping of ancient levees. Adams's solution to the
multi-period-mound problem was to collect diagnostics intensively at each site on the
assumption of upward artifact movement, making it "generally possible to find traces … of a
site's whole span of occupation."

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method's validity rests on several assumptions Matthews (attributed) flags. Surface
material dates a mound only to ~300–400-year resolution — J. A. Brinkman's critique is that
this is of minimal use for specifically historical questions, to which Adams replied that his
interpretations are deliberately "broad-brush." **Population estimates** assume a
proportionality between settlement size and population (Adams used ~200 persons/hectare) and
that a site's earlier extent equalled its latest, absent contrary evidence. Underlying much
survey interpretation is Nissen's uniformitarian premise that "the settlement systems of the
ancient Near East conformed to the same laws as do the systems we find in the modern world."
Survey is biased toward the latest and most visible occupation; alluviation buries whole
landscapes; and Adams and Nissen explicitly chose broad coverage with summary, non-uniform
sherd collection over intensive uniform sampling, in order to address "major historical and
anthropological problems."

## History

The earliest deliberate Mesopotamian-region surveys were Mallowan's Habur survey (1934),
Seton Lloyd's Sinjar survey (1938, first to publish diagnostic dating pottery), Braidwood's
Amuq survey (~1937), and Jacobsen's Diyala survey (1936–7). [[adams-robert-mcc|Robert
Adams]] systematized and scaled the method from the 1950s, making settlement survey the
distinctive contribution of Mesopotamian archaeology. Frank Hole's proposal (1980) for a
consolidated regional inventory of survey data has, per Matthews, never been carried out;
newer directions include high-intensity Mediterranean-style field-walking (Project
Paphlagonia) and backhoe sectioning of buried canals.

## Exemplary Applications

Adams's surveys underpin the standard reading of the [[uruk-expansion|Uruk period]] as one of
rapid urban growth at [[uruk|Uruk-Warka]] coupled with rural depopulation, and the inference
that Late Uruk Lower Mesopotamia held **multiple competing** urban polities rather than a
single state — a premise built into both sides of the [[uruk-world-system-debate]]. Survey is
equally central to the study of [[state-formation|state formation]] and imperial settlement
reorganization (see [[empire]]).

## Debates

The reliability of survey-based population and hierarchy claims — and their coarse temporal
resolution — is a standing methodological concern (Brinkman vs. Adams). Because survey and
excavation answer different-scale questions, their integration, and the weighting of
survey inference against the textual record, connects to the
[[archaeology-assyriology-text-object-debate|text–object debate]].
