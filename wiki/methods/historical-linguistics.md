# Historical-Comparative Linguistics

## What It Does

Historical-comparative linguistics reconstructs the past states, changes, and genetic relationships of languages from present-day evidence — chiefly **regular sound correspondences** (phonetic law) and **shared morphological structure**. It groups languages into families ("stocks"), reconstructs proto-forms, and traces change over time-depths far exceeding written documentation. In the [[boas-franz|Boasian]] tradition and in [[sapir-edward|Sapir]]'s hands it was applied systematically to the unwritten Indigenous languages of North America, feeding both classification and culture history. See [[linguistic-anthropology]].

## Procedure

At summary resolution: (1) identify recurrent, regular correspondences between sounds in candidate related languages; (2) reconstruct proto-phonemes and proto-forms; (3) separate inherited material from **borrowing**; (4) use shared morphology (structure, not just vocabulary) as the strongest evidence of deep relationship. Sapir's Ch. VIII gives a worked 13-step derivation of English *foot/feet*, *mouse/mice* from West Germanic through the umlaut (i-mutation) and the Great Vowel Shift, with parallel German tables.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method rests on the **regularity of phonetic law**: as Sapir put it, "In practice phonetic laws have their exceptions, but more intensive study almost invariably shows that these exceptions are more apparent than real," with residues explained by **analogical leveling**. Key limitations: it is weak at **absolute dating** (glottochronology is contested — flag any date resting on it); the signal decays, so genuinely ancient relationships may be undemonstrable by ordinary means (Sapir cautioned that deep morphological resemblances across apparently "unrelated" American families may be "vestiges" of relationship too old to prove); and **borrowing** can both mimic relationship and, conversely, phonetic features can spread areally among unrelated neighbors (Sapir's Northwest Coast glottalized-consonant example). Sapir also noted **morphological borrowing is rare** — "Language is probably the most self-contained, the most massively resistant of all social phenomena" — which is what makes shared morphology strong evidence.

## History

Grimm's Law and the Neogrammarian doctrine of exceptionless sound change established the 19th-century comparative method on Indo-European. Sapir's *[[sapir-language-1921|Language]]* (1921, Chs. VII–IX) restates the method for a general audience and extends it: his concepts of [[linguistic-drift|drift]] (directional change) and the [[phonetic-pattern|phonetic pattern]] (which "changes far less readily than the sounds that compose it") reframe why related languages change in parallel, patterned ways. Sapir's own classificatory work on American language stocks is the applied counterpart.

## Exemplary Applications

- Sapir's English/German umlaut-plural parallel (independent, non-inherited drift; Ch. VIII).
- Reconstruction of Indo-European and the treatment of "stock / language / dialect / branch" as "purely relative terms" (Ch. VII).
- Detection of areal phonetic diffusion vs. genetic inheritance (Ch. IX).

## Application — Vansina, oral tradition's auxiliary discipline (1961)

[[vansina-jan|Vansina]]'s *[[vansina-oral-tradition-1965|Oral Tradition]]* (1961/1965) enlists historical linguistics as one of the four **auxiliary disciplines** of [[ethnohistory]]. Two of his cautions matter here. First, an early statement of the **four-way non-identity** rule: "an affinity of language provides insufficient grounds for assuming ethnic affinity," though behind a genetic relationship an ethnic relationship can *sometimes* be inferred — language, culture, and people are not to be equated automatically. Second, he flags **lexicostatistical dating (glottochronology)** — dating language splits from basic-vocabulary replacement rates — as at that time "not yet been fully worked out, and moreover... not yet been accepted as valid by linguists in general" (his cited application: Mongo and Rwanda separating ~32 centuries ago), consistent with this wiki's standing flag on glottochronology. His worked example is Greenberg's classification placing Bantu with Benue-Cross languages, implying a Bantu homeland near the Cross River and a relatively rapid Bantu expansion. See [[comparative-method]].

## Renfrew 1987 — archaeology's demands on the method

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin]], *[[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987|Archaeology and Language]]*, esp. chs. 4–5.)* Renfrew accepts sound laws, comparative reconstruction, and a (partial) family-tree model for [[indo-european-languages]], while stressing Schmidt **wave/isogloss** overlays and rejecting pure Trubetskoy convergence for IE. He attacks **linguistic palaeontology** as a homeland fixer: loan-words, semantic shift, and non-unique plant/animal cognates (beech/salmon/robin problems; Pulgram's Proto-Romanic reductio) do not uniquely support a south Russian steppe *Urheimat*. He flags **circularity** when archaeologists and palaeontologists prop each other up (e.g. Friedrich's tree study baking steppe geography into the analysis). **Glottochronology** (Swadesh): rates need not be constant; tables do not cleanly match branching; fitting a farming chronology might require roughly doubling Swadesh's loss rate — still not a secure absolute clock. Programmatic demand: general models of language relatedness and **replacement** (demography/subsistence, élite dominance, system collapse) before matching pots to protolanguages. Homeland disputes: [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]], [[anatolian-hypothesis]].

## Debates

The status and dating of deep genetic relationships (macro-family proposals) and the reliability of glottochronology remain contested; homeland and macro-family arguments belong on `debates/` pages ([[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]), not in wiki voice. The independence of linguistic classification from racial and cultural classification is treated at [[race-and-culture-debate]]. Relation to the general anthropological [[comparative-method]]: linguistics supplies the comparative method's most rigorous instance (exceptionless correspondence), against which looser cross-cultural comparison is often measured.

### Anthony densification (2007) — applied reconstruction and linguistic palaeontology defended

*(Attributed — [[anthony-david-w|David W. Anthony]], *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* 2007.)* Archaeologist’s applied use of the comparative method and palaeontology for the IE homeland problem — method claims, not wiki-voice homeland verdicts.

- **Reconstruction as real-language reference:** Against the objection that reconstructed PIE is “only a hypothesis,” Anthony holds absolute certainty is not the standard of secular inquiry; reconstructed PIE is fragmentary, homogenized, and multi-period (like a dictionary) but still refers to a **real spoken language**, analogous to imperfectly recorded Sumerian or Egyptian (R2-004; R1-036). ~1500 reconstructed PIE roots in the standard lexicon (R1-016).
- **Sound-change regularity and reliability:** Sound change is regular and rule-governed; Grimm’s Law (Pre-Germanic k>h, etc.) as classic demonstration; reliability thresholds, Hall’s Romance reconstruction test, Hlewagastiz *gastiz confirmation, Linear B labiovelar confirmation, and Saussure’s laryngeals confirmed in Anatolian are presented as method successes (R1-023–030, R1-047). Limits of the comparative method and of negative vocabulary evidence are stated (R1-026; R2-006).
- **Glottochronology critiqued:** Swadesh core vocabulary and constant rates are treated as problematic; Anthony revises or sets aside simple glottochronological clocks for absolute dating of PIE (R1-037–038) — consistent with this page’s standing flag.
- **Linguistic palaeontology as terminus tool (not pure homeland geography):** Wagon/wheel five-term semantic field and wool vocabulary date the reconstructable stage of late PIE **after** ~4000–3500 BCE (wheels more secure terminus than wool) (R1-033, R1-062–069; R2-005). He rejects treating wagon terms as post-PIE loans or as independent parallel creations of *kwel- → ‘wheel’ in every branch (R1-087–088). Temperate flora/fauna, honeybee distribution, and horse term constrain ecology without reviving obsolete “salmon/beech = northern Europe only” pins (R2-012–015). This is Anthony’s **defense of a carefully restricted palaeontology** against Renfrew’s broad circularity critique (see Renfrew section above) — both positions live on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]].
- **Language ≠ genes; migration still required for IE:** Language and genes correlate only exceptionally (R1-010). IE family is not explained by convergence/creolization alone (R1-017; R2-007–008). Persistent multi-century material-culture frontiers can approximate linguistic frontiers under specified conditions, without pots=people (R1-019; R2-036–044).
- **Cladistics:** Ringe–Warnow branching order used for relative daughter sequence (R1-060).
