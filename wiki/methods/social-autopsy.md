# Social Autopsy

Klinenberg’s label in *[[klinenberg-heat-wave-2002|Heat Wave]]* for investigating a mass-mortality event as a **social** product: not only causes of death on death certificates, but the social conditions that make some people die and others survive under the same meteorological stress.

## What It Does

Combines epidemiological patterns with ethnography, institutional analysis, and media study to explain *excess* and *unequal* mortality.

## Procedure

*(Summary — Klinenberg 2002.)* (1) Establish layered mortality series and differentials (age, race, sex, living alone, community area). (2) Reconstruct the **social production of isolation** among the elderly. (3) Compare neighborhood ecologies of support (e.g. North Lawndale vs Little Village). (4) Analyze city service failure and political PR. (5) Analyze news production of the catastrophe. (6) Synthesize a “formula for disaster” from typical urban extremes meeting meteorological stress.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes social organization is causally relevant beyond individual risk factors (CDC-style case-control); comparison neighborhoods must be carefully matched and interpreted; official heat-related death counts are themselves institutional products.

## History

Klinenberg explicitly links the phrase to earlier public-health and social-medicine traditions (Virchow et al. named in conclusion) that treated epidemics as social as well as biological. *Heat Wave* made “social autopsy” a portable slogan in disaster sociology.

## Debates

Scope of municipal responsibility vs weather; strength of neighborhood-comparison design.
