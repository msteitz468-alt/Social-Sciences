## What It Does

Biostratigraphic dating uses fossil content to date sites relative to one another when physical overlap between deposits and independent absolute dating are unavailable. Fossils have two advantages over artifacts for this purpose, per Klein (1999): they can date sites lacking artifacts or predating artifact production, and, for early human evolution, fossil-based biostratigraphic units can cover larger areas and shorter time spans than artifact-based typological units.

## Procedure

The best biostratigraphic index taxa are those that spread rapidly and widely, disappeared broadly simultaneously across a region, or evolved rapidly enough that their evolutionary stage itself becomes a relative-age clue. A site's fossil assemblage is compared against the known geographic and temporal range of such index taxa to place the site in relative sequence against others. Klein (1999) singles out two regional applications: microtine-rodent biostratigraphy is the most productive tool in Eurasia for dating human-evolution sites, while elephants, pigs, and horses are the most useful taxa for the same purpose in Africa.

In Eurasia, microtine rodents (voles and lemmings, marked by hypsodont molars) are the most successful current biostratigraphic group in the Northern Hemisphere, providing detailed sequencing in central and north-central Europe, France, and Britain. The Norway lemming (Lemmus) immigrated to Europe from northern Asia in the mid-Pliocene, roughly 3.5 million years ago; the collared lemming (Dicrostonyx) arrived at or shortly after the start of the Pleistocene, 1.7–1.6 million years ago. Key morphological transitions in the Mimomys–Arvicola–Microtus vole lineage — cementum first appearing between molar prisms around 3.25 million years ago, and molars becoming rootless and ever-growing around 600,000–500,000 years ago (marking Arvicola's appearance) — anchor three microtine biostratigraphic stages spanning the past 1.7–1.6 million years (Villanyan, Biharian, Toringian), a finer sequence than the two coarser large-mammal stages (late Villafranchian and Galerian) covering the same span. Dates for microtine migration events depend mainly on correlation with global paleomagnetic stratigraphy (see [[paleomagnetic-dating]]).

In Africa, elephants, pigs, and horses provide the main basis for relative dating and for cross-checking absolute dates. Elephas appeared in Africa shortly before 4 million years ago; Elephas recki, evolving before 3 million years ago, shows molars with progressively higher crowns and thinner, more tightly folded enamel, allowing sites to be bracketed into roughly 500,000-year ranges. The mammoth lineage (Mammuthus) originated in Africa around 2.5 million years ago before becoming extinct there while new species arose in Siberia and spread into Europe, with successive species showing increasing molar plate number and thinner enamel usable for relative dating. Three-toed horses (Hipparion) spread across Africa between 12 and 10.5 million years ago; one-toed horses (Equus) dispersed shortly before 2 million years ago, overlapping roughly 1.5 million years before the three-toed species went extinct around 500,000 years ago.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Biostratigraphy carries a risk of circularity if index-fossil ages are used uncritically to date sites while the fossil ages themselves rest on assumptions drawn from those same sites, rather than on independent calibration. Physicochemical relative-dating methods (see [[relative-dating]], [[fluorine-dating]]) are more geographically restricted than fossil-based biostratigraphy, since fossil taxa's ranges and evolutionary transitions travel further than local groundwater chemistry.

## History

Comparison of one-toed horse and pig biostratigraphy between Koobi Fora and Lower Omo in East Africa revealed an apparent radiopotassium dating discrepancy of roughly 600,000 years, later shown to be an error: the original Koobi Fora radiopotassium dates were roughly 700,000–600,000 years too old. The episode illustrates biostratigraphy's role in cross-checking absolute dating methods rather than merely depending on them.

## Exemplary Applications

Used to sequence deposits at [[koobi-fora]] and [[omo-river]] in East Africa via elephant-pig-horse biostratigraphy, and to build fine-grained chronologies for European Pleistocene sites via microtine-rodent biostratigraphy. Discussed in Klein, *The Human Career* ([[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Debates

The Koobi Fora/Lower Omo horse-and-pig biostratigraphy discrepancy is Klein's (1999) key cautionary case: it shows biostratigraphic cross-checking can expose errors in absolute (radiopotassium) dates rather than merely deferring to them, while also illustrating the circularity risk if biostratigraphic sequences are treated as unquestioned ground truth. See [[potassium-argon-dating]] for the radiopotassium side of this episode.
