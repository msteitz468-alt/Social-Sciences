# Community study

## What It Does

A **community study** investigates a whole local community — town, city, or district — as a unit of social life, aiming to describe how major activities and institutions interlock rather than isolating one problem or occupation. In American sociology the genre's charter exemplar is the Lynds' *[[lynd-middletown-1929|Middletown]]* (1929): a mid-sized city approached, in Clark Wissler's Foreword phrase, "after the manner of social anthropology," with the community as the unit analogous to the anthropologist's tribe or village.

Related but distinct: [[participant-observation]] (role-based immersion, often in a subcommunity or institution); settlement-house surveys and muckraking community inventories; [[chicago-school|Chicago]] [[ecological-method|ecological]] studies of natural areas within a metropolis; national social surveys that sample individuals, not one place as a system.

## Procedure

Summary resolution (wiki, not manual) — as exemplified in *Middletown* Note on Method and Ch. I–II (attributed design facts):

1. Select a community by explicit criteria (size band, diversified industry, "common features," compactness/homogeneity — the Lynds also sought small minority populations to isolate cultural change).
2. Enter as outsiders (Institute-funded; not locally invited or paid); open a field office; reside long enough for ties (Lynds/Flournoy Jan 1924–June 1925).
3. Define a comparison horizon (1890) and fieldwork present (1924–25); do not attempt every intervening year in equal depth.
4. Organize around major activity domains (six trunks); build schedules from observed behavior rather than importing fixed instruments first.
5. Differentiate internally when the city cannot be treated as one actor — the Lynds' [[business-class-and-working-class|business/working-class]] cut.
6. Combine: participation in local life; documentary/newspaper series; compiled statistics; interviews (casual to multi-hour scheduled); family schedules (124 WC / 40 BC wives, native-born white, school-age children); high-school questionnaires (with explicit sample limits).
7. Write a synchronous total-situation portrait; raise questions rather than prove a single thesis or better/worse verdict.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Unit assumption:** "community" is a meaningful bounded system — increasingly strained as cities grow and networks nationalize (a theme the Lynds themselves note).
- **Typicality:** selection as "representative" of a class of cities is a claim, not a probability sample.
- **Class dualism:** the Lynds' [[business-class-and-working-class|business/working-class]] dichotomy organizes the book; other cleavages (race, ethnicity, party) can be underweighted relative to that frame.
- **Observer position:** staff are outsiders with institutional sponsorship; "complete objectivity" is acknowledged as never wholly avoidable risk (Ch I).

## History

Antecedents include English and American social surveys, settlement studies, and anthropological monographs of small societies. *Middletown* (fieldwork 1924–25, published 1929) became the U.S. reference point for studying "ourselves" with outsider techniques. The Lynds' *Middletown in Transition* (1937) and later restudies (e.g., Caplow and collaborators — not yet ingested) made Muncie a multi-generational laboratory. Chicago natural-area monographs and Whyte's *Street Corner Society* (Cornerville; society page [[cornerville]]) are adjacent genres: subcommunity or institutional depth rather than whole-city six-domain coverage.
