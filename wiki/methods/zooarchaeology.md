# Zooarchaeology

Analysis of **animal remains** (bones, teeth, shells, otoliths, and residues) from archaeological contexts to infer subsistence, domestication, seasonality, site function, and human–animal relations. Closely related to (and sometimes synonymous with) [[faunal-analysis]]; Renfrew & Bahn 2012 treat zooarchaeology as the postwar interpretive program that goes beyond species lists to ask **how remains arrived** and what they mean for past economies. Overlaps [[taphonomy]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[isotope-analysis]], and [[paleoethnobotany]] in multi-proxy subsistence studies.

## What It Does

- Identifies taxa (including from fragments via collagen protein ID for sheep vs goat, etc.).
- Distinguishes **human agency** from natural death, carnivores, owls, burrowers — the first interpretive question, especially in the Lower Paleolithic.
- Separates **dietary** use from skins, tools, raw-material depots, or ritual.
- Builds **age, sex, and slaughter profiles** (herd management; seasonal kills).
- Quantifies relative abundance (NISP, MNI, bone weight) and models meat yield cautiously.
- Tests **domestication** via morphology, demography, pathology, art, and DNA.
- Reads **secondary products** (milk, wool, traction) and shell/fish seasonality clocks.

## Procedure

(As synthesized in Renfrew & Bahn 2012, Ch. 7, attributed.)

1. **Recovery and recording.** Screen/float as appropriate; record provenience; preserve surface marks for microscopy.
2. **Identification.** Reference collections; element, side, age indicators; collagen species ID for difficult fragments.
3. **Taphonomic screening.** Cutmarks vs carnivore marks vs trampling (SEM can help but is not sufficient alone — context and mark position matter: Shipman & Potts vs Oliver/Behrensmeyer). Binford’s ethnoarchaeological criterion: many bone **cylinders** and few intact articular ends → carnivore/scavenger destruction.
4. **Age, sex, seasonality.** Antlers, canines, pelvis, size clusters; epiphyseal fusion and tooth eruption/wear for survivorship curves; fetuses, newborns, antler cycles, immature shaft lengths for season of death (presence in some seasons rarely disproves presence in others).
5. **Quantification.** **NISP** (fragment counts — often misleading) and **MNI** (minimum animals from most abundant element side). Grimes Graves and Moncin show NISP vs MNI can **reverse** species importance when preservation and age profiles differ. Meat-weight models treat bones as **butchery units**, not whole carcasses.
6. **Domestication and management tests.** Non-indigenous introductions; morphological change (size reduction unreliable alone); slaughter-pattern shifts (e.g. Hesse’s Ganj Dareh goats: juvenile male cull, adult female survival); traction pathologies; penning disease; DNA histories.
7. **Integration.** Combine with plant remains, residues, and human-bone isotopes; calorie-yield calculations for shell middens demote mollusks from “staple” status when vertebrates dominate energy.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Only stomach contents/feces prove consumption**; otherwise inference is contextual (cut/burned bone, residues).
- **Microfauna** are better environmental indicators than macrofauna (sensitivity, natural accumulation, sample size); large mammal bones are often predation-selected and cannot represent full fauna (Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 6).
- Presence of a cold-tolerant species proves **tolerance**, not cold climate (woolly rhinoceros example).
- Quantification describes the **excavated bone sample**, with unknown relation to original fauna.
- High immature proportions alone do **not** prove domestication; wild/domestic is a spectrum of herd-management relationships.
- Growth-rate and ethnographic analogies for seasonality assume transferability to the past.

## History

- Long comparative-anatomy tradition; ecological archaeology ([[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]]) elevated animal remains as economic evidence.
- Post-WWII zooarchaeology shifts from species lists to taphonomy, butchery, seasonality, and domestication.
- Actualistic and ethnoarchaeological programs: **C.K. Brain** (Swartkrans — australopithecines as prey, not “killer man-apes”); **[[binford-lewis|Lewis Binford]]** (bone breakage, hunting vs scavenging); Shipman & Potts SEM cutmark studies.
- Multidisciplinary farming-origins expeditions (Braidwood’s [[jarmo|Jarmo]] team including **Reed** on animal domestication) institutionalized zooarchaeology in Near Eastern prehistory. In *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* (1959), only **goat** is treated as sure domesticate at Jarmo; sheep/cattle/pig/horse/dog remain provisional pending Reed (attributed).
- Later revisions: Star Carr seasonality overturned (Clark’s winter antler model → Legge & Rowley-Conwy early-summer kills); Sherratt’s **[[secondary-products-revolution|Secondary Products Revolution]]** (classic 1981–97 package of milk, wool, [[animal-traction|traction]]) reframed earlier and more widespread for **dairying** by lipid residues into early Neolithic — timing challenge, not automatic rejection of traction/wool clustering. Primary coining/synthesis source now ingested: [[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]].

## Exemplary Applications

| Case | Point (per Renfrew & Bahn 2012) |
|---|---|
| **Star Carr** | Classic economic-prehistory seasonality revised by later faunal work |
| **Ganj Dareh goats** | Sex/age cull pattern as early herd management |
| **Abu Hureyra** | Gazelle ~80% into Early Neolithic; sheep/goat rise only after gazelle decline — farming as stepwise package |
| **Boarding School / Gull Lake bison drives** | Mass-kill demography and butchery intensity |
| **Elands Bay Cave** | Sea-level rise restructures fauna and subsistence |
| **Oronsay otoliths** | Seasonal site use from saithe size distributions |
| **Kidosaku Jomon midden** | Multi-resource calories; deer ~60%; shellfish supplementary |
| **Garnsey bison** | Binford cylinder criterion: primarily human assemblage |

## Debates

- **Hunting vs scavenging** for early hominins (open; formation-process discrimination is the path to resolution).
- **Big-game extinction** — Martin’s overkill vs Lundelius climate vs Owen-Smith cascade (complex mosaics preferred).
- **NISP vs MNI** and utility-index transfer from ethnoarchaeology.
- **Wild/domestic boundary** and secondary products timing.
- Relation of faunal patterning to lithic facies debates (see also [[faunal-analysis]], [[binford-bordes-mousterian-debate]] if present).

## Method history — Ucko & Dimbleby 1969 seminar

*(Attributed — animal-methods papers in *[[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969]]*.)*

The 1968 London seminar is a landmark consolidation of [[animal-domestication|domestication]] zooarchaeology beyond species lists:

| Author | Method contribution (as of 1969) |
|---|---|
| **Bökönyi** | Definition of domestication; keeping vs breeding; multi-criteria recognition list; transitional forms for local domestication; critique of age-structure-only proofs |
| **Chaplin** | Non-morphological criteria (demography, context, MNI) |
| **Berry** | Genetics of domestication; morphology not inevitable |
| **Ducos** | Age-profile method on Palestinian sequences |
| **Grigson** | Absolute size wild vs domestic cattle; sexual dimorphism limits |
| **Payne** | Sheep vs goat metacarpal metrics (population separation) |
| **Cranstone** | Ethnographic husbandry as external evidence |
| **Clutton-Brock** | Site-level carnivore analysis ([[jericho]]) |
| **Reed** | Regional synthesis integrating behaviour, environment, culture |
