# Diatom Analysis

Diatom analysis reconstructs the history of aquatic environments from the silica shells (frustules) of diatoms — unicellular algae — preserved in lake, mire, and coastal sediments. [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]], Technical Box IX) presents it as fulfilling "the same function for aquatic ecosystems that palynology does for terrestrial vegetation," and as the decisive tool in resolving the [[freshwater-acidification|acidification]] and [[eutrophication]] debates of the later 20th century.

## What It Does

Diatoms (typically 5–500 µm) are identifiable to species and sub-species and are narrowly sensitive to water chemistry. Their assemblages index past pH, salinity, water depth, nutrient status, and productivity. Because the preserved fossil is the whole organism rather than a dispersed reproductive product, diatom records avoid the production- and dispersal-weighting problems of [[pollen-analysis|pollen]] (as reviewed by Battarbee 1986).

## Procedure

Frustules are cleaned (oxidation of organic matter, removal of clays), mounted, and counted at ×200–1000. Past water chemistry is reconstructed either by classic indicator groups — the German diatomist Friedrich Hustedt's five pH-preference categories, from acidobiontic to alkalibiontic, formalized as Renberg and Hellberg's numeric "index B" — or by modern **transfer functions** statistically relating fossil assemblages to a calibration set of modern lakes (Birks et al. 1990).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Reconstruction assumes present species–environment relationships applied in the past, and that frustules are preserved without selective dissolution (a problem in strongly alkaline waters). It requires large, well-characterized modern training sets and specialist taxonomy.

## History

Diatoms were used as water-quality indicators from the early 20th century; their systematic palaeolimnological application, especially at University College London under Richard Battarbee, made them central to acid-rain and lake-pollution research from the 1980s. [[radiocarbon-dating|Radiocarbon]], [[lead-210-dating|²¹⁰Pb and ¹³⁷Cs]] chronologies date the recent records.

## Exemplary Applications

Roberts (1998, attributed) reports the Gårdsjön (Sweden) diatom-pH record — a gradual ~1.0 pH-unit early-Holocene decline followed by an abrupt >1.5-unit fall dated to c. AD 1950 — and the Galloway (Scotland) lochs study, where afforested and unforested catchments acidified alike, showing atmospheric acid deposition, not afforestation, as the cause of [[freshwater-acidification|freshwater acidification]]. Diatom-inferred phosphorus reconstructions likewise dated the onset of cultural [[eutrophication]] in Lough Neagh and other European lakes.

## Debates

Diatom evidence was decisive in the "natural or cultural?" disputes over both acidification and eutrophication, distinguishing gradual natural lake ontogeny from abrupt 20th-century, pollution-driven change.
