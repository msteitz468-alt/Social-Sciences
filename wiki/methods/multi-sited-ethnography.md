# Multi-Sited Ethnography

## What It Does

*(Program statements attributed to [[marcus-george-e|Marcus]] throughout — this is a research program's self-description, primary-ingested from *[[marcus-ethnography-thick-and-thin-1998|Ethnography Through Thick and Thin]]* (1998), which collects the proposal's evolution 1989–1997.)*

Marcus distinguished two modes of embedding ethnography in the world system: the common mode keeps the intensively studied **single site** and supplies context by archival or macro-theoretical means; the emergent mode "moves out from the single sites and local situations of conventional ethnographic research designs to examine the circulation of cultural meanings, objects, and identities in diffuse time-space" — constructing its object **by tracing connections across sites**. Crucially, the goal "is not holistic representation… For ethnography, then, there is no global in the local-global contrast now so frequently evoked. The global is an emergent dimension of arguing about the connection among sites." The precursor formulation was "multi-locale ethnography" (Marcus & Fischer 1986; Marcus 1989), framed as a shift in ethnography's **chronotope** (Bakhtin): "from a place, to places, simultaneously and complexly connected, by intended and unintended consequences." Marcus also distinguished the **multi-sited research imaginary** (a disposition to hold two or more ethnographically conceived sites in juxtaposition) from literal multi-sited fieldwork.

## Procedure

Marcus's six "modes of construction" (tracking strategies), with his exemplars (1995):

| Strategy | Exemplars (per Marcus) |
|---|---|
| Follow the people | Malinowski's *Argonauts* (archetype); Rouse on Mexican migration |
| Follow the thing | Mintz *Sweetness and Power*; Appadurai *The Social Life of Things*; Wallerstein's commodity chains; Myers on Pintupi acrylics; Latour |
| Follow the metaphor | Emily Martin *Flexible Bodies* — "the most fully achieved multi-sited ethnography… thus far" |
| Follow the plot, story, or allegory | Brooks *Reading for the Plot*; Boyarin on social memory |
| Follow the life or biography | Fischer on life history; Fischer & Abedi *Debating Muslims* |
| Follow the conflict | [[extended-case-method]] lineage; Ginsburg on abortion; Gaines on copyright |

A **strategically situated (single-site) ethnography** is the "foreshortened" variant: it stays in one place but designs that place against what happens elsewhere (Willis's *Learning to Labour* school/factory relation; Marcus's own *Lives in Trust*). His 1991 "requirements" essay adds a six-point design program — problematize the spatial (against "community"), the temporal (memory over historical metanarrative), and voice (against "structure"); plus dialogic exegesis, **bifocality** (ethnographer–subject coevalness, crediting [[fabian-johannes|Fabian]] 1983), and critical juxtaposition of alternatives.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Marcus himself catalogued three "methodological anxieties": **testing the limits of ethnography**, **attenuating the power of fieldwork** (uneven intensity; he answers that translation remains essential and more demanding), and **loss of the subaltern** (multi-sited work decenters resistance-and-accommodation; he denies it is mere "studying up"). [[clifford-james|Clifford]]'s objection (1996 conversation, reported by Marcus): multi-sited work loses the **depth** of interaction "on which the entire ideology and ethos of fieldwork has depended"; Marcus's reply redefines depth as interpretive depth mediated by the ethnographer's pre-existing connection to the object. The mobile fieldworker becomes a **circumstantial activist** — commitments shift across sites where "not taking sides" is untenable — with [[complicity]] replacing rapport as the figure of the fieldwork relation. Comparison is emergent from tracing, not controlled: cross-site non-independence is unaddressed by design (a Galton-type caveat this wiki flags on comparison pages).

## History

Grew out of the [[writing-culture]] moment and *[[marcus-fischer-anthropology-as-cultural-critique-1986|Anthropology as Cultural Critique]]*'s multi-locale proposal; incubated, per Marcus, not in high theory but in interdisciplinary arenas — media studies, science and technology studies (Latour, Haraway), and the *Public Culture* diaspora/globalization project (Appadurai, Breckenridge). Canonical statement: "Ethnography in/of the World System," *Annual Review of Anthropology* 24 (1995): 95–117. Marcus read the method's rise biographically as the discipline's "second projects" pattern — post-tenure moves from culture-area fieldwork to dispersed phenomena.

## Exemplary Applications

Martin *Flexible Bodies* (1994); Marcus *Lives in Trust* (1992); Fischer & Abedi *Debating Muslims* (1990); Holmes on the European right (see [[complicity]]); precursors read retrospectively: Willis, Mintz, Latour. Within this vault, [[malinowski-argonauts-study|Malinowski's kula work]] is Marcus's own named archetype of "follow the people."

**Scheper-Hughes — Organs Watch, "follow the bodies."** In *[[scheper-hughes-wacquant-commodifying-bodies-2002|Commodifying Bodies]]* ("Commodity Fetishism in Organs Trafficking," 2001/2002), [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]] describes Berkeley Organs Watch (founded Nov. 1999 with [[cohen-lawrence|Lawrence Cohen]]) as **multi-sited** research on the global [[human-organs-trade]]: mandate and dictum to **"follow the bodies"** — views from operating table and mortuary slab, bridging elite medical centers and shantytowns. Sites named include Argentina, Brazil, Cuba, Ecuador, India, Israel, the Netherlands, South Africa, Turkey, and the USA. She acknowledges the classic multi-sited risks Marcus flagged (thinness; sacrifice of leisurely single-site pace) and blends soft evidence (rumors, innuendo) with hard medical records and interviews. Aligns methodologically with "follow the people/thing" strategies, applied to organs, brokers, patients, and transplant capital; genre blend with surveillance and human-rights work is treated under [[militant-anthropology]].
