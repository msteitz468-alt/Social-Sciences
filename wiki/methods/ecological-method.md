# Ecological Method (urban / human ecology)

## What It Does

Studies human communities through **spatial and temporal relations** — distribution, segregation, succession, mobility — using maps, rates, land values, and [[natural-area|natural-area]] units. Part of the [[human-ecology|human ecology]] program in *[[park-burgess-city-1925|The City]]* (1925).

## Procedure

As stated in the 1925 program (attributed):

1. **Describe expansion** as extension, succession, concentration (and decentralization).
2. **Assess metabolism**: when disorganization exceeds organization under rapid growth.
3. **Measure mobility** as pulse: excess of movement indices over population growth; land values (± rents) as sensitive index.
4. **Map natural areas** and formations (land-value centers/peripheries).
5. **Stage invasions**: initial → developmental → climax; classify use-change vs occupant-type change (McKenzie).
6. **Cross-section cases** ("under the microscope") — e.g. Burgess's West Side Jewish Ghetto + Lawndale.
7. **Institutional natural history** (Park on the newspaper) as parallel method for urban institutions.

Burgess (via Park): science concerns **forces** (type-factors in typical situations), not only concrete **factors** of unique events.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes spatial position systematically shapes institutions and behavior; that plant-ecology analogies are generative; that rates by area index community process. Fails when spatial patterns are read as natural without power, when ecological correlations are treated as individual causes, and when ideal zones are reified.

## History

Formalized Chicago 1920s; McKenzie definitional essay 1925; later factorial ecology, social area analysis, multilevel neighborhood models (reception).

## Exemplary Applications

[[park-burgess-city-study|The City]] program; Seattle counts (McKenzie); listed student monographs (Anderson, Thrasher, Zorbaugh, etc.); later Shaw & McKay (not ingested).

## Debates

Human ecology vs cultural and political-economy urban sociology; social disorganization vs collective efficacy reformulations.
