## What It Does

Fission-track dating counts microscopic tracks formed by the spontaneous fission of uranium-238 in naturally occurring glasses and minerals. These tracks anneal (are erased) under heat and begin reforming once the material cools, so the density of tracks relative to the sample's 238U content dates the last time the material cooled — as Klein (1999) puts it, "the last time a sample cooled can be determined from the density of tracks."

## Procedure

A polished sample surface is etched to reveal fission tracks, which are counted under a microscope and compared against the sample's measured uranium-238 content to calculate elapsed time since the tracks began accumulating (i.e., since the last cooling/annealing event).

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method requires the sample to contain moderate 238U content and is applied mainly to volcanic extrusives, paralleling radiopotassium dating's material requirements (see [[potassium-argon-dating]]). Low-temperature annealing, particularly in glasses, tends to bias fission-track dates too young by prematurely erasing some tracks. Klein (1999) notes the method has seen less use than radiopotassium dating largely because it is more tedious to apply.

## History

Developed as an independent radiometric check on volcanic-rock chronologies, used where applicable alongside radiopotassium dating of the same deposits.

## Exemplary Applications

Applied, where feasible, as a cross-check on radiopotassium-dated volcanic horizons in early hominid-bearing deposits discussed in Klein, *The Human Career* ([[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Debates

No major methodological dispute is recorded for fission-track dating in Klein (1999) beyond its known bias toward under-aging from low-temperature annealing, a limitation shared in kind (though not mechanism) with other dating methods requiring an unambiguous zeroing event.
