# Personal Documents Method

## What It Does

The **personal documents method** (also life-history method, human-documents method) treats letters, autobiographies, diaries, and related first-person materials as primary sociological data. [[thomas-william-isaac|Thomas]] and [[znaniecki-florian|Znaniecki]]'s *[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|The Polish Peasant]]* is the charter demonstration. In Vol I as ingested, the Methodological Note states an **inductive** program on "concrete materials": private letters of the first two volumes "have needed relatively little selection, particularly as they are arranged in family series"; notes "isolate single attitudes, show their analogies and dependences, and interpret them in relation to the social background"; authors claim the analysis "contains nothing not essentially contained in the materials themselves" (attributed — later contested by Blumer). Vol I enacts the method through specimen letters, a **bowing-letter** typology (solidarity performance under separation), and six family series. Later volumes (not in this ingest) add the Władek life-record and American institutional materials. At least one series (Gościak) was assembled partly via newspaper advertisement and paid letters — documents produced for research as well as found.

## Procedure

1. Collect series of documents from related persons (family letter groups) or continuous life-records.
2. Translate/edit with notes that supply social context (Vol I footnotes interpret peasant references).
3. Interpret documents as expressions of attitudes toward values (familial solidarity, property, religion, status).
4. Generalize carefully toward laws of social becoming and toward types of organization/disorganization — without, the authors claim, abandoning concrete complexity.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Documents are **not raw experience**; they are produced for recipients, under literacy and postal constraints, often in migration.
- Translation and editorial selection (Thomas & Znaniecki chose and framed series) are already interpretations.
- Blumer's appraisal later argued the theoretical scheme is not uniquely determined by the documents.
- Epistemic strength is **depth and subjectivity**; weakness is **representativeness and independent verification**.

## History

Landmark public demonstration: *The Polish Peasant* (1918–20). Line continues through Chicago life-history dissertations, Blumer's critical appraisal (1939), and later oral history and narrative sociology. Related but distinct: Cooley's "personal ideas" methodology; Mead's analysis of the social act (not document-based).
