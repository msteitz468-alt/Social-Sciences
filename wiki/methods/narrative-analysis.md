# Narrative Analysis

## What It Does

*(As treated by [[rosaldo-renato|Rosaldo]] in *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]*, drawing on philosophers of history.)* Narrative analysis treats sequences of action as emplotted concatenations of ideas, events, and institutions rather than as "one damned thing after another," making **historical understanding** and **human agency** central to social analysis.

## Procedure

*(As argued by Rosaldo.)* Emplot an action-sequence as an unfolding concatenation of agency, structure, and events; hold the parts together in what Louis Mink called a **"synoptic judgment"** rather than reviewing them seriatim; and sustain a **"double vision"** (W. B. Gallie) that oscillates rapidly between the analyst's standpoint and the protagonists' — treating subjects' own narratives as knowledge, not raw data.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Rosaldo, following Gallie, stressed **retrospective intelligibility** (later events made sense of by earlier ones) as distinct from predictive, hypothetico-deductive explanation (Hempel, Nagel). He warned that when analyst and subjects use **culturally divergent narrative forms** (e.g. A. L. Becker on Javanese *wayang*), the "double vision" breaks down more deeply than Gallie or Hexter allowed, and the analyst's "sovereign viewpoint" is challenged — a problem of the [[positioned-subject|positioned subject]]. He criticized Ricoeur for privileging the historian's synoptic judgment over protagonists' viewpoints and assuming all accounts aggregate into one master narrative.

## History

Rosaldo drew on W. B. Gallie, Louis Mink ("synoptic judgment"), Paul Ricoeur, J. H. Hexter, Hayden White, and Jerome Bruner, and on Ricoeur's reading of Braudel's Mediterranean as an implicit narrative. He positioned narrative as the form of his broader **"processual analysis,"** counterposed to systems/structural models (see [[culture]]).

## Exemplary Applications

Rosaldo's readings of Ilongot hunting stories, of E. P. Thompson's *Making of the English Working Class* (a "melodramatic" emplotment), of [[turner-victor|Victor Turner's]] Ndembu "social drama," and of changing Chicano narratives (Américo Paredes; Sandra Cisneros) in [[cultural-borderlands|the borderlands]].

## Debates

Bears on the qualitative/interpretive vs. objectivist-explanatory divide in social analysis, and on the [[writing-culture-debate|Writing Culture]] critique of ethnographic realism.
