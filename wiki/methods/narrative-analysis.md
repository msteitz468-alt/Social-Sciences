# Narrative Analysis

## What It Does

Narrative analysis is a **family of methods**, not a single school. Two strands already in this wiki’s sources:

### Linguistic / Labovian strand (Labov & Waletzky 1967; Labov 1972 ch. 9)

[[labov-william|William Labov]] and Joshua Waletzky formalized oral **narratives of personal experience** as sequences of clauses matched to inferred event order (temporal juncture). A fully formed narrative may show six structural parts—**abstract, orientation, complicating action, evaluation, result/resolution, coda**—of which only complicating action is essential to recognize a narrative. Evaluation answers “so what?” and is realized both as a focal section and as “waves” of devices (intensifiers, comparators, correlatives, explicatives) that depart from basic narrative syntax. Detail lives on [[labov-narrative-model]]. proposed_by: [[labov-william]] · operationalizes: reportability / evaluation · applies: [[observers-paradox]]

### Interpretive / anthropological strand (Rosaldo 1989)

*(As treated by [[rosaldo-renato|Rosaldo]] in *[[rosaldo-culture-truth-1993|Culture and Truth]]*, drawing on philosophers of history.)* Narrative analysis treats sequences of action as emplotted concatenations of ideas, events, and institutions rather than as "one damned thing after another," making **historical understanding** and **human agency** central to social analysis.

## Procedure

### Labovian elicitation and coding

Labov elicited personal-experience narratives to obtain **casual speech** and reduce monitoring under the [[observers-paradox|observer’s paradox]]: prototype **Danger of Death** question; fight prompts (“Were you ever in a fight with a guy bigger than you?” … “What happened?”); Yes-No commitment before extended telling; topic schedule rather than fixed questionnaire. Coding identifies narrative vs free clauses, structural parts, and evaluative device classes; age and group comparisons count device density (e.g. Harlem preadolescent / adolescent / adult fight narratives in *[[labov-language-inner-city-1972|Language in the Inner City]]* ch. 9). See [[labov-narrative-model]] for the full operational scheme.

### Rosaldo / process procedure

*(As argued by Rosaldo.)* Emplot an action-sequence as an unfolding concatenation of agency, structure, and events; hold the parts together in what Louis Mink called a **"synoptic judgment"** rather than reviewing them seriatim; and sustain a **"double vision"** (W. B. Gallie) that oscillates rapidly between the analyst's standpoint and the protagonists' — treating subjects' own narratives as knowledge, not raw data.

## Assumptions and Limitations

**Labovian strand (attributed).** Assumes that personal-experience narrative is a cross-speaker method of recapitulating events via clause order; that evaluation is recoverable from syntactic and interactional form; that interview narratives still address an outside observer even when monitoring drops. Risk: reifying the six-part model as a universal folk template; reportability hierarchies (danger of death always tellable) may be culture-specific; Danger of Death prompts are ethics-sensitive.

**Rosaldo strand.** Rosaldo, following Gallie, stressed **retrospective intelligibility** (later events made sense of by earlier ones) as distinct from predictive, hypothetico-deductive explanation (Hempel, Nagel). He warned that when analyst and subjects use **culturally divergent narrative forms** (e.g. A. L. Becker on Javanese *wayang*), the "double vision" breaks down more deeply than Gallie or Hexter allowed, and the analyst's "sovereign viewpoint" is challenged — a problem of the [[positioned-subject|positioned subject]]. He criticized Ricoeur for privileging the historian's synoptic judgment over protagonists' viewpoints and assuming all accounts aggregate into one master narrative.

The two strands **complement rather than replace** each other: Labov supplies clause-level structure and fieldwork elicitation; Rosaldo supplies process, agency, and critique of sovereign emplotment.

## History

**Linguistic founding line.** Labov & Waletzky 1967 established the structural framework; Labov 1972 ch. 9 (“The Transformation of Experience in Narrative Syntax”) expanded evaluation from section placement to internal syntax and age-graded device development, using south-central Harlem materials and comparison groups ([[south-central-harlem-youth]], [[labov-language-inner-city-study]]). Waletzky primarily carried out the narrative analysis for that chapter. The model became a reference point across sociolinguistics and narrative studies.

**Anthropological / historical line.** Rosaldo drew on W. B. Gallie, Louis Mink ("synoptic judgment"), Paul Ricoeur, J. H. Hexter, Hayden White, and Jerome Bruner, and on Ricoeur's reading of Braudel's Mediterranean as an implicit narrative. He positioned narrative as the form of his broader **"processual analysis,"** counterposed to systems/structural models (see [[culture]]).

## Exemplary Applications

**Labovian.** Fight and danger narratives from Harlem peer-group verbal leaders (Boot; Larry H. of the Jets; Norris W.) and adults (John L.); age tables of evaluative devices; contrast of personal vs. vicarious (TV) narratives; use as a technique for vernacular speech in variationist interviews ([[variationist-sociolinguistics]]).

**Rosaldo.** Rosaldo's readings of Ilongot hunting stories, of E. P. Thompson's *Making of the English Working Class* (a "melodramatic" emplotment), of [[turner-victor|Victor Turner's]] Ndembu "social drama," and of changing Chicano narratives (Américo Paredes; Sandra Cisneros) in [[cultural-borderlands|the borderlands]].

## Debates

Bears on the qualitative/interpretive vs. objectivist-explanatory divide in social analysis, and on the [[writing-culture-debate|Writing Culture]] critique of ethnographic realism. The Labovian strand additionally feeds the [[deficit-difference-debate]]: Labov used narrative skill and evaluative syntax in [[black-english-vernacular|BEV]] to challenge school-based pictures of linguistic inadequacy (closing claim of *Language in the Inner City* ch. 9, attributed). Tensions between universal structural skeletons and culturally specific narrative forms remain open—Rosaldo’s *wayang* problem and Labov’s reportability hierarchy are different angles on the same issue.
