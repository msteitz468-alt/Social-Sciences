## What It Does

Pollen analysis (palynology) reconstructs past vegetation by identifying and counting the pollen grains preserved in stratified deposits. Because pollen survives in identifiable form in unaerated sediments, counting a standard number of grains per sample at intervals through a section yields a sequence of vegetation change — and, once diagrams are zoned, a relative chronology that can be tied to archaeological material. The technique was developed for forest trees by the Swedish scientist Lennart von Post in 1916 and extended to the full range of pollen-liberating vegetation from 1937 (per Clark 1957).

## Procedure

Samples are taken at intervals through a polliniferous deposit (lake sediment, peat, buried soil); a standard count of grains per sample is identified to taxon and expressed as a spectrum; successive spectra are assembled into a pollen diagram and divided into zones. The most detailed results come from Late Glacial and postglacial lake sediments; regional zonation schemes (Knud Jessen's for Jutland; H. Godwin's for the British flora) provide the correlative framework.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The deposit must be unaerated and pollen-preserving. Applied to dry-ground monuments (pioneered by H. T. Waterbolk in the Netherlands; tried by G. W. Dimbleby in England) it works **only on sufficiently acid soils and fails on limestone and chalk** — which Clark flagged as "particularly unfortunate for British archaeologists," given how much British settlement sits on chalk (attributed, Clark 1957). For dating, the archaeological material must have entered the accumulating deposit accidentally, since an occupied surface itself represents a break in the sequence. Bog stratigraphy can mislead where hunters camped on floating peat "islands" that later sank (the Danish Aamosen case).

## History

From von Post's forest-tree work (1916) the method broadened through the 1930s–50s into a standard tool of environmental archaeology. Standard mid-century references Clark cites: K. Fægri & J. Iversen, *Text-book of Modern Pollen Analysis* (Copenhagen, 1950); H. Godwin, *The History of the British Flora* (Cambridge, 1956).

## Exemplary Applications

- **Detecting farming without artifacts.** Forest clearance registers as a fall in tree pollen and a rise in grass and herb pollen, so, in Clark's words, "the near presence of prehistoric farmers can be detected from pollen diagrams even though no archaeological material whatever is available" — the empirical hinge of [[economic-prehistory]]. At Hoxne, Middle Acheulian flints coincide with an abrupt forest-pollen decline, corroborating their primary context.
- **Multi-phase site correlation.** Peacock's Farm, Shippea Hill (Cambridgeshire fens): three stratified occupation phases (Late Mesolithic, Neolithic, Early Bronze Age) tied to sedimentary changes by microscopic pollen study.
- **Dating portable organic objects** (bog-found sledge-runners, skis, paddles, timber trackways) by fitting them into the pollen zonation.

## Debates

Pollen analysis is one of the natural-science methods that let archaeology build an environmental chronology independent of [[typological-method|typology]]; its results are cross-checked against grave-goods and [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] dates. It complements rather than competes with these, and its farming-clearance signature is central to reconstructing past economic life (see [[economic-prehistory]]).

## From Renfrew & Bahn (2012)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]* 6th ed., Ch. 6.)* Renfrew & Bahn credit Lennart von Post with developing palynology: durable pollen exines are extracted from suitable sediments (acid peat, lake beds, caves), identified by morphology, and plotted as percentage curves. Adjustments are required for **differential pollen production and transport** (e.g. wind-blown pine vs local insect-pollinated lime), site orientation, mixing/intrusion, and human impacts; samples should be taken **outside as well as within** archaeological sites. Postglacial pollen zones characterize changing plant communities; deep-time applications include Bonnefille’s wetter Hadar/Omo environments ~3.5–2.5 mya. Fallahogy (N. Ireland) shows forest clearance c. 4150 BC with grass/sorrel/plantain rise then regeneration — evidence of non-intensive early farming. **Phytoliths** complement pollen (survive sediments hostile to pollen); Piperno’s Gatun Basin (Panama) work found maize and clearance ~4850 BP in phytoliths, about 1000 years earlier than pollen, attributed to small clearings masked by forest pollen rain. Insect evidence (e.g. *Scolytus scolytus* as Dutch elm disease vector) can offer natural alternatives to purely anthropogenic explanations of the ~5000 BP elm-pollen decline (attributed).

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]].)*

Roberts calls palynology "the single most important branch of palaeoecology for the late Pleistocene and Holocene" and works through its taphonomy (the life → death → fossil assemblage model; differential production and dispersal, Andersen's 1973 productivity weightings). He distinguishes **percentage** from **absolute (influx)** pollen counts — the latter revealing an early-Holocene "protocratic" biomass rise invisible in percentage diagrams (Bennett 1983) — and treats the regional pollen zones of Godwin as now known to be **time-transgressive**. He uses the mid-Holocene **elm decline** (~5800 Cal. yr BP) to introduce *equifinality* (one outcome, several possible causes: disease, farming, climate, competition), and reports numerical **pollen–climate response surfaces** (Webb; Bartlein et al. 1986) for palaeoclimate reconstruction, harder to apply in long-disturbed Europe. Cross-checked against [[coleopteran-analysis|beetle]] data showing vegetation lagged early-Holocene warming. See [[deforestation]], [[holocene-climate-change]].
