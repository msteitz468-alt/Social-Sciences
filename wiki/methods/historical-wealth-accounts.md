# Historical Wealth Accounts

## What It Does

Reconstructs national or private **capital stocks** over long periods and relates them to national income, yielding the [[capital-income-ratio]] β (stock ÷ annual income flow) and composition by asset class. In [[piketty-thomas|Piketty]]’s program (*[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]*), wealth accounts are the second main data family alongside [[top-income-shares]] fiscal series: they make the U-curve of capital intensity (Europe, Japan) and the private/public split legible as objects of analysis, not only of national accounting.

Piketty uses “capital” and “wealth” interchangeably for nonhuman assets that can be owned and exchanged on some market—real property plus financial and professional capital—explicitly **excluding** human capital (skills, training). Exception treated separately: slaves counted in slaveholder wealth in the southern United States before 1865.

## Procedure

1. **Define national wealth** as total market value of everything owned by residents and government that can be traded: nonfinancial assets + financial assets − financial liabilities. Identities:
   - National wealth = private wealth + public wealth
   - National wealth = domestic capital + net foreign capital
   - Domestic capital ≈ farmland + housing + other domestic capital (firms, infrastructure, machinery, patents, etc.)
2. **Assemble stocks** from national balance sheets, estate inventories, property records, and market valuations; express capital as years of national income (β = 6 means six years of income).
3. **Decompose** asset types and public vs private nets over time (Britain and France from early eighteenth century; Germany from ~1870; US from ~1770; multi-country 1970–2010 annual series for eight rich economies).
4. **Link to flows:** private saving (household saving + corporate retained earnings, net of depreciation ~10–15% of NI) and growth g enter the long-run law β = s/g ([[second-fundamental-law-of-capitalism]]).
5. **Estate and inheritance side:** estate-tax and gift records yield wealth concentration and annual inheritance flow $b_y = \mu \times m \times \beta$ (μ = average wealth at death / average wealth of living; m = adult mortality rate)—see French fiscal vs economic flow comparison in [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]].

**Illustrative 2010s levels (Piketty’s series):** developed-country β typically 5–6; Japan and Italy higher (>6); US and Germany lower (~4–5). Per capita illustration: national income ~€30,000–35,000; private net wealth ~€150,000–200,000. France 2012 (Table 3.1): national capital ~605% of NI; private ~574%; public net ~31%.

## Assumptions and Limitations

| Issue | Content (as stated in the book) |
|---|---|
| Market valuation | Asset prices volatile; short-run bubbles (Japan 1990; Spain ~2007 private capital ~8 years NI) dominate volume effects; long-run law assumes asset prices evolve with consumer prices on average |
| Public/private boundary | Government bonds are claims of lenders on taxpayers—excluded from *national* wealth, included in private; privatizations (Italy 1970–2010 private capital 240%→680% NI, public 20%→−70%) reallocate without creating national capital |
| Scope of wealth | Durable household goods treated as consumption (not capital); valuables ~5–10% of NI today; foundations/nonprofits usually <5–10% of private wealth |
| Pure land residual | β = s/g applies only to accumulable capital; pure natural-resource land can raise β without savings; Piketty’s residual pure land is small (~0.5–1 year NI historically and today for pure urban land) |
| Slavery | Adding slave market value to capital is ethically and conceptually “dubious” but measures importance for owners; US slave capital ~1.5 years NI late 18th–mid-19th c. (≈ farmland); South 2.5–3 years of *southern* NI |
| Coverage | Strongest for Britain, France (to early 1700s); Germany post-1870; US; Sweden; Japan (U-curve); non-rich world mostly from 1990, β ~4 years average; Global South thinner |
| Tobin’s Q | Market/book firm valuations differ (Anglo ~100%+ vs “Rhenish” Germany/Japan often 50–80%)—market-value private wealth can understate book-value capital |

## History

Isolated national-wealth estimates ~1700 (Petty, King; Boisguillebert, Vauban)—often tax-modernization agendas; Lavoisier 1789/91; nineteenth-century capital-stock estimates prized before WWI (Giffen; Foville, Colson). Annual national-*income* series between wars (Kuznets/Kendrick US; Bowley/Clark Britain; Dugé de Bernonville France); postwar official GDP; wealth accounting revived 1990s–2000s with central banks. Piketty stresses national accounts are a **social construct** concerned with aggregates—distribution requires estate and tax sources.

Estate/gift tradition: French registration from the Revolution (1791–1901 proportional ~1–2%; progressive from 1901); British homogeneous probate for all property types mainly from ~1919–20; US federal estate tax 1916 covering a tiny minority. Collaborative extensions: Goldsmith balance sheets; Postel-Vinay & Rosenthal on Parisian estates; Zucman on Britain/France historical wealth and tax-haven stocks (unreported financial assets ~10% of global GDP as lower bound).

Piketty *Capital* packages multi-country β series as core evidence for [[piketty-capital-dynamics]] Parts One–Two and inheritance analysis in Part Three (Ch.11).

## Exemplary Applications

- **Britain and France 1700–2010:** β ~6–7 years of NI through the eighteenth–nineteenth centuries to 1914; plummet to 2–3 years in the 1950s; recovery to ~5–6 by 2010. Composition: farmland four–five years NI in early 1700s → <10% of NI today; housing from ~1 year to >3; other domestic capital ~1.5 → ~3 years. Pre-WWI foreign assets: Britain ~2 years NI; France >1 year; near zero after empire/wars.
- **Germany 1870–2010:** similar U-curve; private wealth >4 years NI by 2010 (below France/Britain); net foreign assets ~50% of NI by early 2010s; “Rhenish” stakeholder valuation lowers market β.
- **United States 1770–2010:** structurally lower β (colonial ~3 years; ~4–5 for much of twentieth century) than Europe; smaller U-amplitude; slave capital stabilizes total wealth ~4.5 years NI if included.
- **Eight richest countries 1970–2010:** private capital from 2–3.5 years NI to 4–7 years—[[patrimonial-capitalism|new patrimonial capitalism]]; privatization + slow growth + asset-price catch-up as three factors.
- **Global β:** ~500% today (≈ eve of WWI); central scenario toward ~700% by end of twenty-first century if s ~10% and g falls toward 1.5%.
- **Inheritance flow (France):** by = μ × m × β; annual inheritance/gifts ~20–25% of NI in nineteenth century → ~4–5% mid-twentieth → ~15% by 2010 (V-curve).

Landmark packaging: [[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-study]] Parts One–Two and Ch.11–12.

## Debates

Measurement of capital, pure land, human-capital stock accounting (Piketty rejects capitalizing labor income as “human capital stock” outside slave societies), Tobin’s Q / Rhenish capitalism, and β = s/g assumptions—see [[piketty-capital-debate]].
