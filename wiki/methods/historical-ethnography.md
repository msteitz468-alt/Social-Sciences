# Historical Ethnography

> Given its fullest methodological statement by [[comaroff-jean|Jean]] and [[comaroff-john-l|John L. Comaroff]] and exemplified in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991).

## What It Does

Historical ethnography reads the documentary record — here the [[christian-mission|missionary]] archive — with an ethnographer's eye for meaning, symbol, and everyday practice, in order to reconstruct the cultural world and consciousness of a past society. It is the operating method of [[historical-anthropology]].

## Procedure

Sources (letters, journals, memoirs, printed matter, oral testimony, material remains) are treated not as transparent windows but as *rhetorically constructed texts* whose form is itself evidence. The analyst reads **against the grain** — recovering the colonized "point of view" from recorded behavior, mockery, and the disrupted subtexts of colonizer accounts — and cross-reads the archive against later ethnography and the researcher's own fieldwork, flagging every use of ethnographic analogy.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The Comaroffs are explicit that "ethnographic research does not, of itself, equip anthropologists to read the social arrangements of an earlier period," and that in colonial archives "the Tswana speak through the European text." The method's characteristic threats: **archive politics and survivorship**, **the asymmetry** by which subordinated voices are mediated, and the **analogy problem** of projecting later ethnography (e.g., [[schapera-isaac|Schapera]]-era or 1970s material) back onto an earlier moment.

## History

Consolidated in the 1980s–90s as anthropology took its historical and reflexive turn; it converges with parallel work by [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] and the *Writing Culture* generation.

## Exemplary Applications

*[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]* — the reconstruction of the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]] world and the [[colonization-of-consciousness]] from the mission record.

## Debates

The method sits inside the wider dispute over whether subaltern consciousness can be recovered from colonial archives at all, and over the legitimacy of retrospective ethnographic analogy — the two chief lines of critique of *[[comaroff-revelation-revolution-study]]*. It also bears on the [[difaqane-mfecane-causation-debate]], a case where reading the same missionary/traveler record for and against colonial provenance yields opposed reconstructions.
