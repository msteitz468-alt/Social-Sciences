# Object Biography

## What It Does

Traces the **culturally shaped career** of a thing (or class of things) across production, exchange, use, diversion, [[singularization]], and disposal. Complements person-centered ethnography with what [[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]] calls **methodological fetishism**:

> "even though from a theoretical point of view human actors encode things with significance, from a methodological point of view it is the things-in-motion that illuminate their human and social context."

Concept twin: [[cultural-biography-of-things]] (Kopytoff).

## Procedure

Summary resolution only:

1. Identify the thing or class and the [[regimes-of-value]] it moves through.
2. Reconstruct path: origin, exchanges, uses, reclassifications, diversions from prescribed paths.
3. Note commodity **phase / candidacy / context** (Appadurai) and singularization events (Kopytoff).
4. Ask Kopytoff’s biographical questions (origin, ideal career, ages, end-state).
5. Relate trajectory to politics of demand, knowledge asymmetries, and status contests ([[tournaments-of-value]]).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes culturally available “career” structures; archival silence limits prehistoric cases (Renfrew’s Varna analysis works from grave wealth and ranking, not individual object life-histories). Does not replace production or class analysis — supplements exchange-centered and person-centered blind spots. Methodological fetishism is a **minimum** corrective, not a claim that things have agency independent of human attribution *(Appadurai)*.

## History

Programmatically stated in *[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]* (1986): Appadurai intro + Kopytoff essay. Intellectual roots: Rivers 1910 ownership paths; museum object histories; archaeological life-histories of artifacts; Bohannan spheres of exchange; Munn’s kula paths.

## Exemplary Applications

| Case (SLOT) | Biographical focus |
|---|---|
| Kopytoff slavery model | Person as temporary commodity |
| Davenport Solomon valuables | Commodity → sacred non-commodity transform |
| Geary medieval relics | Gift / theft / sale; authentication cycles |
| Spooner oriental carpets | Authenticity along weaver–dealer–consumer path |
| Cassanelli qat | Restriction breakdown → commodity expansion |
| Bayly Indian cloth | Collective biography; partial commoditization |
| Reddy French cloth | Knowledge regimes (connoisseur → entrepreneur) |

## In Archaeology (Lucas 2002)

[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]] (*[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches
to Fieldwork]]*, 2002) draws Kopytoff's biography idea into archaeological theory to argue
(attributed) that an artifact is itself an **"eventful context"** — a palimpsest of
manufacture, use-wear, breakage, and deposition — so that "the real dialectic is not between
matter and form, natural and cultural, but between past and present: the paradox of the
object… is a temporal paradox, not a material one." This underwrites his broader
temporalisation of the [[archaeological-record]] and the [[formation-processes|formation
process]] critique, and connects to his reading of typological essentialism (see
[[typological-method]]).

## Debates

Relation to Marxist production-centered analysis and to sharp gift/commodity dualism — Appadurai critiques overdrawn Marx–Mauss contrast *(attributed)*. Farriss’s foreword frames the volume’s ethnohistory problem: combining system with process over long trajectories.
