## What It Does

Paleomagnetic (geomagnetic) stratigraphy tracks reversals in Earth's magnetic field polarity, recorded in the alignment of ferromagnetic particles as volcanic rock cools or fine-grained sediment settles. Klein (1999) describes it as "perhaps best described as a cross between numerical and relative dating" — it establishes a fixed sequence of reversals but needs independent dating to anchor that sequence in calendar years.

## Procedure

Long, stable-polarity intervals are termed polarity chrons (formerly epochs); shorter reversed intervals within them are polarity subchrons (formerly events). The chron/subchron sequence for the past 5 million years has been dated by pairing radiopotassium readings on volcanic rock with paleomagnetic readings from the same rock, and by pairing paleoclimatic data with paleomagnetic readings from deep-sea sediment cores. Because polarity reversals occur globally and essentially simultaneously — unlike a biological taxon's time-transgressive first appearance in biostratigraphy — paleomagnetic boundaries provide globally consistent chronological markers once dated.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method itself cannot directly assign a calendar age; it requires either radiopotassium dating of the enclosing volcanic rock or paleoclimatic correlation of enclosing sediment. The existence and precise dating of some paleomagnetic subchrons, especially within the Brunhes Normal Chron, remains controversial, with only some examples widely accepted.

## History

The Matuyama Reversed Chron/Brunhes Normal Chron boundary is dated to roughly 780,000 years ago; the Pliocene/Pleistocene boundary is conventionally fixed at the top of the Olduvai Subchron, roughly 1.75 million years ago (see [[pleistocene]]).

## Exemplary Applications

At Zhoukoudian (Peking Man, China), paleomagnetic dating placed the Brunhes/Matuyama boundary below the earliest Homo erectus fossils and artifacts there, implying — combined with paleoclimatic correlation — that the deposits span roughly 500,000 to 230,000 years ago. At Trinchera (Gran) Dolina, Atapuerca, Spain (see [[atapuerca-gran-dolina]]) — the oldest unequivocal hominid occupation site in Europe — paleomagnetic dating places the site's artifacts and fossils at roughly 800,000 years, below the Brunhes/Matuyama boundary. Discussed in Klein, *The Human Career* ([[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]].)*

For the Holocene, Roberts notes that full polarity reversals are too infrequent (last >700,000 years ago) to help; the useful signal is **secular variation** in declination, inclination, and intensity. London measurements show magnetic north swinging from 20°W (AD 1580–1820) to ~11°W today; **archaeomagnetism** of baked hearths extends the record before AD 1000 (Aitken 1990), and wet lake-sediment cores carry it deeper (Thompson and Oldfield 1986). Secular variation gives only relative ages until tied to an independently (e.g. ¹⁴C-) dated master curve; Holocene master curves exist for six world regions (Thompson 1984).
