# Collective Biography

## What It Does

**Collective biography** (closely related to historical **prosopography**) assembles standardised data on all members of a defined population — offices, birth year, education, career path, fate — to analyse group structure and change. Exemplar in this collection: Mawdsley and White's study of all CPSU Central Committee members, 1917–1991 ([[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study]]).

## Procedure

1. Define the population boundary (here: full and candidate CC members).
2. Build a biographical dataset from archives, directories, published stenograms.
3. Code variables (generation, education, sector/job-slot, ethnicity, turnover).
4. Analyse period slices and long-run trends; optionally combine with elite interviews.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes the boundary captures the theoretically relevant elite; official biographies under-report repression and informal power; token members complicate pure office-based models.

## History

Long used for Roman senators, medieval office-holders, and modern parliamentary elites; in Soviet studies, a way past "squat, flat-capped figures" anonymity (Mawdsley & White preface, citing Hayter and Hough).

## Exemplary Applications

[[mawdsley-white-soviet-elite-study]] (N=1,932 CC members; 22 CCs; job-slot tables; four generations; turnover/ATR; 15 non-famous career vignettes; ~100 elite interviews 1992–93). Related contrasts cited there: Politburo-only studies (Löwenhardt et al.); Rigby on full party membership.

## Debates

Elite boundary disputes; quantitative prosopography vs ethnographic/interview depth; Galton-like non-independence of careers within a single appointment system; missingness in official biographies (especially repression).
