# Hypothetico-Deductive Method

## What It Does

The hypothetico-deductive (H-D) method frames inquiry as: state a hypothesis, deduce
its observable consequences, test those consequences against data, and generalize or
reject accordingly. The **[[processual-archaeology|New Archaeology]]** adopted it —
via the covering-law model of Carl Hempel — as the route to a scientific,
law-seeking archaeology, against the untested description of culture history.

## Procedure

On Johnson's textbook example (attributed): *Hypothesis* — early states involve
differential access to resources; *Test* — excavate an early-state cemetery and
analyse diet chemically; *Deduction* — élites ate more meat; *Generalization* —
early states show such differential access, subject to further testing.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Presupposes that observations are theory-neutral "facts" against which hypotheses can
be checked — a premise attacked from the philosophy of science (observation is
theory-laden; data underdetermine theory). Critics note that archaeologists rarely
follow the scheme in its pure deductive form, and that confirmation of predictions
does not prove a hypothesis true. Johnson uses the method to stage the wider debate
over **[[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate|archaeology as science]]**.

## History

Introduced into archaeology in the 1960s as part of the New Archaeology's
positivist program; increasingly qualified after post-processual critiques of
positivism and objectivity. Johnson (2011, attributed) links the H-D method
specifically to the narrower, methodological sense of **[[positivism]]** (separating
theory from method, requiring generalizing/predictable explanation) rather than to
Comte's broader developmental thesis, and reports that New Archaeology's early search
for universal behavioural "laws" proved very difficult, settling instead into a
"softer" approach to covering laws — broad cross-cultural generalizations rather than
strict laws (see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]]).

## Exemplary Applications

Processual studies of settlement, subsistence, and social organization framed as
explicit hypothesis-testing.

## Debates

Central to the [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate|science-or-humanism debate]]
and the [[processual-postprocessual-debate|processual/postprocessual debate]].
