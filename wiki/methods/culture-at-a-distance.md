# Culture at a Distance

## What It Does

**Culture at a distance** is the wartime and early-postwar anthropological practice of reconstructing the cultural patterns of a society the researcher cannot enter — typically an enemy nation in wartime — from documents, films, literature, propaganda, interviews with people raised in that culture who are available in the researcher’s country, and related secondary materials. *[[benedict-chrysanthemum-and-sword-1946|The Chrysanthemum and the Sword]]* is the method’s most famous product. The phrase is a later methodological label for what Benedict describes without using those exact words: studying Japan while barred from a field trip.

## Procedure

As reconstructed from Benedict’s Chapter 1 method statement:

1. **Define applied questions** (propaganda, surrender, occupation, enemy habits of thought and emotion, popular sanctions).
2. **Interview** people reared in the target culture living in the researcher’s country — concrete personal experience, not only elite opinion.
3. **Read** extensively (residents’ accounts, self-writing, antiquarian detail) in a Darwin-style note-taking mode: “What is wrong with this picture?”
4. **View films** produced in the target society with co-interpreters who see characters as locals would.
5. **Treat wartime behavior** (POW testimony, propaganda themes, combat conventions) as cultural data.
6. **Compare** institutions with other known cultures (Pacific/Asia for Benedict) for likeness and difference.
7. **Synthesize configurationally** — seek the “design for living” / lenses (“spectacles”) that make isolated acts consistent, rather than trait lists or polling-first designs.

## Assumptions and Limitations

**Assumptions (Benedict’s):** Cultures are patterned wholes ([[configurationism]]); customary practices are widely shared enough that a few confirmations suffice for etiquette-level facts; qualitative recovery of assumptions must precede polling; difference is an analytic asset.

**Limitations:** No participant observation in the target society; informants are selected by migration, internment, capture, or exile; literature and film are produced under ideological constraints; the applied brief (defeat the enemy / manage occupation) shapes which patterns are salient; the method easily produces [[national-character]] essentialism. See [[national-character-debate]].

## History

Developed under WWII applied-anthropology programs (OWI Foreign Morale Analysis Division and related work involving Benedict, [[mead-margaret|Mead]], Clyde Kluckhohn, and others). Distinct from classic [[participant-observation]] field ethnography and from pure armchair library synthesis of the Frazer/Tylor type by its systematic use of living informants and media under access constraints. Professionally contested after the war as national-character work declined.
