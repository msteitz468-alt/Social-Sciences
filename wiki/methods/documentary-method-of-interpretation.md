## What It Does

The documentary method of interpretation treats "an actual appearance as 'the document of,' as 'pointing to,' as 'standing on behalf of' a presupposed underlying pattern. Not only is the underlying pattern derived from its individual documentary evidences, but the individual documentary evidences, in their turn, are interpreted on the basis of 'what is known' about the underlying pattern. Each is used to elaborate the other," as [[garfinkel-harold]] glossed it. The method was originally described, per Garfinkel, by [[mannheim-karl]] in the essay "On the Interpretation of Weltanschauung" as the search for "an identical homologous pattern underlying a vast variety of totally different realizations of meaning." Garfinkel adapted the concept in 1967 to argue that ordinary members of society — and, he contended, social scientists using survey and questionnaire methods — routinely rely on this same interpretive procedure to read scattered appearances as evidence of a coherent, intended state of affairs.

## Procedure

Garfinkel's demonstration (Chapter 3 of *Studies in Ethnomethodology*) is the "counselor" advice-giving experiment: ten undergraduates were solicited under the pretext that a psychiatry department was researching alternatives to psychotherapy. Each subject described a personal problem to a supposed "student counselor in training" and then asked a series of yes/no questions through an intercom, at least ten per subject. Unknown to the subjects, the sequence of "yes" and "no" answers was fixed in advance by a table of random numbers and bore no relation to the actual questions asked. Subjects nonetheless treated the arbitrary answers as genuinely responsive, continuously revising the sense of earlier questions to fit later answers ("answers in search of questions"), rationalizing apparent contradictions by imputing additional knowledge to the "counselor," and reporting surprise, on later reflection, at how actively they themselves had supplied the content of the advice they received.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method is inherently circular: because pattern and evidence are defined in terms of each other, no independent check can adjudicate whether the "underlying pattern" that emerges is genuinely present in the source material or merely projected onto it. Garfinkel argued that this circularity is not confined to lay reasoning or "soft" clinical judgment but underlies rigorous survey research and questionnaire coding as well, wherever an investigator must decide correspondence between an observed appearance and an intended event — a claim that unsettles any sharp boundary between common-sense and scientific interpretation. The phenomenon the method describes — that the sense of an utterance depends on, and is retroactively reshaped by, its context — is the substantive content later formalized as [[indexicality]] and [[reflexivity-of-accounts]], though Garfinkel does not use those exact terms in presenting the counselor experiment.

## History

Mannheim's original description of the method appears in his essay on interpreting a Weltanschauung, part of the sociology-of-knowledge tradition; Garfinkel credited Mannheim as having furnished "an approximate description of one process" and adapted the method for [[ethnomethodology]] in Chapter 3 of *Studies in Ethnomethodology* (1967), reframing it as continually "membershipping" a corpus of advice or knowledge rather than merely admitting propositions to a scientific record.

## Exemplary Applications

- [[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967]] — the counselor advice-giving demonstration (Chapter 3) and the husband-wife colloquy illustration (Chapter 2) are the method's canonical exemplars.
- [[garfinkel-harold]] — adapted and extended the method from Mannheim into the ethnomethodological program.

## Debates

The method's circularity is both its analytic point and the ground of dispute. Garfinkel
treated the mutual elaboration of evidence and pattern as an ineliminable feature of all
sense-making, lay and professional alike — including the coding and survey work of
conventional sociology. Critics counter that acknowledging interpretive circularity does not
force the conclusion that sociological findings lack objectivity. The disagreement is recorded
at [[ethnomethodology-and-conventional-sociology-debate]]; it is closely tied to the status of
[[indexicality]] and [[reflexivity-of-accounts]].
