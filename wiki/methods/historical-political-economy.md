# Historical Political Economy

## What It Does

Analyzes societies, states, and markets as **historically evolving** phenomena, asking how resources and [[social-labor|social labor]] are made available and commanded over time. In anthropology, [[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] (1997 preface) distinguishes this from market-economics techniques applied to state fiscal policy: his political economy studies foundations of different polities in changing trajectories, refusing to generalize capitalist categories to all times and places.

## Procedure

1. Establish a baseline (Wolf: world in 1400) of interlocking networks.
2. Trace expansion of systems of power (empire, trade, capitalism) across space-time.
3. Use [[mode-of-production|mode of production]] / related relational concepts as research guides.
4. Follow commodities, labor flows, and institutional forms that articulate local groups to wider fields.
5. Reassemble what case-study methods disassembled — connections first.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes interconnection is often consequential (not that everything connects to everything). Risks: teleology toward capitalism; underplaying contingency and local meaning; dependence on uneven secondary literatures. Wolf treats mode concepts as suppositions for discovery, not closed laws.

## History

Descends from classical political economy and Marx's critique of it; mid-20th-c. dependency and [[world-systems-analysis|world-systems]] programs; anthropological uptake via peasant studies and *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (1982).

## Exemplary Applications

- [[wolf-europe-people-without-history-study]] — global ethnohistory of expansion and capitalism
- Wolf's *Peasants* / *Peasant Wars* — agrarian political economy
- [[mintz-sweetness-and-power-study]] — sugar as production–consumption circuit; plantation as industrial form; drug foods and proletarian diet ([[mintz-sweetness-and-power-1985]])
