# Geophysical Survey

Ground-based (and related underwater acoustic) remote sensing that detects subsurface archaeological features by measuring physical contrasts in soils and sediments. Textbook synthesis follows [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Renfrew & Bahn 2012]] (Ch. 3). Closely related to broader [[remote-sensing]] and often paired with [[archaeological-survey]] and [[aerial-archaeology]].

## What It Does

Geophysical survey maps ditches, pits, walls, graves, hearths, kilns, metal, and other buried features **without excavation**, by active sensing (energy passed through soil, response measured) or passive sensing (natural magnetism/gravity measured without injected energy). Renfrew & Bahn (2012) prefer non-destructive ground-based methods before or instead of excavation to learn more about sites with less disturbance than invasive probes (rods, augers, STPs). Excavation remains the check on reliability: survey tells a little about a large area; excavation a great deal about a small area.

## Procedure

### Method suite (as presented in Renfrew & Bahn 2012)

**Ground-penetrating radar (GPR).** Short radio pulses; echoes map changes (ditches, graves, walls) and depth via travel time. Antenna dragged in walking-speed transects. Digital 3D datasets enable **time-slices** at successive estimated depths—revealing walls, rooms, and doorways for sample excavation instead of total uncovering.

**Earth resistance (resistivity).** Damper soil conducts better (lower resistance); silted ditches/pits retain moisture versus stone walls/roads. Works well for ditches/pits in chalk/gravel and masonry in clay. Remote + mobile electrode frames; variants include resistivity profiling/pseudosections and electrical tomography toward 3D/time-slices. Wheeled arrays mitigate slow ground contact. Usable in some urban areas near power lines/metal where magnetic methods fail.

**Magnetic survey.** Among the most widely used methods—especially for fired clay (hearths, kilns), iron objects, pits, and ditches. Measures slight distortions of Earth’s field. Baked clay (~≥700 °C) permanently aligns iron-oxide grains (**thermoremanent magnetism**; also basis of magnetic dating). Pits/ditches show higher magnetic susceptibility than surrounding subsoil. Instruments: fluxgate gradiometers (vertical component; dual sensors; often dual instruments/carts) vs more sensitive/expensive alkali-metal vapor (caesium) total-field systems. Computer filtering (e.g. directional filtering) enhances subtle anomalies; multi-sensor mobile arrays are common.

**Seismic / acoustic methods.** Sonar relocating tombs; oil-prospecting-style seismic for foundations; underwater echo-location and multibeam sonar for seabed terrain and wrecks (see [[underwater-archaeology]]). Gravitational anomalies for caves.

**Metal detectors.** Electromagnetic coils detect buried metal via field distortion—valuable for general results and locating modern near-surface metal. Widely used by non-archaeologists; archaeological value must be balanced against looting risk (Britain ~30,000 users; Portable Antiquities Scheme harnesses responsible amateurs, e.g. Staffordshire hoard).

**Thermal and geochemical complements.** Thermography (tiny temperature contrasts)—mostly aerial/space; ground cameras little used except concealed building variations. Vegetation indicators of recent disturbance. **Phosphate / geochemical survey** (e.g. sampling every meter): Swedish 1920s–30s work linked settlement to high soil phosphorus; phosphates most diagnostic of inorganic occupation residues; can map activity areas (stalls, drains, hearths)—slow complementary technique for zones already flagged by air/surface survey.

### Workflow norms

Integrate geophysics with surface survey, aerial images, and GIS layers; use results to site sample excavations or manage sites without total dig; re-survey as technology improves (non-destructive, repeatable).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Contrast requirement.** Features must differ physically from matrix (moisture, magnetism, dielectric properties, acoustic impedance). Smallest features (postholes) rarely show except on clearest high-resolution data—paralleling aerial detection limits.
- **Method-specific failures.** Resistivity fails if soil is too hard/dry; best on shallow single-phase rather than deep complex sites. Magnetics compromised by modern metal and power lines. GPR interpretation depends on velocity assumptions for depth conversion.
- **Ground-truthing.** Like satellite and aerial data, geophysical anomalies need excavation or probe verification when claims about function/date are required.
- **Invasive probes as alternative risk.** Rods/augers and STPs can damage fragile artifacts/features; geophysics preferred when contrast allows.
- **Metal detecting ethics.** Context destruction by unrecorded digging nullifies archaeological value of finds (see [[provenience]], [[archaeological-context]]).

## History

- Mid-20th-c. adoption of soil resistivity and magnetic prospection in archaeology; Carlo Lerici (Italy, 1950s) combined aerial photography and resistivity to locate 6th-c. BC Etruscan tombs, then used periscope/camera probes—examining ~3500 tombs, most empty (saving excavation), and finding >20 painted tombs.
- Late 20th-c. GPR time-slice visualization and large-area multi-sensor campaigns (e.g. Wroxeter) made city-scale plans without total excavation.
- Underwater acoustic remote sensing (proton magnetometer, side-scan, sub-bottom profiler, multibeam) developed in parallel with scientific wreck archaeology after ~1960.
- Geochemical phosphate mapping has roots in Swedish settlement archaeology of the 1920s–30s.

## Exemplary Applications

As reported in Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

- **Wroxeter (Viroconium) Hinterland Project (1994–97)** — Roman civitas capital ~78 ha, <1% previously excavated; ~63 ha gradiometer (>2.5 million points), ~15 ha resistance, >5 ha GPR plus seismics/conductivity/caesium magnetometry: elite buildings center/SW, artisans east/north, agro-industrial pitting NW, possible forum boarium, “reversed” magnetic data as major fire, Bronze Age ring ditches under defenses—most complete plan of a Romano-British civitas capital; repeatable as technology improves.
- **Forum Novum GPR** — walls/rooms/doorways guiding sample excavation.
- **Kanmachi Mandara burial mound (Japan, ~AD 350)** — mapped by 50-cm profiles where excavation was legally barred.
- **Kent Weeks Valley of the Kings sonar (1987)** — relocated lost tomb near Ramesses II (T-shaped, ≥150 chambers).
- **Lerici Etruscan tomb program** — resistivity + aerial + periscope.
- **Cefn Graeanog** — phosphate maps of Romano-British farm activity areas.
- **Teotihuacan** — later geophysical cave/tunnel mapping under Manzanilla (with surface survey tradition).
- **Underwater** — Bass et al. echo-location of Roman wreck after bronze statue in sponge net; multibeam 3D wreck images.

## Debates

- **How far can geophysics replace excavation?** Renfrew & Bahn position non-destructive methods as preferred first tools and sometimes as long-term alternatives for layout, but excavation remains central for activities, change through time, and confirmation.
- **Metal detectorists** — research partners (PAS) vs site vandals.
- **Active vs passive / multi-method combination** — no single technique suits all geologies; complementarity (resistivity where magnetics fail, etc.) is standard practice advice.
- **Urban and deep tell limits** — complex multi-phase deep deposits challenge resistivity and interpretation of overlapping anomalies.
