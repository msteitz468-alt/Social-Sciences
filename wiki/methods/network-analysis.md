# Network Analysis (Social)

Analytic approach that represents social structure as a set of actors and the ties among them, using graph-theoretic and sociometric tools to study paths, clusters, bridges, and diffusion. In this wiki's first primary treatment, [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]] (1973) argues that network analysis is the most fruitful **micro–macro bridge** for sociological theory.

## What It Does

Answers questions about how patterns of interpersonal (or organizational) relations enable or block the flow of information, influence, resources, and mobilization — questions that individual-attribute models and small-group psychology alone cannot settle. Distinctive leverage: **positional and relational** properties (bridge, density, path length) rather than only node attributes (class, attitude).

## Procedure

*(Summary resolution — not a manual.)*

1. **Specify nodes and relations** (persons, organizations; friendship, advice, job tip, etc.).
2. **Collect relational data** — sociometric choice, contact frequency, observed interaction, archival ties.
3. **Represent as graph or egocentric network** (ego + alters ± alters-of-alters).
4. **Compute or reason about structure** — density, path length, bridges/local bridges, components, clustering.
5. **Link structure to outcomes** — diffusion timing, job finding, collective action, encapsulation.

Granovetter's 1973 paper is largely **qualitative-structural** with mathematical leads in footnotes, not a full formal model.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Ties often treated as positive and symmetric (explicit 1973 restriction).
- Fixed-choice sociometry biases toward strong ties and under-represents weak ones — a validity threat Granovetter stresses for diffusion research.
- **Boundary problem:** who is in the network? Egocentric vs complete-network designs answer different questions.
- **Galton-like dependence:** ties are not independent observations; standard statistical assumptions fail without network-appropriate models (later literature).
- Content of ties (what flows) is analytically distinct from strength and is easily elided.

## History

- **Sociometry** (Moreno tradition) — precursor; long peripheral to core sociological theory (Granovetter's judgment, 1973).
- **Mathematical biophysics / graph theory** (Rapoport, Harary) — formal tools often motivated by neural rather than social nets.
- **British and colonial social anthropology** (Bott, Barnes, Mitchell, Boissevain, Kapferer) — egocentric networks, density, multiplexity in urban and work settings.
- **Harvard / White school** — structuralist network program; Granovetter acknowledges Harrison White as origin of the SWT paper.
- **Granovetter 1973** — portable middle-range theory of [[tie-strength]] and bridges; makes weak ties theoretically central.
- **Burt 1992** — the [[structural-holes|structural-hole]] apparatus: formal ego-network measures of redundancy, effective size, and [[network-constraint|constraint]] (implemented in the software STRUCTURE), extending the cohesion and structural-equivalence traditions and borrowing a hierarchy index from Coleman's mathematical sociology. See [[structural-holes-theory]].
- **Later (not yet ingested):** blockmodels, other software packages, statistical network models, online network data, economic sociology of [[embeddedness]].

## Exemplary Applications

- [[granovetter-strength-of-weak-ties-study]] — job information, diffusion reanalysis, community organization argument.
- Milgram small-world experiments (as reanalyzed in SWT).
- Rapoport & Horvath large sociogram tracing.
- Coleman, Katz, and Menzel medical innovation (diffusion; centrality vs marginality debate).
