# Use-Wear Analysis

Microscopic study of wear traces on artifacts (especially stone, also bone, antler, shell, and textile fibers) to reconstruct how tools were used and on what materials. Also called **microwear analysis**. Textbook synthesis follows [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Renfrew & Bahn 2012]] (Ch. 8 Technology). Closely paired with [[experimental-archaeology]] for calibration baselines.

## What It Does

Even hard stone retains polishes and striations from use. Renfrew & Bahn (2012) report that stone tools sometimes survive so little damaged that archaeologists can examine microscopic patterns of wear on cutting edges and learn, for example, whether tools cut wood or animal hides. In their chapter summary position, **despite ethnography and experimental archaeology, only microwear studies can prove how a stone tool was used and what material it was used on**—because one tool can serve many purposes and the same tasks can be done by many tools.

Use-wear also narrows functional hypotheses for organic implements (e.g. perforated antler batons) and distinguishes human from natural perforations and long-term handling traces on shell, bone, antler, and ivory. SEM fiber microwear on textiles distinguishes cutting, tearing, and fatigue.

## Procedure

As synthesized from Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

1. **Select specimens** with edges/surfaces sufficiently preserved (not heavily rolled or reworked beyond reading).
2. **Clean carefully** with research questions in mind—over-cleaning can destroy possible blood residues and other traces (field-lab rule; see [[residue-analysis]]).
3. **Microscopy:** optical and especially SEM-enabled examination of polishes, striations, and microflaking.
4. **Compare to experimental reference collections** of tools used for known tasks on known materials.
5. **Assign worked-material and motion categories** where traces match calibrated types.
6. **Integrate with spatial analysis** (refitting, activity areas) when assemblage-scale patterning is the goal.

**Keeley’s calibrated categories** (durable polishes, verified in a blind test): wood, bone, hide, meat, antler, and non-woody plants; plus motion traces (piercing, cutting, scraping).

**Complementary lithic analytics:** refitting demonstrates manufacturing sequence and sometimes movement; microdebitage (<1 mm knapping “sawdust” from wet sieving/flotation) pinpoints stoneworking locations rarely cleared away—neither is use-wear proper, but both are routinely combined with it.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Experimental calibration is essential.** Without known-task reference tools, polish types are underdetermined.
- **Equifinality and debate history.** After Semenov, debate focused on striations vs microflaking as primary indicators before SEM-enabled polish discrimination stabilized material categories (as textbook narrative presents it).
- **Multi-function tools.** Functional proof is use-episode specific; typology alone cannot assign function.
- **Post-depositional modification.** Vertical mixing, trampling, and reuse can add non-use traces; spatial studies (e.g. Rekem) must show that disturbance has not erased horizontal activity patterning even when vertical mixing occurs.
- **Organic tools.** Distinguishing purposely made bone tools from natural accidents remains difficult for pre-Upper Paleolithic claims; wear helps but context matters.
- **Not a substitute for residues.** Hafting mastics, blood, lipids, and starches are [[residue-analysis]] domains that may co-occur on the same tools.

## History

- **Sergei Semenov (1957)** pioneering microwear work showing polishes and striations on hard stone.
- **Post-Semenov debate** on which microscopic attributes carry functional information.
- **Lawrence Keeley** SEM-enabled program with experimental tool kits and blind-test verification of material-specific polishes—standard reference point in Renfrew & Bahn’s account.
- Integration with refitting and spatial archaeology (e.g. Late Upper Paleolithic Rekem) as late 20th-c. practice.
- Extension to bone/antler function (Glory; Campana), shell perforation criteria (Peter Francis; d’Errico), and textile fiber SEM.

## Exemplary Applications

As reported in Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

- **Keeley experimental/blind-test program** — six worked-material categories plus motion traces.
- **Rekem, Belgium (Late Upper Paleolithic)** — Marc De Bie’s refitting and Jean-Paul Caspar’s microwear of ~25,000 lithics across 16 concentrations: projectile manufacture in isolated knapping spots, disposal of used points near hearths, segregated fresh vs dry hide scraping; post-depositional vertical mixing did not erase fine-grained horizontal activity patterning.
- **Perforated antler batons** — of 40+ functional hypotheses, André Glory’s wear-pattern study indicated rubbing by thong/rope, narrowing functions; Douglas Campana’s analysis of a perforated deer scapula from Mugharet El Wad supports shaft-straightening, with experimental support (ethnography classically favors arrowshaft-straighteners).
- **Bone/shell perforation microscopy (d’Errico; Francis)** — human vs natural perforations; handling traces.
- **Textile fiber SEM** — cutting vs tearing vs fatigue on arid-preserved Andean and Egyptian cloth.
- **Early African bone fragments** — wear suggesting termite foraging (expediency tools debate context).

## Debates

- **Striations vs microflaking vs polish** as primary functional signals (historical methodological debate).
- **Proof standard.** Textbook summary privileges microwear over ethnography/experiment for stone-tool function—specialists may still treat all three as a triangulation set.
- **Pre-Upper Paleolithic bone tools** — existence of shaped organic tools vs natural bone modification.
- **Blind-test generalizability** — how far Keeley-type categories travel across raw materials, post-depositional environments, and multi-use tools.
- **Combination with residues** — blood-residue claims (Loy) remain controversial; use-wear and residues should be designed together in sampling and cleaning protocols.
