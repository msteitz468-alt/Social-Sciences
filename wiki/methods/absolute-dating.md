# Absolute Dating

## What It Does

**Absolute dating** assigns ages in years (calendar years or years before a fixed present) rather than only earlier/later order. In Colin Renfrew and Paul Bahn’s textbook framing (*[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice]]*, 6th ed. 2012, Ch. 4), absolute dates are what let archaeologists ask how quickly changes such as the introduction of agriculture occurred, and whether they happened simultaneously or at different times in different regions (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

They contrast this with **[[relative-dating]]**, which orders artifacts, deposits, societies, and events into sequences without knowing stage durations or calendar ages (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). Absolute and relative systems are complementary: an absolute date on one object in a sealed deposit can anchor the deposit and associated finds; interconnecting stratigraphic sequences with absolute methods is, in their view, the most reliable basis for dating sites and contents (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

## Procedure

### Regular time-dependent processes

As Renfrew & Bahn present it, any absolute method depends on a regular, time-dependent process. The earth’s annual solar cycle can generate measurable sequences (calendars, tree-rings, varves) when those sequences are long, gap-free, linked to the present, and related to the structures or artifacts under study (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

Radioactive decay is treated as uniquely regular among natural clocks—uninfluenced by temperature or other environmental conditions—so “radioactive clocks” (especially [[radiocarbon-dating]]) underpin modern absolute chronology. Other natural processes can order samples relatively and yield absolute ages only if independently calibrated, often afresh per site or region (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

### Conventions (BP, BC/AD)

Scientists using radioactive methods count years back from the present (BP), taking BP to mean **before 1950**—approximately the year of the first radioactive dating method, radiocarbon. For recent millennia Renfrew & Bahn recommend converting BP into BC/AD for clarity. For the Paleolithic they use “BP” and “years ago” interchangeably; even the most precise Paleolithic dates give only coarse intervals of thousands of years, so research design shifts toward long-term evolutionary questions rather than event-by-event “conventional history” (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

### Historical chronologies and termini

Where literate historical chronologies exist, absolute dating may rest on reconstructing a ruler list, linking it to our calendar, and associating artifacts or structures with that list (e.g. an inscription naming a dated ruler). Egyptian dynastic chronology, Maya Long Count, and coin *termini post quem* are textbook cases; exports/imports enable **cross-dating** into neighboring non-literate lands. Outside historic/literate lands, Renfrew & Bahn state that cross-dating and broad typological comparisons have been almost entirely superseded by scientific absolute methods, so that “all the world’s cultures can be assigned absolute dates” (as of their 2012 synthesis; attributed position).

A coin or named artifact dates its *manufacture*; inclusion in a sealed deposit establishes only a *terminus post quem*—the deposit is no earlier than that date and may be much later (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Precision shapes research design.** How archaeologists carry out research “depends very much on the precision of dating obtainable for the period of time in question” (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).
- **Error is partly inevitable.** Age brackets over centuries or millennia can be unavoidable; Renfrew & Bahn hold that while dating science is refined, **the main source of errors remains the archaeologist**—poor sample choice, contamination, or misinterpretation of results (attributed position).
- **Method-specific material and range limits.** Historical chronologies require literacy and association; [[dendrochronology]] and [[varve-analysis]] need annual records linked to the present; radiocarbon needs organic carbon and calibration; [[potassium-argon-dating]] needs volcanic rock; [[thermoluminescence-dating]] needs fired crystalline minerals and dose-rate control.
- **Correlation caution.** Correlating methods strengthens chronology (e.g. tree-rings calibrate radiocarbon), but long-distance “teleconnections” of tree-rings or varves must be treated carefully—risk of plausible but incorrect matches (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

## History

As of the 2012 textbook, Renfrew & Bahn report that only in the last ~60 years have independent means of absolute dating become widely available, transforming archaeology; before then virtually the only reliable absolute dates were historical ones (e.g. Tutankhamun’s reign) (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

**Hierarchy of methods (their ranking):** the three most commonly used absolute methods for archaeologists are calendars/historical chronologies, tree-ring dating ([[dendrochronology]]), and [[radiocarbon-dating]]. For the Paleolithic, [[potassium-argon-dating]] and uranium-series are vital; genetic dating is beginning to date population events. Before radioactive methods, varves and dendrochronology were the most accurate scientific clocks; “today… radiocarbon is the single most useful dating method” (attributed positions, Renfrew & Bahn 2012 summary). Trapped-electron methods ([[thermoluminescence-dating]], optical dating, ESR) overlap radiocarbon and can go earlier.

## Exemplary Applications (as textbook synthesis)

- **Historical:** Egyptian dynasties linked to Alexander 332 BC; Maya Long Count zero date 13 August 3114 BC (Gregorian) as they report; coin termini and Egyptian export/import cross-dating.
- **Annual cycles:** Scandinavian [[varve-analysis]] for ice-sheet retreat ~13,000 years ago; Southwestern and European [[dendrochronology]] (Pueblo villages, Sweet Track, LBK wells).
- **Radioactive clocks:** [[radiocarbon-dating]] for the last ~50,000 years worldwide; K-Ar/Ar-Ar at Olduvai, Hadar, Atapuerca; uranium-series on cave carbonates; multi-method Atapuerca early Europe chronology.
- **Correlations:** tephrachronology (volcanic ash “freeze-frame”); Thera eruption dating controversy as a case where ice-core, tree-ring, radiocarbon, and Egyptian-linked ceramic chronologies disagree (attributed framing, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

World-prehistory outlines they attach to absolute frameworks (early hominins, Out of Africa expansions, peopling of Americas/Oceania, independent origins of food production, early states) are textbook syntheses as of 2012—not primary field findings of this wiki page.

## Debates

- **What counts as “absolute.”** Historical chronologies, annual counts, and radioactive methods all claim year-scale ages but rest on different assumptions and error structures.
- **Archaeologist vs laboratory error.** Renfrew & Bahn’s position that field context and sample handling dominate errors redirects debate from pure physics to [[archaeological-context]] and [[provenience]].
- **Correlation vs single-method reliance.** Their preferred future is multi-method correlation; single dates without relative frameworks are weak.
- **Precision vs period.** Paleolithic research cannot reconstruct conventional event-histories; later periods can support generation-scale Bayesian chronologies when ¹⁴C and stratigraphy are combined (see [[radiocarbon-dating]]).
