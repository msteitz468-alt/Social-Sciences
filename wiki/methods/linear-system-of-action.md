# Linear System of Action

The formal apparatus of Part V of
[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|*Foundations of Social Theory*]], in
which [[coleman-james|Coleman]] restates the verbal theory of
[[rational-choice-theory|rational choice]] as a mathematical system. Actors hold
**control** over events/resources and have **interests** in them; exchange
redistributes control until a **competitive equilibrium** is reached, at which the
**value** of each event and the **power** of each actor are determined
simultaneously.

> Attributed to Coleman. This is a model, not a measurement instrument — its
> "results" are theorems about an idealized system, not empirical findings.

## What It Does

Given a matrix of actors' **control** over events (c_ij) and a matrix of their
**interests** (x_ji), the model derives an equilibrium distribution of control, a
**value** for each event (v_j, its system-wide exchange rate), and a **power** for
each actor (r_i = Σ c_ij v_j, the summed value of what one controls). Power and value
are mutually defining: power resides in control of valuable events; value lies in the
interests powerful actors have in an event. The two-person, two-good exchange
(an Edgeworth box; the contract curve = the "core") is the base case; the system
generalizes to many actors and goods, where equilibrium control is c*_ik =
(x_ki · r_i)/v_k.

## Procedure

Coleman restricts utility to a **Cobb–Douglas** form (U_i = Πc_ij^{x_ji}, Σx_ji = 1),
sacrificing the generality economists retain in exchange for an algebraic micro–macro
correspondence and fit to quantitative data. The **competitive equilibrium** is the
solution point; Debreu and Scarf's (1963) theorem (the core shrinks to the
competitive point as actors → ∞) justifies focusing on it. The model is designed for
**empirical estimation** from observed pre- and post-exchange holdings (C, C*), so
values and powers can be recovered without observing subjective interests directly.
Extensions cover open systems, [[trust]] (a promise as a p-discounted good),
[[social-norm|norms]] and externalities (ch. 30), [[corporate-actor|corporate
actors]] (constitutional vs. postconstitutional stages), and [[the-self]]
(internalization as constructing an internal constitution).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Divisible, transferable control; a closed system (relaxed for open systems by fixing
external interests at internal values); fixed interests; the Cobb–Douglas form (unit
income- and price-elasticities). Externalities, public goods, and
[[free-rider-problem|free-rider problems]] are departures requiring added structure —
in the idealized "perfect social system" they vanish, so real deviations are
attributed to transaction costs and imperfect [[social-capital-coleman|social
capital]]. The competitive equilibrium is an analytical construct, not a claim that
real systems reach it.

## History

Grows out of Coleman's *Introduction to Mathematical Sociology* (1964) and his
work on collective decisions; presented as the formal counterpart to Parts I–IV.

Shares the standing critiques of [[rational-choice-theory]] over the realism of
