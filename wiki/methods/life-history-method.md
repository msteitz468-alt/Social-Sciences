# Life-History Method

The systematic collection and analysis of individual life-stories as sociological or anthropological evidence — the biographical document treated as data about social process, not merely about a person.

## What It Does

The life-history method reconstructs a life (or a set of lives) as a sequence unfolding in time, in order to study how social structures are inhabited, reproduced, and changed at the level of lived experience. Its classic warrant is that some objects — the formation of an identity, a conversion, a trajectory of mobility or marginalization, the making of a [[hegemonic-masculinity|masculinity]] — are *processes* that only a temporally extended, first-person account can capture.

## Procedure

A semi-structured narrative interview (or a set of personal documents — letters, diaries) elicits "the story of your life," typically using institutional transitions (school entry, first job, marriage) as memory anchors and covering the major structures of the subject's life across successive periods. Analysis commonly proceeds in two moves: (1) each life is written up as an individual case — narrative sequence plus structural analysis; (2) cases are compared in groups to find collective trajectories. In [[connell-raewyn|Connell]]'s [[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|use]], the structural grid is the three-fold model of gender (power, production, cathexis) and the dynamic unit is the "gender project."

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method trades breadth for depth — "one cannot sample a broad population … while gaining any depth." It depends on retrospective memory and on the subject's narrative construction of a coherent self; a purely textualist reading risks losing the life behind the story, while a naïve reading risks taking the story at face value. Samples are small and purposive, so findings illuminate particular situations rather than generalize to populations. Positionality and the interviewer–informant relation are part of the data, not noise.

## History

The method's foundational use is Thomas and Znaniecki's *[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-1918|The Polish Peasant in Europe and America]]* (1918–20), which built its analysis around migrants' letters and a long autobiography. It was central to the early [[chicago-school|Chicago School]]'s documentary sociology, drawn on by Sartre's "progressive-regressive method," codified for later researchers by Ken Plummer's *Documents of Life*, and used to study social change through lives (Blauner's *Black Lives, White Lives*; Connell's *Masculinities*).

## Exemplary Applications

- *[[thomas-znaniecki-polish-peasant-study|The Polish Peasant]]* (1918–20) — migration, disorganization, and reorganization through personal documents.
- The *[[connell-masculinities-lifehistory-study|Masculinities]]* life-history study (1995) — four groups of Australian men, to study the dynamics of masculinity.
- [[abu-lughod-lila|Abu-Lughod]] *[[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-1993|Writing Women's Worlds]]* (1993) — Migdim and other Awlad ʿAli women: event stories, songs, and arguments rather than a Western "life story" form (Migdim refused formal life-story requests in 1979 and deflected the form in 1986); narrative particularity as [[writing-against-culture]] ([[abu-lughod-writing-womens-worlds-study]]; [[life-history-method]] used as one strand within narrative ethnography).
- [[biehl-joao|Biehl]] *[[biehl-vita-2005|Vita]]* (2005) — single-life ethnography of Catarina: medical archives ("file self"), family interviews, genetic collaboration, and her 21-notebook "dictionary" as joint theoretical labor; dialogic form; verification against institutional records ([[biehl-vita-study]]).

## Debates

The standing methodological argument is between realist and narrativist readings of life-story data — whether the story is evidence about a life or only about the cultural forms of self-narration. Connell states the realist position pointedly: "if the language is all we can see, then we are missing the point of a life-history."
