# Cultural Ecological Method

## What It Does

The method of [[cultural-ecology]] (Steward) analyzes how a society's culture adapts to its environment by examining the functional interrelations among **environment**, **exploitative technology**, and **behavior patterns** involved in subsistence — then relating those to social organization features of the [[cultural-core]]. Program theory: [[cultural-ecology]]; evolutionary methodology: [[multilinear-evolution]].

## Procedure

[[steward-julian-h|Steward]] (*[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]*, Ch. 2) stated **three fundamental procedures**:

1. **Technology–environment.** Analyze the interrelationship of exploitative or productive technology and environment. In simpler societies this centers on subsistence devices (weapons, containers, transport, water/fuel, clothing/housing against climate); in more developed societies, agriculture, herding, manufacturing, and (in industrial worlds) capital, credit, and trade. Relevant environmental features depend on the culture (e.g. spacing of water holes for seed-gatherers; game habits for hunters; fish runs for riverine/coastal groups).
2. **Behavior patterns of exploitation.** Analyze behavior patterns involved in exploiting a particular area with a particular technology. Some patterns tightly constrain social life (e.g. seed-gathering women working alone or in small groups, fragmenting groups unless resources are abundant); others allow latitude (individual vs collective hunting; cooperative surrounds, drives, corrals).
3. **Effects on the rest of culture.** Ascertain the extent to which those behavior patterns affect other aspects of culture — the step that articulates ecology with social organization, demography, and the [[cultural-core]]. Steward held this third step requires a holistic approach.

He stressed that procedures must match the **complexity and level** of the culture: an independent foraging community and an outpost of a wealthy nation sustained by long-distance logistics are not the same ecological problem. Focus remains the cultural core; secondary features are left more open to diffusion and local history.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes culture (not only genetics) mediates human–environment relations; assumes core features are more tightly constrained by ecology than peripheral ones. Does not claim environment *causes* culture alone. Failures: over-reading necessity into core; underplaying politics, meaning, and long-distance institutional force (Steward addresses national systems partly in Ch. 4).

## History

Distinguished by Steward from biological ecology and from Chicago [[human-ecology]] / social ecology. Systematized in *[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]* (1955), Ch. 2 (newly written for the volume).

## Exemplary Applications

Great Basin Shoshoneans; [[patrilineal-band]]; composite hunting bands; Southwest lineage/clan; Carrier Indians; early irrigation civilizations; Puerto Rico patterns (*Theory of Culture Change* applications).

## Debates

[[cultural-evolutionism-debate]]; contrasts with [[historical-particularism]]; relation to environmental determinism (Steward rejects the charge).
