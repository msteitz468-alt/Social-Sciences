# Logico-experimental method (Pareto)

## What It Does

[[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]]’s methodological program for general sociology in *[[pareto-mind-and-society-1935|The Mind and Society]]*: study human society by **experience and observation** alone, seeking **uniformities** (laws) that are contingent within known limits of time, space, and experience — never the absolute, necessary, or certain (Ch I). The method underwrites [[paretian-sociology]]: classification of [[non-logical-conduct]], isolation of [[residues]] and [[derivations]], and analysis of [[paretian-social-equilibrium]].

## Procedure

Summary resolution (not a lab manual):

1. **Field.** Confine inquiry to the experimental field; keep it “absolutely and in all respects distinct” from the non-experimental (theology, pure metaphysics) (Ch I TOC; §§1–6).
2. **Principles as course-indicators.** State principles not as demonstrated dogmas binding all implications, but as indications of the path chosen among many possible paths (§§4–5).
3. **Definitions as labels.** Definitions are tags for things; names may be replaced by letters of the alphabet; nothing follows from etymology (§§ on language of the sciences; residue naming §868).
4. **Objective vs subjective.** Every social phenomenon has an objective aspect (as it is, relative to the classifier’s knowledge) and a subjective aspect (as it appears to actors) (§149).
5. **Classify elements, not wholes.** Concrete actions and theories are synthetic mixtures; classify constituent elements (logical/non-logical genera; residue classes; derivation classes) (§§147–148).
6. **Isolate *a* and *b*.** In non-logico-experimental theories, separate relatively constant residues (*a*) from variable derivations (*b*) by comparative elimination across cases (Ch V–VI).
7. **Keep truth ≠ utility.** Never infer social utility (or harm) from experimental absurdity alone, or experimental truth from social success ([[truth-utility-distinction]]).
8. **System and equilibrium.** Treat chosen elements as a social system; define equilibrium as the state that reacts to restore itself after artificial modification (§§2066–2076).
9. **Successive approximations.** Science develops continuously; laws are provisional uniformities open to revision.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Sentiment division.** Pareto held no one is free of bias but one can “divide himself in two” and lay sentiments aside in scientific work (Pasteur/Newton illustrations, §§142–143) — harder in social than natural science.
- **Indefiniteness of residues.** Residues “usually wanting in definiteness,” unlike scientific principles *A* (§§870–874) — epistemic cost of the taxonomy.
- **Quantitative ideal.** Full determination would require equations equal to unknowns; practically insuperable for the whole social problem; still useful for local properties (§2062).
- **Sampling.** Explicit confinement to Europe and Mediterranean (§2065).
- **Derivations as objects, not tools.** The investigator must not use persuasive derivations as scientific method (§1403).

## History

Named and systematized in the *Trattato* (1916/1923). Draws on pure-economics and engineering equilibrium habits. Distinct from Comtean [[positive-method]] (encyclopedic hierarchy, three stages) and Durkheimian social-fact rule, though all claim scientific sociology. English 1935 transmission via Livingston.
