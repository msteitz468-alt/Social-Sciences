## What It Does

Settlement-pattern archaeology treats the spatial arrangement of habitation sites, agricultural features, and monumental architecture across a landscape as a primary object of analysis — not merely as context for recovering portable artifacts. It reconstructs land use, community organization, and social/political structure from the distribution and form of built and modified landscape features themselves.

## Procedure

The approach involves systematic survey (rather than excavation alone) of a defined valley or district, mapping house platforms, agricultural terraces or field systems, ceremonial architecture, and their spatial relationships, then interpreting these patterns for evidence of settlement hierarchy, land tenure, and demographic scale.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Requires that surface (or near-surface) features be reasonably well preserved and mappable at landscape scale, and that contemporaneity among features can be established — a nontrivial problem where occupation spans centuries. Full-coverage survey is labor-intensive relative to targeted excavation.

## History

Introduced to Polynesian archaeology by [[green-roger]], who was trained in the settlement-pattern approach by [[willey-gordon-r]] at Harvard (attributed, Kirch 2017). Green first applied the method in the 'Opunohu Valley, Mo'orea, Society Islands (Green 1961; Green et al. 1967), then in a major multi-year project in Western Samoa (Green and Davidson 1969, 1974). Green's programmatic statement of the method's rationale (quoted, Green 1967b:102, via Kirch 2017): "the day has passed when such monuments or their structural features can afford to be treated only as contexts for portable artifacts and not as artifacts in their own right."

## Exemplary Applications

Green's 'Opunohu Valley and Western Samoa surveys; subsequent applications across Polynesia to reconstruct chiefdom-era land division and political organization (e.g., the Hawaiian ahupua'a land-division system).

### From Sharer & Traxler 2006 (Maya area)

All claims in this subsection attributed to [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]].

**Maya origin of the modern settlement program.** They credit [[willey-gordon-r|Gordon Willey]]’s **1953 Belize River valley / Barton Ramie Settlement Patterns Project** as the first major Maya investigation aimed solely at settlement remains apart from a major urban center (Willey had pioneered the approach in the Virú Valley, Peru). After Barton Ramie, full-range settlement study became standard for reconstructing social organization; nonelite household excavations showed commoners produced and consumed rich goods including polychrome pottery once thought elite-only. Processual archaeology (cultural ecology/materialism) drove mid–late twentieth-century growth via settlement-pattern studies, multidisciplinary projects (Tikal Project), and problem-oriented research (Cozumel).

**What settlement archaeology supplies.** Multi-source reconstruction of Maya social/political organization: settlement archaeology defines **form and spatial distribution** of occupation remains; deciphered texts uniquely record elite kinship, succession, and alliances. Research bias long favored large Classic cities over smaller/non-Classic sites and elite architecture over the nonelite majority (well over 90% of past Maya people, absent from royal histories).

**Household units and lexicon.** Basic unit begins with material traces of households (nuclear family assumed by analogy). House mounds = platforms for perishable *naah*; minimum roofed space ~20 m²; not all mounds are dwellings (shrines, kitchens, storage). Yukatek settlement terms mapped to archaeological units: *naah* (house); *naahlil* (household cluster, typically 2–6 residences); *chinaah* (ward/barrio); *caah* (town/city/site). Tikal **Plaza Plan 2**: patio with three residential sides and eastern ancestral shrine over founder burial.

**Late Classic landscape.** By Late Classic most lowlands covered by settlement (cities, rural communities, isolated families), densest near water and good soils; hinterlands integrated into polities ruled from capitals and subordinate centers. Capitals concentrate monumental architecture, ball courts, palaces, fortifications, dynastic monuments. Structure densities (Rice & Culbert 1990 as summarized): central Copan highest (1,449 strs/km²); Late Postclassic Mayapan ~986–996; most Classic sites 200–450; hinterlands often ~100 strs/km² (city/rural edges blurred; Tikal earthworks help). Hinterland population densities ~200 people/km² LC (Tikal–Yaxha; Peten lakes); upper Belize River valley around Xunantunich 340–525/km². Combining urban ~600 with rural low-end ~200 implies Late Classic central lowland populations “in the tens of millions” — one of the densest preindustrial societies — raising overpopulation/environmental stress as factors in Terminal Classic political failures.

**Population methods and contemporaneity problem.** Estimates from residential structure counts × average family size, adjusted because not all structures occupied simultaneously; thorough compilations use ~**150 years** average occupational span per household cluster. Uncounted structures corrections: Tikal ~+10%; Copan +39–50%; Santa Rita Corozal +50–100%; Sayil also estimated from chultun water capacity. Chronological control (14C, pottery styles) generally not precise under **50–100 years**; Classic calendrical inscriptions are exceptions but rare and elite-associated. Dual population peaks Late Preclassic and Late Classic (larger), each followed by decline (Terminal Classic most precipitous).

**Hierarchy signatures.** Chiefdoms: three-tier site hierarchies; states: four-tier hierarchies with palaces, fortifications, territorial boundaries (their Table 2.1). Dispersed tropical settlement treated as adaptive (in-city food production) rather than evidence against true cities — Tikal Project established preindustrial urban scale against empty “ceremonial center” models. Further Maya applications: Willey Copan Valley Project 1975–77; Sabloff & Tourtellot Sayil 1983–88; local-community work (Rio Amarillo near Copan; Xunantunich hinterlands; Three Rivers, northern Belize); Mayapan dense walled packing (~4,000 buildings, up to ~20,000 people).

**Sampling honesty.** Current understanding rests on well under **ten percent** of the archaeological record; looting destroys context; almost every new excavation can overturn prior conclusions.

### Huron country: Heidenreich's corpus and Trigger's method demonstrations (from *The Children of Aataentsic*, 1976/1987)

All claims in this subsection attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]], *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]*, drawing on Conrad Heidenreich's settlement survey (Heidenreich 1971; Heidenreich et al. 1969).

**Base survey.** Heidenreich's corpus (village size, field acreage, house dimensions, trail networks) documents 18–25 Huron villages by the 1630s, about six large ones (40+ multi-family dwellings, fortified, population 1,500–2,000 each), average village 4–6 acres (up to 15 acres reported), sited on defensible terrain with breaks in slope on two or more sides; villages relocated roughly every 10–15 years as soil and firewood depleted; over 200 miles of trails linked settlements.

**Site identification, held uncertain.** Cahiagué, the Arendarhonon rallying town Champlain visited in August 1615, is only tentatively identified with the Warminster archaeological site, Medonte Township: two adjoining palisaded villages (9.5 and 5 acres) within 600 feet of each other, forming an apparent single large double village, massive palisades of three to seven rows of staggered posts, an estimated ~100 houses and ~3,000 people, cornfields over 1,000 acres. Trigger explicitly withholds certainty — he notes it is "strange" Champlain never mentioned a divided double village, and treats the Cahiagué/Warminster correspondence as unresolved pending fuller publication of the excavation data.

**The "index of female movement" method.** Because pottery-making was women's work and vessels were rarely carried on overland trips (too fragile and heavy), foreign-type pottery on a site is read as an index of foreign-born women present via marriage, refuge, or captivity; asymmetric distribution (host-type pottery absent from the "sending" village) can distinguish captive-taking/conquest from ordinary intermarriage or exchange. Trigger states the method's own limits explicitly: it cannot distinguish among proto-Huron groups themselves, whose material culture was too similar, and is impractical before the late Iroquoian period, when ceramic styles were still widespread across groups.

**House-floor and ceramic-clustering residence inference, with caveats.** Trigger uses house-floor artifact distributions (e.g., the Summer Island site, per Brose 1970) to infer household composition and residence rules from spatial patterning of gendered artifact types. He also reports Deetz's (1965) prediction that matrilocal communities should show greater clustering of ceramic design attributes than patrilocal ones (related women living together transmit pottery styles to daughters), and Whallon's (1968) finding that stylistic clustering in Owasco-era New York pottery increased through the Owasco sequence but was already high in its early phase — read as evidence matrilocal residence was established by Early Owasco times. Trigger flags the underlying economic-dominance premise (Schneider and Gough 1961, "a team of a woman and her daughters") as contestable, arguing male activities (fishing, forest clearance, house-building, trade, defence) were of comparable economic importance — a caution against over-reading the ceramic-clustering proxy as a settled result.

**Trade-goods site-count dating.** Trigger dates the onset of the Huron fur trade using the changing quantity and type of European trade goods recovered per site (his own extrapolation, which he calls "highly circumstantial," on the order of ~29 sites per decade), placing the earliest small quantities of goods (iron celts and awls, copper knives, sheet brass, rolled brass/copper beads, no glass) in the late 1580s — corroborated independently by William C. Noble's (1971) ceramic-sequence dating of "not much before 1580." Slightly later sites (c. 1615) add brass rings, awls, beads, iron hatchets, and glass beads.

**Radiocarbon dates — flag likely uncalibrated.** Several prehistoric Iroquoian sites Trigger cites carry bare "radiocarbon dated" or "A.D." labels with no calibration or ± range stated in the text (e.g., the Miller site, Pickering culture, "radiocarbon dated c. A.D. 1125"; the Bennett site, c. A.D. 1260; Laurentian Archaic dates as early as 3290 BCE at Allumette Island I versus 4610 BCE in New York). Given the 1970s publication date, these are almost certainly uncalibrated radiocarbon determinations and should not be compared directly against calibrated dates elsewhere in this wiki without re-derivation.

## From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — Formative Mesoamerica

All claims attributed to Flannery, Plog, Earle, Reynolds, Winter, and named third parties as cited in *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* (1976); study [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]]. Complements [[archaeological-survey]] (Plog efficiencies) and [[site-catchment-analysis]] (regional resource rings).

### Settlement pattern vs settlement system (Flannery)

Flannery drew a load-bearing distinction for Formative regional work: a **settlement pattern** is the pattern of sites on the regional landscape, empirically derived by sampling or total survey; a **settlement system** is the set of “rules” that generated the pattern in the first place. He called the leap from complex patterns to the systems that generated them “difficult but important,” noting (via Haggett) that Christaller/Lösch central-place models are static while hierarchy is temporal. Two Formative pattern types treated at length: **linear river strings** of villages; **dispersed nonlinear hierarchical** systems with spacing regularity.

Complex Formative systems (Flannery after MacNeish, Oaxaca surveys) integrate multiple site types — hamlets, small/large villages, winter microband camps, summer/fall macroband camps — not a single “village” type. Hierarchical survey data convert “dots” (Plog Ch. 5) into tiered levels (Earle Ch. 7). Flannery argued major central-place roles were unlikely in the **Early** Formative outside Veracruz–Tabasco; more by Middle Formative; widespread Late Formative–Classic. Early Mesoamerican central-place services, he suggested, were more ceremonial-civic than economic; three-tiered hierarchy plus upper-tier equidistance can arise at **chiefdom** level with cacique competition (Earle: equidistant spacing from competition for support/tribute, not only Western marketing). Christaller vs Lösch hierarchies distinguished: Marcus (1974) lowland Classic Maya more Löschian; Earle implied similar for Gulf Coast intermediates (e.g. Potrero Nuevo local admin not concerning San Lorenzo).

### Linear stream patterns and riverside rules (Flannery; Reynolds)

Flannery reported that Formative settlements in many parts of Mesoamerica formed long linear strings along rivers, distinct from the dispersed or hexagonal patterns Earle analyzed. For Late San José phase (1000–850 B.C.) Etla arm (six sites Santa Marta Etla–Tierras Largas), he measured intersite distances 4.8, 5.5, 4.4, 5.3, 5.5, 4.9 km — remarkably even **~5 km** spacing despite high-alluvium width varying from ~1 km to over 4 km, arguing against purely environmental spacing tied to valley width. Mid-point land partition + Kirkby maize yields + Marcus household estimates: [[san-jose-mogote]] ~80–120 households vs land capacity of hundreds; [[tierras-largas]] only ~8 households vs ~193 capacity — no village near productive limits; social factors set approximate distance, **site catchment** selects exact location. Conjectured Etla-type “rules” (too few sites for statistical test): first settlement near good ford near valley center; symmetrical upstream/downstream expansion; daughters midway parent–limits then midway between prior villages; fill to socially determined spacing (~5 km); ±0.5 km latitude for site selection; bank choice for best catchment of mountains/piedmont/special resources; outlying resource barrios as pattern fills. Robert G. D. Reynolds modeled Grijalva Pre-Classic colonization with Markov-type sequences (40 one-mile cells; Early Pre-Classic 7 sites → Late 18), arguing such models provide a rigorous comparable framework usable with incomplete surveys and directional “memory.”

### Plog survey sampling and settlement pattern experiments

Stephen Plog tested relative efficiencies of simple random, stratified, systematic, and stratified systematic unaligned designs (Haggett) plus **quadrats vs transects** on three Valley of Oaxaca intensive-survey blocks (Etla 18 km² / 17 sites; Zaachila 54 / 21; Valdeflores 55 / 33). Nearest-neighbor coefficients (all periods pooled) near randomness (0.90–1.13). On settlement-pattern experiments (Etla only), he found clustered patterns (NN ≈ 0.55) generally decrease sampling precision; hexagonal (NN ≈ 2.05) and linear arrangements increase it; as NN rises (clustered → random → hexagonal), squared SE falls. Full efficiency ranking and Flannery’s “Trouble with Regional Sampling” live on [[archaeological-survey]].

### Earle nearest-neighbor analysis (NNA)

Timothy K. Earle applied Clark & Evans (1954) nearest-neighbor statistic R = r̄A / r̄E (r̄E = 1/(2√ρ); R from 0 max aggregation → 1 random → 2.1491 max hexagonal regularity) to eastern Valley of Mexico (Parsons Texcoco, Blanton Ixtapalapa, Parsons Chalco; MF–TF) and southern Gulf Coast (Sisson Chontalpa + major Olmec centers). Method limits he stressed: boundary effect inflates R; **quadrat samples unusable** for NNA (extreme boundary effect); missing hierarchical sites distort levels; needs large continuous blocks (Plog’s blocks up to 55 km², not 0.5–1 km quadrats).

**Valley of Mexico (Earle):** regional R all habitation sites MF .7141 → LF .7953 → TF .9128 (slight aggregation, trend toward random/regular). Size hierarchy: hamlets 0.5–7 ha; small villages 8–24; large 25–49; towns over 50 ha. Only Level 1 shows definite increasing-R trend under Parsons’ population-expansion → competition hypothesis; Level 4 Terminal Formative within Texcoco reaches unusually regular R=1.677. Earle’s trajectory reading (Service 1962 language): MF Level 2 regular → competitive social units (possibly lineages); LF Level 4 dominant (chiefdom-like); TF Level 2 collapses, Level 4 subordinated as early **state** at Teotihuacan integrates aggregates.

**Gulf Coast / Chontalpa (Earle after Sisson 1970):** Formative villages highly aggregated in western survey area; mean nearest-neighbor distances rise Molina 4.0 → Castañeda 8.9 km across phases. Major Olmec centers (La Venta, San Lorenzo, Tres Zapotes ± Laguna de los Cerros) show tendency toward **regular spacing** (R greater than 1), which Earle attributes to mutual antagonism / intercenter competition (e.g. ~86 km San Lorenzo–La Venta). Sketch model: competing major centers regularly spaced (min ~44 km under Heizer labor/density assumptions); secondaries ~15 km out; food production localized without hierarchical control; MF Gulf Coast organization ~analogous to LF Valley of Mexico but more extensive/low density.

### Community growth as settlement dynamics (Winter)

Marcus C. Winter compared [[tierras-largas]] (stable ~8–12 households across seven Early–Middle Formative subphases) with [[san-jose-mogote]] (grew to more than 20× original size; by 900 B.C. ~10× Tierras Largas, by 550 B.C. ~15×). He challenged the untested Mesoamerican assumption that villages start small, grow, then shrink to abandonment — arguing growth–climax–decline may fit only large nucleated villages (SJM, San Lorenzo, Chiapa de Corzo), while most hamlets plateau at a **modal size** once they reach 8–12 households. Nearly half of Valley of Oaxaca regional population growth 1500–500 B.C. he attributed to growth of San José Mogote alone (~5 ha/century for ~700 years). Late Middle Formative changes at Tierras Largas (possible high-status center residence; larger spacing without more households) **cannot** be attributed to population pressure at the hamlet itself — point to intercommunity / valley-wide processes (paired with Brumfiel’s Valley of Mexico pressure test in the same chapter).

## Nissen densification (1988) — Mesopotamian settlement geography

*(Attributed throughout — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East, 9000–2000 B.C.]]* 1988. Complements Adams Heartland survey method; Nissen co-authored *The Uruk Countryside* (1972) with [[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]].)*

**Program.** For early preliterate (and early literate) periods Nissen will not write ruler/dynasty history nor restrict himself to site sequences: from **position, type, and spatial relations of settlements** he seeks statements on economic and social structures and organizational centrality, using settlement geography’s basic theories/methods — without projecting later situations backward. Basic patterns oppose centralization and decentralization: growth of centrality as hierarchical subordination; **degree of centrality as measure of the organizational stage** of a settlement system. Places with central functions (administration, centralized cult, etc.) are assumed bigger than dependent settlements and roughly equidistant/reachable from outlying settlements — hence geographical centers of settlement groups; location, size, and chronology are archaeologically tangible if ancient systems obey the same laws as modern ones (claimed demonstrated by many examples). Limitation: if the central place differed only qualitatively not quantitatively, greater size as mark is missing.

**Surface finds and survey procedure.** Statements about settlement systems depend on survey projects mapping surface remains (especially discarded pottery sherds) for location, size, and period classification. Results are period-by-period maps of site location and size — the basic information for system-change statements. Surveys available for the great plains of Babylonia and Susiana and a few smaller Zagros plains; elsewhere interpolations across huge gaps tend to obscure local differentiation. Surface finds give a rough but generally faithful dating picture vs excavation when verifiable, though they are no substitute for excavation. Last-phase remains sit undisturbed on the surface, but older phases are covered; experience shows all phases usually appear on surface (wells/shafts bring up older material) yet deeper levels yield fewer remains — **size/duration figures must usually be treated as minima**. Stratigraphic pottery sequences from Uruk and other Babylonian sites date out-of-context surface pottery; complementary excavation + surface survey are particularly important for center–hinterland interdependence (Uruk hinterland as primary case). Relative period-to-period population change preferred over absolute figures (e.g. 100–200 inhabitants per 1000 m² occasionally suggested).

**Akkad pottery dating problem.** During the Akkad Dynasty, pottery became so mass-produced and so similar to ED III and Ur III wares that **unequivocal classification of a site as Akkad-period from surface pottery alone is hardly ever possible**; seals, tablets, and inscribed bricks that would date sites are not everyday surface finds. Settlement-size distribution can still be evaluated (Babylonia remains almost exclusively urban centers with very few rural settlements under Akkad), but fine chronological resolution of system change in that phase is poorly recoverable.

**Survey vs literary evidence.** Nissen argues archaeological survey outlines can at times be **more objective than literary evidence** and can inform settlement origins, systems, changes, and non-hostile inter-settlement contacts that texts (aside from economic texts) rarely cover — texts mostly mark wars/conquests. Written sources, when they appear, are a welcome complement; the method of interpretation does not change with the first names of rulers. Environment is emphasized without ecological determinism; artifacts are limited windows on society (technology the most certain link). Pottery fails for Neolithic sociopolitical structure; settlement-distribution methods useful later are useless in the early Neolithic because of wide spacing and terrain barriers.

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — Oaxaca SPP role

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

The book treats the Valley of Oaxaca **Settlement Pattern Project** as one of three coordinated empirical pillars (with Human Ecology excavations and the Zapotec Monuments Project). [[blanton-richard|Richard Blanton]] began “Prehistoric Settlement Patterns of the Valley of Oaxaca” in 1971, joined by Feinman, Finsten, [[kowalewski-stephen|Kowalewski]], and Nicholas: intensive survey of the **6 km²** city of Monte Albán (1971–73); full **~2150 km²** Valley of Oaxaca (1974–80); extensions to Ejutla (Feinman & Nicholas) and Miahuatlán (Brockington & Markman); outward toward Mixtec valleys. Bernal had earlier located ~280 mound sites; SPP scaled to full-coverage regional demography.

Marcus & Flannery (via Flannery’s urging of Blanton) hold that no civilization can be understood without a regional perspective on the network of cities, towns, and villages. SPP population estimates from surface remains and Human Ecology excavation estimates sometimes disagree, and “most typical” pottery types for excavators vs surveyors differ, yet the three projects agree “**90 percent of the time**.” In the 1996 synthesis, SPP numbers are the backbone of Rosario three-tier / Period II four-tier / IIIa peer-city [[settlement-hierarchy]] claims, valley population (~115,000 in IIIa; final prehispanic valley >160,000), and the map of 38 fortified/defensible IIIa sites. Sabloff & Renfrew’s foreword present coordinated long-term regional survey plus meticulous excavation as rarer but necessary for answering complexity questions — Oaxaca as oft-cited model.

## Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979 — Appendix E retrospective sampling experiment

All claims attributed to [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]] (study: [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]). Having surveyed the Basin of Mexico at 100% coverage rather than by sample (see [[archaeological-survey]] for the full-coverage rationale, the Virú Valley critique, and non-engagement with the Binford-era sampling revolution), the authors retrospectively tested what various **simulated sampling designs** would have found against their actual full-coverage data for the Cuautitlán region, Middle Horizon phase (38 sites, population 11,598; site-count and population-share distributions inversely related across the hierarchy — 50% of sites were hamlets holding only 4.9% of population, while 23.6% of sites classed as provincial centers/large villages held 78.7%). They tested simple random and stratified (by three environmental zones) designs against square quadrats, rectangular quadrats, and 1-km transects, each at a ~20% sampling fraction, run 10 times per design.

Results (self-described by the authors as "most tentative, not established fact"): **no technique reliably predicted total population size** — the nucleation of Middle Horizon population into a few large sites made estimates swing wildly depending on whether those sites were sampled. **Transect sampling** was the single most accurate design overall (predicting 4 of 5 target variables, though precisely only for general settlement pattern); large rectangular quadrats were next-best; small quadrats performed worst regardless of random or stratified design, sometimes falsely suggesting a dispersed, hamlet-centered pattern where the true pattern was nucleated. Stratification gave only small precision gains over simple random sampling — a finding the authors link to Plog's (1976) independent conclusion that "for surveying unknown areas, the simplest sampling designs may well be the most practical." The authors' methodological conclusion: different sampling techniques suit different target variables and settlement conditions (dispersed vs. nucleated), no single design is universally "best," and because "archaeological surveys are 'one shot' opportunities," a single-application sampling program (unlike their 10-repetition simulation) cannot itself estimate its own error — the retrospective test led them to endorse their original full-coverage choice, made partly through "methodological naiveté" rather than foresight.

## Ancient Oaxaca 2nd edition (Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas 2022) — settlement-hierarchy trajectory

*(Attributed — Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas, [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State]], 2nd ed., 2022, extending the [[blanton-richard|Blanton]]/[[kowalewski-stephen|Kowalewski]] Settlement Pattern Project data reported in Marcus & Flannery 1996.)*

The 2022 synthesis reports the Valley of Oaxaca settlement-hierarchy trajectory from the Rosario phase through Monte Albán Late I in finer resolution: civic-ceremonial hierarchy (sites with two or more mounds) grew from ~14 sites in the Rosario phase to 57 sites in Early Period I (a four-level hierarchy: Monte Albán alone at the apex; 4 places with 10–14 mounds; 12 sites with 5–7 mounds; 40 sites with 2–4 mounds) to 94 sites in Late Period I (a five-level hierarchy; Kowalewski et al. 1989, ch. 6). Regional population grew in step: ~15,000 across 250+ settlements in Early I to more than 55,000 across ~750 settlements by Late I, with roughly 75% of Late I growth concentrated within 20 km of Monte Albán. Highland Oaxaca overall is reported to have grown from ~90,000 (500–300 BC) to ~108,000 (300–100 BC), with the Valley of Oaxaca alone tripling to ~51,000–55,000 in the same interval (the authors give both figures in different passages, likely reflecting different Late I sub-phase cutoffs rather than a contradiction). A population-tier system is used for macroregional comparison (Level III towns, 900–2,500 people; Level IV, 325–900; Level V, smaller): by 300–100 BC the Valley of Oaxaca had twice as many Level IV and 50% more Level III settlements than before. The authors describe the five-level ca. 300 BC population hierarchy used across highland Oaxaca as a subjective analytical division for comparison, not a natural statistical break in the data.

## The Diyala charter (Adams 1965)

*(Attributed — [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-1965|Land Behind Baghdad]]; study [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-study]].)* The 1957–58 Diyala survey (with [[jacobsen-thorkild|Jacobsen]]'s 1936–37 reconnaissance) is the Mesopotamian founding application: 867 sites over ~8,000 sq km dated by ceramic index fossils to sixteen periods, canal networks reconstructed from levee remains. Its Appendix A states the population-estimation apparatus with unusual transparency: 200 persons/ha of settlement (defended against Frankfort's ~400/ha and Yeivin's 357/ha "ideal pattern taken as average," with Russell's 150/ha judged "decidedly nearer the mark"); 1.4 cultivable ha/person; all sites within a period assumed simultaneously occupied; maximal site area assumed for all attested periods (deliberately overstating early periods to offset alluviation burying early sites under up to 10 m of silt); length-×-breadth areas ~20% high. Adams argued relative inter-period values, not absolute figures, are the method's product. Chapter 9 makes the programmatic claim: systematic reconnaissance "sets us face to face with patterns of sequent occupance from which an understanding of at least certain aspects of long-term change may emerge" — survey as the empirical test of developmental models rather than their assumption.


## McIntosh 2005 — Middle Niger urban hinterlands

*(Attributed — [[mcintosh-ancient-middle-niger-2005]] Ch. 5.)*

Argues systematic (preferably randomized, pedestrian) hinterland survey is required by Trigger’s city definition. Critiques judgmental inventaire and vehicular Méma surveys as non-comparable for process. Reports multi-stage Jenné and matched Dia 50% surveys: density, settlement hierarchy, abandonment chronology, functional complementarity within [[urban-cluster|Urban Clusters]].
