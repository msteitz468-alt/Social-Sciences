# Bit-Wear Analysis

## What It Does

Examines horse teeth — especially lower second premolars (P₂) — for beveling and wear patterns consistent with bit use, supporting inferences about **riding or driving** in prehistoric contexts where organic tack rarely survives. Bits guide horses from behind (ride or drive), not pack-pulling from the front; bit wear therefore indicates riding or driving. [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]] calls bit wear potentially "the smoking gun in the long argument over the origins of horseback riding and, by extension, in debates over the domestication of the horse" (*[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]*, 2007).

**Absence of wear means nothing:** nosebands, hackamores, and other bitless controls leave no dental marks. Presence, under controlled thresholds, is positive evidence.

## Procedure

Anthony and Dorcas Brown's program (from 1985 onward), as synthesized in Anthony 2007:

1. **Anatomical focus.** Following Hilary Clayton's X-ray fluoroscopic videos of bitted horses, they established that horses elevate and retract bits into the premolars; wear concentrates on lower P₂. (Clutton-Brock and Azzaroli had earlier noted possible bit-made facets on archaeological P₂s.)
2. **Comparative sample.** 139 P₂s from 72 modern horses (40 lab domesticates with metal bits; SUNY Cobleskill; Assateague feral; 16 never-bitted Nevada mustangs); George later added 113 never-bitted Pleistocene *Equus "leidyi"* P₂s from Leisey, Florida.
3. **Microscopy.** SEM: >90% of bitted horses showed P₂ wear. Metal/bone bits create microscopic "a" abrasions on occlusal enamel plus mesial bevel; soft bits (rope/leather) create polished bevel **without** "a" wear — identified by bevel depth. ~150 hours riding per organic bit type at SUNY Cobleskill.
4. **Threshold.** Soft bits wore ~1 mm enamel in 150 hours. **3.0 mm bevel** set as minimum archaeological threshold for bit wear. Never-bitted vs bitted means differ at p<.001; never-bitted vs daily-bitted >4 SD apart. Among 105 never-bitted mature P₂s, bevel >2.0 mm <3%, ≥2.5 mm <1% (one Leisey tooth at 2.9 mm); bevels ≥2.5 mm in 58% of mature bitted teeth. Multiple mature horses ≥3 mm at one site "probably were bitted." Exclude horses ≤3 years (unerupted uneven premolars).
5. **Context control.** Interpret only with full faunal, stratigraphic, and dating control — the [[dereivka|Dereivka]] cult stallion episode shows why.

Related methods: [[experimental-archaeology]], [[zooarchaeology]], [[radiocarbon-dating]] (direct AMS on teeth).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Bits are not the only control method; soft bits and nosebands complicate signatures.
- Soft-bit wear is diagnosed by bevel depth, not surface abrasions — different optical expectations than metal-bit pathology.
- Museum collections and survival of measurable P₂s may be biased.
- Age-at-death and natural malocclusion can produce rare bevels; statistical multiples at a site matter more than single outliers.
- **Intrusive specimens:** the Dereivka "cult stallion" P₂s published as Eneolithic bit wear (1991) were later AMS-dated to the Scythian era (~800–200 BCE) and retracted (2000) — an object lesson in dating the tooth, not the settlement layer.
- Size-variability and age/sex culling methods for early horse domestication are independent and often ambiguous; bit wear addresses **riding/driving control**, not the full domestication sequence (meat-keeping → riding → specialized breeds → cavalry).

## History

Anthony and Brown developed archaeological bit-wear analysis after leaving the Smithsonian in 1985 (first archaeological specimens examined included Malyan equids), through veterinary collaboration (Clayton; Penn, Cornell, SUNY Cobleskill), SEM study, organic-bit riding experiments, and field study of Ukrainian (Kiev 1989) and Kazakh (Petropavlovsk 1992) collections.

**Dereivka arc:** Telegin's 1964 head-and-hoof stallion deposit at [[dereivka]] (Sredni Stog settlement) was declared domesticated by Bibikova (1967); [[bokonyi-sandor|Bökönyi]] and Nobis agreed; [[gimbutas-marija|Gimbutas]] used Dereivka horses for Kurgan-wave narratives. Levine (1990) argued age/sex ratios fit wild hunting. Anthony & Brown 1989–91 molded cult-stallion P₂s (bevels 3.5 and 4 mm with metal-bit-like abrasion) and announced earliest riding ~2000 years earlier than then known; direct AMS later showed Scythian intrusion; 2000 *Antiquity* retraction. The episode undermined a key early claim without invalidating the experimental method.

**Botai-Tersek positive case (Anthony 2007):** Of 42 Botai P₂s examined, 19 measurable; 5 significant bevels (3–6 mm) representing ≥3 horses in different contexts (not one intrusive pit); 12% of full sample / 26% of measurable P₂s — too high for rare malocclusion; polished facets like soft bits. Kozhai 1 (Tersek): 2 of 12 P₂s with 3 mm facets. Anthony concludes horses were bitted and ridden in northern Kazakhstan beginning ~3700–3500 BCE. Combined with Olsen's stabling/dung and carcass-transport arguments and the Atbasar→Botai economic transformation, he takes riding as established there while allowing that most Botai bones may still be wild hunted horses (75–90%).

Student organic-bit experiments and the American Indian war bridle are used to reject claims that rope/leather bits, Eneolithic horse size (~Roman cavalry size), or seat geometry made early riding impossible.

## Exemplary Applications

- [[botai-culture]] / Tersek (primary positive case in Anthony 2007)
- [[dereivka]] (historiography of a failed early claim; method still used)
- Steppe Eneolithic/Bronze Age horse assemblages; Maikop/ETC and Danube contexts where horses increase after ~3500 BCE
- Sequencing in Anthony's model: symbolic/dietary horse keeping possibly from ~4800 BCE → riding (bit control) by Botai-Tersek ~3700–3500 BCE (and likely earlier in Pontic-Caspian) → specialized breeds/size variability ~2500 BCE → effective mounted archery/cavalry only ~1000 BCE

## Debates

Timing of horse domestication vs riding; Botai domestication split (Zaibert, Olsen et al. for ridden/domestic vs Levine, Benecke & von den Dreisch for all-wild); whether Eneolithic riding enabled raiding that contributed to Old Europe's collapse (Anthony's position on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]); four-way non-identity — ridden horses ≠ IE speakers ≠ Yamnaya culture.
