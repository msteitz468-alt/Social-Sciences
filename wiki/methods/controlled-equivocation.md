# Controlled Equivocation

## What It Does

**Controlled equivocation** is [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo|Viveiros de Castro]]'s name (essay: "Perspectival Anthropology and the Method of Controlled Equivocation," *Tipití*, 2004; elaborated in *[[viveiros-de-castro-cannibal-metaphysics-2014|Cannibal Metaphysics]]*) for a conception of anthropological comparison and translation. Its premise is that anthropology "compares *for the sake of translation*, and not in order to explain, generalize, interpret" (attributed, glossing Talal Asad). Translation succeeds not by finding a common referent for two representations but by keeping controlled the **equivocation** — the referential alterity — between concepts that sound like homonyms across worlds. "To translate is first of all to translate its image of translation, which is of a 'controlled equivocation' ('controlled' in the sense that walking is a controlled way of falling)."

The method is the epistemological face of [[perspectivism]]: where Western anthropology assumes a "natural synonymy between human cultures," Amerindian "alter-anthropology" assumes a "counter-natural homonymy between living species" — the jaguar's "manioc beer" and ours are the same *word*, not the same *thing*. "One meaning, multiple referents."

## Procedure

There is no algorithm; the method is a stance. (1) Distinguish two senses of comparison: the explicit nomothetic cross-cultural comparison (a "regulative rule"), and the *implicit* comparison built into every translation of the studied's concepts into the observer's apparatus (a "constitutive rule"). (2) Refuse to erase the anthropologist's own discourse from the comparison — the standard "triangle" of comparing, e.g., "the Kachin and the Nuer" is not truly triangular, because the analyst unilaterally fixes the terms and then vanishes. (3) Aim, with Benjamin/Pannwitz, at a translation that "betrays the destination language, not the source" — letting the studied concept deform the analyst's language rather than domesticating it.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Equivocation is theorized as "not a subjective weakness but a machine for objectification... the limit condition of every social relation"; its opposite is not truth but **univocation**. This reframes miscommunication as productive rather than as error. The method is programmatic and philosophical, not procedural; abstracted from Amazonian [[perspectivism|perspectivist]] material, its general portability is untested, and critics may worry that celebrating "excess interpretation" risks losing the *control* that is supposed to distinguish it from mere indeterminacy.

## History

Introduced by Viveiros de Castro in the 2004 *Tipití* essay and made a keystone of *Cannibal Metaphysics* (2009/2014), where it underwrites both the [[perspectivism|perspectivist]] theory of translation and the rereading of [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]]'s myth analysis (a myth as "a translation... a perspective on a different language"). It draws on Walter Benjamin's translation theory, Talal Asad on cultural translation, and [[wagner-roy|Roy Wagner]]'s dictum that "their misunderstanding of me was not the same as my misunderstanding them."

## Exemplary Applications

The Lévi-Strauss/Las Casas Antilles anecdote (Spaniards asking whether Indians have souls; Indians drowning Spaniards to see whether their corpses putrefy) as the paradigm case of two "insufficiently interpreted" perspectives; the analysis of Amazonian shamanic and warfare discourse (see [[perspectivism]], [[viveiros-de-castro-eduardo]]); the general program of the "[[ontological-turn|ontological turn]]."

**Andean political ethnography (de la Cadena 2015).** *[[de-la-cadena-earth-beings-2015|Earth Beings]]* adapts controlled equivocation beyond multinaturalism to partial connections of state, tourism, and museum: “peasant movement” as equivocation of in-ayllu confrontation with Lauramarca; *historia* / *willakuy* / *kwintu* as competing modes of telling; NMAI “repatriation of ancestors” vs *suq’a*; *paqu* / “spiritual leader” captions vs yachaq practice; elections and ronda leadership as representational/non-representational knots (attributed — Stories 1–2, 6–7). De la Cadena stresses **comedy of equivocations** when power occludes misunderstanding rather than staging it.

## Debates

The status of Amerindian perspectivism — concept vs. ontological type — and hence of the translation problem it poses is contested between Viveiros de Castro and [[descola-philippe|Descola]] (see [[ontological-turn-debate]]). Portability to Andean land politics and multicultural institutions is demonstrated ethnographically by de la Cadena rather than as a formal method manual.
