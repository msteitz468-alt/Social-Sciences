# Paleoethnobotany

Also archaeobotany: the recovery and interpretation of **plant remains** from archaeological contexts. Renfrew & Bahn 2012 place paleoethnobotany at the center of subsistence reconstruction alongside [[zooarchaeology]] and human-bone [[isotope-analysis]], distinguishing short-term **meals** from long-term **diet**. Complements environmental proxies such as [[pollen-analysis]] (regional vegetation) with on-site economic plant use.

## What It Does

- Recovers **macrobotanical** remains (seeds, fruits, tubers, wood charcoal) via screening and flotation.
- Uses **microbotanical** evidence: phytoliths, starch grains, diatoms (environmental), plant DNA from exceptional contexts.
- Interprets **crop-processing stages** (threshing, winnowing, screening, storage, grinding, cooking) via ethnoarchaeology/experiment (“external evidence”) or internal context patterning.
- Identifies **domestication** markers (e.g. non-brittle rachis in cereals) and tracks farming origins multi-regionally.
- Reads **residues** in pots and on tools (lipids, isotopes partitioning legumes / terrestrial non-legumes / marine plants).
- Contributes fuel, timber, garden, and land-use histories (pollarding, coppicing, forest clearance when combined with pollen).

## Procedure

(As synthesized in Renfrew & Bahn 2012, Chs. 6–7, attributed.)

1. **Assess preservation.** Modes: desiccation, waterlogging, **charring** (principal mode on most habitation sites), mineralization, exceptional volcanic burial. Preservation conditions determine extraction technique (Ch. 2 formation literacy is a prerequisite).
2. **Sample design.** Many samples from varied deposit types of one period; preferably >100 grains each where quantification matters. Flotation enables scale; fragile remains may require **dry sieving** (Wadi Kubbaniya: remains disintegrated in water).
3. **Identification.** Reference collections; SEM for difficult taxa and residues; phytolith and starch protocols for taxa that char poorly (bananas, roots).
4. **Context and function.** Plant remains often **reveal** a location’s function (threshing floor vs occupation floor) rather than vice versa. Crop-processing residue signatures structure interpretation.
5. **Quantification caution.** Weight, counts, and MNI-equivalents are problematic: the most abundant plant may be overrepresented by chance charring (Jane Renfrew’s Sitagroi example).
6. **Domestication and seasonality.** Morphological, phytolith, pollen-timing, and DNA criteria; ethnographic ecology for staple candidacy (e.g. nutgrass tubers as half-year+ staple calories).
7. **Multi-proxy integration.** Combine with pollen outside the site, phytoliths (can detect small clearings pollen misses), fauna, human isotopes, and experimental/ethnoarchaeological analogues (Butser farm).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Only stomach contents and desiccated feces prove consumption**; charred grain in ovens or residues are contextual inference.
- Differential pollen production/transport and human selection of wood bias environmental and fuel reconstructions.
- **Any line between wild and domestic plants does not necessarily correspond to gathering vs agriculture** (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, attributed).
- Legumes lack foolproof SEM domestication criteria; pollen rarely separates wild/domestic cereals.
- Near Eastern first domesticates are C3, so bone-collagen carbon isotopes are **less diagnostic** for Old World farming origins than for New World maize (C4) uptake.

## History

- Ecological approach ([[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]] and others) elevated non-artifactual plant evidence.
- Postwar multidisciplinary farming-origins projects: Braidwood’s [[jarmo|Jarmo]] enlisted **Helbaek** for paleoethnobotany of transitional cereals; van Zeist on pollen; parallel work at Jericho, Çatalhöyük, and Levantine sites. Popular program statement: *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* (1959) — wild wheats/barley restricted to [[hilly-flanks]]; Jarmo wheat intermediate wild→bred (attributed).
- Gordon Childe’s “[[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic Revolution]]” reframed as multi-millennial, regionally specific processes from ~10,000 BC, not a single event.
- Microbotanical revolution: phytoliths (Piperno), starch on tools (Kilu taro ~28.7 kya; Ohalo II grindstone ~23 kya; MSA Mozambique ≥105 kya), residue chemistry in ceramics.
- Independent centers: Kuk Swamp (New Guinea) as pristine domestication center; multi-proxy island impact studies (Easter Island deforestation).

## Exemplary Applications

| Case | Point (per Renfrew & Bahn 2012) |
|---|---|
| **Wadi Kubbaniya** (19–17 kya) | Diverse charred plants; wild nutgrass tubers as dietary staple; dry sieving |
| **Abu Hureyra** | Epipaleolithic broad-spectrum → Neolithic few staples; domestic cereals early; hunting continues ≥1000 years |
| **Ohalo II** | World’s oldest known cereal grains (wild wheat/barley ~19 kya) |
| **Kuk Swamp** | Drainage/mounding ~10–7 kya; banana phytoliths, taro starch; independent center |
| **Butser Experimental Farm** | Prehistoric wheats more productive/protein-rich than expected → population estimates |
| **Gatun Basin phytoliths** | Maize/clearance ~4850 BP, ~1000 years earlier than pollen |
| **Upper Mantaro pot residues** | C/N isotopes + SEM: boiled/mashed tubers including potatoes |
| **Fallahogy pollen** | Early farming clearance ~4150 BC then regeneration (non-intensive) |

## Debates

- **Farming origins models:** demographic pressure (Binford 1968), social competition/feasting (Bender), cognitive “mental mutation” (Cauvin), long-term exploitationships (Higgs) — multipath, not settled.
- Dual Taurus–Zagros vs Levant trajectories combining into a farming package.
- Domestication criteria and the wild/domestic continuum.
- Single-proxy vs multi-proxy vegetation and diet reconstruction (textbook insists no single category is adequate).

## Insoll 1999 — crops, alcohol, and under-sampling

*(Attributed — [[insoll-timothy|Insoll]], *[[insoll-archaeology-of-islam-1999]]*.)* Notes that paleobotanical research for the Islamic period is under-developed (citing King). Crop species (rice, sugar cane, wheat) associated with medieval Islamic agriculture do not acquire a “Muslim identity”; cultural association is time-stamped. Case: Qasr Ibrim (Egypt) — cessation of bicolor sorghum at late Christian/early Islamic interface (~1500) possibly linked to beer production and alcohol prohibition (Rowley-Conwy via Insoll). Sugar-processing moulds (Raqqa, Susa, Feifa) track technology transfer alongside trade.
