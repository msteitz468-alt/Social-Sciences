# Anthropometry

## What It Does

**Anthropometry** is the systematic measurement of the human body — stature, head length
and breadth, face width, limb proportions, and derived indices such as the **cephalic
index** (skull breadth as a percentage of length). Craniometry, the measurement of the
skull, is its most historically loaded branch. As a data-collection method it converts
bodily form into numbers that can be compared across individuals, populations, sexes,
ages, and time periods, and subjected to statistical analysis.

## Procedure

Standardized instruments (spreading and sliding calipers, anthropometer, tape) take
measurements between defined bony landmarks, following a convention (e.g., the Frankfurt
agreement) so that different observers' figures are comparable. Raw measurements are
reduced to means, variabilities, and indices; comparative designs range from simple
cross-population contrasts to **parent–child** and sibling regression ("similarity")
analyses that partition trait variation into hereditary and environmental components.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Form is not ancestry, meaning, or capacity.** A measurement describes shape; the
  inference from shape to "race," intelligence, or descent is a separate, contestable
  step. The method's darkest history is its enrollment in [[scientific-racism]] — the use
  of craniometry to rank populations and naturalize hierarchy.
- **The "type" fallacy.** Treating a population mean as a discrete biological **type**
  mistakes a statistical summary of variable family lines for a natural kind. [[boas-franz|
  Boas]] argued that a population is a complex of overlapping genetic lines, not a pure
  type — and that with enough postulated subtypes any distribution can be fitted, making
  typological explanation unfalsifiable.
- **Environmental plasticity.** Traits once assumed fixed can move. Boas's immigrant
  study (below) showed head form itself responding to environment — a validity threat to
  any use of anthropometry as a stable racial marker.
- **Instrument and observer error**; landmark ambiguity; and the sampling-frame problem
  (who gets measured) all bear on reliability.

## History

Anthropometry was systematized in the nineteenth century (Quetelet's statistics of the
"average man"; Broca's craniometric standardization) and became the core instrument of
physical anthropology and of race science alike. In the twentieth century its typological
use collapsed under internal critique — Boas prominent among the critics — even as the
measurement techniques survived into growth studies, forensic anthropology, ergonomics,
and, latterly, geometric morphometrics. The method's rehabilitation lay in separating the
measurements (useful) from the typological race concept they had been built to serve
(discarded).

## Exemplary Applications

- **[[boas-changes-in-bodily-form-study|Boas, *Changes in Bodily Form of Descendants of
  Immigrants*]] (1910–1912).** The canonical demonstration that anthropometry could be
  turned *against* fixed-type race science: the cephalic index of American-born children
  differed from their immigrant parents'. Boas insisted the finding did not "destroy the
  value of anthropometry" but showed its power for studying "the effect of social and
  geographical environment upon man." Later reanalyzed and contested (Sparks & Jantz 2002;
  Gravlee et al. 2003) — see the study page.
- Boas's earlier *The Half-Blood Indian* (1894) applied anthropometry to race mixture,
  finding mixed-descent populations more variable and often taller/more fertile than
  either parent stock — against degeneration doctrine.
- Growth studies (physiological vs. chronological age; the secular trend in stature).
