## What It Does

Aerial archaeology detects and records sites and landscapes from the air, revealing patterns invisible at ground level. Buried and levelled features betray themselves from height through **relief** (low earthworks and lynchets in raking light), **shadow-sites** (best under long, low-angle illumination, or picked out by snow), **soil-marks** (colour differences where ploughing cuts buried features), and **crop-marks** (differential crop growth — taller and greener over buried ditches and pits, stunted over walls and ramparts). It shifted archaeology's scale of discovery from the single monument to the whole settled landscape (per Clark 1957).

## Procedure

Oblique or vertical photographs are taken from aircraft (historically balloons, then military and survey aircraft), interpreted for the four mark-types above, and followed up by ground survey and excavation. Expert-directed photography timed to season and light (Clark singles out Major G. W. G. Allen and Dr J. K. St Joseph) far outperforms routine military survey cover, and works symbiotically with **ground-following** (O. G. S. Crawford trebled the known round barrows of the Royston district by walking to features in Allen's photographs) and with **excavation planning** (crop-marks guiding where to trench).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Detection is contingent: crop-marks appear only under the right crop, soil, and moisture conditions and **fade through the growing season**, so a site visible one week may be invisible the next (the Arminghall example). Air photography reveals **plan, not date or depth**; it must be confirmed on the ground. Like all survey it is biased toward features that register at the surface.

## History

The method traces to First World War reconnaissance (the German Dr Theodore Wiegand traced the Roman *limes* in the Dobrudja from the air); an earlier 1886–7 Indian proposal (Major Esdale) went unexecuted. The first published air-photograph of a site was of Stonehenge in 1906 (Lt P. H. Sharpe, from an army balloon). Systematization is credited to Colonel Beazeley and above all **O. G. S. Crawford**, working with the Ordnance Survey, who established routine sharing of RAF training photography (per Clark 1957).

## Exemplary Applications

- **Woodhenge and Arminghall** (English henge monuments) were *discovered* from the air (Wing-Cdr Insall, 1929); Woodhenge's six rings of post-holes were known only from the air-photograph before Mrs Cunnington's ground excavation.
- **Romano-British "Celtic" field systems** in the East Anglian Fens revealed by a 1937 flood; G. Hatt's Early Iron Age field system in Himmerland, Denmark, traced from the air.
- Snow-revealed stone field patterns used by Therkel Mathiassen to trace Eskimo house-ruins near Hudson Bay.

## Debates

Aerial archaeology is complementary to ground-based [[survey-research|survey]] and to non-destructive prospection (resistivity and percussion sounding, both discussed by Clark as emerging techniques). It is a discovery and mapping method: its results feed excavation and, ultimately, the reconstruction of settlement pattern that [[economic-prehistory]] treats as evidence of past economy.

## From Renfrew & Bahn (2012)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice]]* 6th ed., Ch. 3.)* Renfrew & Bahn treat airborne and spaceborne imagery as a single analytic class of **“aerial images”** (satellite, multi-/hyperspectral, and conventional air photos differ mainly in scale/resolution). Images do not themselves reveal sites — the photographer and interpreter do. **Crop-marks** remain the principal medium of aerial discovery: crops grow taller/thicker over sunken features (ditches, pits) and stunt over buried walls through soil-depth and moisture differences; more features have been found this way than by any other prospection form. Landscape-scale products include Rog Palmer’s ~450 km² Danebury hinterland mapping and NPS aerial work on the Chaco Canyon road network (~2400 km estimated; ~208 km ground-verified).

**LIDAR / ALS** (laser pulses from aircraft with differential GPS) yields digital elevation/surface models; filtering “first return” canopy returns can expose woodland earthworks and allow synthetic sun-angle adjustment — advantages over conventional photography (e.g. Stonehenge landscape corrections). The Chase Caracol (Belize) project is their flagship case: >25 years of ground mapping had covered 23 km² under forest; weeks of air survey extended the city plan to 177 km². **Satellite** suites (Ikonos ~1 m; QuickBird 60 cm; GeoEye 40 cm; LANDSAT for large features; CORONA historic ~2 m; SAR/SLAR for terrain and canopy) are an “aerial revolution” but obey the same visibility rules as conventional photos — absence on one date is not absence of sites. Satellite remote sensing **supplements, does not replace**, survey and excavation (attributed).
