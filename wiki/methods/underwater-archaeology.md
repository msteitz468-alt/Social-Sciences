# Underwater Archaeology

Archaeological survey, excavation, and analysis of sites under water—shipwrecks, sunken harbors, drowned settlements, wells, sinkholes, and springs—using diving, remote vehicles, and adapted recording/conservation techniques. Textbook synthesis follows [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Renfrew & Bahn 2012]].

## What It Does

Underwater archaeology extends the same goals as terrestrial fieldwork—provenience, context, assemblage integrity—to submerged environments. Scope (Renfrew & Bahn 2012) covers wells/sinkholes/springs (e.g. Chichen Itza), submerged lakeside settlements, shipwrecks, sunken harbors (Caesarea), and drowned cities (Port Royal). Results include ship construction (e.g. mortise-and-tenon plank joinery at Kyrenia and Uluburun), shipboard life, cargoes, trade routes, metallurgy, and glassmaking. Marine conditions can both scatter remains and coat metals with thick casings of metallic salts that help preserve artifacts within; untreated removal allows salts to react with air and produce destructive acid—**electrolysis** is a standard treatment leaving metal clean and safe.

More than 100 sunken vessels excavated (as of the textbook account) yield construction and economic insights unavailable from land sites alone.

## Procedure

### Reconnaissance and remote sensing

- **Historical leads:** archival clues (e.g. Basque whaling at Red Bay); sponge-diver and fisherman finds (still the majority of Mediterranean wreck leads).
- **Geophysics:** towed proton magnetometer (iron/steel); side-scan sonar (seafloor surface images); sub-bottom profiler (buried objects); multibeam sonar for dense 3D seabed terrain models and clear measurable wreck images.
- **Access platforms:** scuba; mini-subs; ROVs with sonar/video extending depth (Roman wrecks to ~850 m; Phoenician deep wrecks among oldest deep-sea vessels).

### Excavation and recording

Underwater excavation is complex and expensive (plus demanding conservation/analysis). Techniques include:

- Lift bags/balloons for raising objects.
- Air lifts for sediment removal.
- In situ measuring and recording; if hull survives, 3D plans for reconstruction.
- Occasional full raises when preservation and funds allow (e.g. Mary Rose).
- Latex molding of timbers in situ then raise/record/rebury (Red Bay strategy).

### Conservation

Waterlogged finds, especially wood, deteriorate rapidly when uncovered and must be kept wet until laboratory treatment or freeze-drying. “Wet archaeology” is estimated to cost about four times as much as “dry archaeology” (Renfrew & Bahn, reporting wetland cost estimates that apply equally to waterlogged underwater organics). Plan conservation expertise into the research design (field-lab principle: do not process wet wood without capacity).

### Experimental / reconstruction link

Reconstructing vessels requires craftsman knowledge (e.g. Steffy; Khufu ship rebuild); replicas based on excavated remains outperform art-only reconstructions ([[experimental-archaeology]]).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Cost and logistics** constrain sample of wrecks that can be fully excavated; many sites remain reconnaissance-only.
- **Discovery bias:** Mediterranean majority still from sponge-diver leads rather than systematic survey grids.
- **Conservation failure risk:** rapid drying/cracking of wood; salt reactions on metals if untreated.
- **Depth and safety:** scuba limits vs ROV/sub capability; cold-water projects (Red Bay) require extreme support.
- **Legal and ethical jurisdictions** over wrecks and cargoes are complex (textbook flags heritage ethics generally; underwater law is part of that problem set).
- **Sea as formation agent:** scattering vs concretion—taphonomic pathways differ from terrestrial burial ([[formation-processes]], [[taphonomy]]).

## History

- **1853–54 Swiss lake low water** exposing posts and pottery—major impetus for wetland/lake archaeology (later reinterpreted as predominantly lake-edge settlements rather than romantic whole villages on stilts).
- **1960 George Bass** excavation of the Bronze Age Gelidonya shipwreck off southern Turkey—the first ancient vessel excavated in its entirety on the sea bed; many standard underwater techniques invented or developed there. Renfrew & Bahn treat this as the start of underwater archaeology as serious research method.
- Subsequent flagship wreck projects (Kyrenia; Uluburun ~1300 BC, Bass and Pulak) documented multi-origin Bronze Age cargoes and construction.
- Integrated land–underwater programs (Red Bay) model full industry reconstruction from archival, terrestrial, and submerged evidence.

## Exemplary Applications

As reported in Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

- **Gelidonya (Bass, 1960)** — first complete ancient sea-bed ship excavation; founding methodological moment.
- **Uluburun (~1300 BC; Bass & Pulak)** — ~10 tons Cypriot copper ingots, nearly a ton of tin, Canaanite amphorae with terebinth resin, Baltic amber, African ebony-like wood, ivory, glass ingots, mixed Egyptian/Levantine/Mycenaean bronzes—near-complete cargo snapshot of east Mediterranean trade.
- **Kyrenia (4th c. BC)** — plank ship with mortise-and-tenon joinery.
- **Red Bay, Labrador (Parks Canada; Grenier / Tuck)** — 16th-c. Basque whale-oil industry; land excavation of blubber-rendering structures; underwater search for *San Juan* (sank 1565) at 10 m; 1980–85 full excavation among four galleons; up to 15 divers + support, barge workshop, heated seawater, 14,000 dive hours in near-freezing water; latex molding then rebury; 1:10 research model showed fine-lined whaler (not “tubby” merchant stereotype), unusual single-beech keel/garboard; DNA of whale bones → bowhead not right whale as target; 5-volume report 2007.
- **Ozette** — wet mudslide preservation of Makah village (coastal wet site; excavation with ocean-pumped water hoses)—related wet-site methodology though not classic open-sea wreck archaeology.
- **Deep ROV work** — Roman wrecks to ~850 m; Phoenician deep wrecks.

## Debates

- **Treasure salvage vs archaeology.** Context recording and conservation standards separate research from commercial recovery of valuables (aligned with textbook ethics on looting and unprovenienced antiquities).
- **Raise vs rebury.** Full museum raises (Mary Rose) vs in situ molding and reburial (Red Bay) trade display against cost and long-term preservation risk.
- **How much of trade history is wreck-biased?** Cargo snapshots are extraordinary but sample ships that sank, not the full fleet economy.
- **Lake-dwelling romanticism.** Post-1940s research replaced “villages on stilts over water” with predominantly lake-edge settlement models.
