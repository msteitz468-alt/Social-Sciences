# Systematic Social Observation (SSO)

## What It Does

**Systematic social observation** records neighborhood conditions — physical disorder (litter, graffiti, vacant housing), social disorder (loitering, public intoxication, etc.), and related streetscape features — via trained observers (often video or drive-by protocols) using standardized coding. In the PHDCN / *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* program, SSO provides measures **independent of resident surveys**, crucial for testing broken-windows and disorder claims.

## Procedure

### Reiss definition (as Sampson reports)

[[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] attributes to Albert J. Reiss Jr. the advocacy of SSO: "systematic" means observation and recording by explicit rules permitting replication, with means of observation independent of what is observed; "natural social phenomena" means events and organizational properties can be observed more or less as they occur. SSO is especially suited to physical conditions and social interactions surveys may miss.

### PHDCN 1995 protocol (Sampson 2012)

Between June and October 1995, NORC-trained observers drove an SUV at 3–5 mph down every street in a stratified probability sample of **eighty neighborhood clusters (NCs)**. Unit of observation was the **face-block** (two sides of the street facing a home). Team: driver, videographer, two observers; dual side video captured both face-blocks simultaneously with observer logs and audio commentary; observations 7 a.m.–7 p.m.; tinted windows and common SUVs reduced notice.

**Scale (as Sampson reports):** **23,816 face-blocks** observed and videorecorded (avg ~298 per NC, ~120 per tract). Observer logs focused on land use, traffic, building condition, physical disorder. A random subsample of **15,141 face-blocks** was coded from videotape into **126 variables** on physical conditions, housing, businesses, and social interactions.

**2001–2 follow-up:** revised citywide SSO via observer logs for the block around each Community Survey respondent's house (with CCAHS), enabling stability/change measurement beyond the original 80 NCs.

Aggregate face-block codes with [[ecometrics|ecometric]] multi-level models to neighborhood scores; pair with community survey, census, and crime archives.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes codes capture theoretically relevant disorder; short observation windows may miss rare events; cultural coding of "disorder" is not neutral. Sampson's own Ch. 6 analyses treat SSO as the *observed* side of a perceived/observed distinction — SSO does not capture shared meaning, reputation, or stigma.

## History

Observational urban research has deep roots. Sampson places PHDCN SSO in the early [[chicago-school|Chicago School]] tradition of observing public places ("Much like the original Chicago School of urban sociology, SSO takes researchers to the streets…") updated with video technology. Reiss provided the systematic-rules definition; Sampson–Raudenbush formalized large-scale reliability for Chicago neighborhood clusters.

## Exemplary Applications

### Sampson / PHDCN (2012) — disorder and broken windows

In *[[sampson-great-american-city-study|Great American City]]* ch. 6, Sampson and Raudenbush show (attributed) that public disorder can be reliably measured at neighborhood level via SSO; **concentrated poverty and mixed land use** significantly forecast observed physical and social disorder; **[[collective-efficacy]]** predicts lower observed disorder after sociodemographic/land-use, perceived-disorder, and prior predatory-crime controls. Observed disorder is only a moderate correlate of burglary/homicide; the connection shrinks once structure is controlled (homicide included), supporting a common-cause rather than strong disorder→crime reading — with **robbery** a partial exception. Critically, **racial/immigrant composition predicts perceived disorder more than SSO-observed disorder**; shared prior perceptions dominate concurrent SSO in forecasting later individual perceptions, poverty, and population change. See [[broken-windows-debate]] · [[broken-windows]] · [[ecometrics]].

Later work notes Google Street View as an extension of SSO-like systematic street coding at scale (Sampson 2012 synthesis).
