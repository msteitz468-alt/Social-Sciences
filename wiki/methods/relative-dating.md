# Relative Dating

## What It Does

**Relative dating** orders artifacts, deposits, societies, and events into sequences—something is older or younger *relative to* something else—without knowing how long each stage lasted or how many years ago changes took place. Colin Renfrew and Paul Bahn define this idea as “the basis of relative dating” (*[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]*, 6th ed. 2012, Ch. 4; attributed).

In their chapter summary, relative sequencing is “the first and often most important step in archaeological research” (attributed position). **[[absolute-dating]]** then supplies year-scale ages needed for rates of change and inter-regional simultaneity; the most reliable site chronologies interconnect sealed stratigraphic sequences with absolute methods (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

## Procedure

### Stratigraphy and association

In [[stratigraphic-excavation]], the key relative principle is that the underlying layer was deposited first and is earlier than the overlying layer; a succession of layers yields a sequence from earliest (bottom) to latest (top)—the [[law-of-superposition]] applied to deposits (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). Good excavation must detect human or natural disturbance (later pits, burrows) since deposition, or the sequence is unreliable.

**Association:** objects in the same sealed deposit became buried at the same time and are no more recent than the deposit; but the date of *burial* (e.g. pottery in a garbage pit) need not be the date of *use*, which may have been tens or hundreds of years earlier. One must always be clear which activity is being dated (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). If one associated object later receives an absolute date (coin, radiocarbon charcoal), that age can anchor the sealed deposit and other associated finds.

### Typology and seriation

[[typological-method|Typology]] groups artifacts into types by shared attributes of material, shape, and decoration for relative dating. Renfrew & Bahn rest typological relative dating on two ideas: (1) products of a given period and place have a recognizable style characteristic of the producing society; (2) change in style is often gradual/evolutionary (“like goes with like”). Pottery typologies usually form the backbone of regional chronological systems; surface decoration often changes fastest (decades) and is preferred for short-term sequences (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

[[seriation]] arranges assemblages of artifacts in succession taken as relative chronology. Frequency seriation uses “battleship curves” of type popularity (rise–peak–decline) to order assemblages across sites (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

### Climate and linguistic sequences (relative uses)

Renfrew & Bahn also treat climatic and environmental sequences as relative frameworks: deep-sea cores (foraminifera), polar ice cores, and pollen-zone sequences can place sites and finds in regional order; with tree-ring or radiocarbon control, absolute regional chronologies can be worked out (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). They no longer rely on complex glacial advances alone as the basis for Paleolithic dating.

Linguistic comparison of related vocabularies is of “real interest” for relative chronology; earlier claims that lexicostatistics/glottochronology could yield absolute dates have been “widely (and rightly) rejected,” and they call Swadesh-style constant-rate glottochronology a “rather suspect discipline” (attributed positions). More recent phylogenetic linguistic methods (e.g. Gray & Atkinson on Indo-European) are reported as an ongoing research avenue, not as settled absolute clocks (as of 2012 textbook).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Sequence ≠ duration.** Relative order does not fix how long stages lasted or calendar spacing.
- **Disturbance and formation processes.** Unrecognized pits, mixing, or reuse break superposition and association logic.
- **Activity being dated.** Sealed deposits date co-burial, not necessarily manufacture or primary use.
- **Style change assumptions.** Typology and seriation assume gradual stylistic evolution and continuous traditions; functional or regional variation can masquerade as time.
- **Climate/pollen zones are regional.** Pollen zones are not uniform across large areas (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).
- **Linguistic rates are not constant.** No basis for assuming a constant quantifiable rate of vocabulary change (attributed position).

## History

Relative dating long predates scientific absolute methods: geological and archaeological stratigraphy, 19th-century evolutionary typology, and Petrie’s sequence-dating of Egyptian graves. Renfrew & Bahn present stratigraphy of sealed deposits and typological sequencing (recognizable style; gradual evolutionary change) as the key relative methods in the modern toolkit (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). Independent absolute methods (especially [[radiocarbon-dating]]) transformed the field after ~1950 but did not replace relative frameworks; absolute dates gain reliability when embedded in relative sequences.

## Exemplary Applications (as textbook presents)

- Stratigraphic sections and sealed deposits (e.g. Wheeler Indus Valley section: Harappan seal of known age in undisturbed layer 8 dating that layer and abutting wall—illustrative case as textbook presents it).
- Pottery typologies as regional backbones (e.g. Hohokam bowl styles, American Southwest).
- Frequency seriation validated against stratigraphy (Frank Hole, Deh Luran, Iran—Neolithic bowl-type frequencies matching true stratigraphic sequences with no serious contradictions; attributed finding as R&B report it).
- Pollen zones for northern European bog finds and sites (~last 18,000 years as they report).

## Debates

- **Relative as primary vs absolute as primary.** Renfrew & Bahn’s position keeps relative sequencing first; absolute methods answer rate and simultaneity questions relative order cannot.
- **Association vs use-life.** Misreading co-burial as coeval use is a classic chronological error.
- **Typological evolutionism.** Gradual style change is a working assumption with Darwinian roots in 19th-century archaeology, not a universal law (see [[typological-method]]).
- **Linguistic absolute claims.** Textbook rejects constant-rate glottochronology while retaining interest in relative linguistic ordering and newer phylogenetic approaches.
