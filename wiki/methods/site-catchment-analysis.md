# Site Catchment Analysis

## What It Does

**Site catchment analysis** studies the relationship between a settlement and the resources within a defined distance or travel-time radius (the "catchment"), typically to assess subsistence potential, site location choice, and carrying capacity.

## Procedure

Define site location; delimit catchment bands (often 1-hour / 5-km agricultural, 2-hour / 10-km hunting radii in the Vita-Finzi–Higgs tradition); inventory land types and resources; compare catchments across sites or phases. Formative Mesoamerican applications in [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976]] (Rossmann on San Lorenzo; Flannery on Oaxaca/Tehuacán; Zarky on Ocós). Flannery (ed. 1976 Ch. 4) framed three complementary approaches: (1) Rossmann — Gulf Coast in Vita-Finzi format with ring weighting + crop yield vs population; (2) Flannery — Oaxaca/Tehuacán, resources taken as given from remains, reconstruct catchment size; (3) Zarky — Guatemalan Coast, regional universe given, catchment circles as statistical samples. Brumfiel later used 5-km catchments as a stepping-stone to regional population–yield analysis (Ch. 8).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Circular catchments idealize terrain; social boundaries and tenure may not match economic radii; paleoenvironmental reconstruction is required for deep-time cases. Flannery (1976) warned against “cookbook” application of Vita-Finzi–Higgs ring weights and against equating **low arable %** with non-agricultural economy (see below).

## History

Developed in Old World prehistoric ecology by **Vita-Finzi & Higgs (1970)** — concentric rings weighted by distance (e.g. land 1–2 km given 50% weight, 2–3 km 33%, etc.). Transferred and critically reworked for Mesoamerican Formative villages in Flannery (ed.) 1976 Ch. 4 (*[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]*; study [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]]). Flannery treated catchment as a useful, relatively objective method for comparing sites within and between regions, but argued latecomers had followed Vita-Finzi & Higgs “as slavishly as if it were a cookbook.”

## Exemplary Applications

San Lorenzo Veracruz catchment (Rossmann); Oaxaca and Tehuacán empirical catchments (Flannery); Ocós Guatemala statistical catchments (Zarky); Texcoco/eastern Valley of Mexico (Brumfiel, 5-km agricultural radius after Vita-Finzi & Higgs 1970) — all in or tied to [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]].

## From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — The Early Mesoamerican Village

All claims attributed to authors named; volume = Flannery ed. 1976.

### Vita-Finzi / Higgs lineage and Flannery’s critiques

Vita-Finzi & Higgs (1970) weighted concentric rings and used arable-land percentages as location/subsistence indicators. Flannery accepted that beyond some distance land is unlikely to be exploited from a site, but stressed mountain deer-hunting localities may be served by **temporary camps as annexes**, so distant resources need not be written off. His major critiques:

1. **Arable % fallacy:** small communities can be agricultural even if arable land is a tiny share of a ~7900-ha (5-km) catchment. At modern Mexican maize ~1 metric ton/ha/family/year, he argued a 350-person village (70 families) could support itself on **less than 1%** of that catchment; most early Mesoamerican villages had fewer than 70 households.
2. **Relative vs absolute arable:** absolute low arable % can still be *regionally* high (Pueblo Southwest catchments often under 10% arable while the region may be under 1%; Nahal Oren’s 8% arable sits on the only coastal-side arable strip on the map).
3. **Circular catchments and ring weighting:** useful sketch of walking-distance resources, but mute on institutions of resource control (Rossmann’s summary); weighted rings overrepresent near land and can invert modern land-value rankings (see Rossmann).
4. **Spacing ≠ exclusive agricultural catchments:** ~5 km San José-phase spacing on the upper Atoyac is *not* simply exclusive 2.5-km agricultural catchments — Ch. 6 land-surplus arguments show such circles far larger than most villages needed; social factors set approximate spacing, catchment selects exact location.

Two answerable questions (Flannery): (1) whether catchment composition differs from the regional universe (Zarky’s chi-square selection test); (2) whether yield supports estimated population (population estimate + land yield, e.g. Kirkby maize).

### Rossmann — San Lorenzo (Gulf Coast)

David L. Rossmann applied Vita-Finzi-style catchments to [[san-lorenzo]], Potrero Nuevo, and Tenochtitlán near the Coatzacoalcos River (Coe 1966–68 excavations + ~75 km² survey). Peak San Lorenzo phase ~1150–900 B.C.; Coe house-mound estimates ~1000, ~1000, and ~250 persons respectively. Method: abstract sites as circles (SL diameter 1 km; Tenochtitlán 0.6 km; Potrero Nuevo 0.3 km); catchments to 5 km from site edge; overlapping circles split geometrically; planimeter on Coe soil maps for land classes by kilometer ring. Land types: lomas (*tierra barreal*/*grava*), *tierra de primera* (river levee), *tierra de potrero* (acid savanna), marshes, water.

Rossmann reported unweighted catchments differing sharply by site (e.g. San Lorenzo 1-km nearly all lomas over 95%; Potrero Nuevo 1-km ~two-thirds savanna, ~1% levee). Vita-Finzi-style distance weighting pushed San Lorenzo toward ~¾ lomas and cut Potrero Nuevo *primera* from ~40% to under 15%. At modern yields he argued even with heavy fallow San Lorenzo’s weighted lomas could feed far more than Coe’s 1000; if Early Formative maize ~250 kg/ha (Kirkby) and one lomas crop/year, ~800 ha cultivated yearly would be needed for 1000 persons — reduced fallow and stress. He noted the **inverse** relation of village size to *primera* access would, under raw Vita-Finzi logic, imply preference for lomas over levee — contradicting modern cacique preference for permanent levee. Institutional reading: floodplain sites may be missing; SL as ceremonial-civic center may have commanded levee as tribute; Potrero Nuevo and Tenochtitlán might be barrios of one giant San Lorenzo catchment. Catchment sketches resources within walking distance; it does **not** sketch the network by which resources (including 10-ton basalt from ~50 km) were controlled.

### Flannery — Oaxaca and Tehuacán (empirical catchments)

Flannery **reversed** the Vita-Finzi frame: start from empirical plant, animal, and mineral remains at Formative villages and ask how far they must have come — not decide farming from % good farmland in 5 km. For [[san-jose-mogote]] (San José phase 1150–850 B.C.) he nested rings: village itself (dogs, rare turkey, secondary-growth fruits, stored maize); under 1 km river (mud turtle, sand, opossum, raccoon); within 2.5 km over 1400 ha high alluvium (over 400 t potential maize at period yields), teosinte, chile, squash, field weeds, cottontail; 2.5–5 km piedmont seasonal wild plants/game; beyond 5 km mountains for pine beams/fuel (preferred 5:1 over oak), deer, peccary, acorns, walnuts; plus items possibly over 200 km (Pacific marine shell, Gulf mussels, fish spines, shark teeth, jadeite/nephrite). Citing Drennan (1975): 2.5-km circles give San José Mogote 76% high alluvium vs Fábrica San José 12% — a sixfold difference that does *not* mean agriculture was minor at Fábrica (at ~300 kg/ha, Fábrica’s 235 ha alluvium could support far more than Drennan’s 10–12 households). Nested exclusivity: 2.5-km circles of Atoyac EF villages do not overlap; 5-km circles overlap nearest neighbors; at 50 km one valley-wide catchment. Tehuacán: Purrón Cave treated as extension of Ajalpan’s catchment (wild plants often unavailable within 2 km of the village but within 5 km); distance/travel-time reasoning after Vita-Finzi & Higgs. Summary position: catchment helps **locate** villages but did not determine **spacing**.

### Zarky — Ocós (statistical catchments)

Alan Zarky (Guatemalan Pacific Coast / Ocós) treated catchment circles as **samples from a regional universe**, using simple statistics (chi-square selection tests) to ask whether site locations select non-random resource mixes — answering Flannery’s Question (1) rather than reconstructing absolute yields alone. (Detail of Ocós results lives on the volume’s coastal comparisons; methodologically Zarky is the volume’s formal “selection from universe” design.)

### Brumfiel link

Elizabeth Brumfiel applied Vita-Finzi & Higgs’s 5-km agricultural radius in eastern Valley of Mexico population-pressure tests, with adjustments for overlapping catchments (split at intersection chords) and topography (exclude lake bed, land above 2750 m maize limit, steep barrancas) — catchment as input to regional growth analysis rather than end product.

## Brumfiel (1976) modified catchment analysis and the Sanders–Parsons–Santley disagreement

Attributed to [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]] (study: [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]), reporting on Brumfiel (1976). Using her modified 5-km catchment method against First Intermediate Phase Three settlement data, Brumfiel argued that eastern Valley of Mexico settlements **over 30 ha in size would have had trouble obtaining sufficient food within a 5-km radius** — unlike most Phase Two (smaller) settlements — and concluded that tribute demands, not population pressure per se, were the more important driver of increasing sociopolitical centralization and incipient state formation in this period.

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) state they endorse her broader case for growing sociopolitical differentiation between Phase Two and Three, but explicitly reject her specific causal claim: "we cannot fully agree with her rejection of population pressure as a causal factor in political centralization" — a direct authorial disagreement recorded in the source, relevant to the population-pressure/carrying-capacity debate over Basin state formation. Timothy Earle's (1976) independent nearest-neighbor analysis of the same period (see [[settlement-pattern-archaeology]]) is described by the authors as "dovetail[ing] fairly well" with their own reading of accelerating political centralization via forced resettlement around imposed administrative centers.

## Debates

How far catchment bands can support demographic or political inference; relation to territorial models and [[settlement-pattern-archaeology]]. Flannery’s arable-% critique and social-vs-catchment spacing thesis remain standing methodological positions from the 1976 volume.
