# Genealogy (Foucauldian)

## What It Does

Genealogy is [[foucault-michel|Foucault]]'s historical method (attributed) for showing that objects taken to be natural or timeless — madness, the delinquent, "sexuality," "sex" itself — are historical constructs produced within [[power-knowledge]] apparatuses. Adapted from Nietzsche, it writes a "history of the present": it traces the descent and emergence of a category not to recover its true origin but to make the present strange, showing that what seems given was contingently fabricated. In *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]*, genealogy is turned against [[the-repressive-hypothesis|the repressive hypothesis]]: rather than ask "why are we repressed?" Foucault asks "why do we say we are repressed?" and reconstructs the discursive machinery that produced modern sexuality.

## Procedure

- **Start from local centers of power-knowledge**, not from a theory of the state or of law (Foucault's "rule of immanence"): e.g., the penitent–confessor relation, the surveilled body of the child.
- **Track modifications, not fixed distributions** (the "rule of continual variation"): ask how relations of power-knowledge change, not "who has power."
- **Trace two chronologies** (as in the "Periodization" of *History of Sexuality*): the chronology of techniques/devices, and the separate chronology of their diffusion and effects — which, Foucault argues, do not coincide with the conventional periodization of a rising-then-falling repression.
- **Read discourse for its tactical function** (the "rule of the tactical polyvalence of discourses"): the same vocabulary can serve domination and resistance; "there can exist different and even contradictory discourses within the same strategy" (attributed).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Genealogy presupposes the [[analytics-of-power|analytics of power]]: that power is productive, not merely prohibitive, and that "between techniques of knowledge and strategies of power, there is no exteriority" (attributed). Critics note this makes the method's findings hard to separate from its premises.
- It uses the archive **selectively and by design** — the exemplary anecdote (the 1867 Jouy case), the marker date (Westphal 1870 for "the homosexual") — and does not aim at representative sampling. Social historians contest several of its specific continuities and datings. Treat genealogical claims as Foucault's readings, in attributed voice.
- Scale: genealogy characteristically links micro sites (the family, the clinic) to macro transformations via the "rule of double conditioning," but explicitly denies simple homology ("the father in the family is not the 'representative' of the sovereign").

## History

Foucault developed genealogy in the 1970s as a shift from the "archaeology" of his 1960s work (*The Order of Things*, *The Archaeology of Knowledge*) toward an explicit analytics of power. *Discipline and Punish* and *The History of Sexuality* Vol. I are its exemplary applications. In this vault only the latter is ingested; the archaeology/genealogy relation is documented from this source's own retrospective framing.

## Exemplary Applications

- *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality, Vol. I]]* — genealogy of [[deployment-of-sexuality|the deployment of sexuality]], of [[scientia-sexualis]], and of [[the-repressive-hypothesis|the repressive hypothesis]] itself as a class-differentiating discourse.

## Debates

- The status of genealogy as knowledge — critical tool vs. self-insulating method — connects to the broader reception of Foucault (see [[foucault-michel]] and [[repressive-hypothesis-debate]]).
