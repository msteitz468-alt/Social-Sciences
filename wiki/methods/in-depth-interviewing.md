# In-Depth Interviewing

## What It Does

Extended, usually semi-structured or lightly structured conversations designed to elicit detailed personal narratives, meanings, and event sequences. Distinct from closed-ended survey items and from pure [[participant-observation]] (though often paired with neighborhood fieldwork).

## Procedure

Typical elements (as in *[[edin-kefalas-promises-i-can-keep-2005|Promises I Can Keep]]* Appendix A–B): interview guide spanning biography, relationships, pregnancy, mothering, marriage aspirations, and work/welfare; rapport via neighborhood intermediaries; audio or detailed notes; thematic analysis across cases; purposive sampling for heterogeneity.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes respondents can and will narrate; assumes language maps onto action imperfectly (self-report ≠ behavior — Evidence-Class Framework). Small purposive samples yield analytic, not statistical, generalization. Power and trust shape what is said (especially on violence, drugs, income).

## History

Core of Chicago School and later qualitative sociology; professionalized with tape recording, coding software, and IRB regimes in the postwar and contemporary eras.

## Exemplary Applications

- [[edin-kefalas-promises-study]] — 162 mothers, multi-neighborhood recruitment, joint fieldwork + interviews; ≥2 taped conversations per mother (often 2–3 hours, usually kitchen-table at home); $20/interview; full-transcript field coding (e.g. pregreact, breakup); interview guide spans biography, pregnancy decision, father relationship, mothering norms, marriage ideals, networks, future plans (Appendix B). Paired with neighborhood [[participant-observation]] and intermediary networks (≤5 referrals/source; fliers/cold contacts ~half of sample).

## Debates

Qualitative–quantitative divide; generalizability vs depth; whether interview talk is “attitudes” or accounts produced for the interview situation.
