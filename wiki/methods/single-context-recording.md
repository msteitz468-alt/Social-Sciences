## What It Does

Single-context recording documents an excavation as a set of individually-recorded
**stratigraphic units ("contexts")** — each deposit, cut, or surface described on its own
pro-forma sheet, drawn on its own single-context plan, and given a unique number in one
running sequence across the whole site. A "context" is "any single action, whether it
leaves a positive or negative record within the sequence" (Westman 1994): a deposit (a
positive unit, with surface *and* depth) or a cut (a negative unit — the [[harris-matrix|Harris
matrix]]'s "feature interface"). The site's temporal sequence is not read from a standing
section but reconstructed *analytically* by assembling the individual units into a Harris
matrix. The account here follows [[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]]'s critical history (*Critical
Approaches to Fieldwork*, 2002).

## Procedure

A context is defined **spatially/physically**: even a single apparent feature is split into
separate units where there is no physical contact between its parts (a dump layer bisected
by a later cut is recorded as two units, their later identity noted separately). Each unit
is planned separately, so no drawing composites multiple phases; x–y cartesian coordinates
(introduced with the Biddles' co-ordinate planning at Winchester, replacing the older
lettered-grid-square notebook of Petrie and Kenyon) allow one site-wide number sequence.
Combined with the Harris matrix, this let baulk sections be dispensed with entirely and
everything excavated **in plan** — so that, in Lucas's phrase, "stratigraphy became truly
independent of the section."

## Assumptions and Limitations

Its central working assumption is that each recorded unit corresponds to a **single
action/event** whose spatial limits also define its temporal limits. Lucas argues
(attributed) this is challenged on two fronts: Schiffer's observation that one depositional
event can produce material in several deposits and one deposit can contain many events; and
the deeper question of whether stratigraphic units correspond to *events* at all or are
merely labelled as *objects* (pit, ditch, layer). A whole complex site becomes "a
palimpsest of discrete events" — "the apotheosis of techniques developed in the late 1960s
and 1970s" — reconceiving the site as "no longer a physical site, made of dirt and sharp
stones… but an abstract, immaterial structured set of lines, numbers and text." Lucas ties
this to a wider **"technicisation"** of excavation (Barker's digger as "technician
producing evidence from the ground regardless of its date or function"; Alexander's
diagrammatic team hierarchy ranking people beside tools), while noting the digger, not the
supervisor/director, is what gets removed from interpretation.

## History

The lineage Lucas traces: **Martin Biddle's Winchester Research Unit** (founded 1961)
pursued urban excavation without period bias and, with Kjølbye-Biddle (1969), criticized
Wheeler's grid as "imposed on the site" and developed **"digging by phase"** — a coordinate
grid with temporary baulks kept only until a "major layer," then removed. Its acknowledged
weakness (the qualitative decision of when to pause and record "may not really reflect an
equally important phase") assumes site-wide phases rather than a multi-linear trajectory —
the very assumption that motivated **Edward C. Harris** (at the Winchester unit 1967–71) to
devise the [[harris-matrix|Harris matrix]] (1973) for multi-linear sequences.
**Single-context planning** was suggested to Harris by Lawrence Keen and developed with
Patrick Ottoway at the New Road site, Winchester, in 1975; the London **Department of Urban
Archaeology (DUA, later MoLAS)** adopted the methods from 1974–75 (GPO site, 1975). A 1974
statement that the excavator's interpretation "depended entirely on sections, so they stand
as the only record of that interpretation" (Grinsell et al.) "swiftly became untrue."

## Exemplary Applications

Deeply stratified British urban sites (Winchester; the City of London under the DUA/MoLAS).
[[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]'s [[reflexive-fieldwork|reflexive]] project at Çatalhöyük works
within single-context logic while trying to make finds–stratigraphy interaction more
iterative (see [[reflexive-fieldwork]]).

## Debates

Single-context / open-area recording versus [[box-grid-excavation|grid-and-section]]
excavation: whether the primary stratigraphic document is the recording system's
analytically-built [[harris-matrix|matrix]] or the physically preserved section. See
[[stratigraphic-recording-debate]] and [[stratigraphic-excavation]].
