## What It Does

Electron spin resonance (ESR) dating measures trapped electrons that accumulate in the crystal lattice of dental enamel from background radioactivity, in principle analogous to luminescence dating but applied chiefly to teeth rather than heated stone or sediment.

## Procedure

Enamel forms with initially empty electron traps. As background radiation (from uranium, thorium, and potassium in the surrounding sediment and the tooth itself) irradiates the tooth over time, electrons accumulate in lattice defects. Measuring the trapped-electron population and dividing by the annual dose rate gives an estimate of time elapsed since tooth formation, provided the history of radioactive dose to the sample can be reconstructed.

## Assumptions and Limitations

ESR dating requires the same background-radioactivity dose-rate estimate needed for luminescence methods, but with an added complication: teeth continue to take up uranium from groundwater after burial, and this postmortem uptake contributes to the radiation dose in a way that must be modeled. Klein (1999) reports that researchers apply two competing uptake models — early uptake (most uranium absorbed soon after burial) and linear uptake (uranium absorbed at a constant rate over time) — and treat the true age as lying somewhere between the two models' estimates. Where uranium-uptake history is more complex than either model allows (including leaching of previously absorbed uranium), ESR dates can be substantially in error.

## History

ESR dating developed as an enamel-specific complement to thermoluminescence dating, useful because unlike TL it does not require the sample to have been heated to "zero" the trapped-electron clock — enamel simply starts with an empty trap population at formation.

## Exemplary Applications

Applied alongside thermoluminescence dating (see [[thermoluminescence-dating]]) at Middle Paleolithic sites in Israel discussed in Klein, *The Human Career* ([[klein-human-career-1999]]), bearing on the chronology relevant to the modern-human-origins-debate ([[modern-human-origins-debate]]).

## Debates

[POSITION, attributed to Klein 1999]: ESR and TL dates are inconsistent at some Israeli sites — ESR suggests a notably shorter Middle Paleolithic span there than TL does. Klein attributes the discrepancy possibly to uranium-uptake histories (including leaching) more complex than either ESR model captures, rather than to a flaw in TL. The discrepancy remains a live methodological caution rather than a resolved question (as of Klein 1999).

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Source: [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]]; as of Stringer 2012.*

**Mechanics.** ESR detects microwave-measurable electron changes in tooth enamel from environmental radiation; clock starts when the tooth grows. Uranium uptake in fossils complicates dose — addressed by **coupling ESR with U-series** on the same enamel; laser ablation enables tiny enamel samples.

**Landmark applications Stringer recounts.**

- **Levant reordering** (Stringer, Grün, Schwarcz): ESR on animal teeth from [[skhul]] and [[tabun]] showed Skhul early moderns at least as old as [[qafzeh]]; deep Tabun sequence spans hundreds of thousands rather than tens of thousands of years; Tabun Neanderthal burial much older than ~40 ka ¹⁴C — perhaps ~120 ka, comparable to Skhul/Qafzeh moderns.
- **Florisbad** molar enamel (Grün & Stringer with Brink et al., 1996): ~**260 ka**, not the ~40 ka peat radiocarbon assumption — repositioned from late southern African “relic” to potential deep ancestor.
- **Broken Hill (Kabwe)** tooth enamel: ESR signal ~**200–300 ka** (closer to ~200 ka) rather than classic ~500 ka age — undercuts gradual long heidelbergensis→sapiens sequences (as of Stringer 2012).
