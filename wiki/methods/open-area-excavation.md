## What It Does

Open-area excavation opens a large continuous horizontal surface **without standing
baulks**, so that the plan of a phase — building outlines, settlement layout, activity
areas, and features such as postholes — can be seen and recorded whole, rather than in the
square-by-square fragments of the [[box-grid-excavation|Wheeler grid]]. The historical
account here follows [[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]]'s critical treatment (*Critical Approaches
to Fieldwork*, 2002), which argues the method's reputation as a clean break is overstated.

## Procedure

In its mature form (from the 1960s) an area is stripped of overburden — increasingly by
**mechanical excavator** — and each stratigraphic unit is recorded across the whole
exposure by [[single-context-recording|single-context recording]], the sequence assembled
in a [[harris-matrix|Harris matrix]] rather than read from a section. Note that
[[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]'s own term "open area" named one of *three* investigation
methods and was distinct from his "box"/grid system; modern usage — "open area" as the
*absence* of baulks — differs from Wheeler's.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method assumes that continuous horizontal relationships (plan) carry as much
stratigraphic information as the vertical section — and gives up the section's running
control accordingly, which is why it depends on disciplined single-context recording. Lucas
argues (attributed) that **Gerhard Bersu's** method "has perhaps gone down as more
pioneering than it really was": at Little Woodbury it consisted of parallel strip-trenches
later backfilled from "seams" between them, not a true single-pass strip — "only in the
final plan does the excavation look like open-area work," which Lucas notes is equally true
of Wheeler's grid once the baulks come down. The genuinely transformative shift, he argues,
was **interpretive** (recognizing postholes as houses) and came ~20 years before the
technical capacity (mechanical stripping) that made true single-pass open-area work routine.

## History

Settlement archaeology with baulk-free methods runs back into 19th-century Continental
Europe (C. Schuchardt's posthole structures from the 1870s; contemporaries of Bersu
included van Giffen in the Netherlands and Gudmund Hatt in Denmark). **Gerhard Bersu**
brought German open-area practice into Britain with the Iron Age settlement excavation at
**Little Woodbury** (1938–39), influencing Grahame Clark, W. F. Grimes, and the Hawkeses.
Lucas argues the Bersu–Wheeler contrast was exaggerated (mainly graphic style, plus the
"pit-dwelling" interpretive dispute); Bersu's real impact was replacing the "pit-dwelling"
theory with the recognition of **postholes as evidence of timber houses**. By the 1960s
(Hope-Taylor at Yeavering; Rahtz at Cheddar) mechanical excavators enabled large single-pass
stripping. A parallel North American concern developed in the 1930s, especially in the
eastern states where structures survived only as postholes, driven by New Deal rescue
programmes (WPA, TVA, CCC) — work that helped prompt the founding of the Society for
American Archaeology (1934).

## Exemplary Applications

Little Woodbury (Bersu, 1938–39); Yeavering (Hope-Taylor) and Cheddar (Rahtz) in the 1960s;
New Deal-era eastern US settlement excavation.

## From Renfrew & Bahn (2012)

*(Attributed — *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]]* 6th ed., Ch. 3.)* Renfrew & Bahn report advocates of open-area excavation (e.g. **Philip Barker**) arguing that balks are often wrongly placed or oriented and block large-area spatial patterning. Better practice is to open large continuous areas and cut **“running sections”** only where complex stratigraphy demands them, recording the vertical by accurate 3D measurement and reconstructing sequences on paper — made feasible by advanced field recording/computers and now the **norm in much British archaeology**. The method is especially effective for near-surface single-period deposits (e.g. Native American and European Neolithic long houses) where time is expressed as lateral rebuilding, and is common in salvage when land will be destroyed. Their Jamestown Rediscovery box illustrates hybrid practice: an initial 3-m grid through post-fort plow and Civil War layers, then feature-based open-area once the 17th-century level is exposed (attributed).
