# Extended Place Method

## What It Does

The **extended place method** is [[duneier-mitchell|Mitchell Duneier]]'s name (in *[[duneier-sidewalk-1999|Sidewalk]]*, 1999) for an ethnographic strategy that begins at a bounded site and then **follows that place's institutional and causal connections outward** to the other locations that made it what it is. Its purpose is to make the **micro–macro link** — to show how a single sidewalk is produced by distant decisions — without either abandoning close observation or dissolving the place into abstraction (attributed).

Duneier states the logic directly (attributed): "For me to understand the sidewalk, that place could only be a starting point. Later, I needed to move my fieldwork on out, across spaces, to some of the other places where things had happened that had a role in making Sixth Avenue what it is."

## Procedure

Starting from the Sixth Avenue block, Duneier extended the fieldwork to: **Pennsylvania Station** (with the informant Mudrick) to trace where many vendors had lived in the 1980s; **Washington, D.C.**, to interview attorneys in the Amtrak exclusion lawsuit; the former **city councilman** (Edward Wallace) who authored the 1982 written-matter vending law; and the offices of midtown **Business Improvement Districts** that lobbied to restrict vending. Each move is chosen because a chain of events at that site demonstrably shaped the block — not because it is a representative sample.

Duneier pairs the method with two allied commitments (attributed): continuous **tape-recording** with verbatim transcription, and **"checking stuff out"** — investigative verification of informants' accounts against relatives, officials, and records.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **No theory-test design.** Duneier began without a precise question or a theory to test; the method is exploratory and "diagnostic" (a term his colleague Erik Olin Wright applied to his work).
- **Judgment-dependent site selection.** Which connections are worth following is the ethnographer's call; the method offers no sampling logic and cannot claim representativeness.
- **Interpretive, not replicable.** Like the ethnography it serves, it is a strategy for a single case, not a procedure that yields comparable datasets.

## History

Duneier coined the term to distinguish his approach from **Michael Burawoy's extended case method**, which (Duneier argues, attributed) conflates two separable goals — reconstructing theory and linking micro to macro — whereas Duneier pursues only the micro–macro link. He credits **George Marcus's multi-sited ethnography** as intellectual kin (following a subject or object across sites), while his own emphasis is on following one *place's* connections. The method belongs to the wider [[chicago-school|Chicago]] tradition of grounding urban ethnography in institutional and legal context.

## Exemplary Applications

- [[duneier-sidewalk-study|Duneier, *Sidewalk*]] (1999) — the founding and, so far, defining application: the Sixth Avenue sidewalk traced outward to Penn Station, the Amtrak suit, city legislation, and the BIDs. Deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/duneier-sidewalk-study]].

## Debates

Relation to [[participant-observation]] (of which it is a spatial extension) and to Burawoy's extended case method is the live methodological conversation; the broader dispute over whether close ethnography can capture macro-structure runs through Stephen Steinberg's "ethnographic fallacy," which the method is partly designed to answer.
