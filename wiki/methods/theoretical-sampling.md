# Theoretical Sampling

## What It Does

**Theoretical sampling**, as defined by [[glaser-barney-g|Glaser]] and [[strauss-anselm-l|Strauss]] in *[[glaser-strauss-discovery-grounded-theory-1967|The Discovery of Grounded Theory]]* (1967, Ch. III), is data collection for **generating** theory in which the analyst jointly collects, codes, and analyzes data and **decides what data to collect next and where to find them**, under the control of the **emerging theory** (substantive or formal).

> Theoretical sampling is the process of data collection for generating theory whereby the analyst jointly collects, codes, and analyzes his data and decides what data to collect next and where to find them, in order to develop his theory as it emerges. This process of data collection is controlled by the emerging theory, whether substantive or formal.

Initial decisions rest only on a general sociological perspective and a general subject or problem area — **not** on a preconceived theoretical framework. The researcher may begin with partial "local" concepts naming gross structural features of the setting (e.g., knowing a hospital has doctors, nurses, wards, admission procedures) as a foothold, without treating them as a finished scheme.

## Procedure

Summary resolution (wiki, not a manual):

1. **Start** from a problem area and provisional local concepts; collect initial data.
2. **Jointly** code and analyze; generate categories and properties (see [[constant-comparative-method]]).
3. **Sample next** groups, incidents, or "slices of data" for **theoretical relevance** — to develop properties, maximize or minimize differences among comparison groups, saturate categories.
4. **Pace** sampling: early breadth of groups; later depth on core categories; short late sampling as theory densifies.
5. **Stop** sampling for a category at [[theoretical-saturation|theoretical saturation]] — when no additional data develop properties of that category.

Glaser and Strauss contrast theoretical purpose with structural/statistical sampling for verification: the criterion is contribution to emerging theory, not representation of a pre-defined population. They discuss maximizing vs minimizing differences among comparison groups (their Chart 1 logic), interchangeability of indicators, depth of sampling on core categories, and theoretical sampling in the **library** as well as the field (Ch. VII).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Assumes mid-stream theory can usefully guide sampling without collapsing into ad hoc fishing.
- Rejects statistical sampling as the primary logic for **generation** (not for all science).
- Path dependence and non-replicable sample sequences; skill- and memo-dependent audit trail.
- Institutional friction with fixed-protocol designs.

## History

Named and systematized in Glaser & Strauss 1967 Ch. III; continuous with comparative fieldwork practice and Chicago-style case comparison. Later grounded-theory textbooks elaborate variants and split over procedures — **not yet ingested**.

## Exemplary Applications

Illustrations in *Discovery* draw on the authors' dying research (*Awareness of Dying*, 1965 — monograph not yet ingested): e.g., sampling sites and awareness contexts to develop properties of dying care. Library/document caches and secondary quantitative materials are also proposed as sampleable media (Chs VII–VIII).

## Debates

Central to the [[generation-vs-verification-debate]]: verification traditions treat flexible sampling as unsystematic; G&S reply that generation requires different canons. Adjacent to Blumer's [[exploration-and-inspection]] (flexible acquaintance before fixed protocols) without identity of procedure. Tavory & Timmermans (*[[tavory-timmermans-abductive-analysis-2014|Abductive Analysis]]*, 2014) retain flexible, theory-guided expansion of cases (variation-driven) but reject the inductivist claim that theory should not enter early; their [[abductive-analysis]] prefers delaying locked multi-site comparisons until puzzles require them.
