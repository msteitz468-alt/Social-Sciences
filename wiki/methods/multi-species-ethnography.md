# Multi-Species Ethnography

## What It Does

Extends ethnographic attention beyond the human to the co-making of worlds by humans and other species. Exemplified at book length in *[[tsing-mushroom-2015|The Mushroom at the End of the World]]* (2015), where matsutake fungi, host pines, and human foragers/traders jointly structure the field. Related Tsing program: [[ethnography-of-global-connection]] (*Friction*, 2005) tracks multi-locale human connection; multi-species work extends connection motifs past the human.

## Procedure

- Follow more-than-human entanglements across locales.
- Combine [[participant-observation]] with ecological and historical materials.
- Practice [[arts-of-noticing]]; treat contaminated diversity and indeterminate encounter as objects.
- Hold nonhumans as world-makers without abandoning analysis of capital, state, and war.
- Tsing's practice: multi-locale matsutake seasons 2004–2011 + collaborative team research (Matsutake Worlds); categories develop with research *(Tsing 2015)*.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes cross-species relations are ethnographically and historically trackable. Limitations: anthropomorphism risk; uneven access to scientific mycology; must still attribute human institutional power carefully. Tsing pairs multi-species noticing with [[salvage-accumulation]] so that political economy is not replaced by natural history *(Tsing 2015)*.

## History

Grows from feminist STS, animal studies, and environmental anthropology; related to Haraway's companion-species work and to assemblage/actor-network debates. *Mushroom* is among the most cited book-length anthropological exemplars of the 2010s turn.

Relation to political ecology and Marxism; to Latourian actor-networks; to purity/wilderness conservation; to whether multi-species work romanticizes ruins. No dedicated debate page from this ingest.
