# Epigraphy

## What It Does

**Epigraphy** is the study of inscriptions—documenting, cataloguing, and interpreting texts carved, painted, molded, or written on durable (and sometimes perishable) media. In Mesoamerican archaeology it is the principal bridge between mute monuments and historical narrative once a script is (partially) decipherable. For the [[classic-maya|Classic Maya]], modern [[maya-hieroglyphic-writing|Maya hieroglyphic]] epigraphy recovers Long Count and Calendar Round chronology, royal names and [[emblem-glyph|Emblem Glyphs]], event clauses (birth, accession, war, death), parentage, toponyms, and ownership labels on objects. [[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012|Coe (2012)]] defines writing as speech put in visible form so that an instructed reader can reconstruct the vocal message, and treats successful Maya epigraphy as proof that the script is **glottographic** (speech-encoding), not pure “ideography.”

## Procedure

1. **Corpus production:** photographs, drawings, rubbings, and published corpuses (Maudslay’s plates; Förstemann’s 1880 Dresden facsimile; Ian Graham’s Corpus of Maya Hieroglyphic Inscriptions after the 1969 Guttman/Peabody initiative; ceramic rollouts such as Justin Kerr’s archive). Coe holds that no major decipherment proceeds without an adequate text corpus—the early Maya deficit, relative to Napoleonic Egypt, delayed breakthroughs.
2. **Sign inventory and catalog:** Thompson-style catalogs; allographs and rare logograms (~800 signs including one-use archaisms; ~200–300 in active use at a time—Coe’s summary figures).
3. **Structural reading without full phonetics:** date patterns, monument sets, Emblem Glyph distribution, event-glyph sequencing ([[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Proskouriakoff]]’s Piedras Negras method; Berlin’s Emblem Glyphs).
4. **Phonetic/logosyllabic testing:** keys and bilinguals (Landa’s *Relación* day/month names and “A,B,C”); Knorosovian CV syllabics, synharmony/disharmony, phonetic complements; free **substitution** to prove values ([[stuart-david|Stuart]], Fox & Justeson polyvalence, [[houston-stephen|Houston]] homophony of four/snake/sky).
5. **Linguistic control:** living Mayan languages and Colonial dictionaries ([[lounsbury-floyd|Lounsbury]]; Ch’olan as Classic monumental language in Coe’s synthesis); full-text syntax (verb–object–subject patterns, Distance Numbers, couplets).
6. **Publication and workshop transmission:** journals, Research Reports, Mesas Redondas, Texas workshops under [[schele-linda|Schele]]—Coe notes 1980s discoveries often outran print and circulated by letter.

## Assumptions and Limitations

**Assumptions:** marks encode language (not only decoration or pure ideas); surviving media sample the system; modern linguistic relatives license readings; structural patterns (dates, titles, Emblem Glyphs) are systematic.

**Failure modes (as stressed in Coe’s historiography):**

- **Ideographic/mentalist fallacy:** treating “hieroglyphs” as language-free symbols (Horapollo → Kircher → Seler/Schellhas/Thompson line, per Coe)—blocked phonetic work for decades.
- **Misuse of Landa:** Brasseur’s pure-alphabetic Troano failure threw phonetics into ~a century of opprobrium; the same “A,B,C,” re-read as letter-names/CV signs, later became the Rosetta-like key (Coe’s position).
- **Calendrics-only trap:** Förstemann/Goodman/Morley successes on numbers and astronomy without reading non-calendrical speech.
- **Elite genre bias:** monuments as public royal statements (deeds, descent, supernaturals, war)—not peasant history; four Postclassic codices are mainly religious-astronomical; literary, economic, and diplomatic genres largely lost (Coe).
- **Political reception:** Coe claims [[thompson-j-eric-s|Thompson]]’s authority held phonetic decipherment back ~four decades; Cold War framing distorted reception of [[knorosov-yuri|Knorosov]]—treat as **positions** on [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]].
- **Field vs glyph rift:** some dirt archaeologists treated texts as propaganda or “epiphenomenal”; epigraphers counter that ignoring a major evidence class is anti-intellectual (Coe’s 1989 letter quote and Schele’s “embrace or dismiss” alternative).

## History

**Comparative models.** Old World successes (Champollion; cuneiform; Linear B; Hieroglyphic Hittite in ~two pre-WWII decades) supplied Coe’s foil for Maya delay. He denies a single-hero Maya Champollion narrative: a century of gropings ending in collective enlightenment, with Knorosov closest to Champollion and Thompson cast as mentalist/symbolist foil (Coe’s analogy).

**Maya arc (Coe’s periodization, compressed):**

| Phase | Markers |
|---|---|
| Forefathers | Brasseur recovers Landa (1862/64); Förstemann Long Count, vigesimal math, Venus/lunar tables from Dresden; Cyrus Thomas reading order (paired columns L→R, T→B, 1882); Maudslay corpus |
| Thompson age | Deep calendrics and catalogs; skepticism of history-on-monuments and of phonetics; Whorf-type proposals marginalized |
| Dual hinge | Knorosov logosyllabic principles (from 1952); Proskouriakoff historical structure (1960); Berlin Emblem Glyphs (1958) |
| Take-off | Linguist allies (Lounsbury, Kelley); Thompson’s death 1975; Primera Mesa Redonda Palenque 1973; Albany Phoneticism conference 1979; Schele Texas workshops; Stuart/Houston Young Turks |
| Consolidation | ~85% of texts readable in some Mayan language (estimates Coe reports); integrated projects (Copán/Fash, Petexbatún/Demarest, Houston at Piedras Negras, etc.) |

**Two threads:** Coe frames a phonetic-linguistic thread (abortive after Brasseur; revived by Knorosov–Kelley–Lounsbury–Stuart) and a calendrical-astronomical thread (triumphant earlier, mainly German, Förstemann). Full modern practice braids both with art history and settlement archaeology.

## Exemplary Applications

- **Piedras Negras historical pattern** (Proskouriakoff): niche stelae, Toothache = accession, Upended Frog = birth, lifetime-length monument sets.
- **Palenque dynasty** (Schele–Mathews–Lounsbury): six kings in an afternoon; dual Pakals; *chumwan* seating verbs.
- **Syllabic grid** (Kelley at Albany; Mathews 1984 appendix; Stuart’s Ten Phonetic Syllables).
- **Name-tagging:** Mathews *u tuup* “his earspool” (Altun Ha); Stuart *u bak* on Tikal bones; Houston & Taube PSS as vessel ownership (*u lak*, *y-uch’ib*) plus Stuart’s *kakaw*.
- **Copán:** Altar Q as sixteen *ajawo’ob*; *lakam tun* stela names; Hieroglyphic Stairway integrated with excavation.
- **way** reading (Houston, Stuart, Grube 1989): co-essences on ceramics and Vision Serpents.

## Debates

Principal filing: [[maya-script-decipherment-debate]] (phonetic vs ideographic; historical vs calendrical-only; epigraphy vs settlement-first archaeology; Coe’s Thompson thesis). Related: construct status of [[emblem-glyph]] readings as “holy lord of X” vs polity vs place; ceramic PSS as name-tagging vs funerary chant (MacLeod’s dual solution per Coe). Literacy rates: Coe rejects Gelb/Goody-style claims that logosyllabaries block literacy, citing Schele’s one-week workshops teaching amateurs to scan texts.

## Reflexivity

Maya epigraphy’s production context includes colonial destruction of books (Landa’s Maní auto-da-fé), antiquarian recovery of codices, Carnegie institutional power under Thompson, Soviet isolation of Knorosov, U.S.–Mexican professional politics (Ruz/Mesa Redonda boycott claims in Coe), looting and unprovenanced ceramics, and workshop democratization that unsettled credential hierarchies. Coe is both historian and participant-advocate; his *Breaking the Maya Code* is indispensable historiography and a **position-laden** source—use attributed voice for causal claims about who “held back” or “enabled” decipherment. As of Coe’s third edition synthesis, monumental and ceramic texts are largely readable while the full rare-sign inventory and lost genres remain incomplete.

## Historical-epigraphy program popularized by Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel, *A Forest of Kings* (1990)]]. Popular synthesis of the post-Proskouriakoff historical program, not a first discovery of that program.)*

**Program thesis:** With decipherment, Classic monuments record rulers’ life events (birth, accession, marriage, conquest, death); Maya join pristine civilizations with named individuals. Inscriptions are **elite propaganda** people would believe — sacred capacity to capture cosmic order, not mass communication; few ancient Maya were literate. Interpretation rests on **conjunction and disjunction** between hieroglyphic winners’ history and archaeological remains of commoners.

**Method braid they popularize:** (1) Proskouriakoff historical hypothesis (1960–64) as “filter change”; (2) Berlin Emblem Glyphs and historical names; (3) Knorosov mixed logosyllabic system (despite Western resistance they narrate); (4) syntactic word-order analysis (time → action → object → actor) crystallized at Dumbarton Oaks mini-conferences and the **1973 Primera Mesa Redonda de Palenque** — where Schele and Peter Mathews identified five Palenque rulers in three hours. Schele’s standing method: find patterns intrinsic to art, writing, and architecture; treat interpretation as provisional as patterns change.

**What the book does with the method:** multi-site political narratives (star wars, patronage, dynastic recovery) written as readable history for a general audience, with Joy Parker smoothing narrative flow — the public face of historical Maya epigraphy for the 1990s. Freidel’s dirt archaeology (Cerros, etc.) supplies Preclassic depth where full royal texts are absent (isolated glyph tags, not yet Classic Emblem chronicles).

**Limitations they themselves flag:** paper books lost; surviving texts mute about ordinary people; public art maps strategies of spiritual–political legitimation. Later readers revise many 1990 nickname readings and some war/overlord models — keep S&F as **historiographic landmark and attributed narrative**, not final epigraphic state of the field.
