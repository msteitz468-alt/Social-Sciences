# Archaeological Survey

Field methods for locating, recording, and spatially analyzing archaeological remains at site and landscape scales—ground reconnaissance, surface collection, sampling designs, and mapping—often integrated with [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]], and [[geophysical-survey]]. Textbook synthesis here follows [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Renfrew & Bahn 2012]] (especially Ch. 3).

## What It Does

Survey discovers and documents sites and “off-site”/“non-site” evidence (artifact scatters, plowmarks, field boundaries) across defined regions. Renfrew & Bahn (2012) argue that fieldwork traditionally meant site discovery and excavation, but focus has broadened to whole landscapes; regional survey is now major fieldwork, and excavation’s cost and destructiveness push non-destructive surface survey and remote sensing. Survey can produce different information from excavation, is relatively cheap/quick/non-destructive, and may be chosen when excavation cannot answer regional questions.

**Complementarity (textbook formulation):** excavation tells us a lot about a little of a site and can only be done once; survey tells us a little about a lot of sites and can be repeated. Small check excavations may still be needed for chronology, contemporaneity, function, or hypothesis testing.

Survey is also the primary field route to **settlement pattern** and social scale: relationships among sites, site hierarchies, and territories (Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 5 synthesis).

## Procedure

### Research design

Under New Archaeology’s scientific rigor and pioneers such as Pitt-Rivers and Wheeler, Renfrew & Bahn (2012) prescribe an explicit **research design** with four stages: (1) formulate a strategy to resolve a question or test a hypothesis; (2) collect and record evidence; (3) process, analyze, and interpret against the original idea; (4) publish. Strategy is refined iteratively as evidence accrues; the broad objective should stand even if tactics change.

### Ground reconnaissance

Practical distinction: **ground reconnaissance** (discovery at ground level) vs aerial/space discovery; projects usually employ both. Sources of location include:

- **Documentary and place-name leads** (e.g. Homeric geography → Troy; Viking sagas → L’Anse aux Meadows; map names for “stone”/“tomb”; street names for medieval towns)—with caution that biblical archaeology can cloud impartial assessment if texts are treated as absolute religious truth.
- **CRM / salvage** inventories before roads, buildings, dams, peat-cutting, or wetland drainage (US CRM laws strengthened in the 1970s).
- **Systematic coverage** beyond prominent ruins: many sites appear only as artifact scatters; low-density off-site scatters represent significant activity (especially sparse mobile-lifeway records).

### Designing regional survey

Renfrew & Bahn (2012) prescribe:

1. **Define region boundaries** (natural, cultural, or arbitrary—natural often easiest).
2. **Assess landscape history** (covering sediments, removal of surface material, disturbance by herds/burrowing animals).
3. **Set intensity** by time, resources, and access (arid environments often best visibility; rainforest often limited to river exposures unless trails cut).
4. **Stratify** mixed landscapes into visibility zones; recognize phase- and mobility-dependent visibility.
5. **Decide collection policy**: collect vs examine in situ; total vs sample collection.
6. **Prefer systematic over unsystematic walking**: grids or equally spaced transects reduce concentration bias on rich areas and ease plotting; multi-year/seasonal repeats improve results (visibility varies with vegetation and land use); standardized forms/handheld computers help; observer skill differences are never fully eliminated.

### Sampling strategies

Total investigation of large sites/regions is usually unaffordable. Designs discussed in Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

| Design | Procedure | Characteristic bias/trade-off |
|---|---|---|
| Simple random | Random-number selection of units | Clustering of selected squares leaves gaps |
| Stratified random | Zones (e.g. cultivated vs forest); squares proportional to zone area | Matches landscape proportions |
| Systematic | Equally spaced grid (e.g. every other square) | Risks missing or hitting regular spatial patterns |
| Stratified unaligned systematic | Combines random, stratified, and systematic elements (e.g. Redman & Watson at Girik-i-Haciyan: 5-m grid, 3×3 blocks, one random square per block) | Textbook-preferred even distribution |
| Transects vs squares | Straight paths vs block units | Transects easier in dense vegetation; squares expose more area; often combine both |

### Site surface survey and mapping

After discovery, assess size, type, and layout for excavation decisions, management, or settlement study without excavation: map surviving features and surface artifacts; sample when finds are too numerous or contexts badly disturbed; total collection on small sites with resources. **Mapping** is key: topographic (contours) vs planimetric (building relationships); lat/long or UTM; ownership, condition, alphanumeric site designations. Workflow: handheld GPS for control → Total Station for detailed X,Y,Z → GIS layers.

**GIS** (per Renfrew & Bahn): hardware/software plus geographic data for storing, managing, analyzing, and displaying spatial information—beyond CAD mapping to statistical analysis of distributions and cost-surface analysis. Predictive models of site location (e.g. Shawnee National Forest logistic regression from known sites) are widely used in CRM but criticized as environmentally deterministic; viewsheds pursue more humanistic landscape questions.

### Invasive low-impact probes (survey suite)

Where surface visibility is poor (e.g. eastern North American forests), **shovel test pits (STPs)** at consistent intervals preview subsurface content. Soil rods/augers map hollows and depths (with risk of damaging fragile materials). These sit between pure surface survey and full excavation; non-destructive [[geophysical-survey]] is preferred when feasible.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Visibility is not presence.** Absence on one survey date or image is not absence of sites; vegetation, crops, moisture, and recent land use control what is seen.
- **Surface as subsurface proxy is debated.** Shallow/single-period sites may show more reliable surface–subsurface links than deep multi-period tells; yet multi-period surface collections often reflect many phases (via erosion, animals, plowing), not only the latest—though quantitative bias toward recent periods is still expected. Wise practice tests with pits or excavation when possible (Renfrew & Bahn 2012).
- **Sampling incompleteness.** Informal sampling by accessibility/prominence biases results; probabilistic designs need larger, better-designed samples than opinion polls because archaeological “populations” are poorly structured.
- **Time cost of intensity.** Sydney Cyprus Survey Project intensively covered only ~9.9% of 75 sq km over ~6 years—diachronic survey also needs classifiable, datable pottery typology.
- **Settlement hierarchy sampling.** Dual strategy recommended for social scale: intensive systematic transects (ideally total survey) plus search for centers via monuments, rich finds, local knowledge, literature, and remote sensing—random stratified sampling alone risks Flannery’s “Teotihuacan effect” (missing a huge dominant urban site).
- **Thiessen polygons / XTENT / Central Place** models for territories assume contemporaneity and idealized landscapes; polygons ignore site-size rank unless restricted to same-rank sites.

## History

- **Kidder’s regional blueprint** ordered reconnaissance → chronological criteria → seriation → problem-oriented stratigraphic excavation → more detailed regional survey and dating.
- **Willey’s late-1940s Virú Valley** study (maps, air photos, ground survey, excavation, potsherd collection) is treated as a pioneering settlement-pattern project; William T. Sanders’s Basin of Mexico work continues the regional tradition.
- **Processual / New Archaeology** emphasis on explicit questions, regional environments, intensive survey, selective excavation, statistical sampling, and improved recovery helped create a geographically and temporally broad “world archaeology.”
- **CRM expansion** from the 1960s–70s made inventory survey a dominant professional mode under development pressure.
- **Intensive multi-disciplinary surveys** (e.g. SCSP 1992–1998) and micro-regional total mapping (Teotihuacan Mapping Project) define late 20th-c. practice; Adams et al. extensive Mesopotamian syntheses illustrate large-scale landscape reconstruction from combined projects.

## Exemplary Applications

As reported in Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

- **Sydney Cyprus Survey Project (Knapp & Given)** — 75 sq km Troodos foothills; multi-stage design questioning the “site” notion; 50-m-wide N–S transects (walkers 5 m apart) at 500-m intervals plus block survey of special-interest areas; Pottery Index adjusted for visibility (manuring vs farmstead vs major settlement densities).
- **Teotihuacan Mapping Project** — aerial + surface survey with small tests redefined the metropolis beyond the ceremonial center; >5000 structures/activity areas.
- **Oaxaca total pickup (Hole et al.)** — 1.5-ha open site on 5-m grid; density contours guided excavation of primary activity contexts.
- **Tell Halula (Mottram)** — stratified random surface collection (4% of 12.5 ha) revised occupation history from “mainly Halaf” to ten major phases with most extensive occupation Pre-Pottery Neolithic.
- **Mohenjodaro craft distributions** — surface craft debris scattered as small workshops throughout the city rather than a single manufacturing zone.
- **Robert Adams Mesopotamia** — extensive survey syntheses of village clustering, Early Dynastic distribution centers, canals.
- **Giza Plateau Mapping Project GIS** — integrates multi-year excavation, survey, and remote sensing; spatial patterns in food remains across house sizes.
- **Foley Amboseli off-site survey** — dense sampling of stone tools across environmental zones models annual discard across hunter-gatherer home ranges.
- **Settlement hierarchy / XTENT / viewsheds** — analytic uses of survey maps for social archaeology (Warka; Wessex long barrows).


### Mesopotamian regional survey (Adams 1981)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Robert McC. Adams]]'s *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]* is a landmark application of multi-period ceramic surface survey + aerial photograph interpretation on the central Euphrates floodplain (study: [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]). Key design elements Adams reports:

- **Primary mapping** where modern maps fail; KLM-scale air mosaics for levee/canal traces; ground control and base-map conventions.
- **Narrow-grid reconnaissance** to recover faint "pot drops," not only high mounds; four campaigns 1968–75; breadth-over-depth strategy.
- **Dating by ceramic index fossils** on multiperiod mounds (not full phase-size sampling); intensive 5 m diagnostic collections for Uruk subphases.
- **Inventory scale:** ~1,605 sites in the intensive zone (including 466 from the prior Warka survey); restudy sample suggested possible ~⅓ undercount of small satellite sites.
- **Self-limitations (ch. 2):** no soundings check; incomplete coverage; functional silence of surface data; provisional ceramic criteria; size ≠ population; unequal period lengths; chronological bins ~ten generations.

[[jacobsen-thorkild|Jacobsen]]'s foreword treats Adams's refinements (air photos, size emphasis, tight grid) as transforming earlier incomplete surface surveys of Babylonia. Related: [[aerial-archaeology]], [[remote-sensing]], [[settlement-hierarchy]], [[rank-size-rule]].

## Debates

- **Survey vs excavation as substitute.** Cost and destructiveness encourage surface survey as stand-in for digging; Renfrew & Bahn insist only excavation fully checks surface reliability, confirms remote sensing, and shows what remains—methods are complementary.
- **Site vs off-site archaeology.** Whether low-density scatters should be treated as “sites” or as continuous landscape discard (mobile foragers; manuring scatters).
- **Surface reliability** on multi-period tells vs shallow sites (above).
- **Predictive GIS models** vs charges of environmental determinism; cultural/social landscape variables map poorly.
- **CRM inventory vs research design** tension: compliance recording vs question-driven regional inquiry.
- **Publication obligation.** All survey effort is largely wasted unless results are published (interim then full monograph)—Renfrew & Bahn’s closing field-process norm.
