# Moral statistics

## What It Does

Analysis of official statistics on “moral” behaviors — paradigmatically [[suicide]] rates, also crime, marriage, divorce — to establish social regularities. [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]]’s *[[durkheim-suicide-1897|Suicide]]* is a foundational application: the **social suicide-rate** treated as a factual order whose permanence and variability express collective tendency.

George Simpson (1950) stressed that in the 1890s repositories were sparse and techniques primitive; Durkheim “invented” relational inferences without formal correlation coefficients (Galton/Pearson still pathfinding).

## Procedure (as in *Suicide*)

1. Compile multi-country and subnational rates from official publications.
2. Establish constancy within society and variation between societies.
3. Eliminate extra-social explanations that fail to track the rate.
4. Compare rates across structural categories (confession, family status, occupation, military, season).
5. Construct ratios such as the **coefficient of preservation** (how many times less frequent suicide is in one group than another at the same age) and military **coefficients of aggravation**.
6. Distrust “apparent causes” (official motive tables) as aetiology — they reflect classifiers’ opinions.
7. Separate tables of facts from interpretive argument.

Auxiliaries: history, ethnography, maps (Ferrand); bulk reclassification of judicial records (Mauss, ~26,000 cases).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Ecological inference** — group rates do not identify which individuals act or why.
- **Recording** — undercount, stigma, medico-legal classification differ by place and time.
- **Quételet’s average man** — Durkheim argued the average inclination cannot explain rare but stable suicide totals; probability of the rate ≠ average individual disposition.
- **Causation vs association** — lawful covariation licensed strong causal language in Durkheim; later standards demand design-based identification.
- **Available series** — analysis shaped by what ministries published.

## History

Lineage through political arithmetic and 19th-c. moral statisticians (Guerry, Quetelet, Wagner, Morselli, Oettingen, Legoyt, etc. — engaged as sources in *Suicide*). Durkheim’s innovation is less inventing rates than **tying them to a theory of social facts and social types** and using elimination + comparative controls systematically.

A pre-statistical precursor: [[martineau-harriet|Martineau]] (*[[martineau-how-to-observe-1838|How to Observe]]*, 1838) already treated **[[suicide]]** character and number, crime patterns, and vital/health registers as traveler’s indices of morals and religious sentiment under [[social-observation]] and [[things-before-discourse]] — without rate construction or elimination design. Related but not identical: her program is qualitative-comparative institutional observation; moral statistics is quantitative rate analysis.
