# Children of Immigrants Longitudinal Study (CILS)

School-based **longitudinal panel** of children of immigrants in southern Florida and southern California, designed and directed by [[portes-alejandro|Portes]] and [[rumbaut-ruben|Rumbaut]]. Principal report in the collection: *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]* / [[portes-rumbaut-legacies-study]].

## What It Does

Measures adaptation of the new [[immigrant-second-generation|second generation]] across adolescence: language knowledge and preference, ethnic/racial self-identification, family cohesion and conflict, self-esteem and depressive affect, educational aspirations and expectations, grades and school attrition — with a parental survey for first-generation SES, outlooks, and family structure.

## Procedure

1. **Baseline (1992):** **N = 5,262** students in **49 schools**, 8th–9th grades, Miami/Ft. Lauderdale and San Diego areas. Eligibility: foreign parentage (U.S.-born children of immigrants or foreign-born children arriving young). Roughly half native-born of foreign parents in reported descriptives.
2. **Youth follow-up (1995–1996):** **N = 4,288** (**81.5%** of baseline); authors compare retrieved vs lost on baseline predictors.
3. **Parental survey (1995–1996):** **N = 2,442** parents (~90% biological); multi-language instruments (English version in *Legacies* Appendix B).
4. **Analysis:** cross-sectional and change models; nationality contrasts as proxies for [[modes-of-incorporation]]; multivariate models (Appendix C variable catalogue for chs. 6–9).

**Key operationalizations (*Legacies*):** English Knowledge Index and Foreign Language Index (unit-weighted speak/understand/read/write, range 1–4); **fluent bilingual** = English ≥3.75 and foreign language ≥3.25; Rosenberg self-esteem; CES-D; Stanford achievement percentiles; multiyear (grades 9–12) dropout rates.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes school rosters capture second-generation adolescents in these metros (misses pre-baseline severe leavers; limited coverage of groups rare locally). Self-report validity for identity, discrimination, and effort. Two-site design trades depth for national representativeness. Adolescent school success is treated as a leading indicator of adult [[social-mobility|mobility]], not a substitute for adult outcomes.

## History

Conceived after *Immigrant America* (authors date the project conversation to an Atlanta café, 1988). Fieldwork 1992–1996; synthesis in *Legacies* (2001). Funders acknowledged include NSF, Mellon, Spencer, and Russell Sage. Later adult waves exist in the wider literature but are not this volume.

## Exemplary Applications

[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-study]] — tests of [[segmented-assimilation]]; predictors of fluent bilingualism; identity change and [[reactive-ethnicity]]; psychosocial and school models.

## Debates

[[segmented-assimilation-debate]]; generalizability of gateway-city panels; whether adolescent indicators predict adult class location.
