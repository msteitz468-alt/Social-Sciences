# Flotation

A **recovery technique** that uses water (and often a froth or wash process) to separate light organic remains — especially carbonized seeds and chaff — from archaeological sediments. Central to modern [[paleoethnobotany]]; complementary to dry sieving and to macrofaunal recovery for [[zooarchaeology]].

## What It Does

- Raises recovery rates for small botanical remains relative to troweling or coarse screening.
- Makes quantitative comparison of crop, weed, and wild plant spectra feasible across samples and sites.
- Underpins many post-1960s claims about early farming and [[domestication]] that earlier excavators simply could not sample.

## Procedure

(Summary resolution — wiki, not a field manual.)

1. Sample sediments by context (features, floors, pits, hearths).
2. Agitate in water so light fraction floats or is washed to sieves; heavy fraction may be wet-sieved separately.
3. Dry, sort under low-power microscope; identify against reference collections.
4. Record densities and ubiquity; interpret with taphonomic caution (charring pathways, processing stages).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes remains survive charring and handling; water-sensitive assemblages fail. Abundance confounded by seed production rates and chance of burning. Domestication status still requires morphological/genetic criteria beyond mere presence. [[paleoethnobotany]] surveys (e.g. Renfrew & Bahn textbook tradition; J. M. Renfrew’s methods paper in [[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969|Ucko & Dimbleby 1969]]) stress sampling design.

## History

Became standard as agricultural-origins research demanded direct plant evidence rather than sickle gloss and impressions alone. The 1968–69 domestication seminar sits at the moment when botanical recovery and identification methods were being codified for archaeology (see plant-methods papers in Ucko & Dimbleby 1969).

## Exemplary Applications

Early farming sites where carbonized cereals/legumes structure chronology and economy (Near Eastern tells; later multi-regional programs). Specific 1969 case applications densified from extraction.

## Debates

Recovery-method debates (flotation vs dry sieve; mesh size; what “absence” means) belong with paleoethnobotanical method critique rather than a free-standing theory dispute unless promoted.
