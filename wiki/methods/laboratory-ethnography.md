# Laboratory Ethnography

Close ethnographic study of scientific laboratories (or equivalent knowledge-production sites), treating scientists' daily work as a culture of practice rather than as the transparent application of method. Charter application in this wiki: Latour's fieldwork for *[[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979|Laboratory Life]]* (with Woolgar).

## What It Does

Applies anthropological [[participant-observation|participant observation]] and related micro-methods to bench science: who does what with which instruments, how traces become papers, how claims gain or lose credibility in conversation and literature. Aims to recover the **construction** of facts before they are black-boxed in textbooks and retrospectives.

## Procedure

*(Summary resolution — not a manual.)*

1. Gain access to a working laboratory; negotiate role as observer (often "anthropologist of science").
2. Observe daily bench and office work: assays, purification, writing, meetings, phone/mail, sample exchange.
3. Collect inscriptions (graphs, drafts, protocols), conversations, publication lists, CVs, and instrument configurations.
4. Reconstruct trajectories of particular claims (e.g. TRF) across local work and external networks.
5. Write with explicit attention to how the ethnographer's own text constructs its object (reflexive variants).

Latour at the [[salk-institute]] (1975–77) combined extended presence in Guillemin's group with later collaborative analysis with Woolgar.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Estrangement works.** Treating scientists "like a tribe" yields analytic purchase; risks exoticism and scientist hostility.
- **Access is political.** Labs control secrets, priority, and image; observed work may differ from closed work.
- **Technical opacity.** Observer may miss chemical/biological detail scientists treat as decisive — or over-read literary surface.
- **"As it happens" trap.** Postscript warns that claiming privileged real-time truth repeats the realism the method critiques if reflexivity is dropped.
- **n = 1.** One lab (or few) supports mechanism claims better than universal laws of science.

## History

Late 1970s emergence with *Laboratory Life* and concurrent laboratory studies; 1980s expansion; later multi-sited and post-laboratory STS. Related to SSK, ethnomethodology of science, and anthropology of knowledge.

## Exemplary Applications

- [[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-study|Laboratory Life]] — Salk Institute neuroendocrinology (TRF/H case).

## Debates

Instrumental vs. reflexive ethnography (Woolgar); whether construction talk is relativist; laboratory isolation vs. wider networks (authors' own later critique).
