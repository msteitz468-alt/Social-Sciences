# Positive Method

## What It Does

As framed by [[comte-auguste|Comte]] in the *Positive Philosophy* ([[comte-positive-philosophy-martineau-1853|Martineau]]), the **positive method** is the scientific mode of philosophizing that seeks **invariable laws** of succession and resemblance through combined observation and reasoning, and refuses first causes, final causes, and absolute essences (Introduction ch. I). Across sciences it supplies the **unity of method** Comte thought necessary for a complete positive philosophy (he denied necessary unity of a single universal doctrine or law).

Applied to society (Book VI), it becomes the method of [[social-physics]]: still nascent, still entangled with political art, but in principle continuous with positivity in astronomy through biology. Book VI ch. XIII maps five successive phases of the positive method — mathematical, astronomical, physico-chemical, biological, sociological — through which the scientific spirit rises to the philosophical spirit; social science completes the method by historical filiation.

## Procedure

Summary resolution (not a lab manual):

1. Wait on / draw from prior sciences in the [[hierarchy-of-the-sciences]] for logical and scientific preparation.
2. Use theory to guide observation (facts without theory are "desultory"); use observation to correct theory. Theological beginnings were necessary provisional theories that opened observation (Introduction).
3. Prefer laws and general facts to exceptional events and minute chronicle; science is composed of laws, not isolated facts (Book VI ch. XIII).
4. Modes of observation (Book VI ch. III): pure observation, experiment (severely limited for society), comparison, and — distinctive of sociology — the **historical method** of gradual filiation of social states ("predict the past" before predicting the future).
5. For dynamics: abstract historical series concentrated on the most advanced progression; names of men and nations as expositional aids only.
6. Separate abstract social science from concrete histories and from immediate political recipes — while still aiming at prevision and reorganization.
7. Present mature science **dogmatically** (systematic reconstruction) rather than only as a chronology of discoveries; use historical method where the science is new.
8. Statical vs dynamical study (similitude vs succession) corresponds to order and progress (Book VI; ch. XIII).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Phenomena are law-governed; absolute causes are inaccessible or vain.
- Social phenomena are natural phenomena of a higher complexity, not exempt from positivity.
- The three-stage law structures what counts as progress in method itself.
- Individual education should reproduce the race's three stages rapidly (Book VI ch. XIII) — method tied to philosophy of history.
- Validity threats: confirmation via grand narrative; selection on the European path; political project guiding "laws" of history; thin operationalization of "observation" in social statics; racial framing of the scientific series.

## History

Codified in the *Cours* (1830–1842); popularized in English by Martineau (1853). Comte dated a marked rise of positive philosophy to about two centuries earlier under Bacon, Descartes, and Galileo (Introduction). Later "positivism" in philosophy of science and "positivist" methods in sociology diverge — some retain law-seeking and anti-metaphysical rhetoric; few retain the full Comtean encyclopedia.
