# Phenomenological Landscape Archaeology

Field-interpretive method for studying how **monuments and locales sit in and act on the experienced landscape** — walking, looking, relating architecture to topography — associated with [[tilley-christopher]]'s *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* (1994) and [[phenomenological-archaeology]].

## What It Does

Fills the gap Tilley calls "middle-range" between the **site plan** and the **regional distribution map**: neither mole-in-the-trench nor aircraft-over-the-map, but on-the-ground relations among places, paths, and dominant natural features.

## Procedure

*(As demonstrated in Tilley 1994 Parts I–II.)*

1. **Theoretical framing** of space/place/landscape and bodily perception (Ch. 1).
2. **Ethnographic conceptual background** (not direct models) for land–myth–power relations (Ch. 2).
3. **Regional walking survey** of Mesolithic findspots and Neolithic monuments, recording:
   - relations to coast, rivers, rock outcrops, spurs, escarpments, ridges;
   - chamber/passage orientations and approaches;
   - intervisibility among monuments;
   - alignments to cursus/enclosures where relevant.
4. **Long-term comparison** Mesolithic locales → Neolithic monumentalization as process of enculturing land.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes some continuity of **topographic structure** as meaningful reference even when vegetation/detail is unknown. Explicitly refuses direct ethnographic transfer. Mesolithic "locales" are often invisible surface scatters — method for Neolithic upstanding monuments is stronger than for Mesolithic presence.

## History

Emerges within Anglophone [[post-processual-archaeology]] as a landscape and embodiment strand. [[tilley-christopher|Tilley]]'s *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* (1994) is the vault's flagship demonstration; related earlier critique appears in Shanks & Tilley theory volumes (queued). Later work formalizes viewsheds (GIS) and reconceptualizes dwelling ([[dwelling-perspective]]) without identical method packages.

## Exemplary Applications

Pembrokeshire portal dolmens/chambered cairns; Black Mountains Cotswold–Severn long cairns; Cranborne Chase earthen long barrows, Dorset Cursus, Hambledon Hill complex ([[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]]).

## Debates

Relation to [[processual-archaeology|processual]] site-catchment models; subjectivity critiques within [[processual-postprocessual-debate]]; later dwelling/taskscape programs ([[dwelling-perspective]]).

Use [[landscape-archaeology]] as the broader method page; keep Tilley 1994 as the phenomenological demonstration.

## Uptake and Critique in Historical Archaeology

De Cunzo & Ernstein trace the method's uptake into historical archaeology's landscape studies via Julian Thomas's "experiential space" — the claim that people build intimate relationships with inhabited spaces, transforming space into place through sequential, narrative bodily experience — and via embodiment/feminist approaches such as Lynn Meskell's concept of "somatisation" ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch.14).

A live critique cluster targets this experiential turn as it entered historical archaeology, attributed: **Paul Mullins** (2000) argues the phenomenological emphasis on the meaning-making self risks a neoliberal individualism, since "the individual" is itself an ideological abstraction only loosely tied to power relations; **Ann Stahl** (2002) argues interpretive/phenomenological approaches remain rooted in Cartesian mind–body dualism and privilege linguistic signification over material and structural analysis. This exchange runs parallel to, but is distinct from, the Tilley-era falsifiability critique already on this page — it targets the approach's individualism and dualism specifically as it was absorbed into historical archaeology's landscape-of-ideology debates (see [[landscape-archaeology]] for the adjacent critical-archaeology/ideology strand these critics write against).

## Interpreting Landscapes (2010) — procedure densified

*(Attributed — Tilley, *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010]]*, Ch. 1 and Ch. 10.)*

**Seven-stage field procedure:** familiarisation walks → sensory records at known prehistoric places → seasonal/weather revisits → multi-direction approaches → path-following between places → attention to "natural" places without archaeology → imaginative synthesis of life-worlds.

**Body–Place–Path triangle:** places gather persons and things; paths alter place character; landscapes are material relations embodied as metaphorical mind-in-body. Goal is shared *style* of metaphoric thought, not mind-reading of prehistoric intentions.

**Exemplars added:** [[stonehenge]], [[northern-cranborne-chase]], [[south-dorset-ridgeway]], [[east-devon-pebblebeds]], [[exmoor]], [[bodmin-moor]], [[west-penwith]] — comparative geology as method, not only single-region demonstration.
