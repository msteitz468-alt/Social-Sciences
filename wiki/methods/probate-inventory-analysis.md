# Probate Inventory Analysis

Use of court-ordered estate inventories as a documentary partner to excavated household assemblages. The title phrase of [[deetz-james|Deetz]]'s *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]* comes from an inventory formula noting overlooked minor goods still of value.

## What It Does

Reconstructs household material worlds from legal lists; enables comparison of named object classes with archaeological finds; tracks room names, furniture, and consumer goods over time.

## Procedure

Sample inventories by time, place, and wealth; code object classes and room associations; compare with ceramic/architectural sequences from excavation and standing structures.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Inventories miss much of what archaeologists recover (broken coarseware, faunal refuse, structural posts). Categories are appraisers' ethnocategories. Only inventoried deaths enter the frame.

## History

Long used by social historians; Deetz and peers made the inventory–assemblage dialogue central to HA pedagogy.

## Exemplary Applications

Deetz 1996: title phrase from 1658 Plymouth inventory; room-by-room taxonomies; folk-term pitfalls ("looking glass," "coarse"); Marley Brown dairy-ceramic concentrations (Essex/Plymouth); room-naming shift c. 1660; Parting Ways pension/probate poverty lists ($27 / $61.82½); livestock lists vs excavated bone mismatches (export vs consumption).

## Debates

How far inventories can carry cognitive or "worldview" inference; class and gender silences.
