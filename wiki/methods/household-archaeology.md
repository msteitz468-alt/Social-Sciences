# Household Archaeology

## What It Does

**Household archaeology** treats the **household** — co-residence and collective economic interest — as the primary analytical unit for prehistoric social and economic reconstruction. It asks how domestic groups organized labor, storage, risk, reproduction, and status competition, and how those micro-decisions aggregate into regional patterns (sedentism, farming commitment, inequality).

## Procedure

Summary resolution (not a field manual):

1. Identify architectural modules (houses, compounds, activity areas) as candidate household proxies.
2. Associate features (hearths, storage, refuse, craft debris, burials) with modules where stratigraphy allows.
3. Compare households within a settlement for wealth/status differentiation.
4. Link household developmental cycles (e.g. Fortes-type expansion/fission) to settlement layout change over time.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Co-residence ≈ household** is a working assumption, not identity (Tringham & Krstić and others argue co-residence can be operationally sufficient even when ideal family types fail — via Bogucki 1999).
- One structure may not equal one household; sequential rebuilding and multi-room compounds require caution.
- Wealth differentials may reflect lifecycle stage rather than permanent rank.
- Bottom-up isolation of households can miss suprahousehold arenas (factions, hamlets, public architecture).

## History

- Ethnographic formalization of “household” when ideal family types mismatched observed co-residence (Wilk & Netting 1984, as cited by Bogucki).
- **Charter archaeological program:** *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* (Flannery ed. 1976; study [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]]) made the Formative house, [[household-cluster]], and gendered work areas primary recovery units and nested them in an explicit multi-scalar design (activity area → community → region). Flannery reported that as of that literature search there was **not a single published plan of a complete Early Formative house** — the volume’s house chapter and Winter’s Tierras Largas sampling were framed as correcting that gap.
- European Neolithic household studies (e.g. Tringham) and New World Formative work made domestic units central to inequality debates.
- [[bogucki-peter|Bogucki]] (*[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|Origins]]* 1999) makes **competing self-interested households under risk** the engine of [[transegalitarian]] differentiation and a prerequisite for understanding agricultural commitment via late-forager “proto-households.”

## Exemplary Applications

- Formative Mesoamerica house compounds and clusters (Flannery tradition; detail below)
- Late Neolithic European settlements (Bogucki synthesis cases)
- Hohokam households, ballcourts, and roads (Bogucki Ch.6 case)
- Link to [[aggrandizer]] models and [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]]

## From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — The Early Mesoamerican Village

All claims in this section attributed to Flannery, Winter, and Flannery & Winter in *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* (1976), unless otherwise marked. Primary study object: [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]].

### Multi-scalar program (Flannery)

Flannery framed Formative village research as nested analytical levels rather than sherd-layer “telephone booths.” As he set out the units: **activity area** (spatially restricted task locus — tools, waste, raw materials ± features); **feature** (nonportable facilities such as hearths or storage pits); **male or female work areas** within the house; the **house**; the **[[household-cluster]]** (Winter’s term: house plus surrounding storage pits, burials, middens, and features reliably associated with the same household); then courtyard group / residential ward / **community**, regional valley networks, and interregional exchange. Flannery & Winter diagrammed the nesting as: activity area → feature → male/female work area → house → household cluster → community (Fig. 2.18). The programmatic goal was to make the **region**, not the individual site, the basis of analysis, then excavate so that houses and features within natural/cultural strata had equal opportunity to be recovered and activity areas reflecting different household roles could be seen.

### Winter’s household cluster and Tierras Largas

Marcus C. Winter formalized the **household cluster** as the archaeological unit between house/activity area and community, carefully distinguishing cluster (remains) from household (interacting people). He reported that a typical Early–Middle Formative cluster might include one house, two to six large storage pits, one to three graves, and an open area of 20–40 m, with features of a single cluster at [[tierras-largas]] sometimes scattered over ~**300 m²** — so small exposures can miss the unit. At Tierras Largas (1969 probability sample; see site page), Winter reconstructed ~**8–12 household clusters** per period, separated by courtyards or open work areas 20–40 m across — Flannery cast this as among the first serious applications of probability sampling to extensive horizontal exposure of Formative deposits and as maps approximating Formative village layout.

### Flannery house plans (Oaxaca Early Formative)

Flannery described Oaxaca Early Formative houses as wattle-and-daub structures with whitewashed walls; **Tierras Largas phase** (1400–1150 B.C.) houses often used many small posts (10–15 cm; up to ~20–25/house), while **San José phase** (1150–850 B.C.) houses used fewer larger posts (20–25 cm), largest at corners, with pine preferred for burnt posts (sometimes carried ~20 km from mountains). Complete house recovery required wide horizontal exposure of shallow sites; deep stratified “telephone booths” could not yield full plans. [[san-jose-mogote]] provided extensive residential areas (Areas A and C among them); Flannery noted ~40 acres of Early Formative houses at the site as a research-design problem that demanded sampling rather than total dig.

### Male/female work areas and activity classification (Flannery & Winter)

Flannery & Winter treated **activity areas** as the smallest spatial unit and proposed an intermediate **male/female work-area** level between activity area and house, using ethnographic analogy (Vogt’s Zinacantan hearth vs altar division) while flagging Formative Oaxaca problems: houses usually lacked fixed hearths (cooking often in portable pottery braziers) and rarely had altars. Their candidate women’s tools included metates; they suspected most flint chipping in male work areas. Provisional spatial patterning: some early Oaxaca houses showed presumed women’s tools on one side of a door-midline and men’s on the other (e.g. House 1 at Tierras Largas ~900 B.C.: chert tools left of door, bone needles/cornhuskers/spindle-whorl discs + ash right; House 2 at San José Mogote: chert/shell working left, brazier/food right). They stressed the sample was **not statistically significant** and floors often disturbed.

On inter-household variation they classified activities as: (1) **universal household** (grinding stones, storage pits/jars, maize, cottontail, charcoal braziers — households autonomous in basic subsistence); (2) **possible household specialization** (e.g. pressure-retouch chert specialist and bone-tool manufacture features at Tierras Largas; ground-polished celt manufacture at several San José Mogote wards); (3) **regional specialization** (shell-ornament production nearly every extensively excavated 1150–850 B.C. house at Tierras Largas and San José Mogote; macaw-feather prep in a few Tierras Largas pits; salt at Fábrica San José / Las Salinas); (4) **possibly unique specializations** (e.g. magnetite-mirror production concentrated in one residential ward at San José Mogote).

### Charter status

Within [[processual-archaeology]], the volume made household archaeology a **multi-scalar research program** rather than a residual domestic category: probability sampling within communities (Winter), house-floor mapping, gendered activity inference under explicit caveats, and upward linkage to catchment, settlement systems, and exchange (Winter & Pires-Ferreira household-level obsidian variation as a test of reciprocity vs pooling). Later Wilk–Netting ethnographic formalization refined co-residence vs household; the 1976 operational sequence remains a formative charter for New World Formative domestic archaeology.

## Mesopotamian household archaeology (Matthews 2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]].)* Matthews surveys
household archaeology as a coherent Mesopotamian sub-field with recurring problems of
floor-assemblage formation and inference. Elizabeth Stone's work at Maškan-šapir modeled a
consensual, mixed-residence urban household organization and is notable for its
research-question-driven site selection. At Abu Salabikh, J. N. Postgate's surface-scraping
excavation recovered House H — a mud-brick house with rooms around a courtyard, disrupted by
intra-mural grave-digging (a robbed shaft burial with jewellery and equid remains in the
courtyard; three children buried together with beads and pendants) — and inferred that
roughly 90% of excavated space at the site was residential, supporting population estimates.
Postgate observed that artefacts are rarely found "in their original context of use" —
"cocktail shakers in the lounge and chamber pots in the bedroom" — hence the field's reliance
on micro-debris and micromorphology (pioneered systematically at the settlement scale by
Wendy Matthews) rather than gross artefact distribution. Elizabeth Henrickson's study
(1981; 1982) of Khafajah and Tell Asmar (Diyala region) inferred room functions from spatial
patterning and correlated large multi-suite residences with proximity to temples and
palaces, but Matthews flags a **circularity problem**: house size was used both as evidence
for family type and as the basis for wealth inference. Marlies Heinz (1997) classified 14
Euphrates settlements (4th–2nd millennium BC) by degree of planning and linked layout to
social and economic status. Michael Roaf's excavation of an Ubaid-period house at Tell
Madhhur (Hamrin region, 5th millennium BC) used ethnographic analogy on intact artefact
assemblages to infer domestic activities, though Matthews judges Roaf's own conclusion — that
"we still can do little more than speculate about Ubaid social structure" — as overly
pessimistic given the hypotheses Roaf himself generates. Paolo Brusasco's study (1999–2000)
of 79 Old Babylonian houses at Ur combined spatial-access analysis with ethnographic analogy
and textual archive evidence, finding low segmentation of household activity space and
identifying family types ranging from poor nuclear to rich extended households. Patty Jo
Wattenmaker's work is cited by Matthews for treating non-elite household production as an
active social agent rather than a passive economic backdrop.

## Debates

Whether households are the right *primary* unit vs kin groups, houses-as-social-persons, or community-level analysis; relation to Service/Fried stage labels (Bogucki prefers household language over premature “chiefdom” for many early agrarian cases). Flannery & Winter’s gender-partition inferences remain provisional by their own admission (small samples, disturbed floors, missing hearths/altars). Henrickson's circularity problem (house size as both cause and evidence of wealth inference) recurs across the Mesopotamian household literature ([[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]]).

## North American Historical Archaeology: A Parallel Genealogy

Julia A. King traces a distinct North American strand of household archaeology, developed in **historical archaeology** independently of (but later converging with) the Mesoamerican program above ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], King ch.16). Chesapeake household archaeology began in 1930s New Deal programs, which treated "founding fathers'" homes as sites of national-values formation and reconstructed architectural space and furnishings to emphasize "good taste" — many of the oldest US archaeology programs are still based at these "great-man" sites, now museums (attributed to Wells 1993 via King). The 1960s "new social history" then redirected historical archaeologists toward the everyday lives of ordinary people, dovetailing with New Archaeology's "lifeways" focus; Henry Glassie (1977) described the field as promising "an authentic history" by "rescuing from anonymity the average people of the past." King also traces borrowing from the Mesoamerican household literature summarized above (40+ years of study by 2006) — Bill Rathje, trained in Mesoamerican archaeology, refocused on contemporary Tucson household garbage ("garbology"), informing public policy and revealing late-20th-century domestic social life.

King's chapter argues **households ≠ families** as analytical categories, and that archaeologists have partly resolved definitional disputes by studying what a household *does* rather than what it *is* (attributed to Beaudry 1984, Deetz 1982, Hendon 1996 via King) — a methodological convergence with Mary Beaudry's and James Deetz's broader emphasis on domestic practice.

King periodizes the North American approaches as **adaptive** (1970s–80s, processual, linking domestic assemblage variation to environmental conditions — e.g. Kathleen Deagan's St Augustine ceramic patterning), **structuralist** (Deetz and Glassie reading "deep mental structures" — Deetz's 1977 "medieval" to "Georgian mindset" thesis), and **critical** (1980s, Mark Leone and colleagues at Annapolis reading material culture as ideology naturalizing capitalist social relations). King's own critique, attributed, is that all three approaches treat material culture as merely *reflective* rather than constitutive, and reduce households to normative statistical abstractions — "faceless blobs" identified only via a household head (attributed to Beaudry 1989a; Tringham 1991). Feminist and practice-theory turns (Bourdieu, Giddens) followed, treating class, gender, and ethnicity as continuously negotiated rather than fixed.

King notes the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation estimate that roughly 90% of US archaeology is CRM-driven (compliance) work, and that some of the best contextual household archaeology has come from commercial/CRM practice despite its cost pressures.

**Case study — Patuxent Point, Maryland** (excavated by King and Ubelaker 1996, ahead of residential development): a late-17th-century Chesapeake site with two contemporaneous structures — a larger, better-built principal dwelling and a flimsier one-room secondary structure — interpreted as a household organized into a planter's family (by legal status/kinship) and unrelated bound laborers, with architecture rather than portable goods the principal marker of social identity. The associated 19-person cemetery showed two burial clusters; a youth of likely African ancestry was buried in Christian style, cradling a white clay tobacco pipe, and was **not** spatially segregated from the European-ancestry burials — King uses this to caution explicitly against retrojecting present-day racial categories onto 17th-century evidence, where difference may have tracked religion as much as ancestry (attributed to King, citing Hannaford 1996, S. Jones 1997).
