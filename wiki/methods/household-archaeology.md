# Household Archaeology

## What It Does

**Household archaeology** treats the **household** — co-residence and collective economic interest — as the primary analytical unit for prehistoric social and economic reconstruction. It asks how domestic groups organized labor, storage, risk, reproduction, and status competition, and how those micro-decisions aggregate into regional patterns (sedentism, farming commitment, inequality).

## Procedure

Summary resolution (not a field manual):

1. Identify architectural modules (houses, compounds, activity areas) as candidate household proxies.
2. Associate features (hearths, storage, refuse, craft debris, burials) with modules where stratigraphy allows.
3. Compare households within a settlement for wealth/status differentiation.
4. Link household developmental cycles (e.g. Fortes-type expansion/fission) to settlement layout change over time.

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Co-residence ≈ household** is a working assumption, not identity (Tringham & Krstić and others argue co-residence can be operationally sufficient even when ideal family types fail — via Bogucki 1999).
- One structure may not equal one household; sequential rebuilding and multi-room compounds require caution.
- Wealth differentials may reflect lifecycle stage rather than permanent rank.
- Bottom-up isolation of households can miss suprahousehold arenas (factions, hamlets, public architecture).

## History

- Ethnographic formalization of “household” when ideal family types mismatched observed co-residence (Wilk & Netting 1984, as cited by Bogucki).
- Flannery’s 1976 *The Early Mesoamerican Village* highlighted the scarcity of complete Early Formative house plans — a methodological prod.
- European Neolithic household studies (e.g. Tringham) and New World Formative work made domestic units central to inequality debates.
- [[bogucki-peter|Bogucki]] (*[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|Origins]]* 1999) makes **competing self-interested households under risk** the engine of [[transegalitarian]] differentiation and a prerequisite for understanding agricultural commitment via late-forager “proto-households.”

## Exemplary Applications

- Formative Mesoamerica house compounds (Flannery tradition)
- Late Neolithic European settlements (Bogucki synthesis cases)
- Hohokam households, ballcourts, and roads (Bogucki Ch.6 case)
- Link to [[aggrandizer]] models and [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]]

## Debates

Whether households are the right *primary* unit vs kin groups, houses-as-social-persons, or community-level analysis; relation to Service/Fried stage labels (Bogucki prefers household language over premature “chiefdom” for many early agrarian cases).
