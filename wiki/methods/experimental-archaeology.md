# Experimental Archaeology

Controlled or actualistic replication of past technologies, constructions, and depositional conditions in order to interpret [[artifact|artifacts]], monuments, and the [[archaeological-record]]. Textbook synthesis of scope, procedures, and limits here follows [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Renfrew & Bahn 2012]].

## What It Does

Experimental archaeology addresses how things were made and used, and how materials change after deposition, by re-running processes with technologies available (or argued to have been available) to original makers. Renfrew & Bahn (2012) treat questions of manufacture and function as approachable by four complementary routes—purely archaeological evidence, scientific analysis of objects, ethnography, and experiment—and urge consultation of modern experts in equivalent technologies. They present long-term experimental work as an effective way to study [[formation-processes]] that affect physical preservation. Related but distinct: living observation of material culture under [[ethnoarchaeology]].

## Procedure

Typical procedures (as synthesized in Renfrew & Bahn 2012) include:

1. **Define a technological or formation question** (e.g. how a Folsom point was fluted; how a chalk bank weathers; whether a leather shield works in combat).
2. **Constrain tools and materials** to those available to the original makers where the test is replication of past technique; or use controlled modern conditions where the goal is process physics (heat treatment, wear baselines).
3. **Execute and record** time, effort, product morphology, wear, and failure modes.
4. **Compare** experimental products, wear, and residues to archaeological specimens (often jointly with [[use-wear-analysis]] and [[residue-analysis]]).
5. **Infer only what is warranted**: experiments usually **narrow possibilities**; they do not by themselves prove which of several workable techniques was used historically.

**Stone-tool replication** is a major subtype: making exact copies of tool types with only technologies available to original makers, to assess processes, time, and effort (masters of the tradition include François Bordes and Donald Crabtree; many archaeologists are now proficient). **Refitting** of original flakes and tools (a complementary analytic, not itself experimental) demonstrates the precise knapping sequence; Renfrew & Bahn contrast it with replication, which cannot usually prove which techniques were used.

Experimental baselines also calibrate microwear: Lawrence Keeley’s SEM-enabled polish categories were built against tools used experimentally for known tasks (wood, bone, hide, meat, antler, non-woody plants).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- **Analogical status.** Skill, raw-material quality, social context, and time pressure differ from the past; success shows feasibility, not historical identity of method.
- **Replication vs demonstration.** Renfrew & Bahn (2012) state replication cannot usually prove conclusively which techniques were used, though it narrows possibilities (e.g. Pavel’s lever-and-beam raises of trilithon/pukao replicas make “visiting astronauts” accounts unnecessary and show modern observers often overestimate mechanical difficulty—without proving Stone Age people used that exact method).
- **Craft expertise hierarchy.** For traditional fine-metal techniques still practiced in North African towns and Near Eastern bazaars, Renfrew & Bahn argue careful study of skilled craftspeople with generational experience usually teaches more than experimental archaeology by less adept experimenters.
- **Formation experiments sample short-to-medium timescales.** Overton Down already showed many archaeologically interesting changes occur within decades of burial and can be greater than previously suspected—but multi-century/millennial trajectories remain partly extrapolated.
- **Formation-process discrimination.** Distinguishing human tool cutmarks from beaver tooth marks (and similar human/non-human signatures) is a methodological imperative for reconstruction; experiment and comparative actualism support that discrimination.

## History

- **Formation-process earthworks.** The Overton Down experimental earthwork (southern England, 1960)—chalk and turf bank 21 m long, 7 m wide, 2 m high with parallel ditch, containing buried pottery, leather, and textiles—has been sectioned on a 2–128 year schedule. By the 1990s the bank had dropped ~25 cm then stabilized; textiles weakened within 4 years; 1992 excavations showed better preservation in the less biologically active chalk bank than in the turf core (Renfrew & Bahn 2012, reporting the experiment). The same experimental earthwork informs aerial/remote interpretation: grass-stabilized banks/ditches can “fossilize” in relief within ~16 years (link to [[remote-sensing]] / [[aerial-archaeology]] detection).
- **Lithic replication tradition.** Mid–late 20th-c. knapping proficiency (Bordes, Crabtree, Toth, and others) became a standard part of technological archaeology. Crabtree, guided by a 17th-c. Spanish account of Aztec obsidian blade-making, showed Folsom channel flakes could be pressed with a chest-held T-crutch. Nicholas Toth’s Oldowan-range replication suggested simple flakes may have been primary tools and cores incidental by-products—reversing earlier scholarly emphasis.
- **Heat treatment.** Experimental work (Purdy & Brooks on Florida chert; South African silcrete contexts) shows deliberate heating alters color, luster, microstructure, and flaking properties; ESR and thermoluminescence can detect heat alteration.
- **Agricultural actualism.** Peter Reynolds’s Butser Experimental Iron Age Farm reconstructs ~300 BC farming with period-appropriate tools, crops, and stock, testing house engineering, tillage, storage pits, and yields (including manuring stanol baselines later applied archaeologically).
- **Broader technological experiments** (transport, fitting stonework, armor, metallurgy, pottery temper) form a continuous mid–late 20th-c. program as summarized in Renfrew & Bahn Ch. 8.

## Exemplary Applications

As reported in Renfrew & Bahn 2012:

- **Overton Down** — long-term bank/ditch and buried-organic preservation experiment (formation processes / taphonomy).
- **Toth’s Oldowan replication** — flake-primary vs core-primary functional model.
- **Crabtree Folsom fluting** — pressure with T-crutch solves a Paleo-Indian manufacture puzzle.
- **Boxgrove-style hand-axe butchery** — nine replica flint hand-axes used by a professional butcher on a roe deer carcass; skilled use showed the Acheulian hand-axe as an outstanding versatile butchery tool.
- **Haury bead time-cost** — tiny pueblo beads (~2 mm; cactus-spine perforation) estimated at ~15 minutes each (~480 working days for a ~15,000-bead necklace)—labor as value proxy.
- **Protzen’s Inca stone fitting** — hammerstone bouncing dresses a face in ~20 minutes and achieves a fit in ~90 minutes, matching historical accounts and block scars.
- **Pavel trilithon / Easter Island pukao** — few people with ropes, levers, and lubricated beams raise lintels/topknots in days.
- **Coles’ leather shield** — Bronze Age Irish leather-shield replica withstood combat while a metal counterpart was cut to ribbons—implying surviving metal shields may be prestige/ritual and underscoring organic materials’ functional importance.
- **Somerset Levels woodworking (John & Bryony Coles)** — experimental axe facet signatures (stone vs bronze; individual edge nicks) showed ≥10 axes on one Bronze Age track; beaver tooth marks distinguished from toolmarks.
- **Mining and quarry estimates** — antler picks effective against hard rock; Easter Island and Aswan unfinished products constrain manufacture rates.
- **Batán Grande / Moche metallurgy** — furnace experiments reaching ~1100 °C; Lechtman’s electrochemical gold-plating reconstruction matching Loma Negra artifacts.
- **Haya steelmaking (Schmidt & Avery)** — rebuilt traditional forced-draft furnaces producing low- to medium-carbon steel, archaeologically verified against ancient lakeshore furnaces.
- **Pottery temper experiments** — crushed burnt shell resists heat shock better than coarse sand; finer temper yields stronger pots.
- **Watercraft replicas** — vessels based on excavated remains outperform art-only reconstructions (paired with [[underwater-archaeology]] finds such as Kyrenia and Uluburun).

## Debates

- **Proof vs possibility.** How far can experiment go beyond “it works this way” toward “it was done this way”? Renfrew & Bahn’s textbook position is cautious: experiment and ethnography indicate; for stone tools, only [[use-wear-analysis|microwear]] can prove how a tool was used and on what material, because one tool can serve many purposes and the same tasks can be done by many tools.
- **Experiment vs living craft.** Preference for ethnoarchaeological/traditional craft observation over novice experiment when living mastery exists (fine metalwork).
- **Formation vs technology.** Overton-type programs answer preservation and earthwork evolution; replication programs answer manufacture—both fall under “experimental archaeology” but answer different middle-range questions.
- **Eoliths / geofacts.** Criteria of human agency (bulbs of percussion; multi-sided flaking; retouch) grew partly from experimental and comparative fracture studies (e.g. Pedra Furada natural-breakage baselines)—debates over earliest tools remain context-sensitive.

## Schiffer & Skibo — Strategy 2 and performance matrix (1995 Ch. 22)

*(Attributed — Schiffer and Skibo 1987; *[[schiffer-behavioral-archaeology-first-principles-1995|First Principles]]* Ch. 22.)* Within [[behavioral-archaeology]], experimental archaeology is Strategy 2 (present material culture → general laws), paired with [[ethnoarchaeology]] and distinguished from carefully contrived variable-control experiments vs “living archaeology” observation (Schiffer 1976 / 1995 Ch. 6). Schiffer and Skibo offer a provisional framework for technological change: **recipes for action**, **teaching frameworks**, and **techno-science** as components of technological knowledge; technical choices determine formal properties that affect **performance characteristics** (often with polar effects forcing suboptimal compromises); sources of change include functional field, use-context feedback, and producer pressure. Analytic method: build a **performance matrix** for each artifact (performance characteristics across life-history activities), compare weightings, link compromise patterns via correlates to lifeway/social organization. Their Archaic–Woodland ceramic case (fiber vs mineral temper experiments) concludes Late Archaic designs weighted ease of manufacture and portability; later Woodland designs heating effectiveness and longer use-lives (impact, thermal shock, abrasion) — extending Braun 1983 (attributed). Gaps in modern science of extinct technologies must be filled by experiment (attributed).
