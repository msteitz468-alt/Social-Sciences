## What It Does

Process tracing examines the decision process linking initial conditions to outcomes
within a case, treating each decision, or each perceived action or intention, as a new
observation — often reaching down to the level of the individual actor. King, Keohane,
and Verba (KKV), citing George and McKeown (1985), present it as a strategy for
generating additional [[observable-implications]] from what appears to be a single
case, thereby addressing the observations-versus-inferences half of an
[[indeterminate-research-design]] without necessarily collecting new cases
([[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994]]).

## Procedure

The researcher reconstructs, in sequence, the chain of decisions, perceptions, and
actions connecting a hypothesized cause to an outcome, recording each step as a
distinct observation bearing on the causal hypothesis. Under KKV's framing, this is a
special case of the general strategy discussed under [[research-design]] for
increasing the number of observations available to a design — here, by descending to a
finer level of analysis within a single unit rather than adding new units or new
measures.

## Assumptions and Limitations

KKV insist that process tracing and mechanism-search techniques are extensions of,
not exceptions to, their general causal-inference logic: [[unit-homogeneity]],
[[endogeneity]], and [[omitted-variable-bias]] must all still be addressed at the
individual or decision level, not waived because the evidence is fine-grained or
qualitative. They state the standard explicitly: "techniques such as process tracing
should provide more opportunities to refute a theory, not more opportunities to evade
refutation." On KKV's account, the risk of process tracing is that a researcher
selectively narrates a decision chain in a way that appears to confirm a hypothesis
without exposing it to any real chance of falsification — a concern more general than,
but continuous with, the [[selection-bias]] problems KKV describe for case selection.

## History

The method's name and initial elaboration are attributed by KKV to George and McKeown
(1985); KKV's own 1994 treatment does not introduce process tracing as a novel method
but assimilates it into their broader inference framework, treating it as one
technique among several (alongside formal estimators) for meeting a single underlying
standard of causal inference — a claim that is itself the fullest statement of their
position, in the book's concluding chapter, that qualitative and quantitative research
share one logic of inference, differing in technique but not in the causal-inference
standards each must meet.

## Exemplary Applications

KKV's discussion of process tracing is conceptual rather than tied to a single named
empirical application in the extracted material; it is presented alongside the
book's broader roster of case-selection worked examples (Skocpol, Porter, Tilly,
Kohli, Laitin, Achen and Snidal) as the qualitative, within-case counterpart to those
cross-case selection strategies.

## Debates

KKV's own framing of process tracing — as an observation-generating extension of their
single [[causal-inference]] logic, bound by the same unit-homogeneity, endogeneity, and
omitted-variable-bias standards as any other technique — is one position among several.
Subsequent methodologists (notably Bennett, Collier, and George) developed process
tracing as a distinct causal-inference logic in its own right, grounded in
within-case, mechanism-based reasoning rather than treated as a variable-oriented
technique in KKV's sense. This disagreement is a live position within
[[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]] and bears on the wider
[[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]].
