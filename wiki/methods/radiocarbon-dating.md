# Radiocarbon Dating

## What It Does

Radiocarbon dating measures the decay of carbon-14 (C-14), a radioactive isotope produced in the upper atmosphere and mixed through the living **[[exchange-reservoir]]**, to estimate the time since a sample left that exchange (typically the death of an organism). **[[libby-willard-f|Willard F. Libby]]** developed the method at the University of Chicago with **[[anderson-e-c|E. C. Anderson]]** and **[[arnold-james-r|James R. Arnold]]** in the late 1940s; first dates were announced in 1949; the founding monograph is *[[libby-radiocarbon-dating-1952|Radiocarbon Dating]]* (1952; 2nd ed. 1955). Libby received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1960. Ages are conventionally reported as years **Before Present (BP)** with present fixed at **1950 CE**, on the **[[libby-half-life]]** scale.

## Procedure

### Physical basis (Libby 1952/55 — attributed)

Libby argued that cosmic-ray secondaries produce neutrons in the atmosphere; thermal neutrons react with nitrogen-14 to form carbon-14:

\[
\mathrm{N}^{14} + n \rightarrow \mathrm{C}^{14} + \mathrm{H}^{1}
\]

(attributed, Libby ch. I). The radiocarbon is oxidized to CO₂, enters the global **[[exchange-reservoir]]** (atmosphere, biosphere, surface ocean), and is taken up by living organisms. At death, exchange stops and the residual C-14 decays exponentially with the adopted half-life. Equilibrium between production and decay implies a roughly steady contemporary specific activity in living carbon — the zero-age baseline for dating (attributed, Libby chs. I–II).

### Laboratory practice (1950s Chicago method — attributed)

1. **Sample selection:** prefer materials that retain original carbon atoms from the death/deposition event — charcoal and wood preferred; grasses, cloth, peat usable; shell treated with caution; unburned bone a poor prospect (attributed, Libby ch. IV).
2. **Pretreatment:** physical cleaning (rootlets), acid removal of secondary carbonate where needed.
3. **Conversion:** combustion or acidification to CO₂; purification; in the original solid-carbon method, reduction to elementary carbon and mounting for a **screen-wall Geiger counter** with heavy steel shielding and anticoincidence (attributed, Libby chs. IV–V). Libby's 1965 addendum states the black-carbon method is **obsolete**; later practice uses pure CO₂ (or other gases) in proportional counters, and eventually accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS).
4. **Age calculation:** compare sample specific activity to the contemporary assay; convert via the decay law using the **[[libby-half-life]]** (5568 ± 30 years in the monograph body; conventional reporting later standardized on 5570 while the physical half-life is ~5730 ± 40).

### Calibration (post-Libby)

Raw radiocarbon years are **not** calendar years. Atmospheric C-14 production has varied; tree-ring (**[[dendrochronology]]**) calibration converts uncalibrated BP to calendar estimates (cal BP / cal BCE). Never mix uncalibrated and calibrated dates in one comparison.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Libby's program assumed (attributed, Libby 1952/55):

1. Roughly constant cosmic-ray production of C-14 over the dating range (supported in the book mainly by known-age checks and production–inventory order-of-magnitude agreement; 1965 addendum notes magnetic-field variation as a remaining concern).
2. A well-mixed **[[exchange-reservoir]]** so that living matter shares a common contemporary assay (ch. II world-wide wood tests: mean ~15.3 dpm/g C, no excess geographic scatter beyond counting error — attributed, Libby).
3. Closed system after death (no later carbon exchange or contamination).

**Characteristic failure modes** (Libby; **[[johnson-frederick|Frederick Johnson]]** ch. VII; later users):

- **Contamination** (modern rootlets, fungal carbon, museum handling) → ages too young.
- **Dead / old carbon** (limestone hard-water bicarbonate; reused timber) → ages too old or too young depending on source.
- **Reservoir effects** (marine shell, upwelling) → offset from atmospheric baseline; Libby already noted shell assays ~16.1 vs biosphere 15.3 dpm/g and hard-water lakes at ~77% of modern wood activity (attributed, Libby chs. I–II, IV).
- **Sample ≠ event:** deposit mixing, misassigned strata (Johnson stresses this as frequent).
- **Half-life and production:** 1% half-life error → 1% absolute age error (Libby ch. III); production variation requires calibration, not half-life rescaling alone.
- **Egyptian fifth-millennium discrepancy:** by 1963–65 Libby reported First Dynasty samples at 4000–5000 BP appearing ~500–600 years too young — unresolved then between historical chronology and method (attributed, Libby 1965 addendum).

Quoted errors in early lists are often **counting standard deviations only** (48-hour runs in the Chicago catalogue), not full systematic uncertainty (attributed, Libby ch. VI).

## History

### Invention and monograph (primary: Libby)

Natural radiocarbon was established with concentrated methane samples (A. V. Grosse / Houdry) and Chicago low-level counting; Libby credits Anderson and Arnold as indispensable collaborators and the Wenner-Gren Foundation (~$35,000, 1948–50) plus the University of Chicago as principal funders, with a 1949 Air Force low-level counting contract (attributed, Libby 1st-ed. preface). Sample priorities were set by the **Committee on Carbon 14** of the AAA and GSA — Frederick Johnson (chair), Donald Collier, Richard Foster Flint, Froelich Rainey (attributed, Libby preface).

*Radiocarbon Dating* (1952; 2nd ed. 1955 with expanded date list and Johnson's ch. VII; Phoenix 1965 with addenda) states principles, world-wide assay tests, half-life, laboratory practice, and the Chicago **C-series** dates through fall 1954. Known-age checks (Egyptian royal wood, historically fixed Mesopotamian beams, tree-ring wood) were used to validate the decay curve (attributed, Libby chs. I, VI).

### Archaeological uptake (1950s — attributed positions)

**[[johnson-frederick|Johnson]]** (ch. VII) argued that relative stratigraphic and stylistic chronologies lack calendar precision; radiocarbon offered world-wide, intercomparable absolute ages, but only if samples were carefully collected and dates critically evaluated against — not automatically rejected by — process-rate and ice-sheet opinions (attributed, Johnson in Libby 1955/65). He treated selection/collection errors as dominant, flagged shell and long-stored museum samples, and used early American, Southwestern, Eastern Woodland, and glacial (e.g. Two Creeks) results to press archaeological time-scales.

**[[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]]** (1957) already treated radiocarbon as "the most promising method... provided by natural science," reporting Libby's cosmic-ray theory and the **5,568 ± 30** half-life, and stressing that most errors trace to sample selection and collection (attributed, Clark 1957). In *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* (1961) he restates the promise of Libby's 1949 method for world-wide absolute years, explains the decay principle and the inherent statistical uncertainty of individual assays, and adopts a **pattern-not-point** practice: isolated C-14 dates are generally avoided in the outline; chronology follows what prehistorians generally accept once radiocarbon, other geochronology, and earliest historical extrapolations are taken into account (attributed, Clark 1961 Introduction). All such dates in that book are uncalibrated. **[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]** (1957 preface to *Dawn* 6th ed.) called the technique still experimental and accepted results "with as much reserve as the physicists offer them" (attributed, Childe 1957).

### Mithen 2003 popular synthesis (*After the Ice*)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003.)* Mithen explains ¹⁴C decay dating of once-living carbon, association dating for stone tools, a ~40,000 BC practical limit, probabilistic ± ranges in BP, and calibration via dendrochronology (and other means) converting radiocarbon years to calendar BC. **All dates in the book are calendar years BC**; endnotes give radiocarbon dates with calibrated values at 1σ (attributed, ch. 2). He credits radiocarbon (plus genetics, residues, paleoclimate) as archaeology’s ordering tool of first importance (Epilogue). Exemplary applications he surveys include: Star Carr as first European site of any period radiocarbon-dated ([[clark-grahame|Clark]]); AMS cut-marked bone series for northern European recolonization (Housley et al. 1997); Meadowcroft contamination debates; Monte Verde and Clovis terminal ¹⁴C; Meltzer & Mead / Grayson pruning of megafaunal extinction dates; contested New Guinea pig antiquity; Niah Cave mixing problems; White Paintings Rockshelter fish-layer dating vs soft-sand mixing (all attributed case studies). Reinforces calibration hygiene already required on this page.

### Meltzer 2009 dating convention (*First Peoples in a New World*)

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009]]* 2009, “On Dates and Dating.”)* Meltzer explains that atmospheric/oceanic ¹⁴C has varied, so raw radiocarbon is not calendar age; calibration against known-age tree rings yields “cal BP.” **Because calibrating radiocarbon ages for the late Pleistocene is neither straightforward nor certain, he deliberately does not use calibrated ages in the book; all ages are radiocarbon years BP** for comparability with the bulk of Pleistocene/first-Americans literature, accepting slight loss of chronological precision. He provides an OxCal 3.10 rough conversion table for 18,000–7400 BP at ~500-year intervals for readers. Younger Dryas–age thermohaline switches can scramble atmospheric:ocean ¹⁴C ratios (clock runs too slow or too fast), complicating first-American site dating; early YD plateaus can stretch Clovis radiation and Late Paleoindian “settling in” in calendar years relative to raw ¹⁴C. **Wiki implication:** when integrating Meltzer 2009, stamp ages as **uncalibrated ¹⁴C BP** and do not silently mix with Mithen-style calendar BC or later cal BP syntheses.

### Calibration and the radiocarbon revolutions (Renfrew 1973; Reich 2018)

David Reich frames radiocarbon's adoption in the 1950s–60s as archaeology's "first scientific revolution" — an absolute chronology independent of stylistic cross-dating and diffusionist assumption (as of Reich 2018). Before radiocarbon, European prehistory was widely read through an *[[ex-oriente-lux|ex oriente lux]]* frame. **[[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]**'s *[[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Before Civilization]]* (1973) used calibrated radiocarbon chronology to overturn short diffusionist chronologies for European megaliths and metallurgy (as of Reich 2018; Renfrew 1973).

Renfrew distinguishes two revolutions (attributed, Renfrew 1973): the **first** — Libby's method and a unified prehistoric clock (~1949–60); the **second** — **tree-ring calibration** showing pre-1200 BCE radiocarbon ages systematically too young when atmospheric production varied. Hans Suess's calibration curve (from 1967) and bristlecone-pine work ([[dendrochronology]]) made calendar conversion routine. Reich analogizes ancient DNA in the 2010s to radiocarbon in the 1950s–60s as a second scientific revolution (as of Reich 2018; see [[ancient-dna]]).

**The Milojčić dispute (pre-calibration):** Vladimir Milojčić rejected early radiocarbon results that contradicted his Vinča chronology; Renfrew notes calibration widened rather than closed the gap (attributed, Renfrew 1973). See [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]].

## Exemplary Applications

### From Libby's Chicago catalogue (uncalibrated Libby-scale years — attributed, Libby ch. VI)

- **Known-age checks:** Egyptian wood associated with Zoser, Sneferu, Sesostris; Mesopotamian beams fixed near Hammurabi's calendar (Nippur long counts); tree-ring sequoia / Broken Flute Cave wood.
- **Near East early villages:** Jarmo shell/charcoal series (Braidwood) among the earliest food-producing village pins in the list. In *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* (3rd ed. 1959), [[braidwood-robert|Braidwood]] reports **twelve** Jarmo determinations with preferred average **~6750 ± 200 B.C.** and a wild scatter **3250–9250 B.C.**; he teaches the statistical reading (two chances in three within ±) and contamination caution, and compares more consistent Jericho dates to the noisy Jarmo series (attributed; uncalibrated as of 1959).
- **Europe:** Lascaux charcoal; Stonehenge charcoal (later re-dated and calibrated in other programs).
- **Americas:** Two Creeks forest bed (glacial correlation); Folsom-related Lubbock; Bat Cave / Cochise; Huaca Prieta; Magellan/Palli Aike early South American pins discussed by Johnson.
- **Africa:** early dates on sites such as Florisbad / Cave of the Hearths series (as listed).

### Later synthesis applications

- Renfrew's radiocarbon-based critique of Near Eastern diffusionism for Stonehenge and other European megaliths (as of Reich 2018; Renfrew 1973).
- Radiocarbon chronology for sites and cultures throughout [[reich-who-we-are-2018]], including pre-Clovis Monte Verde and the Anzick Clovis infant (as of Reich 2018).

### Childe’s 1957 posture (*Dawn* 6th ed.) — attributed

Before calibration rewrote European prehistory, Childe treated radiocarbon as a new but untrusted pin — experimental, multi-century errors, Oriental priority not staked on any one absolute scheme. Tree-ring calibration and Renfrew’s synthesis are the later break.

### Clark's 1957 posture (*Archaeology and Society*) — attributed

Clark reported the Libby half-life and cosmic-ray theory, stressed selection/collection errors, and noted emerging final-glacial dates suggesting Zeuner–Milanković astronomical chronology "seem[ed] to be substantially too high" (attributed, Clark 1957). His printed "B.C." figures are uncalibrated radiocarbon years.

## Debates

- **Calibration vs raw BP:** settled as method hygiene — always state calibrated vs uncalibrated; Libby-scale conventional BP remains the reporting base.
- ***Ex oriente lux* short chronology:** underwritten by uncalibrated synchronisms; collapsed for many European cases after calibration (see [[ex-oriente-lux]], [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]], [[pots-and-peoples-debate]]).
- **When relative chronologies "refute" dates:** Johnson (1955/65) held that process-rate and ice-sheet opinions are weak standards for rejecting C-14; contamination and sample context are the first checks (attributed position).
- **Egyptian Old Kingdom / early dynastic offsets:** already flagged by Libby 1963/65; later calibration and historical chronology work continue the case-level discussion.

## Reflexivity

Radiocarbon entered archaeology through a **chemist–anthropologist collaboration** (Chicago lab + AAA/GSA Committee on Carbon 14), funded partly by Wenner-Gren and mid-century military low-level counting contracts. Early date lists privileged materials and regions that committees and excavators submitted — museum hair, royal Egyptian wood, North American charcoal — not a random sample of the human past. Johnson's chapter is itself a 1950s boundary document: optimistic about absolute time, insistent that archaeological hypotheses not casually veto lab ages, and still pre-calibration. Treating Libby-era printed ages as calendar years is a common secondary error; this wiki date-stamps and attributes them as uncalibrated Libby-scale results.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook synthesis (2012)

The following condenses how [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]] and [[bahn-paul|Paul Bahn]] present radiocarbon in *[[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology: Theories, Methods, and Practice]]* (6th ed. 2012, Ch. 4). It is a **textbook layer**—not a rewrite of the Libby primary densification above—and should be read as their 2012 synthesis.

### Role among absolute methods

As they rank methods, radiocarbon is the **main dating tool for the last ~50,000 years** and “the single most useful dating method” overall; calendars/historical chronologies and [[dendrochronology]] share the top tier of common absolute methods, while [[potassium-argon-dating]] and uranium-series matter most for the Paleolithic beyond ¹⁴C’s range (attributed positions, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). They place BP at **before 1950** for radioactive methods and recommend BC/AD conversion for recent millennia (attributed).

### Libby basis and measurement (textbook restatement)

They restate [[libby-willard-f|Willard Libby]]’s 1949 first dates: cosmic-ray neutrons convert atmospheric nitrogen to ¹⁴C; Libby’s half-life estimate **5568 years** (modern physical figure **5730**; labs still use 5568 for consistency—difference moot once calibrated); living organisms take up ¹⁴C until death, then residual concentration declines. Conventional counting of beta emissions (Geiger-counter lineage) needs ~**5 g pure carbon** after purification (~10–20 g wood/charcoal or 100–200 g bone); late 1970s–80s gas counters reduced sample size (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

### AMS

**Accelerator mass spectrometry (AMS)** counts ¹⁴C atoms directly; minimum sample ~**5–10 mg**, enabling precious materials and pollen. They note AMS becoming the **dominant technique**. Datable span remains ~**50,000 years**; hopes to push AMS to ~80,000 years have been hard to achieve partly due to contamination. Precision is often within c. **50–100 years** but with many complicating factors (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

AMS case applications they report: Turin Shroud dated by Tucson, Oxford, and Zurich labs to the **14th century AD** (calibrated AD 1260–1390; remains controversial); a grape pip from Hambledon Hill placing grapes/vines in southern Britain by **3500 BC** calendar years—over 3000 years earlier than previously supposed; AMS on charcoal pigments for Paleolithic cave art, including a Chauvet series centered ~**31,000 BP** that they flag as highly controversial (style, single-lab issues, decontamination, greater uncertainty on results >30,000 BP) (as of textbook; attributed).

### Calibration and the Second Radiocarbon Revolution

Libby’s assumption of constant atmospheric ¹⁴C is “not quite correct” (earth’s and sun’s magnetic fields vary). Tree-ring dating both revealed the inaccuracy and supplies calibration: before ~**1000 BC**, radiocarbon years are increasingly **too young** versus calendar years. Plotting ¹⁴C ages against bristlecone and oak master sequences produces calibration curves—what they call the **“Second Radiocarbon Revolution.”** Tree-ring calibration records extend ~**12,600 years**; as of their 2012 edition, **INTCAL09** reaches **50,000 Cal BP** via varved marine sediments and U-Th corals. Offsets of **4000–5000 years** can occur; **plateaux** (e.g. Iron Age 800–400 BC) make the same ¹⁴C ages span up to ~**400 calendar years** (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). See also Renfrew’s earlier programmatic treatment in [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973]] and [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]].

### How dates are published

Labs quote ages **BP before AD 1950**, form e.g. **3700 ±100 BP (OxA 1735)**, with laboratory code. Errors are one standard deviation (68.2% probability); they advise archaeologists to consider **two SD (95.4%)**. Calibrated dates as “Cal BC/AD” or “Cal BP” with the calibration dataset named. **Conventional age once measured never changes; calibrations do.** Bayesian methods combine non-chronometric archaeological information with ¹⁴C for new probability distributions (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

### Field rules, reservoir effects, and lab precision

Erroneous results often come from poor sampling/interpretation as much as lab error. Major field sources they list: pre-sampling contamination (groundwater, mineral concretions); sampling/handling (foil and sealed containers; avoid paper labels inside, modern carbon, glues; store dark to stop photosynthesis/mold); misunderstanding formation processes of the context—**first rule: do not submit a sample unless sure of its archaeological context**; old wood/reuse/bog oak can predate burial—prefer short-lived samples (twigs, charred grain). Strategy: **“one date is no date”**; build internal relative sequences (e.g. Gatecliff Shelter, Nevada—David Hurst Thomas) (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

**Marine reservoir effect:** marine organisms or humans/fauna with predominantly marine diets yield dates averaging **several centuries older** than contemporary terrestrial dates; a marine calibration curve is needed (e.g. ~400 years adjustment for Mesolithic Oronsay, Scotland), with local variations and no universal marine curve (attributed).

**Laboratory precision may be overestimated:** a comparative study of 30+ labs on the same sample showed uneven error estimation; one lab had systematic errors of ~200 years; safer to assume quoted ±50 might actually be **±80 or more**. Archaeologists should request evidence of accuracy/precision (attributed position).

### Bayesian analysis and wiggle-matching

Combining ¹⁴C with stratigraphic order and depositional knowledge via Bayesian methods (**OxCal**, **BCal**) can resolve chronologies to within a century, sometimes human generations. British Neolithic long barrows once seemed long-lived from raw dates; Bayesian models show short, transient construction-to-abandonment histories (as of textbook). **Wiggle-matching** tree-ring ¹⁴C series to the calibration curve can date felling within **10–20 years**; applied to Thera-related wood (Miletos chair; Thera olive branch) yielding mid-to-late **17th century BC** (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012). They treat the broader **Thera eruption** chronology (radiocarbon ~1610 BC vs Egyptian-cross-dated ~1500 BC) as one of the most intriguing controversies in archaeological science (attributed position).

### Impact

Radiocarbon works **anywhere with organic material** back ~50,000 years (too imprecise for roughly the last 400 years). It established broad chronologies for cultures lacking their own timescales. Calibration heightened this success and helped assert an **independent radiocarbon chronology for prehistoric Europe free from false links with Egyptian historical chronology**—material relevant to the collapse of diffusionist *ex oriente lux* dating ([[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]]) (attributed, Renfrew & Bahn 2012).

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]].)*

**Textbook dating hygiene (as of 2007):** W&O deliberately report uncalibrated “years ago” (b.p.) and “b.c.” dates (1,950-year difference keyed to A.D. 1950) to minimize confusion when calibrated regional sequences cannot be integrated cleanly with older uncalibrated site dates. They note dendrochronology could calibrate 0–12,400 cal B.P., with marine-record advances then allowing terrestrial calibration 12,400–26,000 cal B.P.; B.P. (caps) generally calibrated, b.p. (lowercase) uncalibrated; “cal B.P.” / “cal B.C.” becoming common.

**Principles and limits:** ¹⁴C dating (Libby, 1940s)—atmospheric ¹⁴C enters living organisms, decays after death with half-life ~5,730 years; standard methods reach ~50,000 years; atmospheric variation requires tree-ring correction curves; hemispheres differ; contamination is a risk. **AMS** (1970s): match-head-sized samples, better purification, dating of soot, dung temper, slag, textiles; three labs independently dated the Shroud of Turin linen to about A.D. 1260–1390 (as they report).

**Case they emphasize:** [[wenke-robert|Wenke]]’s team dated charcoal in Egyptian pyramid mortar—majority of dates ~400 years older than Egyptological historical chronology; critics invoked old wood, plant physiology, contamination, or calibration wiggles; 1994–95 resampling with botanical IDs still suggested monuments earlier than the historical chronology (as of W&O 2007).

### Stringer & Gamble — *In Search of the Neanderthals* (1993)

*(Attributed — [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993]]; as of 1993.)*

For the **Middle Palaeolithic / Neanderthal–modern transition**, conventional ¹⁴C is treated as inadequate above ~40,000 years — precisely the Levant and European window where ordering Neanderthals vs Moderns matters. Stringer & Gamble use this limit to motivate reliance on **[[thermoluminescence-dating|TL]]**, ESR, and uranium-series for reordering [[skhul]]/[[qafzeh]] vs [[kebara]]/[[tabun]] sequences (attributed). AMS is noted as improving precision but not yet fully solving the deep Middle Palaeolithic range (as of 1993).

### Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*(Attributed — [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]]; as of Stringer 2012.)*

**Limits for human evolution.** Half-life ~5,700 years; unreliable beyond ~**30 ka**; dates in “radiocarbon years” not calendar years (cosmic-ray/atmosphere fluctuations; can be off by up to ~10% over last 40 ka); one of its **least accurate phases covers Neanderthal demise and much of modern-human spread**. AMS (from ~1977) counts ¹⁴C atoms; milligram samples; contamination critical for 30–40 ka samples; ABOX (charcoal) and ultrafiltration (bone) improve Paleolithic reliability — e.g. Gough’s Cave ultrafiltration (Higham & Jacobi): occupation ~2–3 generations around 14.7 ka matching Greenland ice warming; Kent’s Cavern modern human jaw associated fauna ~40 ka (direct dating failed). Calibration tools: dendrochronology, varves, ice-core layers, coral terraces cross-checked with U-series. AMS also exposed the Sherborne “Ice Age” engraved horse bone as a ~600-year-old modern forgery (Stringer et al. 1995). Recent ¹⁴C at Grotte du Renne suggests Châtelperronian deposits suffered disturbance/mixing — methodological caution for industry–species associations. Bohunician dated by radiocarbon and luminescence ~45 ka.

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]]; Holocene environmental-dating perspective.)*

Roberts frames radiocarbon as the backbone of Holocene chronology: since Willard Libby's development of the method at Chicago in the late 1940s, ~100,000 determinations have been made, about half of Holocene age. He stresses the shift to a **calibrated calendar timescale** — the second edition expresses all ages as **Cal. yr BP**, using CALIB and OxCal, because bristlecone-pine and later tree-ring calibration (following Suess 1965) showed mid-Holocene ¹⁴C dates run up to ~600 years too young. The calibration curve's "wiggles" mean one ¹⁴C date can map to several calendar ages. A notorious ¹⁴C **plateau** at ~10,000 ¹⁴C yr BP (when the "clock stood still" for ~400 real years, attributed to abrupt CO₂/ocean-circulation change at deglaciation) is why he dates the Holocene onset in calendar rather than radiocarbon years (see [[holocene]]). Contamination cautions: the hard-water effect (Deevey et al. 1954) and [[mollusc-analysis|mollusc-shell]] recalcification. For the most recent centuries, radiocarbon fails (Suess and bomb effects) and is replaced by [[lead-210-dating|²¹⁰Pb/¹³⁷Cs]] dating.

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]* Preface.)* Cauvin claims his synthesis is among the first Near Eastern Neolithic overviews to use **calibrated** BC dates systematically (collaboration with Jacques Evin, Lyon-1), after calibration curves extended to ~20,000 BP. He distinguishes calibrated BC from uncalibrated bp/bc and warns that earlier literature (including his 1978 *Les premiers villages*) will look younger. All absolute dates drawn from the book should be tagged **as of Cauvin 2000 calibration**. Notes also record 14C revisions lowering Ganj Dareh and related Zagros sequences.

### Anthony densification (2007) — steppe chronology and freshwater reservoir effect

*(Attributed — [[anthony-david-w|David W. Anthony]], *[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]* 2007, including appendix.)*

- **Calibration practice in the book:** All BCE dates Anthony prints are **calibrated** radiocarbon; uncalibrated ages given as BP; conversion via **OxCal** (Oxford Radiocarbon Accelerator Unit) (R5-079). Reinforces this page’s hygiene: never mix cal and uncal series.
- **Radiocarbon revolution restated for steppe work:** Libby 1949 ended reliance on Near Eastern stylistic cross-dating for European prehistory; AMS and mandatory calibration were the major post-1949 refinements and slowed Soviet reception until revisions became unavoidable (R2-066–067). After 1991, CIS dating programs and lower error rates overthrew many older chronologies, including relationships in Anthony’s own 1985 dissertation (R2-069).
- **Freshwater reservoir effect (steppe-critical):** Old dissolved carbon in river water is absorbed by fish and then by heavy fish-eaters. High **¹⁵N** in human bone signals substantial aquatic diet; steppe cemeteries often show fish ~50% of meat diet even among cattle herders. Human-bone ¹⁴C dates can come out **100–500 radiocarbon years too old** relative to charcoal or herbivore bone from the same grave (R2-068; R5-080). Anthony applies a preliminary Bonsall–Cook-style linear ¹⁵N correction when series rest mainly on human bone, always warning in text; tables list uncorrected BP/BCE (R5-080). Worked example: DDII dates on human bone with average ¹⁵N 11.8% (~50% fish meat); after correction, oldest DDII graves at Yasinovatka and Dereivka ~5200–5000 BCE (R3-037).
- **Implication:** For Pontic-Caspian cemetery chronologies, **sample type** (human bone vs charcoal/herbivore) and diet are first-order reliability facts — same severity class as marine reservoir offsets already noted (Renfrew & Bahn marine curve; Libby hard-water lakes).

## Pacific chronometric hygiene and AMS (Kirch 2017)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]].)* Kirch dates the first Pacific radiocarbon determination to 1951: a sample from Kuliʻouʻou rockshelter, Oʻahu, returning AD 1004 ± 180 — a date that, uncorrected for the region's "old wood"/in-built-age problem (long-lived driftwood and heartwood charcoal dated well before the cultural event that burned it), helped inflate early East Polynesian settlement chronologies for decades. Kirch credits "**chronometric hygiene**" (Spriggs & Anderson 1993) — screening dates for material, context, and standard error — together with AMS dating of small, taxonomically identified short-lived samples (seed, twig, or annual-plant charcoal rather than unidentified wood) as the methodological shift that produced the current East Polynesian **short chronology** (see [[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]]). He also flags routine marine-reservoir correction for shell/marine samples, and reports U/Th (uranium-thorium) dating of coral abraders and branch-coral temple offerings — precise to roughly ±2 years — as an independent, non-radiocarbon check applied at Nukuleka (Tonga) and at Hawaiian and Society Islands temple sites. See [[pacific-settlement-chronology-debate]].

## Bellwood *First Farmers* densification (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; method-in-use cautions for early farming chronologies.)*

- **Epistemic role:** Archaeology’s advantage over pure linguistic reconstruction is evidence created at the time in question, often absolutely dated by radiocarbon or other methods (Bellwood’s framing).
- **AMS on short-lived plant remains:** Abu Hureyra domesticated rye — three grains AMS-dated as early as 10,700 BC (as of Bellwood 2005 synthesis); SE Asian rice cases (Gua Sireh grain in sherd ~2300 BC; Andarayan husks ~1600 BC) and Denham et al. 2003 re-dating of Kuk ditch networks as examples of AMS-driven revision.
- **Calibration plateaux and PPNA tempo:** Available C14 dates for PPNA overlap heavily ~10,000–9000 BC; radiocarbon calibration **“plateau”** prevents chronological precision, so single-region origin vs multi-area contemporaneous development is hard to decide on dates alone.
- **European earliest-Neolithic synthesis:** Gkiasta et al. 2003 radiocarbon synthesis is central to Bellwood’s tempo argument — Mesolithic side-by-side with Neolithic for centuries in parts of western Europe (S/C France, Portugal, Britain, Ireland).
- **Association hygiene (South Asia caution):** Koldihwa claimed rice/cord-marked pottery C14 to 6500 BC largely rejected for Neolithic association (Mahagara 2nd millennium BC; consensus dates do not relate to Neolithic material — Dhavalikar, Glover & Higham; accepted by Chakrabarti 1999): sample ≠ event / wrong cultural association.
- **Proxy misuse:** Hawaiian population curves using the distribution of habitation radiocarbon dates as a demographic proxy — long tail older than AD 1000 mostly old wood; European-contact disease fall-off (Bellwood’s Fig. 2.1 caveats).

Source: [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## Çatalhöyük North Bayesian case (Bayliss et al. 2022)

*(Attributed — Alex Bayliss et al., Ch.19 “A northern timescape,” in [[hodder-catalhoyuk-excavations-2009-2017|Hodder ed. 2022]]; calibrated against **IntCal20**; preferred model defined by CQL2 code **Catal_North.oxcal**. All posterior intervals below are **as of Bayliss et al. 2022**. Complements the textbook Bayesian/OxCal sketch in Renfrew & Bahn above with a full multi-building settlement application.)*

### Design and priors

Bayliss et al. model **North Area** activity as a multi-sequence Bayesian chronology that fuses AMS measurements with Harris-matrix stratigraphy (uniform phases of continuous use for buildings/open areas; inter-building links added only after stand-alone building models flag misfits). Sampling is deliberately **contiguous and stratigraphically related** (northern North Shelter), not a random site sample — maximising useful priors at the cost of spatial representativeness. Export constraints meant **only the North Area** absolute model was complete at publication; South Area time-slices remained pending (as of Farid/Hodder/Taylor/Tung 2022, Ch.3, citing Ch.19). Preferred model retains **142 of 167** North measurements (85%): 16 anomalously recent bones, 6 experimental assays, 2 bulk charred samples with intrusive components, and 1 inserted burial excluded; ~40 included dates treated as **TPQs** (residual material, uncertain taphonomy, or unidentified charcoal). Overall agreement **Amodel: 73** after excluding OxA-37140 (later Neolithic insertion into the B.129 area) and treating Poz-100529 as residual TPQ.

### Bone-collagen exclusion rules (Poznań Technical Note)

Çatalhöyük bone is often poorly preserved near-surface. From review of **71 Poznań bone dates** (49 Neolithic, 22 later), Bayliss et al. adopt laboratory hygiene rules (attributed, Ch.19 Technical Note):

1. **&lt;0.5% collagen by weight** → regarded as **inaccurate and excluded** (all nine experimental low-collagen North samples returned anomalously recent ages vs related short-lived plants or better bone).
2. **Long alkali + ultrafiltration (UF) + &gt;0.5% collagen** → treated as accurate and included.
3. **No alkali / no UF / only short alkali on Neolithic bone** → **probably inaccurate, excluded** (Poznań replicates without alkali systematically more recent than Oxford ABA+UF replicates; regraphitisation confirmed alkali is required for these samples).
4. **Same incomplete protocols on later (post-Neolithic) bone** → often retained (higher yields).
5. **Statistically consistent replicate from another lab** → may override protocol exclusion.

Stage-1 cleaning omitted **24 measurements (14%)** as scientifically inaccurate and incorporated **39 (23%)** as TPQs. The rules instantiate sample≠event hygiene for **lab chemistry**, not only context: incomplete collagen pre-treatment at Neolithic Çatalhöyük systematically biases ages young.

### Preferred North timescape (summary HPDs)

- **Area span (95% HPD):** earliest excavated North activity **6695–6650 cal BC**; final end **6470–6400 cal BC**; total modelled Neolithic deposits **195–275 years** of use; terminal decline over **1–45 years** (probably 1–20 at 68%).
- **Longest dated strand:** B.132 → Sp.602 → B.77 (fire) → B.131 → B.129. Bayesian order confirmed **B.131 later than B.77** where niche intercutting left the relationship ambiguous (as of Farid et al. 2022 / Bayliss Ch.19).
- **Building durations (95% use):** most buildings few decades (e.g. B.1 **5–50 y**; B.132 **1–30**; B.129 **1–35**); **B.114 40–140 y** the strongest multi-generational (“ancestors’ house”) candidate; of ten buildings with reliable durations, ~2/3 median use ≤~few decades and ~1/3 up to ~century.
- **Revision vs Bayliss et al. 2014 B.1/B.5 model (IntCal20 recalculation comparison):** B.1 start median **~35 years later**, end **~42 years earlier**, duration median **~80 years shorter** — driven by B.131/B.129 links and exclusion of late/no-alkali bones. B.5 remains weakly constrained (partial excavation; public display since 1998; single bulk-cereal TPQ for end).
- **Hiatuses as load-bearing findings:** e.g. Sp.490/B.108 **25–115 years**; B.139/B.131 **25–140 years** (“approaching a century,” beyond living memory of B.139 layout); B.5–B.1 break demonstrated where earlier material-culture phasing had assumed continuity. Authors call **localised deposition gaps** “perhaps the most significant finding.”
- **25-year timescape maps** (6675–6650 through 6450–6425 cal BC slices) replace blanket levels with **probabilities of contemporaneity**; joint accuracy of any full slice is low (product of independent lot probabilities). Archaeological **levels ≠ absolute contemporaneity** — North therefore carries two non-identical dating systems (lettered levels vs Bayesian slices) until South is modelled similarly.

### Methodological takeaways for this page

1. **Bayesian multi-sequence modelling** can resolve Neolithic house biographies to generation-scale intervals when dense short-life samples meet strong stratigraphic priors — and can **fail gracefully** by flagging residual TPQs and laboratory outliers rather than forcing every assay into the sequence.
2. **Cross-lab / pre-treatment replication** is a first-order reliability fact for bone collagen at low-yield sites; incomplete alkali/UF on Neolithic bone is not a minor protocol footnote.
3. **Hard-water / freshwater reservoir** risk was checked via agreement of human bone/tooth (and carbonised flesh/brain) with terrestrial plants/fauna; good individual agreement in modelled buildings is taken to indicate **no detectable non-terrestrial diet offset** in those individuals (as of Bayliss et al. 2022) — case-specific, not a general absolution for human bone.
4. **Levels vs time-slices:** radiocarbon+Bayesian slices answer questions about pace, vacancy, and co-presence that Mellaart-style level stacks and even Harris-matrix phasing alone cannot; Farid et al. 2022 note that Volumes 12–15 material studies still largely used the level system pending South absolute slices.
