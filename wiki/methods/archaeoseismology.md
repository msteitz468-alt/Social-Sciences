# Archaeoseismology

## What It Does

**Archaeoseismology** uses archaeological stratigraphy and architectural deformation to identify past earthquakes and assess their role in site destruction. [[cline-eric-h|Cline]] (2014) relies on this literature for an **earthquake storm** hypothesis (~1225–1175 BCE) across the Aegean and Eastern Mediterranean as one factor in [[late-bronze-age-collapse]].

## Procedure

Field recognition of seismic markers Cline lists (following specialist literature): collapsed/patched/reinforced walls; crushed skeletons under debris; toppled columns lying parallel; slipped keystones; walls leaning or offset. Warfare signatures contrast by weapons in destruction debris (e.g. arrowheads in walls at Aphek, Troy VIIA).

## Assumptions and Limitations

- Markers can be ambiguous; rebuild phases may separate quake damage from final abandonment (Ugarit: Schaeffer quake vs final destruction decades later ~1250+ damage, final end early 12th c.).
- Quakes alone rarely explain multi-polity systems failure — Cline treats them as contributory, not sufficient.
- Absolute dating of damage events depends on ceramic/radiocarbon anchors at each site.

## History

Claude Schaeffer’s earthquake reading at Ugarit is an early famous case; later archaeoseismology refined dates and multi-site “storm” models Cline synthesizes.

## Exemplary Applications

[[ugarit]], [[mycenae]], Tiryns, Midea, [[troy]], [[hattusa]] (debated), and other LBA sites in Cline’s list. See [[late-bronze-age-collapse-causes-debate]], [[cline-1177-bc-study]].

## Debates

Weight of earthquakes in LBA collapse · synchrony of the “storm” · over-attribution of destructions to quakes vs war.
