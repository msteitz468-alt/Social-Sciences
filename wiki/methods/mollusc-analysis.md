# Mollusc Analysis

Mollusc (land- and freshwater-snail) analysis reconstructs local environmental conditions and their human modification from shells preserved in calcareous deposits — soils, hillwash, tufa, and ditch fills. [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]], Technical Box VII, "Mollusca analysis") presents it as the complement to [[pollen-analysis|pollen analysis]] on the chalk and limestone landscapes where pollen does not survive.

## What It Does

Snail species have distinct habitat preferences — shade-loving woodland taxa, open-country/xerophile taxa, marsh and freshwater taxa. Shifts in the assemblage through a soil or sediment profile therefore register changes in local vegetation cover and moisture, and in particular the **woodland clearance** and subsequent land-use of the [[neolithic|Neolithic]] onward.

## Procedure

Sediment samples are wet-sieved; identifiable shells and apices are counted and assigned to ecological groups; the changing proportions of shade-demanding vs open-country species are plotted against depth and calibrated by [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] or archaeological context.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Preservation is restricted to base-rich, calcareous substrates, biasing the method geographically. Assemblages record a very local signal (metres, not regions), and burrowing, downwash, and differential shell survival can mix or distort the record. Some widespread species are ecologically tolerant and carry little information.

## History

British land-snail analysis (A. S. Kennard and later workers) was systematized for archaeology by John G. Evans (*Land Snails in Archaeology*, 1972), making molluscs a standard tool for reconstructing prehistoric landscapes on the southern English chalk.

## Exemplary Applications

Mollusc sequences documented the clearance of primaeval forest and the creation of open grassland on the chalk downlands, providing evidence for early [[deforestation]] and [[soil-erosion]] where pollen is absent — used by Roberts (1998) in the "making of the British Isles landscape."

## Debates

Because the signal is so local, mollusc-based clearance chronologies can conflict with regional pollen records; reconciling local (snail) and regional (pollen) evidence is a standing methodological issue Roberts (1998) flags in attributed voice.
