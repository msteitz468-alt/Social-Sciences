# Ethnography of Communication

## What It Does

The **ethnography of communication** is [[hymes-dell|Hymes]]'s name for studies **ethnographic in basis** and **communicative in the range and kind of patterned complexity** they treat (*[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations in Sociolinguistics]]* Ch. 1). Closely related **ethnography of speaking** focuses the same method on speech; Hymes treats both as an approach within sociolinguistics and equates **sociolinguistic description** with ethnography of speaking (Chs. 2, 4). Program: [[ethnography-of-speaking]].

It answers questions grammar-only description cannot: What counts as a communicative event? Who may speak? In what key? With what genres? When is silence structured? What is the community's communicative economy?

## Procedure

Summary resolution (wiki, not manual), from *Foundations* Chs. 1–2:

1. **Take the community (or network) as frame**, not a pre-given language or code.
2. **Inventory means of communication** (speech varieties, channels, surrogates, silence) and their place in the communicative economy.
3. **Identify speech situations, [[speech-event|speech events]], and speech acts]** as nested units with norms.
4. **Analyze components** with the [[speaking-model|SPEAKING]] heuristic (Setting/Scene, Participants, Ends, Act sequence, Key, Instrumentalities, Norms, Genre) — as relations, not a flat checklist.
5. **State rules** of co-occurrence and alternation (what goes with what; what substitutes for what); formalize where possible for comparison.
6. **Validate emically** (native categories and sentiments) under an etic grid; treat cultural grammar ideals (Conklin, Goodenough) as aspiration.
7. **Compare** across communities with elementary dimensions (quantity of speech, valuation of speaking, elaborated/restricted style dimensions, etc.) without assuming universal ranking of components.

Related methods: [[participant-observation]] (access to events); discourse and [[narrative-analysis]] (texts within events); folklore performance documentation.

## Assumptions and Limitations

**Assumptions (Hymes):**

- Patterns of communicative conduct are real and discoverable.
- Message form and context must be kept together (critique of Bloomfieldian abstraction from context).
- No phenomenon is a priori noncommunicative; the boundary of "communication" is analytic, not given.
- Competence includes appropriateness; the dyadic speaker–hearer model is too thin.

**Limitations:**

- Depth vs scale tradeoff.
- SPEAKING checklist capture.
- Observer access and ethics shape the event sample.
- Large mediated publics strain local [[speech-community]] units.
- *Foundations* itself is programmatic more than a full field manual.

## History

Roots: Sapir, Jakobson, Prague, Firth, Malinowski, Burke; Critical-Turn reaction to autonomous generative linguistics. Parallel developments: Labovian variation; Gumperz interactional sociolinguistics; conversation analysis (Sacks et al.) — Hymes cites these as network, not as identical method.

## Exemplary Applications

From *Foundations* (illustrations / cited studies, attributed):

- Wishram communicative networks; Chinookan speech/silence patterning
- Paliyan structured silence; comparative valuation of speech (Gbeya, Anang, etc.)
- Classroom ethnography (Philips 1972 cited for participant structures)
- Folklore performance and genre as structured events (Abrahams, Ben-Amos, Lomax, Dundes)
- Bilingual-education description from repertoire and event, not language label alone

## Early Methodological Checklist (1972)

*[[gumperz-hymes-directions-sociolinguistics-1972|Directions in Sociolinguistics]]* (1972) carries, in its Appendix 2, Darnell and Sherzer's "Outline Guide for the Ethnographic Study of Speech Use" — an early field checklist of basic comparative-study questions for eliciting a community's ways of speaking, offered as a companion piece to Hymes's chapter 1 componential apparatus. It stands alongside Slobin's 1967 Berkeley field manual on child-language acquisition as one of the method's earliest institutionalized elicitation instruments (attributed).
