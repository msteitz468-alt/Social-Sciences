# Archival Research

## What It Does

Systematic collection and interpretation of preserved documents and records — state papers, organizational files, correspondence, press runs, court records, institutional minutes — to reconstruct past social life and institutional behavior. It is the principal data-collection method of the textual/archival evidence class.

## Procedure

Locate relevant repositories; establish provenance and production context of each record class; read against the grain for what documents assume as well as assert; triangulate across independent record classes; cite so claims are traceable to specific documents.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Assumes records survive in usable and findable form, and that their production context can be reconstructed well enough to correct for purpose and bias. Characteristic failure modes: survivorship bias, elite over-representation (literate record-keepers), and treating institutional self-description as behavior.

## History

Source-critical archival method was professionalized in nineteenth-century historical scholarship and became standard equipment for historical sociology, history of the disciplines, and ethnohistory. Organizational archives (e.g., advocacy-group monitoring files) became major twentieth-century sources for studying prejudice and social movements.

## Exemplary Applications

- [[dinnerstein-antisemitism-in-america-study]] — Dinnerstein's 1994 synthesis of press, organizational (AJC/ADL), governmental, and polling records to survey American [[antisemitism]] from the colonial era to 1992.

## Debates

Archive politics and survivorship are standing reflexivity concerns; see the wiki's evidence-class framework discussions on textual/archival evidence.
