## What It Does

Documentary archaeology treats the archive available to historical archaeologists as
plural: written records, oral traditions, and material culture together, read against
one another rather than in a hierarchy with texts on top. The term was coined by Mary
Beaudry as the title of her edited volume *Documentary Archaeology in the New World*
(1988), which argued that documents and artifacts are independent, complementary lines
of evidence about the same past, neither one simply illustrating the other
([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 2, Wilkie).

## Procedure

Practitioners assemble the available documentary types for a site or population —
Sanborn fire-insurance maps, probate inventories, census returns, oral history and
testimony, and, for enslaved and other marginalized populations, sources such as the
WPA-era slave narratives — alongside excavated material culture, and read the two
archives for points of agreement, silence, and contradiction. Laurie Wilkie's
discussion frames this as moving between named documentary categories and specific
material assemblages (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 2).

## Assumptions and Limitations

Barbara Little distinguished "text-aided" archaeology, in which documents supply
background or answer questions material evidence cannot, from an older, more
verificationist tradition associated with Ivor Noël Hume, in which excavation was used
primarily to confirm or illustrate what documents already said. Little's distinction is
attributed as a critique of the latter tendency, not a settled consensus (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 2).
Martin Hall's work on colonial transcripts argued that documentary records themselves
require the same critical, source-critical reading archaeologists apply to material
evidence, since colonial documentation was produced within relations of power that
shaped what was recorded and how. John Moreland's tripartite critique — organized
around Object, Voice, and Word — argued that historical archaeology has too often let
documents (Word) discipline the interpretation of objects and oral testimony (Voice),
and called for treating all three as co-equal evidence classes. Alison Wylie's concept
of "tacking" between lines of evidence — moving iteratively between documentary,
material, and other evidence without letting one silently trump the others — is cited
as a methodological corrective to both Noël Hume-style verificationism and any converse
privileging of the material record (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 2).

## History

The method's name dates to Beaudry's 1988 volume, but its practice is coextensive with
historical archaeology generally, which by definition addresses periods and places with
documentary records. Its methodological self-consciousness — attention to what
documents can and cannot do, and to the risk of a documentary hierarchy — sharpened
through the critiques by Little, Hall, Moreland, and Wylie summarized above.

## Exemplary Applications

Wilkie's own fieldwork at Oakley Plantation and on the Perryman family site is cited as
an application combining probate inventories, oral history, and excavated material
culture. Anne Yentsch's work at the Calvert site in Annapolis, Barry Higman's work at
Montpelier, William Seifert's urban work, and Grace Karskens's work on colonial Sydney
are cited as further exemplars of reading documentary and material evidence together (
[[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 2).

## Debates

The Object/Voice/Word framing (Moreland) and the tacking concept (Wylie) mark a live
methodological concern within [[historical-archaeology]] about whether documentary,
oral, and material evidence are being weighted as genuinely co-equal, rather than
documents implicitly disciplining the rest — not yet promoted to a dedicated debate
page.

## Discordance as method (Hall 2000)

*(Attributed — [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]], *[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]*, 2000.)* Hall's central methodological move treats systematic disagreement between documentary and material evidence as the primary interpretive signal for colonial societies, not noise to be resolved by "digging wider and deeper": "Rather than being evidence of incomplete research, I see inconsistencies and ambiguities as the key to interpreting the colonial order" (Hall 2000, attributed). He argues each evidence class is filtered by its own "taphonomic filters" — differential processes of loss, deposition, and survival — so that documentary and material records of the same household routinely diverge by object class rather than merely by completeness.

Hall's exemplary case: an excavated well (Ziekedwars Street, Cape Town, four stratigraphic layers) associated with the household of Philip Anhuyzer, a Cape Town retail-store owner (occupancy 1791–1829), compared against Anhuyzer's 1829 probate inventory. The probate document records guns, a silver watch, and a sword — the household as presented by and for its male head. The well instead preserved a hairpin, buttons, a brooch, and two thimbles, along with children's items (a doll, a doll's tea-service), military insignia, and everyday food-preparation refuse — material traces of women, children, servants, common soldiers, and slaves who are effectively absent from the probate record (attributed, Hall et al. 1990b; Hall 2000). Hall reads the divergence as evidence that large/valuable items were curated or disposed of separately from everyday refuse, while the well selectively preserved exactly the categories of presence the documentary record was structured to omit — an application of the wider [[hidden-transcript]] concept to a specific pairing of archival and archaeological evidence.
