# Biosocial Analysis

## What It Does

**Biosocial analysis** is the analytic strategy [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]] names and programmatizes in
*[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999) for studying disease as a
phenomenon that is *simultaneously* biological and social, refusing to assign it to either register
alone. Its charter statement (attributed): "nothing less than a biosocial analysis will do — an analysis
that draws freely on clinical medicine and on social theory, linking molecular epidemiology to history,
ethnography, and political economy." Farmer prefers "biosocial" to "holistic" (which he calls "fuzzy,
New Age") precisely because it keeps the biology — drug resistance, immune response, transmission
dynamics — fully in view rather than dissolving it into culture. Fineberg and Wilson, whom Farmer cites,
called such a synthesis the "Holy Grail" of epidemiology, "easy to demand but harder to produce."

## Procedure

At working resolution, a biosocial analysis (as Farmer practices it):

1. Establishes the **biological facts** rigorously — case-fatality rates, cure rates, susceptibility
   patterns, transmission mechanisms (e.g. that untreated active TB kills the majority and is
   air-transmissible; that MDR-TB arises from intermittent therapy).
2. Maps the **social distribution** of those facts — who is exposed, who progresses, who is treated,
   who dies — using critical epidemiology attentive to the sampling frame (Farmer's "bell jar"
   critique of studies that draw the community boundary so as to hide inequality).
3. **Resocializes** the epidemiology — asking how large-scale forces (poverty, racism, gender
   inequality, political violence, structural adjustment) become **[[embodiment|embodied]]** as the
   observed pattern, using history, ethnography, and political economy.
4. Guards against **[[immodest-claims-of-causality|immodest claims of causality]]** by refusing to let
   any single discipline's favored variable stand as the whole explanation.

## Assumptions and Limitations

The method assumes disease causation is genuinely multi-level and that a single analyst (or team) can
command clinical, epidemiological, ethnographic, and political-economic competence at once — a demanding
and rarely met precondition. Because it crosses disciplines, it fits awkwardly into any one field's
peer review, and its causal weightings ("differentially weighted, synergistic forces") are argued rather
than formally estimated. Farmer concedes the synthesis is "easy to demand but harder to produce." Its
partisan sibling risk is that "biosocial" becomes a license to foreground the social and slight the
biological — the inverse of the reductionism it criticizes.

## History

Farmer names an earlier lineage: Georgina Feldberg's account of turn-of-the-century American physicians
(Allen Krause, Ellen LaMotte) who held a "firmly biosocial" etiology of tuberculosis — microbes *and*
malnutrition, unemployment, crowding — before, on Farmer's telling, "scientific professionalism...
eroded the therapeutic impulse to social reform" and narrowed the frame to the individual patient after
Koch. Biosocial analysis is thus presented less as an invention than as a recovery of the pre-bacteriological
social-medicine tradition (Virchow) with modern molecular and ethnographic tools added.

## Exemplary Applications

- **[[do-kay|Do Kay / Proje Veye Sante]] TB treatment study** — the near-experimental comparison that
  isolated financial/social support (not etiological belief) as the driver of cure.
- **[[hiv-aids|HIV in rural Haiti]]** — the GESCAP case-control study linking infection to partner
  *profession* (soldiers, truck drivers) and to the [[do-kay|Péligre dam]] displacement rather than to
  "risk-group" behavior or "voodoo."
- **[[tuberculosis|MDR-TB in Peru and Haiti]]** — tracing the "political economy of MDR-TB" from drug
  resistance to health-system failure to debt service.

## Debates

Biosocial analysis is the method underwriting Farmer's side of the
[[culture-versus-structure-in-disease-debate]] — the claim that neither pure biology nor pure culture,
but their linkage, explains why the poor get sick and die.
