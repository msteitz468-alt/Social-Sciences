## What It Does

Fluorine dating measures the fluorine that bone absorbs from groundwater, which increases with the time the bone has lain in the ground. Because uptake is gradual, the relative fluorine content of bones **within a single deposit** reveals whether a specimen is truly stratified with its context or is a later intrusion (such as a burial dug down into older layers). The principle was devised by A. Carnot in France (1893) and refined for archaeology by Kenneth Oakley (per Clark 1957).

## Procedure

Bones from one section are assayed for fluorine and compared to one another; a specimen sharing the fluorine level of its stratum is contemporary with it, while an anomalously low value marks an intrusive, younger bone. The test is valid only within a site, because the rate of fluorine accumulation depends on local groundwater chemistry.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Fluorine dating is **relative and local**: it orders bones within one deposit but yields no calendar age and cannot compare bones across sites. It assumes a locally steady uptake rate and undisturbed burial chemistry.

## History

Carnot's 1893 observation lay largely unused until Kenneth Oakley systematized it around 1948–51 (K. P. Oakley, "The fluorine-dating method," *Yearbook of Physical Anthropology* 5, 1951). It became famous as a tool of the physical-anthropology chronology debates of the mid-20th century.

## Exemplary Applications

- Confirmed the **Swanscombe skull** as genuinely of the Great Interglacial, contemporary with the Middle Acheulian hand-axes in its deposit.
- Showed the **Galley Hill skeleton** — with far less fluorine than its claimed stratum — to be a much younger intrusive burial (per Clark 1957). (Fluorine testing was later central to exposing the Piltdown forgery.)

## Debates

Fluorine dating is one of the relative geochronological checks Clark sets alongside stratigraphy and [[typological-method|typology]]; where such tests contradict a specimen's supposed age they resolve disputes about human antiquity that neither typology nor stratigraphy alone could settle. It is complementary to the absolute methods ([[radiocarbon-dating]], [[dendrochronology]]).
