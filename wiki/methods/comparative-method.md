# Comparative Method

## What It Does

Compares social structures, institutions, or processes across societies or historical periods to infer patterns, types, or developmental sequences — and, in Durkheim’s formalization, to **demonstrate causal relationships** among [[social-fact|social facts]].

## Procedure

*Summary resolution — wiki, not manual.*

**Mainean comparative jurisprudence** (*[[maine-ancient-law-1861|Ancient Law]]*, 1861): compare archaic and developed legal systems (especially Roman, with Hindu, Teutonic, Greek materials) to reconstruct the history of family, property, wills, contract, and delict; reject pure Law-of-Nature and original-contract starting points. Reception (Everyman intro) treats Maine as master of the comparative method in legal-institutional history — source of [[patriarchal-theory]] and [[status-to-contract]]. Evolutionary ordering is explicit in Maine’s progressive/stationary frame.

**Durkheimian use** (*[[durkheim-rules-of-sociological-method-1895|Rules]]* Ch. VI): when facts cannot be artificially produced, **indirect experimentation = the comparative method** is the only way to demonstrate that one phenomenon causes another. Among Mill’s canons, **concomitant variations** (covariation) is the chief sociological procedure: prove that in a number of cases two phenomena vary together, then interpret the relationship. Durkheim rejects a pure “plurality of causes” that would make comparison hopeless; he requires **series**, not isolated examples; scopes of comparison include within one society, among societies of the same [[social-types|species]], and across species (genetic method). He rejects Comte’s claim that a special “historical method” must *supplement* comparison as the master proof. See also [[treat-social-facts-as-things]], [[social-realism]].

**Spencerian evolutionary-comparative use** (*Principles of Sociology*, Scope and throughout): before deductive interpretation of general sociological truths, **inductive establishment** by comparing societies of different kinds and stages to ascertain which traits of size, structure, function, etc., are associated. Cases drawn from historiography and from travellers’ and missionaries’ ethnography. Organized under theoretical headings (political headship, slavery, priesthood, etc.) and often ordered by composition ([[social-compounding]]) and activity type ([[militant-type-of-society]] / [[industrial-type-of-society]]).

Spencer warned that comparisons must control for unlikenesses of size and “civilization,” and that type mixtures and conquest re-complications obscure pure forms.

**Morgan–Engels evolutionary-comparative use** (*[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]*): kinship terminologies, gens institutions, and classical/Germanic historical cases are arrayed on a unilinear [[savagery-barbarism-civilization]] ladder to reconstruct extinct family forms (consanguine, [[group-marriage]]) and pathways of [[state-formation]]. Galton’s problem is not controlled; diffusion and shared descent are not methodologically bracketed. Evidence is second-hand ethnography plus literary history — the method’s leverage is hypothesis generation for deep history, not proof.

**Tylorian evolutionary-comparative use** (*[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]*, 1871): the [[culture]]-element (a custom, myth, rite, numeral, or [[survivals|survival]]) is lifted from its setting and arrayed across societies to reconstruct a universal developmental sequence, warranted by the [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|uniformity of the human mind]] (which licenses reading cross-cultural similarity as parallel independent invention). Unit of analysis is the **trait**, not a bounded society; evidence is second-hand traveller/missionary ethnography; no fieldwork. Critically, Tylor's later paper "On a Method of Investigating the Development of Institutions" (1889) is the **origin of cross-cultural statistical correlation** ("adhesions" between institutions) — and the occasion on which Francis Galton raised the objection, now called **Galton's problem**, that historically related cases are not independent, so a correlation may reflect common descent or diffusion rather than functional connection. This is the founding methodological caveat of all comparative work (see Limitations below, and the mandatory `galton_note` on comparison pages). See [[ethnographic-analogy|ethnographic analogy]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

**Frazerian armchair comparative use** (*[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|The Golden Bough]]*, 1890/1922): customs, myths, and priesthoods drawn from travellers, missionaries, classicists, and folklorists are arrayed under theoretical headings ([[sympathetic-magic]], [[taboo]], [[divine-king]], [[dying-and-rising-god]], [[scapegoat]], [[vegetation-spirit]]) to reconstruct a universal history of mind (magic → religion → science) and to solve the Nemi King-of-the-Wood riddle. Unit of analysis is the **rite or trait**, not a bounded society; [[survivals]] license reading European peasant custom as earlier stages; Galton’s problem is not controlled; no fieldwork. Exemplary application: [[frazer-golden-bough-study]]. See [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].

**Lieberson on quasi-experimental comparison** (*[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]*, 1985, ch. 3): sociological research is inherently comparative, but nonexperimental comparisons often silently treat observed contrasts as experimental contrasts. Counterfactual conditionals are unavoidable; **contamination** (communication, anticipation, diffusion across units) can make control-case rates the wrong counterfactual even under ideal random assignment of units to "treatment" (school-desegregation / "white flight" scenarios as design illustrations). Allied failures he lists: assumed [[asymmetrical-causation|symmetrical causality]], level-of-analysis nonequivalence, misuse of variance. See [[selectivity]], [[control-variables]], [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]].

**Case-oriented / Boolean formalization** ([[ragin-charles|Ragin]], *[[ragin-comparative-method-1987|The Comparative Method]]*, 1987): Ragin argues (attributed) the classic comparative logic descends from J.S. Mill's **method of agreement** and **indirect method of difference** (Mill 1843), which underpin the "case-oriented" strategy but break down under **multiple and conjunctural causation** — with plural or combined causes, Mill's canons wrongly reject genuine causes (see [[multiple-conjunctural-causation]]). Ragin's [[qualitative-comparative-analysis|Qualitative Comparative Analysis]] formalizes and scales the logic: cases are compared as **configurations** of present/absent conditions via a truth table, and **Boolean minimization** (dropping a condition that makes no difference when all else is held constant) operationalizes the ideal experiment-like comparison combinatorially, across many cases and conditions at once, where Mill's pairwise method cannot. Ragin positions this as a **synthesis** of the case-oriented and variable-oriented strategies (see [[case-oriented-vs-variable-oriented]], [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate]]). *(Note: "Mill" here is John Stuart Mill, not the sociologist [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright Mills]].)*

**KKV on the comparative method's hidden assumption** ([[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994|King, Keohane & Verba]], *Designing Social Inquiry*, 1994): KKV argue (attributed) that comparative case-study research rests necessarily on an assumption of [[unit-homogeneity]] — that units sharing a value of the explanatory variable share an expected outcome — or its weaker "constant effect" variant, an assumption they call untestable and requiring explicit scope statements ("Across what range of units do we expect our assumption… to hold?"). They credit the "structured, focused comparison" of George and McKeown as valuable for generating hypotheses but insist it "does not provide a methodology for causal inference" once the design becomes [[indeterminate-research-design|indeterminate]] (more explanatory variables than observations). They also fault Mill's method-of-agreement designs (e.g. Porter's ten-nation study) for lacking a control group of failure cases, and recommend expanding observations via subunits and time periods rather than collecting new whole cases — advice they call "some of the most useful… for qualitative research." KKV's own framing is a contested position; see [[king-keohane-verba-single-logic-debate]].

## Assumptions and Limitations

| Threat | Note |
|---|---|
| **Galton’s problem** | Related cases treated as independent — not controlled in Spencer; Durkheim requires interpretation of concomitance but does not name Galton |
| **Spurious covariation** | Durkheim: concomitant variation needs interpretation (e.g. suicide and education may share a cause) |
| **Source quality** | Second-hand travellers/missionaries; Victorian categories (“savage,” “barbarian”) |
| **Scheme-driven ordering** | Evolutionary frame can dictate arrangement of evidence |
| **Selection bias** | Cases that fit the heading are foregrounded |
| **No fieldwork** | Not participant observation; not modern HRAF sampling |

## History

**Lubbock (*Pre-Historic Times*, 1865/1872).** *(Attributed.)* Comparative method as **ethnographic analogy for prehistory**: travellers' reports on peoples without metal illuminate stone-age archaeology; living groups ranked on a scale of savagery; independent invention of simple arts argued from isolation. Evidence class: second-hand ethnography + archaeological synthesis. See [[lubbock-prehistoric-times-1872]], [[lubbock-john]].



Long comparative tradition (Montesquieu and others); Comtean historical filiation ([[positive-method]]); Spencerian world-wide institutional comparison; **Durkheim 1895** elevates comparison to sociology’s proof procedure (concomitant variations) and, in subsequent debates (Seignobos 1908), defends comparison of institutions against pure documentary-event scepticism; later Boasian and [[historical-particularism|historical-particularist]] reactions against unilinear evolutionism (*[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|Mind of Primitive Man]]*); modern statistical cross-cultural methods rework the problem under explicit sampling rules. applies: [[spencer-principles-of-sociology-andreski-1969]] · [[durkheim-rules-of-sociological-method-1895]]

## Exemplary Applications

- **Mauss, *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* (1925):** careful comparison confined to Polynesia, Melanesia, Northwest America, and selected Indo-European codes; deliberately abstains from diffusion history while building a morphology of [[gift-exchange]] / [[total-prestation]] / [[potlatch]]; secondary ethnographic and legal synthesis (no fieldwork by Mauss). See [[mauss-the-gift-study]].

- [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]], *Rules* Ch. VI — concomitant variations as demonstration of proof; *Suicide* as applied comparison of rates.
- [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], *Principles of Sociology* — evolutionary-comparative institutional analysis (attributed program).
- [[engels-friedrich|Engels]] / [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]], *[[engels-origin-of-the-family-1884|Origin of the Family]]* — kinship stages, gens, state origin (attributed program).
- [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]], *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-study|Systems of Consanguinity]]* (1871) — global [[kinship-schedule-method|schedule]] comparison of relationship systems; descriptive vs classificatory typology (attributed program).
- [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]], *[[frazer-golden-bough-study|The Golden Bough]]* (1890/1922) — global catalogue of magic, kingship, and vegetation ritual under intellectualist headings (attributed program; method emblem of armchair anthropology).
- [[benedict-ruth|Benedict]], *[[benedict-patterns-of-culture-1934|Patterns of Culture]]* (1934) — a **holistic, interpretive** comparison ([[zuni]], [[dobu]], [[kwakiutl]]) that deliberately rejects trait-by-trait comparison for the comparison of whole [[cultural-configuration|configurations]]; the polar opposite of Frazerian element-cataloguing, and a case where "the significant sociological unit is not the institution but the cultural configuration" ([[configurationism]]).
- Comtean historical series (related but different master key) — [[comte-positive-philosophy-martineau-1853]].
- Glaser & Strauss, *[[glaser-strauss-discovery-grounded-theory-1967|Discovery of Grounded Theory]]* (1967) — comparative analysis as **general method for generating** [[grounded-theory]] (units of any size); not limited to large-scale cross-national comparison; used jointly with [[theoretical-sampling]] and the [[constant-comparative-method]] (Chs II–VI).
- [[skocpol-theda|Skocpol]], *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|States and Social Revolutions]]* (1979) — the classic modern **small-N comparative-historical** design: J. S. Mill's **Method of Agreement** (France, Russia, China as positive cases sharing a causal pattern) combined with the **Method of Difference** (England, Japan, Prussia/Germany, 1905 Russia as negative cases). Skocpol defends small-N causal comparison against the large-N/statistical demand (Eckstein) and distinguishes it from the descriptive "natural history" of revolution (Brinton). She candidly notes the method's two limits — the scarcity of well-controlled cases and Galton's problem (case non-independence). See [[france-russia-china-revolutions]]. (Ragin cites Skocpol as an exemplar of the small-N case-oriented design but disputes her claim, via Tilly's "universalizing" label, that longitudinal comparison supplies a true Mill method of difference — 1917 Russia had already lived through 1905.)
- [[ragin-charles|Ragin]], *[[ragin-comparative-method-1987|The Comparative Method]]* (1987) — reworks the whole tradition into [[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]], a Boolean, set-theoretic comparison of cases as configurations that handles [[multiple-conjunctural-causation]] where Mill's canons and small-N comparison fail; worked applications to Rokkan's nation-building data, ethnic mobilization in Western Europe, and U.S. juvenile-court typologies.


- [[boas-franz|Boas]], *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]* (1911/1938) — **critique** of evolutionary comparison: trait similarity ≠ parallel development; control diffusion and incomparability; [[historical-particularism]] (attributed program).
## Geertz: interpretive comparison of incommensurables (1983)

In "Local Knowledge: Fact and Law" (*[[geertz-local-knowledge-1983|Local Knowledge]]*, 1983),
[[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] gives the comparative method an explicitly **interpretive, non-nomothetic**
form (attributed). Comparative law, he argues, should be **"cultural translation"** — "to
formulate the presuppositions... characteristic of one sort of legal sensibility in terms of
those characteristic of another" — not "an exercise in institutional taxonomy" or "a search for
*quod semper aequum et bonum est*" (universals). It compares whole sensibilities
(*[[local-knowledge|haqq/dharma/adat]]*) rather than isolating traits, and rejects
convergence/modernization narratives ("the legal universe is not collapsing to a ball but
expanding to a manifold"). Against Santayana's dictum that "one compares only when one is unable
to get to the heart of the matter," Geertz inverts the premise: "it is through comparison, and
of incomparables, that whatever heart we can actually get to is reached." Borrowing Rorty's
normal/abnormal discourse (after Kuhn), he casts comparison across unshared frameworks as a
hermeneutic, not a taxonomic, task — allied to literary criticism and history of science rather
than "more 'scientific' enterprises." This is the interpretive-anthropology counter-pole to the
Durkheimian and Millian "indirect experiment" conceptions above.

## Exemplary application — Lamont 1992

[[lamont-money-morals-manners-study|Lamont, *Money, Morals, and Manners*]] uses the comparative method so that “the shock of otherness” makes valued cultural traits salient: four-site design pairing Indianapolis with Clermont-Ferrand and New York suburbs with Paris banlieue, occupational matching across national systems rather than abstract class labels. Comparison is of symbolic-boundary patterns, not career structures under identical work conditions (attributed).

## Exemplary application — Rivers 1914 (and Galton's problem inverted)

[[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]]' *[[rivers-melanesian-society-1914|History of Melanesian Society]]* (1914)
applied a morphological variant of the comparative method to Oceanic kinship: comparing systems
of relationship by structure and by linguistic distribution across two dozen societies (see
[[oceanic-relationship-systems]]). It is a rare case in which **Galton's problem is the object,
not the obstacle** — Rivers read the non-independence of cases (resemblances tracking social
structure and diffusion rather than geography) as the very evidence for historical connection,
which made the reconstruction powerful but unfalsifiable in the manner of conjectural history
(see [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]).

### Radcliffe-Brown: systems, not isolated customs (1922)

In *[[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-1922|The Andaman Islanders]]* Ch. V, [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] argued (attributed) that the "true comparative method" compares whole **social systems or types**, not isolated customs of different societies — valid trait comparison requires knowing each custom's meaning in its own system first. This is a founding British structural-functional reformulation of anthropology's comparative tradition against Victorian trait-listing evolutionism. See [[radcliffe-brown-andaman-islanders-study]], [[structural-functionalism]], [[social-structure]].

His essays in *[[radcliffe-brown-structure-function-1952|Structure and Function]]* (1952) sharpen the epistemology (attributed). He distinguished **idiographic** enquiry (history, ethnography — establishing particular facts) from **nomothetic** enquiry (theoretical/comparative sociology — reaching general propositions), and placed social anthropology in the second as "the theoretical natural science of human society." Single-society study "may provide materials for comparative study... [but] cannot give demonstrated results"; only systematic comparison of many societies can test hypotheses, toward a "comparative morphology" classifying types of social structure. He separated an institution's **historical origin** (a unique causal sequence, often unknowable for non-literate societies) from its **sociological origin** (the general condition of social existence it satisfies, discoverable by comparison), and defined the program *against* conjectural history: "My objection to conjectural history is not that it is historical, but that it is conjectural."

## Vansina: comparison of oral testimonies (1961)

[[vansina-jan|Vansina]]'s *[[vansina-oral-tradition-1965|Oral Tradition]]* (1961/1965) applies the comparative method to **oral testimonies** as the second step of source evaluation (after assessing an initial testimony's origin). Comparison establishes **dependence** (borrowing or common derivation) versus **independence**; for **fixed texts** he adapts the philological *stemma codicum* (worked out at length for Kuba clan slogans), noting that oral stemmata "can be more complicated than for written texts." For **free texts** he defines an original ten-term typology of relatedness — Identical, Parallel, Variant, Divergent, Hybrid, Combined (related) and Similar, Aberrant, Contaminated, Alien (independent) — and insists that textual comparison be combined with comparison of the **circumstances of transmission** (informant, locality, type) into a "bundle of probabilities." His strongest claim ties to this method's evidential ceiling: complete concordance between genuinely **independent** sources of different classes raises confidence "almost to certainty" (an anticipation of the independent-convergence standard). Galton's problem recurs in oral form — co-located chains of transmission and fast-diffusing *Wandersagen* mean apparent agreement may reflect borrowing, not independent witness. See [[oral-tradition]], [[oral-testimony]].

## Stocking (1968): the 1800 origin and the stadial genealogy

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]], *[[stocking-race-culture-evolution-1968|Race,
Culture, and Evolution]]*, essay 2, "French Anthropology in 1800.")* Stocking locates a clear
early statement of the ethnological comparative method in Joseph-Marie de Gérando's 1800
memoir *Considerations on the Methods to Follow in the Observation of Savage Peoples*,
written (with a companion race-anatomical memoir by Georges Cuvier) to guide the Baudin
expedition to Australia for the Société des Observateurs de l'Homme. De Gérando assumed a
single, unilinear human development toward western European "civilization," so that the
observer of contemporary "savages" was in effect studying the researcher's own past — "the
*voyageur-philosophe*... travels in the past; each step he makes is a century over which he
leaps." Stocking traces this stadial premise (following Frederick J. Teggart) to
late-eighteenth-century social thought — the Ancients/Moderns quarrel and the Scottish
Enlightenment — transmitted to the nineteenth century partly through Comte, and argues the
Victorian evolutionists (Lubbock, Tylor) inherited the method and framework while purging its
Enlightenment sympathy for the "noble savage" and increasingly explaining savage inferiority
in racial terms. This pre-dates and complements the Tylor/Galton "adhesions" episode below:
the comparative method's evolutionary logic was in place a lifetime before Tylor gave it
statistical form.

## Stocking (1987): tracing up vs. tracing back

Stocking ([[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|*Victorian Anthropology*]], 1987) draws a foundational methodological distinction (attributed) between the classical evolutionists' comparative method and the diffusionist/philological alternative: evolutionism's method traces **UP** — reasoning from the present into the past by treating contemporary "savage" societies as living representatives of extinct developmental stages (the "paleolithic equation," e.g. Tylor's eventual settling on the Tasmanians as proxies for the early Stone Age) — whereas the diffusionist or comparative-philological method traces **BACK**, connecting cases through documented or reconstructible actual historical relationship. "The diffusionist traces back, not up — back in time through one historical connection after another" (Stocking 1987).

Stocking argues the evolutionists' method lacked the controls available to comparative philology or comparative anatomy — disciplines in which documentary evidence or dissection could establish that compared items were actually, historically related. Sociocultural evolutionary comparison made no equivalent attempt: the historical relationship between compared customs was simply set aside, substituted for by the assumption of psychic unity (see [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]) licensing independent parallel invention. Maine voiced the resulting objection early and sharply (attributed): he warned of the danger of taking "one usage from Australia and another from Tibet to construct a theoretical series of institutions, one growing out of the other" without any guarantee the cases were even historically comparable.

The most consequential episode in this history is Tylor's 1888 paper "On a Method of Investigating the Development of Institutions" (Anthropological Institute), already noted above as probably the single most historically cited paper of British classical evolutionism: its statistical "adhesions" method for testing correlations of custom across some 350 societies prompted [[galton-francis|Francis Galton]], then the Institute's president, to raise what became **Galton's Problem** in the discussion following the paper — whether the tribes and races compared were in fact historically independent, or "duplicate copies" related by diffusion or common descent, in which case treating them as independent statistical cases is invalid. Stocking credits Galton's critique with directly provoking [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s turn toward diffusion studies and his general methodological critique, "The Limitations of the Comparative Method of Anthropology," with Edward Westermarck and Fritz Graebner following similarly skeptical lines — so Tylor's own paper became the occasion for the antievolutionary reaction against the comparative method it showcased.

Stocking also notes (attributed) the method's persistence well past classical evolutionism's institutional decline: thirty years of Kalahari Bushman studies (see [[kalahari-debate]]) have, with appropriate antiracialist caveats, supplied evidence about early hunter-gatherer existence "in a way that Tylor would surely have appreciated" — the underlying comparative-ethnographic-analogy logic outlived the racial hierarchy it was first embedded in.

## Stocking (1995), *After Tylor*: the 1888 "adhesions" paper in full, and Galton's problem's origin

*(Attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995]], supplementing the 1987 account above with the fuller record of the paper and its discussion.)* [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s December 1888 paper tabulated the "adhesion" (statistical co-occurrence) of customs — avoidance, teknonymy (a term Tylor coined here), residence rules, the levirate, the couvade — across some 350 peoples, treating co-occurrence at rates exceeding chance ("eleven instances… which should have happened 'between once and twice'") as evidence of "common causation." Stocking reads Tylor as conflating statistical "cause" with historical "origin" and as imputing rational motive to historical actors, leaning throughout on the doctrine of [[survivals]] as the argument's "linch-pin."

At the discussion following the paper, **Francis Galton** objected that Tylor's method did not establish "the degree in which the customs of the tribes and races which are compared together are independent" — the founding statement of what became known as **Galton's problem**: non-independence of cases due to shared historical origin (diffusion) undermines treating them as statistically separate instances. **W. H. Flower** raised a parallel objection about "units of comparison being of equivalent value." Tylor's own response dismissed the objection: he held that "correspondences brought about by historical connexion tend to set off against one another, leaving the results of general human action more or less clear" — i.e., that diffusion-driven resemblance would wash out as statistical noise rather than bias the result.

Stocking frames Galton's objection as foreshadowing the major evolutionism-versus-diffusionism paradigm shift traced on [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]: the 1860s transition from diffusionary "ethnology" to evolutionary "anthropology," and the reverse reaction — a reassertion of diffusionary "ethnological" method — in the early twentieth century, first in the United States via [[boas-franz|Boas]] and then in Germany and England. [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s 1896 essay "The Limitations of the Comparative Method of Anthropology" is presented as a direct transformation of Galton's objection into a general methodological critique: comparison is only legitimate, Boas argued, when preceded by historical study, since superficially similar phenomena may result from entirely different historical processes. Boas received Tylor's 1888 paper within weeks of its delivery, while serving as field ethnographer (from 1888) on the British Association's Northwest Coast committee that Tylor chaired.

## African Political Systems application (1940)

[[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]]'s preface to *[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* restates the comparative method as natural-science classification of political types before laws; comparison by aspect (political organization alone) because societies may resemble in one aspect and differ in another. Fortes & Evans-Pritchard strip cultural idiom to functional terms for structural comparison of Group A/B systems. Galton non-independence is not controlled — theoretical sample, not statistical HRAF design. See [[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-study]].


### Trigger — controlled early-civilization comparison (2003, attributed)

[[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] (*[[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-2003|UEC]]*) applies a **small-N contextualized comparison** (seven cases) against both trait-list HRAF-style screens and extreme particularism that denies comparison. Galton’s problem is handled by selecting predominantly independent early civilizations; shared traits → panhuman patterns or functional coevolution; differences → freer cultural tradition. Thick description of institutions before cross-tabulation; past-tense description of all cases. Exemplar study: [[trigger-understanding-early-civilizations-study]]. Training lineage: Murdock, Ford, Naroll (preface).

### Yoffee — comparison vs analogy; developmental sequences (2005, attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths]]* Ch. 8) revives comparison as **world-historical / social-evolutionary** method while restricting [[ethnographic-analogy|ethnographic analogy]]. Comparison of **developmental sequences** (what changed and what did not) is legitimate; treating living societies as stage fossils is not. Adjudicate rival models by **parsimony and coverage** of published data; high-level theory must scale into contextually appropriate middle-level explanations (his BLT/MLT/HLT stack). See [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

### Diamond GGS comparative (1997, attributed)

[[diamond-jared-m|Diamond]] (*[[diamond-guns-germs-steel-1997|GGS]]*) treats world history as a **natural experiment** across continents and uses Polynesia (Ch.2) as a smaller-scale radiation of societies under varying environments. Explicitly models method on historical sciences (evolutionary biology, geology). Galton/non-independence and cultural diffusion are handled mainly by continental isolation arguments — a methodological choice critics challenge. See [[guns-germs-steel-geographic-determinism-debate]], [[ultimate-and-proximate-causes]].

### Carneiro 2003 — evolutionary comparative method (attributed)

[[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]] (*[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*) reconstructs classical evolutionists’ comparative method as resting on explicit principles (possibility of social science; uniformity of nature; continuity; simplicity→complexity; psychic unity; differential evolution; survivals; etc.) and defends comparison as the inductive base for cultural **laws**. As of ~2003 he judged: Boasian attack nearly killed evolutionary comparison; U.S. revival via Murdock/HRAF/SCCS mostly non-evolutionary; Galton’s problem and sampling remain live constraints; scale analysis (Guttman scalograms of trait order) and selected cross-cultural tests show diachronic inference is possible. Partisan recovery, not a methods textbook. See [[social-evolutionism]], [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]], [[survivals]].

## Leach 1959/61 — against “butterfly collecting”

*(Attributed — [[leach-edmund|Leach]], *[[leach-rethinking-anthropology-1961|Rethinking Anthropology]]* Ch. 1.)* British social anthropology’s comparative aim (Malinowski + Radcliffe-Brown functionalist analysis of social structures) seemed exhausted; colleagues retreated into particularist historical ethnography. Leach keeps the ambition of **general** implications but rejects R-B **comparison of structural types** (sea-shell typology): that is [[butterfly-collecting]] — classification into types/subtypes, often tautological once the type is presupposed (his reading of Richards on matriliny). Prefer **generalization**: treat organizational ideas as a mathematical pattern (topology / degree of connectedness; ratios among neighbouring relationships). Positive models: Lévi-Strauss-style structural generalization and his own [[ethnographic-algebra]]. Not a ban on all controlled comparison — a ban on natural-kind typology of societies. Galton non-independence is not formalized; the method is pattern-inference from reanalyzed ethnography.

## Oceanic phylogenetic comparison (Kirch 2017)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-road-of-the-winds-2017|Kirch 2017]].)* Kirch grounds Oceanic comparison in the same Galton's-problem logic as classical comparative method but **inverts** its usual handling: rather than treating shared descent as a contamination problem to control away, he uses documented shared descent — reconstructed Proto-Oceanic society → [[lapita-cultural-complex|Lapita]] → the dispersed Remote Oceanic societies — as a controlled analytic **constant**, against which varying island environments become the manipulated variable, approximating Ernst Mayr's "natural experiments" of comparative biology. He traces a genealogy of this phylogenetic comparative program: [[rivers-w-h-r|Rivers]] (1914, genealogical method applied to Melanesian social organization) → Otto Dempwolff (1934–38, comparative Austronesian linguistics) → Romney's (1957) "segments of cultural history" → Friedlaender's Solomon Islands Project (biological/cultural co-variation) → Kirch's own "phyletic units" as the operational category for [[cultural-phylogeny|cultural-phylogenetic]] comparison. See [[oceania-as-controlled-comparison]], [[rivers-w-h-r]].

## Bellwood *First Farmers* densification (2005) — historical-linguistic comparative method

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005, esp. language-family chapters.)*

Bellwood’s prehistory program depends on the **comparative-historical method of linguistics** (Kaufman 1990a: identify related groups, reconstruct ancestors, trace member-language development) as an independent evidence class alongside archaeology and genetics:

- **Family vs subgroup:** Families share unique identifying features mainly as *retentions* from an earlier stage; **subgroups** are defined by uniquely shared *innovations* (not mere retentions) — e.g. Blust’s Proto-Malayo-Polynesian innovations absent in Formosan. Caveats: early pan-subgroup borrowings can mimic cognates; shared retentions can masquerade as innovations if distant cognates are overlooked (sample density matters).
- **Proto-lexicons for prehistorians:** Reconstructible ancestral lexical sets (agriculture, seafaring, pottery, etc.) are the main comparative payoff — e.g. Proto-Oceanic agricultural/seafaring package; Chamorro rice vocabulary from Proto-Malayo-Polynesian rather than later borrowing (Blust).
- **Time depth:** Linguistic analysis does not yield absolute time easily (Renfrew et al. 2000); island single-occupation cases give rule-of-thumb rates but no universal clock. Glottochronology (Swadesh uniform rate) is useful mainly 500–2,500 years and is falsified as a constant by unequal retention (Blust 2000b; word taboo; founder effects). Bellwood still holds that *en masse* glottochronological ages for agriculturalist proto-languages tend to correlate with archaeological beginnings of agriculture in likely homelands.
- **Holocene ceiling argument:** If a family took >~10,000 years to reach its geographic extremes, shared basic cognates would fall below recognition thresholds — so major agriculturalist families as identifiable today are Holocene phenomena for Bellwood.
- **Macrofamilies:** Same comparative principles at deeper levels, but multi-family genetic groups are very hard; acrimony at that level supports recent punctuational radiations more than even deep evolution (Bellwood’s lean).

Galton/non-independence is inverted as in Kirch: shared descent is the object of reconstruction. See [[cultural-phylogeny]], [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]], [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## Strathern *Partial Connections* (1991) — postplural comparison (attributed)

[[strathern-marilyn|Strathern]] (*[[strathern-partial-connections-1991|Partial Connections]]*; study [[strathern-partial-connections-study]]) reopens discursive comparison after [[writing-culture]] without restoring societies as containers of countable traits. *(Attributed.)*

- **Scale problem:** scale-switching multiplies detail and loses information so “amount” of information stays roughly constant; complexity **replicates** at every level; distant comparative questions (e.g. presence/absence of initiation) fragment into non-unitary practices on closer inspection.
- **Postplural perception:** every approach partial yet connected — taxonomy and regional-map images fail as totalizers; need [[partial-connections]] (relations through partition/remainder).
- **Units:** even sophisticated Galton/network autocorrelation solutions still locate attributes “in” societies and end up counting societies; the unit of comparison is itself the problem.
- **History does not dissolve analogy:** completeness of trade/transmission would not eliminate the need for partial fit; complexity is not rankable as evolutionary social elaboration.
- **Images:** Haraway cyborg (compatibility without common origin); Cantor’s dust / self-scaling form; Melanesian trees, flutes, figure–ground.

Distinct from Tsing’s [[scale-making]] (who claims global/local) and DOC [[scale-shift]] (contention expanding sites) — Strathern’s scale is **descriptive proportion in anthropological knowledge practice**. Galton note: she does not solve statistical non-independence; she refuses the counting frame that makes Galton the master problem.
