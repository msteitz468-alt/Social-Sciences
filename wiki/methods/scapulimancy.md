# Scapulimancy

## What It Does

**Scapulimancy** is divination by heating animal shoulder blades (and, in Shang practice, turtle plastrons — plastromancy) so that cracks can be read as answers to posed questions. In Chinese archaeology it is both a **Neolithic ritual marker** (uninscribed oracle bones in Longshan and early Shang contexts) and, with writing, the substrate of [[oracle-bone-inscription|oracle-bone inscription]].

## Procedure

A bone or shell is prepared (cleaned, thinned, often with hollows for controlled cracking), heated until it cracks, and the cracks interpreted. In the Shang court the charge and outcome were often inscribed on the same piece.

## Assumptions and Limitations

Uninscribed cracked bones show that divinatory heat-cracking was practiced; they do not carry propositional content. Inscribed Shang bones are royal instruments, not a census of popular belief. Chang (1980) links Shang scapulimancy to earlier North China practice while treating the inscribed corpus as a historically specific political technology.

## History

Chang (*[[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Shang Civilization]]*, 1980) places scapulimancy among the Longshanoid features that prefigure Shang civilization and among the Five Doors of Shang evidence. Scientific recovery of inscribed pieces begins with the 1928–37 [[anyang|Anyang]] excavations under [[li-chi|Li Chi]].

## Exemplary Applications

- Horizon marker for late Neolithic / early Bronze Age North China ritual
- Operational basis of the Shang [[oracle-bone-inscription|oracle-bone]] archive
- Comparative religion/state-formation arguments about elite monopoly of supernatural communication

## Debates

Whether Shang writing "emerged suddenly" with scapulimancy or developed from longer graphic practice; relationship between uninscribed early Shang bones (e.g. Cheng-chou) and the fully inscribed Anyang system.
