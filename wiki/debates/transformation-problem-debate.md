# The Transformation Problem

The longest-running technical dispute in Marxian economics: whether [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s transformation of commodity values into [[price-of-production|prices of production]] (*[[marx-capital-volume-3-1894|Capital, Volume 3]]*, Ch. 9) is coherent — specifically whether his two aggregate equalities (total prices of production = total values; total profit = total surplus-value) survive transforming the *inputs* as well as the outputs. Marx himself flagged the issue: input elements "are generally bought on the market … so that the profit in one branch of industry goes into the cost price of another," answered with an aggregation argument. This page is built from Engels's 1894 preface and Ernest Mandel's introduction to the Penguin edition (1981) — a partisan participant's survey; positions recorded as reported there, pending primary ingests.

## Prehistory: Engels's prize-essay challenge (1885–1894)

In the Vol. 2 preface Engels challenged economists to show how an average rate of profit can form "without violating the law of value." His 1894 preface grades the entries (each a position, in Engels's framing): **Lexis** (value law holds only in aggregate — "a Marxist disguised as a vulgar economist" per Engels); **Conrad Schmidt** (1889 — "extremely ingenious, quite on Hegelian lines" but smuggles in dead labour as value-creating; a second competition-based attempt in 1892–93); **Fireman** (1892 — "put his finger on the decisive point": above/below-value sales cancel in aggregate); **Julius Wolf** (dismissed as confused); **Loria** (an extended, unusually vitriolic dismissal — Engels's own polemical register, not neutral historiography); **Stiebeling** (begs the question algebraically). Engels's verdict: "it is only the Marxian school that has achieved anything."

## The feedback controversy (Bortkiewicz onward)

**Bortkiewicz** (1907; per Mandel's account) objected that Marx transforms outputs while inputs remain in values — a logical inconsistency demanding simultaneous determination. His correction was accepted by **Sweezy**, **Sraffa**, **Seton** and others; **Winternitz** (1948) and **Seton** (1957) offered variant solutions; **von Weizsäcker and Samuelson** (1971) pressed the critique from neoclassical ground; **Steedman** (*Marx after Sraffa*, 1977) concluded value categories are redundant for price-and-profit theory.

Defenses recorded by Mandel: his own **temporal/sequential** reading (inputs are data from the previous period's equalization, already prices of production — no simultaneous system needed, and empirically truer to the annual rigidity of production prices); **Shaikh**'s iterative method; **Salama**'s critique that applying Bortkiewicz's own method consistently eliminates value and exploitation altogether (proving too much); **Farjoun**'s objection that the Bortkiewicz-type solutions illegitimately exclude a Department III (e.g. armaments) from profit-rate formation. Mandel also charges **Sweezy** and **Steedman** with a "monetary confusion" — conflating prices of production (value quantities) with money market prices. Mandel's own conclusion: neither side has "definitively" resolved the problem.

## Adjacent front: the falling rate of profit

The **Okishio theorem** (1961/1963) — profit-rate-raising technique choice cannot lower the equilibrium rate — is the formal challenge to [[tendential-fall-in-the-rate-of-profit]]; Mandel's rebuttals (forced mechanization; fixed capital bracketed out; mass vs rate) and the Shaikh/Perlo empirical dispute over the capital/output ratio are positions on the same value-theoretic terrain.
