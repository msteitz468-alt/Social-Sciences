# The Hegemonic Masculinity Debate

The dispute over the most influential concept in the sociology of men and masculinities — [[hegemonic-masculinity]], formalized in [[connell-raewyn|Connell]]'s *[[connell-masculinities-1995|Masculinities]]* (1995). By the 2000s the concept had been challenged "from several directions," and Connell records that "whether to discard the concept … reconstruct it, or reaffirm it, is still sharply debated." Positions are recorded here in their strongest form; the wiki does not adjudicate.

## Positions

### 1. Reaffirm (Connell)

The concept remains indispensable. "We still require a way of theorizing gendered power relations among men, and understanding the effectiveness of masculinities in the legitimation of the gender order … the concept of hegemonic masculinity, as developed in this book, is still essential." On this view the common misreadings — treating it as a fixed character type or a checklist of traits — are errors the original definition already guards against (it names a *relational, contestable position*, not a personality).

### 2. Reconstruct (Connell & Messerschmidt 2005)

Connell's own preferred path by the mid-2000s: keep the concept but reformulate it — drop the residual trait-like readings, add geographic levels (local / regional / global), take seriously the agency of subordinated groups and the possibility of more positive hegemonic forms, and integrate a better account of the psychological and embodied dimensions. This is now the standard citation for the "reconstructed" concept.

### 3. Discursive / situational critique (Petersen 1998; Wetherell & Edley 1999)

From discursive psychology and poststructuralism: masculinity is not a stable pattern individuals are "permanently committed to," but "situationally specific choices from a cultural repertoire of masculine behaviour." Connell concedes the insight but argues the approach has "significant limits" — it gives "no grip on issues about economic inequality and the state" (citing Segal 1997) and sits awkwardly with psychoanalytic developmental accounts (Chodorow 1994) — a "theoretical impasse" he hopes a "situational approach … connected with the conscious historicity" of ethnographic studies might resolve.

### 4. Hybridization critique (Demetriou 2001)

Demetriou argues the hegemony/subordination/complicity scheme misses **hybridization**: hegemonic masculinity is not sealed off from subordinated masculinities but continually appropriates elements from them (a "dialectical pragmatism"), so the boundaries in Connell's typology are more porous than the model allows.

### 5. Psychosocial critique (Jefferson 2002)

From a psychosocial-criminology direction: the concept, applied to real men, tends to flatten the psychic complexity and ambivalence of individual biographies into a sociological type, losing the very interior dynamics that explain why a given man takes up or resists a masculine position.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

All parties agree that (a) masculinities are plural and hierarchically related, and (b) some cultural-institutional patterns of masculinity are more authorized than others. The dispute is over the **stability and internal structure** of those patterns: whether "hegemonic masculinity" names a durable structural position (Connell), a situated discursive accomplishment (Wetherell & Edley), or a moving hybrid (Demetriou) — and over how much psychic interiority the sociological concept can carry (Jefferson).

## Resolution status

`partially-resolved`. The concept was not discarded; the field largely converged on the **reconstructed** version (Connell & Messerschmidt 2005) while continuing to argue about hybridity, situatedness, and the psychosocial. The vocabulary is now standard even among critics — the debate refined the concept rather than retiring it.
