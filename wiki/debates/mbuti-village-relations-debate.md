# Mbuti–Village Relations: Autonomy or Dependence?

> Positions recorded, not adjudicated. Grounded in [[turnbull-forest-people-1961|Turnbull, *The Forest People* (1961)]]; the opposing (Schebesta) position is reported through Turnbull's own paraphrase in this source, and the later revival (Grinker) is noted from standard reference. Study: [[turnbull-forest-people-study]]; society: [[mbuti]].

## The Dispute

How should the relationship between the [[mbuti|Mbuti]] (BaMbuti) hunter-gatherers of the Ituri Forest and their settled Bantu and Central Sudanic neighbours be characterised? Every Mbuti individual or family was linked to a village **BaKpara** ("master") patron who supplied cultivated food, metal, and tobacco in exchange for meat and forest labour, and whom the villagers described as *owning* "their" Pygmies. Whether that tie was **serfdom/dependence** or a relationship of **mutual convenience masking Mbuti autonomy** is the dispute.

## Position A — Autonomy (Turnbull)

[[turnbull-colin-m|Turnbull]] argued the bond was "not a matter of slavery, rather of mutual convenience." On his account the villagers had no means of physical coercion: the Mbuti retained complete freedom of movement, could simply fail to return from the forest if hunting was good, and could not be pursued there ("a task no villager would undertake for any amount of pay"). The "hereditary" ownership relationship was, he said, a **myth the villagers created and maintained** precisely because they lacked real control. He marshalled cases: Manyalibo giving chief Kapamba minimal labour; Ausu repudiating his "hereditary master" André and evading a village-tribunal judgment by vanishing into the forest. Turnbull further argued that the villagers' ritual holds over the Mbuti — the **nkumbi** initiation, village weddings, the molimo's village finale — were accepted only *instrumentally* and inwardly rejected (initiates broke every nkumbi taboo by night). He read the whole system as demonstrating "the complete opposition of the forest to the village." Turnbull tied his conclusion to **method**: he had lived with the Mbuti away from villager presence, where they behaved as autonomous forest people (see [[participant-observation]]).

## Position B — Dependence (Schebesta, later Grinker)

The missionary-ethnographer **Paul Schebesta**, whose 1920s–30s survey Turnbull was answering, had presented the Mbuti as bound to Negro families "in a form of serfdom," dependent on villagers for food and metal, with Pygmies handed down "from father to son." Turnbull explicitly rejected this ("None of this was true of the Pygmies that I knew"). But later ethnography revived a version of the dependence view: **Roy Richard Grinker** (*Houses in the Rainforest*, 1994) and other students of Ituri "symbiosis" argued that Turnbull understated how deeply the Mbuti were embedded in — and economically and ritually reliant on — village society, and that the forest/village opposition was less absolute than his narrative implied. On this reading the patron relationship is a real, structured interdependence, not a myth the foragers merely humour.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides accept that: villagers *claimed* ownership of named Mbuti and complained the Pygmies evaded labour obligations; the Mbuti *did* supply meat and take cultivated food, metal, and tobacco in return; and the Mbuti *could* and *did* withdraw into the forest. Turnbull reads these facts as autonomy manipulating a powerless patron; the dependence camp reads the same exchange as evidence of genuine structural reliance. Turnbull's own material complicates a clean binary: he recorded patron relationships ranging from exploitative (chiefs Kachui, Isiaka) to affectionate (the blacksmith Baziani; the villager Kaweki, nursed by the Mbuti and honoured with a molimo song "even if he was a villager"), suggesting the tie varied by individual rather than taking one uniform form.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-09)

*The Forest People* is a popular narrative, and its autonomy thesis is inseparable from its acknowledged romanticism; Turnbull's technical case is in *Wayward Servants* (1965, not ingested).
