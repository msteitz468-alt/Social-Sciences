# Old Europe as Goddess Civilization (Gimbutas Package)

Live interpretive dispute over whether Neolithic–Copper Age Europe is best understood as a **peaceful, Goddess-centered, gynocentric civilization** ([[old-europe|Old Europe]] as formulated by [[gimbutas-marija|Marija Gimbutas]]) that was later transformed by patriarchal steppe intruders, or whether that package over-reads iconography, social structure, and the character of cultural change. Distinct from — but linked to — [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] (homeland and IE dispersal mechanisms) and from ethnographic [[myth-of-matriarchy]] analysis.

**Curator's note (2026-07-10):** This page records positions; it does not adjudicate. Gimbutas is a primary archaeological voice for Position A; popular goddess-movement receptions are reception, not identical to her prose.

## Shared Factual Ground

- A dense Neolithic–Copper Age archaeological record exists across SE/central Europe (tells, painted pottery, figurines, early copper/gold, large settlements including Cucuteni–Tripolye super-sites).
- Female figurines and complex imagery are abundant in many of these assemblages; male images exist but are fewer in many published samples Gimbutas cites.
- Major culture-historical labels (Vinča, Karanovo–Gumelniţa, Cucuteni–Tripolye, LBK, etc.) are etic constructs.
- Steppe–farmer interaction and later steppe-related demographic movements (as of post-2015 aDNA / Reich 2018) are real research problems; equating them with Gimbutas’s full cultural package is not automatic.
- “Civilization,” “matriarchy,” “Goddess,” and “Kurgan people” are theory-laden terms.

## Position A — Gimbutas: Old Europe as Goddess Civilization

**Held by:** [[gimbutas-marija|Marija Gimbutas]] (*[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]*, 1991; prior *Goddesses and Gods of Old Europe*; *Language of the Goddess*). All bullets below are **Gimbutas’s positions as of 1991**, not wiki findings.

### Civilization redefined (Preface)

Gimbutas rejects the assumption that civilization requires hierarchical political/religious organization, warfare, class stratification, and complex division of labor — a pattern she calls typical of **androcratic** (male-dominated) Indo-European societies but not of **gynocentric** (mother/woman-centered) Old European cultures. The generative basis of civilization, she argues, is artistic creation, aesthetic achievement, nonmaterial values, freedom that makes life meaningful for all, and balance of powers between the sexes. Neolithic Europe was not “before civilization” (alluding to Renfrew 1973) but a true civilization: in the 5th–early 4th millennia B.C. east-central Old Europeans had towns with considerable population, multi-story temples, a sacred script, spacious houses, professional craft specialists, and long-distance trade. Old Europe 6500–3500 B.C. and Crete until 1450 B.C. enjoyed “a long period of uninterrupted peaceful living.”

### Peace and non-endemic warfare

She argues warfare is not endemic to the human condition; Paleolithic and Neolithic Old Europe lack depictions of man-against-man weapons and (in her reading of ~150 Çatalhöyük paintings) lack war or torture scenes. Villages were sited for water, soil, and pasture, not defense; hill forts, daggers, spears, and halberds are absent until late horizons she attributes to Kurgan contact (weapons except hunting gear only after ~4500–4300 B.C.).

### Goddess religion (Ch.7)

Earliest religious experience, for Gimbutas, arose from female creative power; the Great Mother Goddess becomes a metaphor for Nature as giver/taker of life. She rejects “Venus” and “fertility charm” labels for Paleolithic female images. Upper Paleolithic iconography (V, triangle, meander, vulva, breasts, animal symbols) continues into the Neolithic. Analytic typology under “one deity… Nature herself”: (1) generative/life-propagating; (2) Death Goddess; (3) Goddess of Regeneration (death and regeneration as one); (4) male deities only ~3–5% of Neolithic sculpture. Parthenogenetic “Virgin Goddess” creates from herself; fatherhood, if understood, left no archaeological importance. Temples, temple models, and figurine caches reenact ritual; continuity claimed from Paleolithic through Old Europe into later Mediterranean goddesses (often demoted or demonized after Indo-Europeanization, in her reading).

### Sacred script (Ch.8)

An Old European **sacred script** (~5300–4300 B.C. climactic use; roots early 6th millennium) on religious objects only — not Sumerian-style administration; ~30 core signs; Vinča–Tisza–Karanovo concentration but pan-OE distribution. Disappearance coincides with Kurgan-related culture disintegration c. 4300–4000 B.C.; Aegean survival claimed (see [[old-european-script]]).

### Social structure (Ch.9)

No patriarchal chieftainate of the IE type; no male royal tombs; no megaron residences on hill forts (her negative inventory). Burial and settlement read as matrilineal; grave wealth as economic egalitarianism (Varna treated as a Black Sea littoral exception under steppe contact). She rejects mirror-image **matriarchy** (women ruling by force) as “far from the reality of Old Europe”; prefers Eisler’s **gylany** and the term **matristic** (incorporating matriliny without “matriarchy”). Historic matriliny survivals and folklore are cited as undercurrents.

### Kurgan collision (Ch.10)

End of Old Europe = clash of two ideologies, social structures, and economies (“marble cake” of non-IE and IE elements; trauma analogy to conquest of the Americas). Proto-/Early Indo-Europeans labeled **Kurgan** arrive from southern Russia on horseback; three infiltration waves (I c. 4400–4300, II c. 3500, III soon after 3000 B.C.) of various steppe peoples sharing a tradition — not a single lineage. OE: peaceful, sedentary, matrifocal, matrilineal, sex-egalitarian; Kurgans: warlike, patriarchal, hierarchical pastoralists. By the third quarter of the 3rd millennium almost all Old Europe transformed; Goddess religion survives as undercurrent. Full wave archaeology on [[kurgan-hypothesis]] and [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]] Position A.

### Method commitments

Interdisciplinary: archaeology + mythology + linguistics + early historic data; calibrated radiocarbon; religion treated as inseparable from social structure; polemical stance against purely materialist “rubbish” archaeology (citing Mellaart’s art/science amalgam plea).

## Position B — Processual / Skeptical Reanalysis

**Held by:** many processual and later archaeologists; figurine specialists; gender archaeologists who reject universal mother-goddess readings; aspects of Renfrew’s critique of culture=people=language migrationism.

- Female images ≠ single pan-European Goddess religion; alternative readings (ancestors, initiation, individual identity, toys, household practice).
- Peace and egalitarianism under-determined; fortifications, trauma, and inequality evidence need case-by-case treatment (e.g. [[varna]] wealth ranking).
- “Matriarchy” as recoverable prehistoric polity is not established; distinguish [[matrifocality]] / [[matrilocal-residence]] from political matriarchy and from [[myth-of-matriarchy]].
- Culture-historical ethnic labeling and invasion waves over-unify heterogeneous regional sequences.

## Position C — Partial Retention

**Held by:** e.g. [[anthony-david-w|David Anthony]] (2007) and related steppe archaeologists.

- Retain **agricultural Old Europe** as a real farmer culture-area and non-IE (in their model) frontier world facing the steppe.
- Retain a **narrowed** steppe expansion story (Yamnaya-centered; elite recruitment / franchise rather than pure invasion mythology).
- **Reject** or set aside the utopian Goddess / matristic civilization package as Goddess-movement casting, not required by the steppe-homeland linguistic-archaeological core.
