# Global City Model Debates

Live disputes around [[global-city-theory]] as formulated by [[sassen-saskia|Saskia Sassen]] in *[[sassen-global-city-2001|The Global City]]* (1991; 2nd ed. 2001). Positions stated in strongest form; no wiki-voice adjudication. Study afterlife: [[sassen-global-city-study]]; theory depth: [[hubs/theory/global-city-theory]].

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Major cities contain dense clusters of finance and business services that grew from the 1970s–90s.
- Manufacturing employment decentralized in many advanced economies in the same period.
- Earnings dispersion rose in many OECD economies from the 1980s (consensus Sassen cites for the 1990s literature).
- Electronic markets expanded without eliminating major financial centers.

Disagreement concerns *what these patterns mean* for urban theory, measurement, polarization, and the state.

## Position 1 — Sassen’s global city model

Global cities are historically specific production sites for the specialized services and financial innovations that enable [[practices-of-global-control|practices of global control]]. Dispersal and centralization are joint. Specialized service complexes, not HQ counts alone, specify advantage. Cities form cross-border networks ([[transnational-urban-system]]); there is no single global city. Growth dynamics press toward high-end professional valuation and low-end/informal expansion without requiring middle-class disappearance or sole globalization causation. The global is *endogenized* in national territories through these urban sites, not only that which crosses borders.

## Position 2 — World-city / hierarchical command tradition

World cities (Hall; Friedmann et al.) name major international centers across long periods or as nodes in a hierarchy of command. Useful for comparative hierarchy; Sassen’s counter is that the term lacks specificity to the contemporary global economy’s production infrastructure.

## Position 3 — Network primacy / space of flows

Readings that treat the global city primarily as a node in electronic or organizational networks (Castells-adjacent space-of-flows). Sassen’s counter: the global city is network *and* place — capital fixity and social connectivity are conditions of hypermobility.

## Position 4 — Measurement skepticism

If finance/producer services are a small employment share, or most jobs are domestically oriented, or HQ counts fall, the city is not “global.” Sassen’s counter: global-city status is about the capacity bundle for servicing global firms and markets, which need not be majority employment; employment is a weak metric for value produced by small numbers of professionals.

## Position 5 — Polarization critique

Inequality may rise without “polarization” or dualization; middle classes persist; London/Tokyo social morphology differs from New York decay narratives. Sassen’s clarification: she does not claim the middle disappears or that globalization is the only cause; she claims growth dynamics do not expand the middle as Fordism did, and that dualizing *pressures* appear across diverse cities even when outcomes differ by state and built environment.

## Position 6 — Missing state and politics

The model underplays the regulatory state and the politics of global finance. **Sassen (2001 Epilogue) agrees** that both remain underdeveloped in the book.

## Resolution status

**Open.** Vocabulary widely adopted; measurement and polarization sub-debates continue; politics/state gap partly conceded by the author. Collection holds only Sassen’s second edition as primary — critics’ monographs largely not yet ingested; positions reconstructed from Sassen’s presentation of the critical literature plus her replies.
