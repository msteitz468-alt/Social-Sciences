# KKV and the Single Logic of Inference

The dispute set off by *[[king-keohane-verba-designing-social-inquiry-1994|Designing Social Inquiry]]* ([[king-gary|King]], [[keohane-robert|Keohane]] & [[verba-sidney|Verba]] 1994) over its central thesis: that **qualitative and quantitative research share one logic of scientific inference**, and that qualitative research should be disciplined by that logic. This is narrower than, and nested within, the broader [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate|qualitative–quantitative divide]] — it concerns specifically KKV's book and the rebuttal literature it provoked. Per the Voice and Attribution Protocol, positions are recorded in their strongest form, not adjudicated.

## Position 1 — KKV's unified logic

KKV argue (attributed) that "the same problems of inference exist in quantitative and qualitative research," and that both are "best understood… to derive from the same underlying logic of inference." On this view [[causal-inference]] rests everywhere on a counterfactual [[causal-effect]] and on assumptions of [[unit-homogeneity]] or [[conditional-independence]]; good qualitative design maximizes [[observable-implications]], avoids [[selection-bias]] (never select on the dependent variable), guards against [[endogeneity]] and [[omitted-variable-bias]], and increases the number of observations. The rules are the same in both traditions; only the technique (formal estimation vs. [[process-tracing]] and disciplined comparison) differs. KKV explicitly reject the label "quasi-experiment" and treat interpretation and thick description as valuable for *generating* hypotheses but not for *evaluating* them.

## Position 2 — Interpretivist rejection of the premise

In the tradition KKV themselves cite and contest (Geertz; Ferejohn; Psathas), some social inquiry seeks meaning — an "interpretive [science] in search of meaning" rather than "an experimental science in search of law" (Geertz 1973) — and may have "no observable consequences" in KKV's sense. On this view the demand that every interpretation cash out as differing observable implications mistakes the object of interpretive work. KKV's counter (attributed): interpretations *are* causal hypotheses (the wink/twitch example is at bottom a claim about the effect of an act on an observer), and two theories with no differing observable implications cannot be distinguished by any method, however deep the immersion.

## Position 3 — Shared standards, plural tools (Brady, Collier, Bennett, George)

The most influential reply, gathered in Brady and Collier's *Rethinking Social Inquiry: Diverse Tools, Shared Standards* (2004), accepts that all rigorous inquiry answers to shared standards but denies KKV's single *template*. Its core claim (attributed): qualitative research generates **causal-process observations** — diagnostic pieces of evidence about mechanism and sequence — whose leverage is different in kind from the **data-set observations** of the regression model, and which KKV's "increase the number of observations" injunction cannot capture. Critics also charge (attributed) that KKV import quantitative-mainstream assumptions while presenting them as neutral, and that mechanically raising n risks conceptual stretching and violates the [[unit-homogeneity]] the inference itself requires.

## Position 4 — Rival accounts of causation

Several interlocutors challenge not the qual/quant bridge but KKV's underlying definition of a [[causal-effect]]:
- **Elster** (1983): counterfactual statements cannot render the meaning of causality where a confounding third factor is present (though they retain analytic value).
- **Little** (1991): causation centrally consists of identifiable **causal mechanisms**, not counterfactual difference-making.
- **Ragin** (1987): statistical methods obscure **multiple causation** / equifinality (see [[case-oriented-vs-variable-oriented]]).
- **Lieberson** (1985): social causation involves **symmetric vs. asymmetric** relationships that the counterfactual account elides.
KKV reply (attributed) that each of these concerns the *difficulty of inference or specification*, not a rival *definition*, and that their counterfactual definition remains logically prior.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

- Most social science is nonexperimental; random assignment is usually unavailable (accepted across positions).
- Within-case evidence and cross-case comparison answer different questions and fail in different ways (KKV call these more observations; critics call them a different logic).
- KKV's specific design rules (do not select on the dependent variable; report uncertainty; specify counterfactuals) are widely taught even by scholars who reject the single-logic thesis.
