# Kroeber vs. Spengler: Do Civilizations Die of Themselves?

A theoretical dispute over the nature of civilizational growth and decline, staged explicitly by [[kroeber-alfred-l|A. L. Kroeber]] in the concluding chapter of *[[kroeber-configurations-culture-growth-1944|Configurations of Culture Growth]]* (1944) against Oswald Spengler's *The Decline of the West* (1918–1922). Kroeber shared enough of Spengler's premises to feel the comparison "obvious" and "necessary to define our respective positions" — which makes the points of divergence unusually sharp. Positions are recorded here in their strongest form; no wiki-voice adjudication.

## Shared Ground

Kroeber credited Spengler with two principles he himself accepted (attributed to Kroeber):

1. **Cultures have fundamental patterns.** Spengler's "contribution to history seems to be his recognition of the importance of the fundamental patterns of cultures," with the quantitative and personalized detail of events relatively insignificant against them. Kroeber's own approach "concurs: not as alone legitimate, but as valid and fruitful."
2. **These patterns occur in limited growths.** Both treat high culture as expressed in bounded florescences rather than steady accumulation, and both treat individuals as "indicators of cultural phenomena" rather than prime movers.

Kroeber even defended Spengler's much-mocked organic metaphors ("spring," "ripening," "senility") as no worse than accepted art-historical terms like "archaic" or "flamboyant": "it is pedantic to take these figures of speech literally… let those who can supply better ones suggest them."

## Position A — Spengler (as Kroeber reports him)

- Each great culture is bound "by some nexus into a grand master pattern"; this nexus is inherent, so "great cultures can never blend or assimilate." They may borrow non-significant material content but rework it wholly into their own master plan.
- "Cultures are therefore as distinct as personalities, or even as organisms of different genus."
- Each culture, "having expressed or exhausted itself, must die, to be superseded by others," passing through fixed parallel stages (Spring/Summer/Fall/Winter; Culture → Civilization).

## Position B — Kroeber

Kroeber accepted the two shared principles but "halted" at three Spenglerian "necessarily's," holding each a legitimate but **wholly unproved** problem:

1. **No proven single master pattern.** That a culture's patterns "can necessarily be reduced to a single master or key pattern" is unproved; the seeming master plan may instead be "the more or less fortuitous aggregation of component material and forms from many sources, gradually working itself into a secondary integration" — the general trend of anthropologists being to allow that accident plays a part. Cultures demonstrably *do* borrow patterns, not just content (Japanese reworking of Chinese forms; the Renaissance reworking of Classical and Medieval ones).
2. **No proven parallel stages.** That cultures "necessarily develop through essentially parallel stages" Kroeber left open, having "tried to proceed by more inductive examination, and… come to much less positive conclusions" — notably his finding that growth curves have **no single normal form** ([[growth-configuration]]).
3. **No proven self-caused death.** On **cultural death**: whole culture cannot be lost (no cultureless human society exists), while *specific* cultures die routinely by extinction, absorption, or displacement. Whether a culture can die "of itself, from internal causes alone" is "probably insoluble at present," because "nature, so far as we know, has not performed the experiment" — other cultures always impinge before isolation could reveal a self-terminating course. Kroeber's own positive account substitutes **pattern exhaustion** (a growth's selective direction reaches its limits) for organic senility, so a pattern can end without the culture dying.

Kroeber also faulted Spengler on **method**: "he is so little a scientist or historian that he does not really present evidence… an intuitive dogmatist perfervid about a completed system," who ignores non-fitting facts and uses telling evidence only "as a springboard for another restatement of the system."

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both agree florescences are bounded and pattern-driven and that individuals index cultural currents. Spengler frames the bounding as the **inevitable life-cycle of a closed organism**; Kroeber frames it as the **contingent exhaustion of a selective pattern** within cultures that remain open to borrowing and blending. Where Spengler reads necessity, Kroeber reads an unproved and probably-untestable empirical question.

## Current Standing

The dispute is *partially resolved* in the sense that mainstream anthropology and history followed Kroeber (and, differently, the critics of Toynbee) in rejecting Spengler's closed-organism determinism and inevitable-death thesis; the anti-essentialist, borrowing-and-blending view of cultures is now standard. Kroeber's positive alternative — describable growth configurations without causal law — was itself criticized as descriptively rich but explanatorily thin, and the broader macro-comparative "rise and fall of civilizations" programme (Spengler, Toynbee, Sorokin, later Quigley) remained marginal to the discipline. See also [[configurationism]] (Benedict's independent, and partly Spengler-indebted, holism) and [[superorganic]].
