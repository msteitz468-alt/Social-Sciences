# Archaeology and Nationalism (use and abuse of the prehistoric past)

A standing dispute over whether — and how — archaeology should serve collective identity, and over its documented history of capture by nationalist and racial ideology. The dispute is **ethical and political**, not a disagreement over shared evidence; it turns on the social function of prehistoric knowledge. It is closely tied to the [[pots-and-peoples-debate]] (the culture = people equation is what makes archaeology usable for ethnic-nationalist claims) and to the [[race-and-culture-debate]].

The debate is documented in strong form within a single source: [[clark-grahame|Grahame Clark]]'s *[[clark-archaeology-and-society-1957|Archaeology and Society]]* (1957) states **both** a benign-integration thesis and the case of archaeology's Nazi-era abuse, making the book itself a useful site for recording the positions.

## Positions

### 1. Archaeology as legitimate social integration (Clark's thesis)

Clark argues (attributed, 1957) that archaeology's chief *social* value is its contribution to "social solidarity and integration" by making a shared past "actual." Human societies, on his account, are constituted less by biological inheritance than by "a consciousness of sharing a common past," and states enlarging their scale have "usually" tried to stimulate historical awareness at the widest level — archaeology "providing just the kind of evidence needed to reinforce the sense of belonging." He extends this to a hoped-for **"world community,"** arguing that only "primary prehistory" — the shared human past before civilizations diverged — can serve as a genuinely universal, non-exclusive basis for solidarity: "All men, whatever the colour of their skin and however recently—or long ago—they emerged from prehistory, can recognize in the archaeological traces of their remote prehistoric forebears symbols both of their common kinship and of the glorious fact of human progress" (attributed, Clark 1957). Clark treats interwar national-independence archaeology (Czech, Polish, Hungarian, Baltic, Finnish, Irish) as largely benign and scientifically productive.

### 2. Archaeology as weaponized ideology (the Kossinna / Nazi case)

Against the benign reading stands the documented capture of German prehistory by Pan-Germanism and Nordicism. Clark himself records it at full fidelity (attributed, 1957): the prehistorian **Gustaf Kossinna** (1858–1931), "an ardent exponent of Pan-Germanism and Nordicism," "quite failed and in fact made no real attempt to study the remote past with scientific detachment," instead using archaeological material to assert German "superiority" — inflating German prehistoric chronology so innovations could be credited to German origin and diffusion read as German domination of "inferior" peoples. Kossinna adopted the historian Sybel's dictum: "a nation which fails to keep in living touch with its past is as near to drying up as a tree with severed roots. We are today, what we were yesterday." Under National Socialism the program was made state doctrine: Heinrich Himmler (quoted via Rauschning, *Hitler Speaks*, 1939) defined prehistory as "the doctrine of the eminence of the Germans at the dawn of civilization," and Prof. Hermann Schneider (Tübingen, 1939) wrote that "the year 1933 witnessed the victory of an attitude towards the history of the culture of Germany" in which "the best of what is German... is Germanic and must be found in purer form in early Germanic times." Clark concedes some Nazi-sponsored fieldwork "was of a high technical standard and yielded valid results" even as its motives "were nefarious."

### 3. Nation-building as constitutive of the discipline

A third position, visible between the lines of Clark's own historical survey, holds that "use" and "abuse" cannot be cleanly separated because national purposes were **built into archaeology's institutions from the start**: the Swedish office of King's Antiquary founded by Gustavus Adolphus; Henry VIII's antiquary John Leland after the dissolution of the monasteries; the Elizabethan antiquaries whose aim was, in Joan Evans's words, "frankly patriotic"; national collections functioning, in Clark's phrase, as "veritable shrines." On this reading the Kossinna case is an extreme point on a continuum, not an aberration outside an otherwise apolitical science.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

All sides accept the historical record: antiquarian and archaeological institutions were often founded for national purposes; Kossinna's work and its Nazi elaboration existed and were ideologically driven; some of that work was also technically competent. What divides the positions is the *evaluation*: whether archaeology's integrative social function is a proper end that was merely perverted (position 1), a structural vulnerability that recurs (position 2), or an inseparable part of the discipline's constitution (position 3).

## Additional Case: Ayodhya (attributed/brute fact, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]] adds a further contemporary case
to this debate. **Ayodhya**, in northern India, is held by Hindus as the birthplace of
Rama; a five-century-old mosque on the site was destroyed by Hindu nationalists in 1992,
with associated riots killing over 2,000 people (brute fact). Post-destruction
excavations claimed to find remains of an earlier Hindu temple stratified below the
mosque. Johnson notes (attributed) that most archaeologists would be wary of lending
uncritical support to either side's claim of exclusive ownership of the site — a further
instance of archaeology's structural vulnerability to being conscripted into
nationalist and communal dispute.

**Bibliographic note:** Johnson's own discussion of archaeology-and-nationalism and of
"archaeology under the Nazis" beyond the Ayodhya case is confined, in the chapters
ingested for this book, to a **Further Reading bibliography list** rather than developed
body-text argument — citing (among others) Hamilakis and Yalouri (1996), Diaz-Andreu and
Champion (1996), Kohl and Fawcett (1996), Curta (2001) and Piontek (2006) on Slavic
identity, Piotrowska (1997–8) on Biskupin, Abdi (2001) on Iran, Fontein (2006) on
Zimbabwe, and Abu El-Haj (2001) on Israeli archaeology, plus (for Nazi-era archaeology)
Arnold (1990), Arnold and Hassmann (1995), Härke and Wolfram (1993), and Diaz-Andreu
(1993) on archaeology under Franco. Johnson does not name Kossinna in these chapters;
the book's substantive Kossinna/Nazi-archaeology content, if any, falls outside the
material ingested here. This bibliographic cluster is recorded as further-reading
context, not as new body content for Position 2 above.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-10)

This page is opened from a single ingested source (Clark 1957), which is unusually candid in stating both a liberal-universalist defense of archaeology's social role and the Kossinna/Nazi abuse. Clark's own framing carries mid-century assumptions — a confident narrative of "human progress," a colonial-era description of archaeological services in dependent territories as an imperial "manifest duty," and continued use of "savage/barbarian" vocabulary — which are themselves part of the reflexive record (see [[clark-grahame]] and [[archaeology]] Reflexivity). The positions above should be widened as sources on nationalist archaeology beyond the German case (e.g. Kohl & Fawcett; Trigger on nationalist archaeology) are ingested.

## Renfrew & Bahn textbook densification (2012, Ch. 14 — Whose Past?)

*(Attributed — Renfrew & Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]], 6th ed. 2012, Ch. 14. Pedagogical survey of public meaning, nationalist symbols, destruction, and ownership; position recording, not adjudication. Complements Clark 1957 and Johnson’s Ayodhya note above.)*

### Framing: “whose past?”

Renfrew & Bahn frame “whose past?” as raising meaning, significance, and interpretation beyond what happened and why: the past means different things to different people (e.g. Aboriginal vs white Australian readings of Lake Mungo or Kakadu); presentation (museums) and lessons drawn are subjective and often ideological/political; archaeologists bear special responsibility because excavation destroys evidence. Collectively, cultural inheritance (language, faith, customs) is rooted deep, and archaeology increasingly helps define **national identity** — especially for nations without long written history.

### Nationalism, symbols, and conflict (textbook cases)

- **National emblems** of recently emerged states often draw on archaeological artifacts and sites (e.g. Zimbabwe named from the archaeological site). Use of archaeology to enhance national identity can cause conflict: the Former Yugoslav Republic of Macedonia’s name and Vergina-star emblem (from a 4th-century BC gold casket in Greek territory) provoked Greek outrage over perceived appropriation of Macedonian history.
- **Ideology and reuse:** sectarian reshaping of monuments (churches on pagan temples; Parthenon reused); Mao’s China (“past should serve the present”; artistic treasures framed as products of skilled workers and class struggle); Saddam Hussein mural equating him with Nebuchadnezzar.
- **Politics of destruction:** Hindu fundamentalists tore down the 16th-century Babri Masjid at Ayodhya (December 1992); 2003 court-directed ASI excavations to test claims of an earlier Hindu temple (same case family as Johnson’s note above). Taliban destruction of the Bamiyan Buddhas (March 2001) plus Kabul museum objects — not as emblems of a local rival group but as human images impious to religious extremists; condemned by UN/UNESCO; not wartime military necessity.
- **Great Zimbabwe** (cross-ref Ch. 12): early European non-African builder myths and later diffusionist “influence” arguments resisted Caton-Thompson’s African/Bantu origin conclusion; subsequent research supports African construction — a classic instance of colonial/racist capture of origin narratives.

### Ownership, indigenous claims, and ethics

Until recent decades western archaeologists assumed rights to acquire and excavate worldwide; since WWII former colonies assert control over heritage (repatriation; excavation consent). Paradigmatic museum disputes: Elgin Marbles / Parthenon sculptures; Nefertiti bust; Lydian Hoard; Euphronios Vase. Indigenous ownership questions (e.g. Australian Aboriginal control of deep-time sites) are flagged as major issues alongside CRM and heritage contested along ethnic lines (Introduction). **NAGPRA** (1990) and Kennewick Man litigation, and Australian repatriation of deep-time remains (Murray Black Collection; Kow Swamp; Lake Mungo), exemplify conflict between scientific study and ancestral obligations. World Archaeology Congress (founded 1986, Peter Ucko) is cited as a platform for indigenous and newly emerged nations’ archaeologies; pluralizing pasts links to contested museum displays and critique of Western-formulated Cultural Heritage Management / UNESCO listing as potentially homogenizing.

### Textbook summary position

The past legitimizes national and ethnic identity, sometimes destructively; every archaeological decision should weigh ethics; fringe theories are best answered by skeptical evidential questions; looting for salable artifacts is a major destruction pathway with museums and collectors bearing responsibility; earlier colonial disregard for native feelings is acknowledged without abandoning scientific interest in the past.
