# Theory in Historical Archaeology

The dispute over what theoretical apparatus historical archaeology needs to interpret the material culture of the modern (post-1500, colonial) world. The positions below are staged most explicitly in Hall (2000), who is both a chronicler of and a partisan in the debate; his characterizations of the other positions should be read as such.

## Positions

**Structuralism / world-view ([[deetz-james]], [[glassie-henry]]).** Material culture is generated by shared, largely unconscious cultural "grammars" — sets of rules "mutually accepted by the members of the culture producing them... even if they cannot be explicitly stated by their users" (Deetz 1996: 154). Deetz periodized Anglo-American colonial material culture into successive world-views culminating in the mid-18th-century "Georgian order" ([[georgian-worldview]]), with a parallel African American mindset resisting it. Strength claimed: explains deep cross-media regularities (houses, gravestones, ceramics) across a population. See [[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-study]].

**Critical materialism / dominant ideology ([[leone-mark-p]], Paul Shackel, [[johnson-matthew]]).** Keeps structuralism's attention to the Georgian order but replaces the "mental template" with ideology in a Marxist-Foucauldian sense: clocks, place settings, and formal gardens naturalized emerging capitalist class relations ("The gardens and homes were statements of wishes rather than statements of fact," Leone 1988). Johnson (1996) extended the argument to English enclosure and the closed Georgian house. See [[critical-archaeology]].

**Phenomenology / experiential archaeology ([[tilley-christopher]]).** Meaning is embodied and relational; landscapes and buildings are understood through the situated bodily experience of moving through them. See [[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]].

**Discourse and transcripts ([[hall-martin]]).** All three predecessors "seek closure" — an orderly world without contingency — and produce sanitized accounts that omit colonial violence. Hall argued: structuralism cannot explain change within its own propositions ("a structuralist world is a world without history") and Deetz smuggles in a contradictory diffusionist "infection" metaphor when he does explain change; critical materialism's "blanketing dominant ideology" leaves the dominated as passive dupes and silences subaltern voices a second time; phenomenology rests on "the romantic projection of present, personalized experience into the past," an unwarranted assumption of common humanity (citing Julian Thomas 2000 on the same point). Hall's alternative reads material culture through Foucault's "statement" and Scott's [[public-transcript]]/[[hidden-transcript]], holding local circumstance and world-system context in the same frame and treating discordance between evidence classes as the primary interpretive signal.

## Relation to Adjacent Debates

This is historical archaeology's local version of the wider processual/post-processual contest: all four positions are post-processual in rejecting positivist covering laws, and the dispute is over which post-processual resource — structure, ideology, body, or discourse — carries the interpretive load. Hall's charge that structuralism cannot handle history parallels standard critiques of structuralism across the social sciences ([[structuralism]]).

## Status

Open. No position has been abandoned; Deetz's periodization, Leone's Annapolis corpus, Tilley's landscape method, and Hall's transcripts vocabulary all remain in active use. As of the sources ingested (Hall 2000; Leone 2010), the field treats them as complementary toolkits more than exclusive paradigms — a live, unadjudicated pluralism.
