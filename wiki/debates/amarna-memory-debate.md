# Amarna in Memory: the Osarsiph Legend and the Myth of the Lepers

The dispute: how to interpret the ancient "myth of the lepers" — the legend, first attested in Manetho, of a King Amenophis, lepers led by the priest **Osarsiph** (later identified with Moses), thirteen years of temple desecration, and eventual expulsion — and its relationship to the historical Amarna episode and to the biblical Exodus. Attested in at least seven ancient versions (Manetho, Hecataeus of Abdera, Lysimachos, Apion, Chaeremon, Tacitus, Pompeius Trogus), each varying in anti-Judaic content ([[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|Assmann 1996]] inventories them).

## Positions

- **Disguised Amarna reminiscence.** The legend preserves a distorted folk memory of [[amarna|Akhenaten]]'s iconoclasm — the "lepers" who closed the temples encode the Atenists; Amenophis and Osarsiph point to Eighteenth Dynasty figures.
- **Hyksos memory.** The legend reworks the older trauma of Hyksos rule and expulsion; Josephus himself identified the Hyksos with the Jews, an equation running through classical antiquity though absent from the Bible.
- **Anti-Jewish counterhistory.** The legend is a conscious Egyptian inversion of the Exodus narrative — the two are structural mirror images (Judeophobia answering Egyptophobia), composed in a Hellenistic context of ethnic polemic.
- **Assmann's trauma synthesis.** Assmann argues the positions are not exclusive: the Amarna experience was repressed rather than remembered (no direct tradition survived), and the trauma "encrypted" itself in legend, later attracting Hyksos material and anti-Jewish polemic. He explicitly cautions that the material is "a variant of the Egyptians' Asiatic trauma, and not some more specific kind of anti-Semitism" (Assmann 1996).

## Afterlife

Carlo Ginzburg (reported by Assmann) argues the medieval circulation of Josephus's *Contra Apionem* carried the myth into Europe, feeding the 1321 French leper-poisoning conspiracy trials and subsequent plague-era massacres of Jews — a reception-history claim, attributed to Ginzburg, not a wiki-voice assertion. All positions on guilt or conspiracy in these medieval episodes are recorded in attributed voice only; the pogroms' victims were innocent of the alleged plots.

## Status

Open. The debate continues in Egyptology and biblical studies, sharpened by Assmann's *Moses the Egyptian* (1997) and its critics.
