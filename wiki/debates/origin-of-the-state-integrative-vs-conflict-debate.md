# Origin of the State: Integrative vs Conflict Theories

The oldest running dispute in the anthropology of the state: did centralized
government first arise because it *benefited* the governed (integrative theory), or
because it *coerced* them on behalf of a dominant stratum (conflict/conquest theory)?
The debate structures Service's *[[service-origins-state-1975|Origins of the State and
Civilization]]* (1975), whose two concluding chapters are an explicit "Negative"
(anti-conflict) and "Positive" (pro-integrative) statement. Distinct from the
[[service-fried-political-typology-debate|Service–Fried typology debate]] (which cuts
levels differently); this debate is about the *cause* of the state, not the labels
for its stages.

## Shared ground

All sides study the same transition — from kin-ordered egalitarian society to
centralized, stratified polity with specialized government — and agree that early
states show hereditary rank, monumental works, craft specialization, and (in the
archaic civilizations) writing. They disagree on the **motive force**: benefit vs.
coercion; on whether inequality *precedes and causes* the state or *follows* from
integrative organization; and on whether "class" in early states means economic
ownership or political office.

## Positions

### Integrative / benefit (Service, attributed)

[[integrative-theory-of-the-state|Service]] argued that government "worked to protect,
not another class or stratum of the society, but itself — legitimizing itself in its
role of maintaining the whole society." Political power organized the economy, not
vice versa; the system was "redistributive, allocative, not acquisitive." He held
there is "absolutely no evidence" of private-property capitalism, of worker/peasant
uprisings, or of physical force used by rulers against the general population in the
archaic civilizations or his primitive-state sample; the observed stratification was
"political strata, not strata of ownership groups." Coercion, where it appears, marks
the system's failure, not its origin.

### Conflict / class (Morgan–Marx–[[engels-friedrich|Engels]]; [[fried-morton-h|Fried]])

The state arose as an instrument of force protecting an advantaged stratum. In the
Morgan–Marx–Engels form (attributed), the propertied minority, having accumulated
wealth through commodity production, erected a permanent apparatus of force to protect
its class interest. In Fried's revision (*[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|
Evolution of Political Society]]*, attributed), the driver is not capitalist property
but hereditary kin groups' **differential access to basic resources**; population
pressure prompts "nonkinship mechanisms" (army, militia, police) that "in the final
analysis" defend the [[stratified-society|stratified]] order. Notably, Fried himself
*denied* that warfare or slavery caused pristine states — Service quotes him ("rather
than war… being the source of stratification… stratification is a provoker of war")
to turn Fried against the conquest theorists.

### Conquest / warfare (Spencer, Gumplowicz, Oppenheimer; Carneiro)

The state is a product of conquest and Darwinian intersocietal selection; the winners
permanently subjugate the losers (Oppenheimer: "the moment when first the conqueror
spared his victim in order permanently to exploit him… gave birth to nation and
state"). Carneiro's **circumscription** theory (attributed) is the modern refinement:
where rich land is bounded by poor land or sea, the defeated cannot flee, so warfare
under population pressure yields sustained domination that can consolidate into a
state. Service treats circumscription as a real *enabling* factor — vindicated for
Egypt and coastal Peru — but not as a sufficient cause, folding it into his broader
"governance by benefit."

### Hydraulic / managerial-despotism (Wittfogel)

Wittfogel (attributed) held that maintaining large-scale irrigation and flood control
required a central authority, which then extended despotic power over other spheres —
the "Oriental despotism" of the ancient hydraulic states.

### Anti-origin ([[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Graeber & Wengrow]], 2021)

A later position rejecting the framing itself: "the state has no origin." What modern
theory bundles as the state decomposes into separate elements (control of violence,
information, and charisma) with distinct histories and no necessary fusion. See
[[dawn-of-everything-debate]].

## Service's Negative case (attributed)

Service's Ch. 16 rebuts each single-cause theory:

| Target theory | Service's rebuttal |
|---|---|
| Conquest/warfare | Where conquest produced permanent subjugation (Ankole, Zulu, Nupe) a government already existed; war did not create it. No evidence of class uprisings — documented violence is "wars of princes," not of classes |
| Wittfogel hydraulic | No coincidence of large hydraulic systems and despotism; Peru's canals predate urban empires by ~1,500 years; in Mesopotamia/China political centralization preceded and *created* canal systems — "the political system was the creative agent" |
| Population pressure (Carneiro, Harner) | "An enabler is not a cause" — surplus/scarcity could as easily generate sharing as conflict |
| Urbanism (readings of Childe) | Urbanism is a resultant, often *planned by* an already-existing polity, not a cause |
| Class stratification/repression (Marx–Engels, Fried, Adams) | No evidence of capitalist private property or of rulers' force against the masses; "stratification" was governors-vs-governed political office, not ownership classes (Egypt's officialdom was "stewardship, not ownership") |

## Points of tension (not verdicts)

| Issue | Integrative (Service) | Conflict/conquest |
|---|---|---|
| Motive force | Benefit of coordination | Coercion / defense of advantage |
| Causal order | Bureaucracy first; inequality follows | Inequality/class first; state defends it |
| "Class" in early states | Political office (governors/governed) | Economic stratum / resource access |
| Role of force | Symptom of failure | Origin mechanism |
| Role of war | Post-state amplifier | Generative (conquest school) / provoked by stratification (Fried) |
| Irrigation | Permissive enabler | Causal (Wittfogel) |

## Adjacent position — Yoffee (2005, attributed)

[[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] (*[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]*) recasts the Service–Fried duel as partly beside the point: both camps shared neo-evolutionist **stage modeling** and (when operationalized archaeologically) the **myth of the archaic state** as large despotism. He sketches a quasi-dialectical synthesis of differentiation/integration and power/domination without treating chiefdom→state identification as the research goal. Full position set: [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]].

## Position — Scott 2017: coercion, bondage, and the grain–manpower module

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017. Places him on the **conflict/coercion** side of this duel without equating him to Marx–Engels class theory or Carneiro circumscription. Mesopotamia-focused synthesis; Yoffee is a named specialist guide, not co-author of Scott’s theses.)*

**Strongest form:**

1. **Parasite, not civilizational creator.** Sedentism, farming, the *domus*, irrigation, and towns were **Neolithic achievements already in place** well before anything like a state; the embryonic state arises by harnessing a late Neolithic **grain-and-manpower module** for control and appropriation — that module being the only possible scaffolding for state design (Ch.4–5). States did not invent irrigation or domestication (Introduction).
2. **Not a social-contract magnet.** Early states were not primarily attractive magnets of luxury and culture; they had to **capture and hold** much of their population by forms of bondage and were plagued by epidemics of crowding (Preface). If earliest state formation was largely coercive, Hobbes/Locke visions of the state as magnet of civil peace drawing people by charisma must be reexamined (Introduction Ch.5 thumbnail).
3. **Surplus requires compulsion.** Pre-state domestic mode of production (Sahlins via Scott): open access, no automatic elite-appropriable surplus; Boserup: without land monopoly, lower classes must be made personally unfree or they find other subsistence. Only unfree/coerced labor (corvée, forced deliveries, debt bondage, serfdom, communal bondage, slavery) created surplus under early-state demography when means of production were not monopolized (Ch.5).
4. **Slavery/bondage scaled by the state.** Slavery not invented by the state but elaborated and scaled as essential to maximize productive population and appropriable surplus; Scott is “tempted to assert: ‘No slavery, no state’”; walls as double-edged confinement; warfare prioritizes bodies (captives) over land; mass deportation and helot-type in-situ domination as communal bondage formulas (Ch.5). Bondage appears a condition of ancient-state survival (Introduction).
5. **Grain legibility, not mere ecological richness.** Early states need appropriable, measurable dominant grain plus administrable cultivators — not wetland diversity; wetlands produced wealth and towns but no states for more than a millennium (Ch.4; Pournelle-type revision). Rejects simple Wittfogel “irrigation invents the state” sequence while granting water’s importance (Ch.4–5).
6. **Collapse and barbarians (coercive dynamics extended).** “Collapse” often frees subjects from extractive modules; barbarians and states as competing parasites on the same grain–manpower surplus; secondary primitivism as rational flight from taxes, bondage, war, and epidemics rather than failure to achieve civilization (Ch.6–7). Affinity with [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]] on fragility; not identity with Yoffee’s full program.

**Tension table extension (Scott column, attributed):**

| Issue | Integrative (Service) | Scott 2017 (coercion/bondage) |
|---|---|---|
| Motive force | Benefit of coordination | Appropriation of grain–labor surplus |
| Causal order | Bureaucracy/redistribution first | Neolithic module first; state parasitizes |
| Role of force | Symptom of failure | Origin and maintenance mechanism |
| Role of bondage | Marginal / denied at origin | Condition of early-state survival |
| Irrigation | Enabler | Extended by state; not inventor of state |
| Walls / confinement | Protection of the whole | Protection *and* holding of subjects |

Do **not** merge with [[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Graeber & Wengrow]] anti-origin position: Scott retains a clear “early state” object and a coercion thesis; G&W reject the origin framing itself. See [[dawn-of-everything-debate]] for adjacency only.

## Resolution status

`open`. No consensus adjudicates benefit vs. coercion; contemporary archaeology
(e.g. [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]]) tends to
multi-causal, region-specific pathways and to skepticism that any single mechanism —
integrative or coercive — explains all cases, while
[[graeber-wengrow-dawn-of-everything-2021|Graeber & Wengrow]] reject the origin
question itself. Service's strong "no coercion at the origin" claim is largely not
accepted; his insistence that redistribution and theocratic legitimation can precede
coercive apparatus remains a live position. Scott 2017 is a major contemporary
statement on the coercion/bondage side for archaic grain states. See also [[state-formation]],
[[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]], [[myths-of-the-archaic-state-debate]], [[grain-hypothesis]],
[[the-state]].

### Carneiro circumscription as conflict-side package (attributed)

[[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]]’s [[circumscription|circumscription theory]] (1970; restated in *[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]* 2003) is a major **conflict** position: population pressure + environmental/social circumscription blocks village fission; warfare consolidates multivillage polities → chiefdoms → states. Carneiro ranks population pressure first among determinants for chiefdoms/states while rejecting equal “democracy of causes.” Foils: Service’s integrative theory and his 1968 “Down with prime-movers!” pluralism; multi-factor processual models (Flannery); ideology/agency programs. Not adjudicated here.
