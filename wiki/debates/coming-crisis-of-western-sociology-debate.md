# Coming Crisis of Western Sociology

Standing dispute over whether (and in what sense) Western Academic Sociology — especially [[structural-functionalism|Parsonian structural-functionalism]] — entered a structural **crisis** around 1965–1975, and what follows for method and politics. Named from [[gouldner-alvin-w|Alvin W. Gouldner]]'s *[[gouldner-coming-crisis-1970|The Coming Crisis of Western Sociology]]* (1970). Related: [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate]], [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].

## Positions

### Gouldner (1970)

Western sociology underwent binary fission into **Academic Sociology** (westward, middle-class, reformist) and **Marxism** (eastward, oppositional). Functionalism became Academic Sociology's dominant synthesis; its success, welfare-state entanglement, generational sentiment shift, and internal variability produce **entropy** and open space for rival infrastructures (Goffmanian dramaturgy, ethnomethodology, Homansian exchange, New Left). Crisis means possible metamorphosis, not necessarily death. Exit path: [[reflexive-sociology]] grounded in analysis of [[domain-assumptions]] and [[theoretical-infrastructure]].

### Functionalist / professional defense (as reconstructed in the debate)

Functionalism supplies general sociology, not a narrow sect; critique confuses political disagreement with theoretical failure; value-freedom and system analysis remain scientific necessities. (Gouldner treats Kingsley Davis's 1959 ASA presidential line — Functionalism indistinct from sociology — as itself a *symptom* of boundary loss, not only a defense.)

### Conflict / critical rivals

Order-and-consensus models systematically underplay power, force, property, and conflict ([[mills-c-wright|Mills]], [[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]], Marxist and New Left critics). Agreement with Gouldner *that* Functionalism is inadequate need not imply agreement with his infrastructure theory or reflexive program.

### Later historiographic position

U.S. Parsonian hegemony did break up; many Functionalist *concepts* were absorbed into ordinary sociology; "reflexivity" diversified (feminist, postcolonial, Bourdieusian, ethnomethodological). Crisis as total system metamorphosis is only partly borne out; crisis as end of one hegemony is widely accepted.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Postwar U.S. sociology expanded massively in universities; Parsons was a central theorist.
- 1960s political conflict (civil rights, Vietnam, campus) pressed the discipline.
- Alternative micro and critical programs gained audiences in the 1960s–70s.
- Gouldner cites a 1964 national survey of ASA sociologists (Sprehe dissertation material) with large majority "favorably disposed" toward Functional theory and age gradients of hostility among the young — reception evidence as he reports it, not re-audited here.
- What these facts *mean* for "crisis," "entropy," East–West convergence, and prescription is what is disputed.
