## Background

Beginning c. 1900 BCE, Mohenjo-daro and most other major Indus cities were abandoned or
drastically reduced in scale; Mohenjo-daro shows essentially no habitation between c. 1900
BCE and the Kushan period. Of the five cities Possehl counts as the Indus Civilization's
largest urban centers (Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Ganweriwala, Dholavira, Rakhigarhi), all
ceased to function as urban centers by the end of the Mature Harappan. Numerous smaller
settlements (Chanhu-daro, Kot Diji, Balakot, Allahdino, Kulli, Mehi, Nindowari, Nausharo,
Kalibangan, Ropar, Surkotada, Dholavira, Desalpur, Lothal) were abandoned or severely
depopulated in the same period; production of luxury/prestige craft goods (long barrel-
cylinder beads, etched carnelian beads, inscribed stamp seals) ceased, writing fell out of
use, and long-distance maritime and overland trade contracted sharply. What explains this
transformation — and whether "collapse" is even the right description — has been
contested since Wheeler's original invasion thesis. See [[deurbanization-of-the-indus]]
for the phenomenon itself; this page records the causal dispute over it.

## Positions

### Position 1 — Invasion by Aryan warriors (Wheeler 1947; now widely rejected)

Wheeler read the Rgveda partly as a historical record of conflict between "newly arrived
Aryan warriors" and the indigenous Indus population, citing Rgvedic references to *pur*
(forts) destroyed by the god Indra, and pointed to skeletal remains in Mohenjo-daro's
upper levels as possible massacre victims. Possehl reports this position is now widely
rejected on chronological grounds (the Rgveda's codification is dated by most scholars to
c. 1000 BCE, roughly a millennium after the transformation, so Vedic-era Aryans could not
have been active participants in an event nearly a thousand years earlier) and on
taphonomic grounds (the "massacre" skeletons are argued, by Dales and Kennedy, to be
hasty interments rather than violence victims — see [[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate]] for
the skeletal evidence in full). Possehl further reports (via Krishna Deva and B. B. Lal,
personal communication) that the Aryan-invasion hypothesis may not even have originated
with Wheeler himself but was suggested to him by V. S. Agrawala, a member of the 1946
Harappa excavation team.

### Position 2 — Indigenous sociocultural transformation, not collapse or conquest (Possehl 2002)

Possehl's own thesis, and the book's proposed explanation, locates the cause not in any
single external or natural factor but in an internal, sociocultural and ideological
failure. He characterizes the Mature Harappan civilizational complex — urbanization, an
avoidance of monumental self-glorifying imagery he terms "nihilism," a water-centered
ritual/civic display he terms *wasserluxus*, and sustained technological virtuosity — as,
in his reading, too tightly integrated and harmonious a system, and therefore brittle
under changing conditions. He draws on J. C. Heesterman's argument that long-lived
civilizations require built-in "inner conflict" to force continual adaptive negotiation,
and on Robert McC. Adams's distinction between rigid/integrated systems and
resilient/disequilibrium-tolerant ones, framing the Indus Civilization's roughly 600-year
duration as comparatively short (against roughly 3,000 years for Dynastic Egypt or
Chinese civilization) and attributing this partly to over-integration. He explicitly
rejects the language of "collapse" or "eclipse," preferring "transformation," and notes
the process was regionally uneven: pronounced in Sindh, the Kulli Domain, and (probably)
the Harappa Domain, but much more continuous in the Eastern Domain (Punjab, Haryana,
Rajasthan, western Uttar Pradesh, where settlement counts actually rose) and the Sorath
Domain (Gujarat/Saurashtra), where some sites — notably [[rojdi|Rojdi]] — expanded even as
Mohenjo-daro was being abandoned. Possehl treats this partial regional expansion as a
caution that "the so-called eclipse or end of the Indus Civilization might well be an
overstatement and in need of radical reexamination." This is explicitly Possehl's own
interpretive synthesis, not a documented empirical finding, and should be read as a single
author's historical-sociological argument.

### Position 2b — Localization Era and indigenous legacy (Kenoyer 1998)

[[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] (1998) frames the end of Mature Harappan urban integration as a **Localization Era** within the [[indus-valley-tradition|Indus Valley Tradition]] (following [[shaffer-jim|Jim Shaffer]]), not as civilizational death or Aryan conquest. He argues explicitly that there was **no invasion or mass migration** of new populations into the northwestern subcontinent between roughly 1900 and 600 BCE; bioarchaeological and settlement evidence, on his reading, show continuity of people with reorganization of political and economic networks. Environmental stress (including Saraswati/Ghaggar-Hakra drying and Indus flooding episodes) and agricultural reorganization (spread of summer crops such as sorghum, millet, and rice) are treated as **contributing conditions**, not sole causes. Regionally, he tracks distinct Localization trajectories — Cemetery H in the Punjab, Jhukar in Sindh, Rangpur in Gujarat, and indigenous intensification at sites such as Pirak (per Jarrige) — rather than a single collapse narrative. Continuities into the Early Historic period (weights and measures, bead and copper crafts, multicropping surplus; **not** the script) support his "legacy without hiatus" thesis. Language (later Indo-Aryan dialects) and culture (Painted Grey Ware, Vedic texts) are kept analytically separate from biological population continuity — four-way non-identity. This is Kenoyer's attributed synthesis; it converges with Possehl's anti-invasion / transformation framing while emphasizing craft-technology and tradition-periodization vocabulary over Possehl's "brittle ideology" metaphor. See [[deurbanization-of-the-indus]], [[cemetery-h]].

### Position 3 — Environmental causation (river/climate factors; multiple, competing sub-hypotheses)

Several environmentally-grounded hypotheses have been proposed, none endorsed by Possehl
as a sole or sufficient cause:

- **River avulsion (Lambrick, Mughal).** A course-shift of the Indus in Sindh is proposed
  as the cause of Mohenjo-daro's abandonment specifically, sometimes extended via a
  "domino effect" logic to the wider civilization ("as Mohenjo-daro went, so went the
  Indus Civilization") — an extension Possehl treats as an unproven leap.
- **The Raikes/Dales dam hypothesis.** A natural dam of unconsolidated river sediment near
  Sehwan is proposed to have impounded the Indus and flooded Mohenjo-daro. This has been
  rebutted (Wasson) on geomorphological grounds — unconsolidated floodplain sediments lack
  the structural integrity to hold back the river, by analogy with the 1819 earthquake-
  formed "Allah Bund" ridge in Kutch, breached by the first flood against it. Possehl
  further argues that even if such a dam existed, impoundment alone does not meet
  "sufficient and necessary" criteria for the civilization's end; any such event would
  need to interact with "some deeper, structural aspect of the Mature Harappan
  sociocultural system."
- **Tectonic river capture / Sarasvati desiccation (Agrawal & Sood 1982; provisionally
  endorsed by Possehl as a contributing factor).** Himalayan drainage feeding the
  Sarasvati/Ghaggar-Hakra system was gradually captured by east-flowing streams (forming
  the Yamuna), drying the Sarasvati and Drishadvati over the later 3rd–2nd millennia BCE.
  Possehl frames this explicitly as **tectonic, not climatic** ("apparently has little, if
  anything, to do with broad trends in climatic change") and declines to call the stream
  capture "the direct cause" of the transformation, stating only that the desiccation
  "cannot be ignored" — a `contributed_to` relation, with a stated mechanism (loss of
  irrigation-supporting river flow via tectonically driven drainage capture), but with
  causal weight left explicitly open.
- **Climatic-desiccation hypothesis (G. Singh 1971).** Salinity records from three
  Rajasthan salt lakes near Lake Lunkaransar are read as evidence of reduced rainfall
  driving settlement change. Possehl's counter-evidence: settlement counts in the nearby
  Eastern Domain actually *rise* from 218 (Mature Harappan) to 853 (Posturban) sites, even
  as average site size drops sharply (13.54 to 3.55 hectares) — a pattern Possehl argues is
  inconsistent with a severe regional rainfall decline, and treats the site-size drop
  itself as possibly diagnostic of the character of deurbanization (fragmentation into
  many small sites, rather than depopulation per se).
- **Multi-causal position (Allchin & Allchin 1997).** Decline of Mesopotamian trade after
  c. 2000 BCE, combined with climatic/environmental deterioration in Sindh and Himalayan
  tectonic uplift altering river courses, acting together. Possehl is skeptical on several
  counts: it is uncertain trade with Mesopotamia actually ceased c. 2000 BCE (Meluhha
  references continue into 2nd-millennium Mesopotamian texts); it is unclear trade was
  ever central enough to the whole civilization for its reduction to explain civilization-
  wide change; there is a causal-direction problem (did reduced trade cause the
  transformation, or did the transformation reduce the trade?); and modern Mohenjo-daro
  receives under 12 cm of annual rainfall and would be desert without Indus floods
  regardless of any rainfall reduction, undercutting the weight the "reduced rainfall"
  component can bear.

Consistent with the wiki's evidentiary standard, no river or climate factor above is
recorded as a sole `caused_by`; each is filed, at most, as `contributed_to`, with the
mechanism argued by its proponent stated explicitly.

## Nationalist and political stakes

Possehl explicitly flags that debates over Indus language, ethnicity, and the "Aryan
question" carry contemporary political weight — invasion narratives have historically
mapped onto colonial-era and later Indian nationalist historiography, including the
competing "Out of India"/indigenist positions in South Asian nationalist scholarship,
which Possehl alludes to without fully engaging. He states plainly, regarding the
separate but related BMAC-contact question, that "given the contemporary critique, or
deconstruction, of the concept of race, the question of them being Aryans does not arise"
— an explicit acknowledgment of the race-concept baggage attached to this literature.

**Curator's note** *(2026-07-10)*: This source (2002) pre-dates the ancient-DNA
revolution in South Asian population history entirely — work using aDNA to address
Indo-Aryan migration (e.g., Narasimhan et al. 2019 and later studies) postdates this
ingest by nearly two decades and is not reflected here. Any page citing this debate's
state should carry the explicit flag "(as of Possehl 2002, pre-aDNA)"; the debate should
be updated via the Contradiction and Replication Protocol once aDNA-era sources are
ingested, rather than treating Possehl's linguistic-migration position as current
consensus.
