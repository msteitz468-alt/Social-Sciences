# The Segmentary Lineage Model — Ideal Structure vs Political Actuality

> Anchored by [[evans-pritchard-nuer-study|Evans-Pritchard, *The Nuer* (1940)]]; concept [[segmentary-lineage-system]]. Positions stated in their strongest form; no adjudication in wiki voice.

## The Dispute

Does the **[[segmentary-lineage-system|segmentary lineage model]]** — balanced opposition of nested groups fusing and fissioning by structural level — describe how a stateless society actually works, or does it describe an *ideal* (the natives' own ideology, or the anthropologist's tidy abstraction) that behaviour, history, and material forces repeatedly override? The Nuer are the test case, and the argument has run since the 1950s across political anthropology.

## Positions

**Position A — Segmentary opposition is a real ordering principle (Evans-Pritchard 1940; Fortes).** The Nuer sustain order without a state because political values are relative and self-balancing: "the members of any segment unite for war against adjacent segments of the same order and unite with these adjacent segments against larger sections." The dominant clan's lineage system supplies "the idiom in which [tribal segments'] relations can be expressed," and the [[feud]] is "a structural movement between political segments by which the form of the... political system is maintained." Evans-Pritchard presented this as an equilibrium of fission and fusion, not a fixed framework.

**Position B — The lineage model is ideology/idealization, not behaviour (Kuper 1982; Holy; Verdon; Karp & Maynard).** Critics argue that balanced segmentary opposition is rarely observed in action; that it reflects what people *say* (a jural/genealogical ideology) more than what they *do*; and that the model was over-schematized by anthropologists who read the neat diagram back onto messy politics of leadership, faction, and contingency. Kuper's *Lineage Theory: A Critical Retrospect* argued the whole "lineage model" had outlived its usefulness. Evans-Pritchard himself supplied ammunition: idiosyncratic migrations and feuds, and the concession that "there is... no clear-cut distinction between fighting against another tribe and fighting against a segment of one's own tribe."

**Position C — Ecology and materialism explain Nuer expansion better (Sahlins 1961; Kelly 1985; Gough).** Rather than a timeless structural logic, Nuer expansion into Dinka territory is explained by material drivers — Sahlins reframed segmentary lineage as "an organization of predatory expansion"; Raymond Kelly's *The Nuer Conquest* argued the expansion was driven by the cattle-and-bridewealth economy and its demographic pressures. On this view the structure is an outcome or instrument, not a first cause — against Evans-Pritchard's insistence that "the tendency towards segmentation must be defined as a fundamental principle of their social structure," not reduced to ecology.

## Shared Factual Ground

All sides accept the descriptive record: the Nuer had no chiefs with coercive authority, no courts, and a blood-cattle/feud system; lineage and territorial names coincide; genealogies are telescoped; the Nuer expanded at Dinka expense in the nineteenth century and absorbed Dinka by adoption. The dispute is over what *explains* and *organizes* these facts — a real balancing mechanism, a native/analyst ideology, or a material-ecological process — and how much the 1940 model idealizes the actuality.

## African Political Systems (1940) placement

*[[fortes-evans-pritchard-african-political-systems-1940|African Political Systems]]* supplies the comparative Group B frame (Tallensi, Logoli, Nuer) within which the segmentary lineage model was taught. The Nuer APS chapter is a condensation; full monographic evidence is *[[evans-pritchard-nuer-1940|The Nuer]]*. Fortes’s Tallensi APS chapter develops clanship cleavage and dual chief/*tindaana* offices as a non-Nuer Group B architecture. Later critiques of the model are not staged inside the 1940 volume itself.
