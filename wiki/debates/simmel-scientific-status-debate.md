# Is Simmel's Formal Sociology a Science? (Sorokin vs. Park–Spykman)

A methodological dispute in early-twentieth-century American sociology over whether [[simmel-georg|Georg Simmel]]'s [[formal-sociology|formal sociology]] — the study of the *forms of sociation* through analogical illustration rather than measurement — constitutes genuine scientific method or brilliant speculation dressed as science. The dispute is documented in [[simmel-sociology-wolff-1950|Kurt Wolff's editorial introduction]] to *The Sociology of Georg Simmel* (1950), which reconstructs the American reception.

## Position A — Formal sociology is a science to be returned to

Robert E. Park (with Ernest Burgess, *Introduction to the Science of Sociology*, 1921) and Nicholas Spykman (*The Social Theory of Georg Simmel*, 1925) gave Simmel a foundational place in American sociology, reading his form/content distinction as the basis for a rigorous, generalizing science of social forms — distinct from history and psychology. Leopold von Wiese's *Beziehungslehre* (systematized in Howard Becker's 1932 English adaptation) built an explicit "systematic sociology" on the same formal premise. On this view, Simmel isolated the repeatable structures (subordination, conflict, the dyad, the stranger) that make a cumulative sociology possible, and the discipline should build on him.

## Position B — Formal sociology is speculation, not science

Pitirim Sorokin (*Contemporary Sociological Theories*, 1928) attacked the Park–Spykman program directly: "Simmel's sociological method lacks scientific method... [it] entirely lacks either experimental approach, quantitative investigation, or any systematic factual study... What there is represents only the speculative generalization of a talented man." To "call the sociologists 'back to Simmel,' as Drs. Park and Spykman do, means to call them back to a pure speculation, metaphysics, and a lack of scientific method" (Sorokin 1928, quoted in Wolff 1950). Von Wiese published a rejoinder defending the formal program against Sorokin's charge.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides agree on what Simmel actually *did*: he identified recurrent social forms by marshalling a wide analogical survey of historical and comparative cases to exhibit a form, without measurement, sampling, or systematic data. They disagree on whether that procedure is a *method* (Position A: a legitimate formal-abstractive science, like geometry or grammar, which Simmel himself invoked as analogies) or the *absence* of one (Position B: insight and talent standing in for verification). Wolff himself (1950) sides with neither camp cleanly: he judges Simmel's writing shot through with an unresolved tension between treating sociology as a *method* and as a *subject-matter science*, tracing the vagueness to Simmel's own ambivalence about the "socialization of the spirit" rather than to carelessness.

## Current standing

`partially-resolved`. Simmel was restored to the classical canon by the revival since the 1980s (Frisby, Levine, Poggi), and his specific forms — the dyad and triad, the stranger, conflict as a bonded relation, the *tertius gaudens* — live on across [[dyad-and-triad|small-group theory]], network analysis, and microsociology. But Sorokin's underlying charge was, in effect, conceded by the discipline's history rather than refuted: there is no living "formal-sociological" *science* proceeding by Simmel's analogical method; his contribution survives as [[formal-sociology|absorbed]] vocabulary and problems, not as a research procedure. The "is it science?" framing dissolved as the quantitative and the interpretive wings of sociology each took what they wanted from Simmel.
