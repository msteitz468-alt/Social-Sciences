## The Dispute

Whether the early contact history of Canada (especially New France to 1663) should be written as a **Heroic Age** of European discovery, mission, and settlement — with Indigenous peoples as static background or doomed primitives — or as an **ethnohistorical** process in which native societies were historical actors and French–Indigenous relations (especially the fur trade) were constitutive of the colony. Recorded primarily from [[trigger-bruce-g|Trigger]] 1985; not adjudicated in wiki voice.

## Positions

**Heroic Age / Parkman–Charlevoix tradition.** Historians long treated the period from early explorations (Cabot/Cartier debates) to the establishment of royal government in New France (1663) as a "Heroic Age" in which French explorers, missionaries, and settlers performed noble deeds. Francis [[parkman-francis|Parkman]]'s multi-volume *France and England in North America* popularized evolutionary-racial rankings of tribes, romanticized Jesuits and French adventurers, and cast natives as fierce or vanishing "tenants of the wilderness" without secure title — an ideology, Trigger argues, that justified dispossession. The Charlevoix tradition and its digests supplied tribal stereotypes that prefigured Parkman while retaining a monogenist education thesis. Canadian nationalist and clerical histories absorbed these frames even where Canadian experience (fur trade, Royal Proclamation 1763, Indigenous allies such as Brant and Tecumseh) differed from US land war. Anthropology was charged with studying "peoples without history"; history departments studied Europeans. (As of Trigger 1985's historiographic survey.)

**Native-agency ethnohistory (Trigger 1985).** Trigger argues that:

1. Stereotypes of static, inferior, vanishing natives are **products of colonial and disciplinary history**, not findings — and that simply inverting stereotypes is not objectivity.
2. Archaeology shows deep Indigenous historical change before contact; the [[ethnographic-present]] of Boasian salvage ethnography is a methodological trap.
3. French **traders** and Indigenous partners established working relations indispensable to founding a viable St Lawrence colony — priests and officials often depended on or undermined that base.
4. To ~1632, French–Huron relations were a **creative symbiosis** (nondirected mutual accommodation around the fur trade), not one-way civilizing.
5. After 1632, [[virgin-soil-epidemics]], Jesuit mission factionalism, and Iroquois wars must be analysed as Indigenous as well as European history ([[huron]], [[iroquois]], [[petun]], [[neutral-iroquoians]], [[st-lawrence-iroquoians]]).
6. Method: combine archaeology, ethnography, and documents; treat interest groups (traders, administrators, missionaries, native polities) separately — [[ethnohistory]].

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both traditions use Cartier, Champlain, Jesuit *Relations*, and colonial archives; archaeology is unevenly used. They divide on **whose actions count as historical causation**, whether anthropology's subjects have "history," and whether the fur-trade frontier was a civilizing mission or a negotiated economic/political relationship.

## The *Children of Aataentsic* (1976/1987) layer

*(All claims attributed to [[trigger-bruce-g|Bruce Trigger]], *[[trigger-children-of-aataentsic-1976|The Children of Aataentsic]]*, ch. 1 and the 1987 preface — the book-length case history behind the 1985 summary above.)*

**Two stereotypes, both "products of European imagination."** Trigger argues Canadian historiography of Indian-European relations has been dominated by two competing stereotypes rather than one: (1) the Indian as **bloodthirsty savage** — dominant among Atlantic-seaboard colonists and reinforced by the narrative of American westward expansion; Parkman's 1867 account exemplifies a version that favored the Iroquois as admirably "energetic" against a supposedly weak, priest-dominated Huron; and (2) the **"noble savage,"** traceable to Montaigne, the prevailing sentimental view including among some Indian writers (Trigger cites Newell 1965). He holds that both stereotypes "are products of European imagination and wishful thinking" that excuse historians from explaining Indian behaviour with the same causal detail applied to Europeans — i.e., the dispute is not simply "hostile vs. sympathetic" portrayal but a shared failure of historical method underlying both.

**The Dollard des Ormeaux case.** Trigger cites the Dollard episode as exemplary of historians failing to revise inherited "heroic age" narratives despite contrary evidence: historians know Dollard died attempting to rob Iroquois hunters of their winter catch, "not to save New France," yet W. L. Morton (1965) continued to describe the episode as "heroic and necessary... for without furs New France could not live" (Morton 1965:52).

**Racialized passages in professional 20th-century historiography.** Trigger quotes Gustave Lanctot (1963:330) describing the absence of French-Indian intermarriage as "probably better...for the [French Canadian] race grew stronger being free of native cross-breeding," and W. L. Morton (1963:60–61) similarly framing the absence of intermarriage as having spared New France the "complications" seen in Spanish America — both treated by Trigger as residues of 19th-century racial explanation persisting into 20th-century professional Canadian historiography, not merely popular or antiquarian writing.

**Talbot on Brébeuf.** A Jesuit biographer of Jean de Brébeuf, Talbot (1956:67), described Huron sexual behaviour as a "sewer of sexual filth" practised by "animalized savages," and Huron women as "drudges from infancy" yet "more untamed than the men... more savage in vengeance" — cited by Trigger as an instance of stereotype substituting for understanding within sympathetic (pro-missionary) historiography, not only in hostile accounts.

**Champlain's reputation, revised against Trudel and Bishop.** Established Canadian historiography (Trudel 1966c; Morris Bishop 1948; "the majority of historians," per Trigger) portrays Champlain as an unusually sympathetic and capable interpreter of and ally to Indigenous peoples — praised for wishing French and Indians to "intermarry and become one people" and for humane restraint. Trigger directly contests this: he argues Champlain was "extremely ethnocentric and inflexible," that his early diplomatic successes "were the result more of the situation than of the man," and catalogues personal weaknesses (vanity, poor judgment, self-interest, growing colonial ambition after 1612). He similarly challenges Trudel's (1966) framing of Champlain's 1615 raid on an Iroquois (likely Oneida) village as "the first triumph of the Iroquois over the French" and a "turning point" toward Iroquois control of the lower Great Lakes, calling this "a serious misinterpretation" — no evidence, per Trigger, indicates any Iroquois or Huron participant read the outcome as an Iroquois victory, and the raid's actual aim (blood revenge and prisoner-taking, not conquest) was largely achieved.

**Trigger's critique of George Hunt as its own historiographic episode.** Beyond the empirical rebuttals filed at [[iroquois-wars-motivation-debate]] and [[virgin-soil-epidemics]], Trigger frames his repeated refutation of George Hunt's *The Wars of the Iroquois* (1940) — its fur-trade-determinist "middleman" thesis, its claim of instant fur-trade-driven "social revolution" preceding direct European contact, and its account of Huron/Neutral/Iroquois motives — as itself a case study in how a single influential mid-century monograph can fix a generation's explanatory frame; he credits Tooker (1965) with having already "exposed Hunt's errors" on the gun-supply point before this book.

**The 1987-preface "return to Parkman" charge (attributed, unresolved).** In the 1987 preface, Trigger records that a cluster of scholars — D. K. Richter (1986), Lucien Campeau (1987), Conrad Heidenreich (1986), Francis Jennings (*The Ambiguous Iroquois Empire*, 1984), and William Eccles (1983) — had, to varying degrees, rejected economic explanations of Iroquois warfare in favor of political ones (avenging old injuries, or, for Jennings, empire-building with British help). Trigger characterizes this trend as "to varying degrees... a return to the position of Francis Parkman" — an explicit charge that late-20th-century revisionism was recapitulating the 19th-century Heroic Age narrative it had originally displaced. He judges Eccles's portrayal of native cultures as "economically independent, resilient, and able to determine their own destinies until 1760" an "unjustifiably extreme" overcorrection against Hunt/Innis economic determinism. Trigger does not concede the point: he maintains native dependency on European trade goods was real, explicitly recognized by the Hurons themselves by the 1630s, and a major driver of intertribal conflict — the Richter/Campeau/Heidenreich/Jennings/Eccles position is recorded here as a live, attributed position, not adjudicated.

## Curator's Note

*(2026-07-15)* Filed and densified from Trigger *Natives and Newcomers* (1985) and *The Children of Aataentsic* (1976/1987). The ethnohistorical position is now mainstream in the field; this page records the historiographic struggle Trigger documents (Parkman still shapes popular memory), including Trigger's own report that some 1980s scholarship had partially swung back toward a Parkman-adjacent political-motive reading of Iroquois warfare. Later Indigenous scholarship, land-claims research, and post-1985/1987 archaeology should be added when ingested. Do not treat Trigger as the last word.
