# Sub-Saharan Iron Origins Debate

Live dispute over whether ferrous metallurgy south of the Sahara was **independently invented**, **adopted from external sources**, or some mosaic of both — and over which early radiocarbon claims are methodologically sound. Primary handbook synthesis: Bertram Mapunda, Ch. 42 of *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013|The Oxford Handbook of African Archaeology]]* (2013); technology survey: Shadreck Chirikure, Ch. 10.

**Do not adjudicate** — positions below in strongest form.

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

- Most of sub-Saharan Africa lacks a long distinct **Bronze/Copper Age** before iron; iron is often the first metal widely used (Phillipson 2005; handbook chapters — attributed).
- **Oldest widely recognized** ironworking (e.g. Taruga, Nigeria) and copperworking (Agadez region) cluster in the **middle first millennium cal. BC** (Mapunda, as of handbook ~2012).
- Early “anomalous” dates (Great Lakes; Cameroon/CAR claims of 2300–1900 cal. BC) remain **highly contested** and fail strict association criteria for many specialists (Mapunda summarizing Killick, Alpern, MacEachern).
- Radiocarbon **plateau** in the mid-first millennium BC limits chronological precision.

## Position A — External origin / diffusion

Sub-Saharan metallurgy is late relative to Near East/Mediterranean; copper forms (e.g. Akjoujt) resemble Iberian/North African types; iron and copper may start almost simultaneously, undercutting a local copper→iron pathway. Proposed routes: Carthage/trans-Sahara (problem: Carthage’s regular iron use only 3rd c. BC — after some West African iron); Middle Nile/Meroë; Ethiopia/Red Sea; Indian Ocean. Absence of prior low-temperature pyrotechnologies is used against pure local invention (Wertime; Craddock via Mapunda).

## Position B — Independent invention

Lack of convincing external route evidence; distinctiveness of sub-Saharan smelting (e.g. slag-tapping furnace pits); ethnographic/archaeological technological dexterity implying proprietorship not mere copying; critique of universal pyrotechnology models as imports from Iranian ethnography; early contested dates cited as possible local deep antiquity (Andah; Schmidt; Mapunda; van Grunderbeek; Zangato & Holl — all attributed).

## Position C — Methodological caution (cross-cutting)

Until early dates meet unambiguous charcoal–smelting association, old-wood control, tight SDs, and clear calibration, treat them as **tentative**. Prefer OSL/TL on furnaces, regional ceramic sequences, rigorous reporting (Mapunda; Killick; Chirikure).

## Relation to Bantu Expansion

Diffusion models sometimes package iron with [[bantu-expansion|Bantu]] demic spread from a West African centre near Taruga/Cameroon. Mapunda and de Maret (Ch. 43) stress **missing ceramic links** between West Africa, Nile, and sub-equatorial regions and **no sound linguistic evidence for metallurgy in initial Proto-Bantu** (de Maret & Nsuka) — iron + farming + language is **not** a single proven package (four-way non-identity).

## Curator’s Note

*(Curatorial, not wiki adjudication.)* The handbook leaves the dispute open while hardening chronological hygiene. Vault pages should keep iron-origin claims **attributed** and date-stamped; do not write “Africans independently invented iron” or “iron was only diffused” in wiki voice.
