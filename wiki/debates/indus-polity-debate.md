## Positions

### Position 1 — Stateless, heterarchical, "corporate" civilization (Possehl)

Gregory Possehl argues, in the strongest form the discipline has articulated it, that the
Indus (Harappan) Civilization achieved urban, large-scale sociocultural complexity
**without** the diagnostic markers of an archaic state. He lays out an explicit checklist
of what an archaic state (on the Egyptian/Mesopotamian model) requires — large-scale
economy, ethnic diversity, career specialization, a career bureaucracy, class hierarchy, a
monopoly on legitimate force, strong/centralized kingship, a centralized economy serving
military and diplomatic needs through staff bureaucrats, monumental religious
architecture reinforcing royal legitimacy, and an exclusive elite class — and argues the
Indus record shows none of the two hallmark expressions of it: no temples or monumental
religious architecture, and no monumental royal residences, palaces, or kingly tombs
anywhere in the civilization's ~1,000,000 km² extent. He explicitly concedes there is "no
full consensus" even on the abstract definition of an archaic state.

Possehl imports two bodies of comparative political theory to characterize what the Indus
was instead of a state: **heterarchy** (R. Ehrenreich, C. Crumley, and J. Levy, 1995) —
archaic complex societies organized on lines other than a single centralized hierarchy,
with power distributed and any kingship weak or absent ("firsts among equals") — and the
**corporate/exclusionary** distinction (R. Blanton et al. 1996), under which archaic
states such as Egypt and Mesopotamia exemplify the exclusionary, centralized pole while
Possehl classifies the Indus Civilization as "more corporate than exclusionary" and "more
of a heterarchy than a hierarchy." On this reading, Indus political integration ran
through councils rather than kings: Possehl speculatively reconstructs (explicitly
flagged by him as "I can imagine…") a nested system of civic-level councils, regional
("Domain"-level) councils, and possibly a "supreme Indus Council," with leadership
determined by age, gender, and ideological standing rather than hereditary rule, and
notes a "marked distrust in government… especially strong, centralized government" as a
plausible Indus political value. He draws a functional analogy between the absence of
monumental religion and the absence of monumental kingship: in the absence of both, "the
Indus ideology emerges as of prime importance" as the integrative substitute for
centralized coercion — likening "being a good Harappan" to "being a good Marxist" in the
USSR, i.e., ideological rather than institutional or coercive integration. He extends this
same anti-monumentalist, "no coercive elite display" reading to specific features often
read as state apparatus: the elevated "citadel" mounds at Mohenjo-daro, Harappa,
Kalibangan, Dholavira, and Lothal were not primarily defensive — at Mohenjo-daro the
retaining wall contained fill for an elevated civic/ritual platform (the Great Bath
precinct, the "Warehouse") rather than a fortification; the so-called "granaries" at
Mohenjo-daro and Harappa lack any confirmed evidence of grain storage; and the celebrated
steatite "priest-king" sculpture is, on Possehl's reading, a conventional name for an
object with no demonstrated connection to actual priestly or kingly office — governance
form at the Indus cities remains simply unknown, and "corporate form, without kings or
emperors, seems reasonable" as Possehl's own inference, not an established fact. He also
notes Wheeler's own "citadel" terminology imports unwarranted state/military assumptions
into the description of the evidence — calling it "another archaic thought about these
cities."

Possehl finds independent corroboration for a decentralized reading in G. Smith's
statistical (gravity-model plus cluster analysis) study of Mature Harappan sites over 10
ha, which produced spatial units closely matching Possehl's own "Domains" scheme — Smith
termed these units "Harappan polities," a regional rather than singular-state picture.

### Position 1b — Multi-elite city-states without single valley monarchy (Kenoyer 1998)

[[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Jonathan Mark Kenoyer]] (*[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]*, 1998) articulates a related but distinct alternative to both Possehl's heterarchy and the Wheeler–Piggott priest-king model. On Kenoyer's reading, Indus cities were governed by **competing elite classes** — landowners, merchants, and ritual specialists — who shared ideology and economy rather than by a single absolute ruling dynasty. He treats the major cities as **city-states** (or peer polities) rather than twin capitals of one valley-wide empire; he finds no evidence for a single hereditary monarch ruling the entire Indus system. Walls and gateways, in his analysis, function as much for **trade control and civic definition** as for military defense; craft production of seals, stoneware bangles, and standardized chert weights is read as elite-controlled status technology rather than proof of a Mesopotamian-style palace economy. The absence of palaces, royal tombs, and warfare iconography is for Kenoyer evidence against exclusionary kingship, but the presence of planned cities, massive platforms, and craft monopolies is evidence of **effective political power** distributed among urban elites — closer to a multi-centered archaic-state spectrum than to Possehl's stronger "civilization without the state" formulation. Kenoyer and Possehl thus share anti-Wheeler ground (no priest-kings, no Aryan military empire) while differing on whether "state" vocabulary remains useful for city-level organization. This is Kenoyer's attributed position from the 1998 synthesis, not a field consensus finding.

### Position 2 — The older state-model / "priest-king citadel" reading (Wheeler, Piggott; earlier Marshall paradigm complications)

The rival reading, articulated in the 1940s–50s by Mortimer [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]
and Stuart Piggott (the "Wheeler-Piggott paradigm"), reinterpreted the same excavated
evidence — above all the elevated "citadel" mounds at Harappa and Mohenjo-daro — as
defensive and administrative structures built for "priest-kings" ruling on the model of
Sumerian city-states, explicitly modeled on Wheeler's own understanding of
Mesopotamian temple/Priest-King administration. Piggott's version, in *Prehistoric India*,
described "a state ruled by priest-kings… two main seats of government," casting Mohenjo-
daro and Harappa as "twin capitals" of a single centralized polity. Wheeler went further
and raised, without fully committing to, the possibility that the wide territorial extent
of the civilization was "the product of something more forcible than peaceful
penetration" — i.e., that Indus expansion involved military conquest — while conceding
that "the military element does not loom large amongst the extant remains" and that
fortification-type evidence is confined to the citadel mounds, not the lower cities.
Wheeler and Piggott's broader civilizational characterization (uniformity read as
centralized regimentation rather than shared culture: "dull, a trifle lacking in the
stimulus of individuality," "astonishing sameness… isolation") supplied the interpretive
frame in which the citadel-as-fortress and priest-king readings made sense: a civilization
governed from the top by a small, centralized, quasi-theocratic authority.

This reading itself revised — and, per Possehl, regressed toward — the earlier position of
Sir John [[marshall-john|Marshall]], who had described Mohenjo-daro and Harappa as
peaceful mercantile centers with no evidence of kingship, warfare, or theocratic rule,
attributing their uniformity to shared "common property" technologies and ideas
circulating across a wider ancient world rather than to conquest or centralized rule;
Possehl notes the Wheeler-Piggott move returns strikingly close to the "Indo-Sumerian"
framing Marshall himself had explicitly abandoned in 1926.

### Position 1c — City-states with "vertical integration" and a differentiated society (Wright 2010)

[[wright-rita-p|Rita Wright]] (*[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]*, 2010) develops the city-state reading further and couples it to an explicit argument about social differentiation. Following Kenoyer (1997a) — whom she credits as the first to apply the "city-state" concept to the Indus — she treats the major cities as politically independent, agriculturally near-self-sufficient centres integrated with hinterlands and networked through shared material culture, and estimates (via Kenoyer 1997a) city-state hinterlands at 100,000–170,000 km², with the closest cities (Harappa–Ganweriwala) some 280 km apart — vastly larger and more dispersed than Mesopotamian city-states (20–25 km apart) or Aztec ones. She reaches for Central-Place Theory (Christaller) and Kevin Lynch's "practical" vs. "organic" city typology (classing Indus cities as "practical" — autonomous, undifferentiated sectors), and argues the lack of a *uniform* nonresidential/public building plan across Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, and Dholavira (unlike standardized Mesopotamian temple plans) points to **localized civic authorities rather than one shared religious/administrative authority**. Walls and gateways, on her reading, imply civic control (community- or ruler-imposed), not military fortification; the "granaries" of Harappa and Mohenjo-daro are unproven as grain stores (Kenoyer et al. 1999 found no grain-processing residues), and Wheeler's "workmen's quarters"/semi-servile-labour reading is unsupported — though the massive construction and controlled access still imply a recognized civic authority.

Drawing on [[trigger-bruce|Trigger]] (2003) and Charlton & Nichols (1997), Wright favours a **"vertical integration"** model: governance by councils drawing members from multiple social groups, analogous to Stone's (1997) Mesopotamia where "both elites and manufacturing were firmly embedded in residential neighbourhoods." She reads Indus residential neighbourhoods — mixed house sizes, amenities, and access to prestige goods — as consistent with (not proof of) such council-based, socially diverse governance. Crucially, Wright argues **against** the older view (which she attributes to D. Miller 1985) that the civilization's apparent homogeneity reflected sanctions concealing inequality: on her reading status differences were *visible* in the differential value of artifacts, public amenities, and architecture (see her Chapter 9 analysis — correlation of house scale with statuary, hoards, and rare-material access at Mohenjo-daro). She explicitly rejects the "two-tiered society dominated by a single individual" model for which the [[priest-king-statue|"Priest-King"]] statue served as "the idealized signature." Like Possehl and Kenoyer she finds **no basis to proclaim kingship** (self-glorifying ruler iconography is "totally lacking"), but she emphasizes ranked social diversity over Possehl's stronger "civilization without the state." Political integration across the great inter-city distances, she argues, was maintained chiefly through standardized material culture (weights, seals, script, pottery and figurine styles) and interregional trade rather than centralized coercion — and she notes there is no evidence for centrally controlled irrigation, explicitly refuting a Wittfogel-style hydraulic-despotism model. This is Wright's attributed 2010 synthesis, convergent with Kenoyer on city-states while adding the differentiated-society and vertical-integration arguments.

## Shared factual ground

No excavated Indus palace or royal tomb has been identified anywhere in the civilization;
no site has produced unambiguous evidence of a monarchic administrative center or a
written king-list, royal inscription, or dynastic record (consistent with the [[indus-script-decipherment-debate|Indus script]] itself never having been shown to encode any
administrative or historical text). The Great Bath at Mohenjo-daro is elevated and
spatially set apart from the rest of the city under both readings, and is treated as
elite or ritual space by both — its function (secular-elite civic space vs. religious-
monumental structure) remains itself contested. Both sides agree the "citadel" mounds are
architecturally distinctive, elevated, and walled; they disagree entirely on why.

## Curator's note

*(2026-07-10)* This debate sits at the intersection of two separately contested
questions — what counts as a "state" in archaic-complexity theory generally, and what the
absence of palaces/temples in the Indus record actually licenses an archaeologist to
infer. Possehl's heterarchy/corporate framing is the dominant position in the Possehl 2002
source and is presented in its strongest form above; Kenoyer 1998 (Position 1b) supplies a
live specialist alternative that keeps city-state / multi-elite vocabulary while rejecting
valley-wide monarchy. The older Wheeler-Piggott state model is presented as Possehl (and
Kenoyer) relay it — as a superseded but historically important rival, not a live position
with a current articulate defender in these sources. Absence-of-evidence arguments (no
palace found) are inherently asymmetric: they support decentralized readings but cannot
rule out a state whose seat of power has simply not been excavated (Ganweriwala, one of
the five largest Indus cities, remains wholly unexcavated). `resolution_status: open`
reflects that the *general* question of Indus governance remains unsettled in the
discipline, even though the specific 1950s "priest-king citadel as fortress" reading has
few current defenders.
