# Was the Uruk Expansion a World System?

A live interpretive dispute over whether the [[uruk-expansion|Uruk expansion]] (later 4th
millennium BCE) was a structurally **asymmetric** [[core-periphery|core–periphery]] system
favoring the southern Mesopotamian core — [[algaze-guillermo|Algaze's]]
[[informal-empire|informal empire]] / [[world-systems-analysis|world system]] — or a
**symmetric** set of trade-diaspora relationships in which independent, comparably complex
peripheral societies interacted with intrusive Uruk communities on near-equal terms. It is
one of the central methodological test cases for whether
[[world-systems-analysis|world-systems]] models can be applied to pre-capitalist,
prehistoric contexts at all. Recorded here as positions; **not adjudicated**. A dated
curator's note closes the page.

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

All parties accept: (a) southern Uruk material culture (bevel-rim bowls, cylinder seals,
numerical tablets, tripartite architecture) appears at many peripheral sites across
Syro-Mesopotamia and the highlands in the later 4th millennium; (b) some intrusive
settlements are unmistakably southern implants ([[habuba-kabira|Habuba Kabira]] the
clearest); (c) indigenous Late Chalcolithic societies existed in the periphery before and
during contact; (d) the network ended around 3100 cal BCE. The dispute is over what these
facts *mean* — the **direction and degree of power asymmetry**, and the **prior complexity**
of the periphery. Where Algaze reads the same enclave-at-a-trade-node as an instrument of
core dominance, Stein reads it as one segregated partner in a symmetric exchange.

## Position 1 — Algaze: Asymmetric World System / Informal Empire

*(Attributed — [[algaze-guillermo|Algaze]], [[algaze-uruk-world-system-2005|1993; 2nd ed.
2005]].)* Uruk-period interactions between the alluvium and its periphery were structurally
**asymmetrical**, favoring the southern core over the long run — even though direct
economic proof can never be recovered archaeologically. Evidence marshaled: **inversely
correlated trajectories** (southern cities grow as peripheral centers like
[[tell-brak|Brak]] contract at the height of Uruk expansion); **locational logic**
(intrusive settlements sit at [[gateway-community|route gateways]], suited to channel
resources south); **asymmetric trade content** (periphery exports raw/semi-processed goods,
core exports labor-intensive processed goods, driving self-amplifying urban growth in the
south); **organizational advantage** — "technologies of the intellect" (after Goody):
multi-seal sealing and Uruk IV pictographic tablets versus simpler peripheral single-seal
systems; and **emulation directionality** (peripheral elites adopted Uruk iconography and
glyptic, not the reverse). Algaze modifies Wallerstein to a **multi-core** world system
(competing alluvial polities), since [[informal-empire|informal empire]] presupposes a
single center. His rejoinder to critics: parity findings are **local** conditions at the
system's edge; power "decays with distance" but the system remains asymmetric *in
aggregate*, and the critics cannot explain why Uruk forms were the ones emulated or why
Uruk colonies persisted for centuries while the reverse never occurred.

## Position 2 — Stein: Distance-Parity / Trade-Diaspora Symmetry

*(Attributed — [[stein-gil|Gil Stein]], excavator of [[hacinebi|Hacınebi Tepe]];
*[[stein-rethinking-world-systems-1999|Rethinking World-Systems]]*, 1999.)* Under premodern
transport conditions, "distance and increasing transport costs impair the ability of cores
to exercise economic and political dominance in far-away peripheries," so relationships in
distant peripheries will be "largely **symmetrical**" — Stein's
[[distance-parity|**distance-parity model**]], in which core hegemonic power "decays with
distance." He designed the [[stein-hacinebi-study|Hacınebi excavations]] explicitly as "a
test of the applicability of world-systems approaches to the study of the Uruk Expansion,"
building the test on paired eight-point archaeological correlate sets (production
continuity vs. reorganization, symmetric vs. asymmetric exchange, no vs. sudden complexity
increase). There he distinguished a segregated Uruk [[trade-diaspora|**trade diaspora**]]
community coexisting for 300–500 years alongside an indigenous population that was "equally
complex, socially distinct, and economically self-sufficient": distinct diet and butchery,
distinct craft production, and two parallel administrative systems (indigenous **stamp**
seals versus the Uruk quarter's **cylinder** seals, bullae, and tablet — with INAA sourcing
the Uruk sealing clays partly to the Susa area). Crucially, the local society was **already
complex before contact**, so the contact brought no reorganization of production and no
complexity jump. He concludes Hacınebi "failed to produce evidence for the sorts of economic
and power asymmetries presupposed by world-systems models," and that asymmetry is only *one*
point on a spectrum — pressing further, that anthropology should "slough off" the
world-system "terminological shell" entirely. Stein embeds Hacınebi in a **distance
gradient** — coercive at the large near-home Syrian colonies ([[habuba-kabira|Habuba
Kabira]], Tell Sheikh Hassan), autonomous at the far outposts (Hacınebi, Godin V) — which is
also the concession on which Algaze's rejoinder turns.

## Position 3 — Frangipane: Pre-existing Indigenous Complexity

*(Attributed — Marcela Frangipane, excavator of Arslan Tepe.)* Algaze's original (1993)
reading of Arslan Tepe VIA's elite redistributive "palace" complex as a *response* to Uruk
contact was wrong: excavation of the underlying **Period VII** levels (the massive
tripartite Building XXIX, mass-produced bowls, discarded sealings in storerooms) shows
these institutions were "the culmination of local developments that were underway before
the onset of contacts between the Malatya area and the Uruk world." With Stein, Frangipane
argues Algaze substantially **underestimated the scale and social complexity of Late
Chalcolithic peripheral societies** generally (cited alongside Emberling et al., Oates,
Schwartz, Wilkinson).

## Algaze's Concession and the Third-Party Arbiters

In 2005 Algaze conceded "much of this criticism is well taken" on peripheral complexity, and
made six specific self-corrections (including accepting Frangipane's Period VII roots for
Arslan Tepe). But he maintained that peripheral societies *as a group* were still "no match
for" Uruk societies and that no regional survey compels reading them as more than "complex
chiefdoms" (Frangipane and Oates disagreeing — itself a live sub-dispute). He cites two
**third-party arbiters** — [[matthews-roger|Roger Matthews]] (2003) and Philip Kohl (2001) —
as agreeing that Stein and Frangipane "moved too far in the opposite direction," privileging
peripheral developments while neglecting the south; "I agree," Algaze writes.

[[matthews-roger|Matthews's]] own reasoning (attributed,
*[[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Archaeology of Mesopotamia]]* 2003) makes the arbiter
case concrete. Granting the periphery's sophistication, he still **sides with Algaze's
asymmetry reading**: [[uruk|Uruk-Warka's]] sheer scale (4–5× any other Lower Mesopotamian
settlement) outweighs the single-site periphery evidence, and any core→periphery colonial
relationship is inherently asymmetric absent counter-evidence of *peripheral* colonists
settling in the Uruk heartland — of which there is none. He finds Stein's picture puzzling on
its own terms: centuries of tolerant but rigidly bounded coexistence with **no**
material-culture blending is hard to credit, echoing Holly Pittman's question of why there is
"virtually no evidence at Hacınebi for the interaction of the two traditions." He also rejects
Steinkeller's proposed analogy between the Uruk expansion and the later Old Assyrian
Kültepe-Kaneš trade colony — where the pattern is *inverted* (the Assyrian traders acculturated
almost entirely to local material culture, took local wives, and are visible mainly through
their cuneiform archives) — as differing "totally … in kind and degree." His broader
methodological verdict is that both Algaze and Stein lean too heavily on untested historical
analogy, and that the Uruk phenomenon — "a purely archaeological phenomenon" (Postgate,
Steinkeller) — should be approached "on its own terms."

## Resolution Status

`open` (as of 2005). Algaze did not abandon the asymmetric world-system framework but
**absorbed** Stein's distance-parity insight as a qualification (asymmetry decays with
distance, persists in aggregate); Stein and Frangipane hold that the framework
over-reads symmetric coexistence as domination. The underlying question — whether
[[world-systems-analysis|world-systems]] vocabulary can travel to pre-capitalist
prehistory without smuggling in modern dependency assumptions — connects this debate to the
broader [[world-systems-analysis|precapitalist world-systems]] dispute (Kohl; Edens & Kohl;
Frank & Gills) and to the [[modernization-vs-dependency-world-systems-debate]].

**Curator's note (2026-07-15):** On the ingested evidence, the dispute is partly a matter of
scale of analysis — Stein and Frangipane argue from single well-excavated peripheral sites
(Hacınebi, Arslan Tepe) where parity/local-complexity is demonstrable, while Algaze argues
from the aggregate settlement and exchange pattern. These are not straightforwardly
contradictory: local symmetry at the far edge is compatible with system-wide asymmetry, as
Algaze's "decay with distance" modification concedes. The genuinely unresolved core is
whether *aggregate asymmetry* is demonstrated or merely inferred from the core's own growth
— a claim that, by Algaze's own admission, rests on evidence that "can never be recovered
archaeologically." No wiki-voice verdict.
