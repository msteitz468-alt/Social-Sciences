# Origins of Hereditary Inequality — Created vs. Automatic

How did permanent, heritable social inequality first arise? The dispute is over the
*kind* of explanation required, not over any single dataset.

## Position 1 — Materialist / adaptationist

*(Attributed.)* Hereditary inequality is a fairly predictable outcome of material
conditions: the adoption of agriculture and the possibility of **storable surplus**
allow some households to accumulate and others to fall into debt; **population pressure**
and environmental **circumscription** (Carneiro) force subordination where escape is
impossible. On this reading inequality is an adaptive or structural consequence — once
the conditions are present, stratification tends to follow.

## Position 1b — Lenski ecological-evolutionary (technology–surplus)

*(Attributed — [[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]], *[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]* 1966; [[ecological-evolutionary-theory]].)* Material near-equality where little surplus (hunting-gathering; largely need-based distribution); rising institutionalized and eventually **hereditary** power and privilege as technology and surplus grow (simple → advanced horticultural → agrarian peak). Second [[laws-of-distribution|law of distribution]]: power governs nearly all surplus. Not pure “automatic agriculture,” but a strong **technology/surplus gradient** with secondary variation (environment, military participation, political cycle). Industrial **reversal** is a later twist, not a denial of the pre-industrial rise.

## Position 2 — Agency / social-logic (Flannery & Marcus)

*(Attributed — [[flannery-marcus-creation-inequality-2012|Flannery & Marcus 2012]].)*
Inequality is **created**, not triggered. The raw material is the **social logic** that
egalitarian societies already possess — clan organization, ancestor veneration, notions
of debt, marriage payments, and the distinction between senior and junior descent lines.
Ambitious agents exploit these to convert **achieved renown** into **heritable rank**.
Agriculture is neither necessary (some foragers, e.g. the Nootka, built rank without it)
nor sufficient (many farming societies stayed egalitarian for millennia). Crucially,
inequality is **often resisted and reversed** — through leveling ideologies, assassination
of aggrandizers, or collapse of chiefly lines — so the record shows cycling, not a
one-way ladder.

## Three Named Scenarios (the Tibeto-Burman cases)

Within Position 2's "how does it actually happen?" question, Flannery and Marcus lay out
three competing scenarios for how achievement-based Kachin/Naga society first produced
hereditary rank — offered as alternative, non-adjudicated hypotheses:

1. **External intermarriage (Edmund Leach).** Kachin chiefs who controlled the jade mines
   received noble Shan brides from the more complex neighboring Shan state over several
   generations, imitating Shan hierarchy. Its strength is documentary grounding; its
   weakness (per Flannery & Marcus) is that it requires external intervention.
2. **Internal cosmological reinterpretation (Jonathan Friedman).** One lineage convinces
   the others that the village spirit (*nat*) is *its* ancestor — the shift from "they must
   have pleased the *nats*" to "they must be descended from higher *nats* than we are."
   Flannery and Marcus warn against an oversimplified derivative reading that treats
   **competitive feasting alone** as the cause: "if feasting were all it took... there
   would have been no achievement-based societies left for anthropologists to study."
3. **Debt slavery (Flannery & Marcus's own "third scenario").** Bride-price obligations and
   loans, backed by a social-substitutability rule holding a debtor's whole lineage liable,
   drove many Kachin into bondage — Leach estimated up to half the Kachin (*mayam*) were
   slaves of the chiefs and headmen who extended the loans. (The Kachin word *hka* meant
   both "debt" and "feud.")

## Position 3 — Archaeological multi-process (Kirch 1984, Polynesia)

*(Attributed — [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]], *[[kirch-evolution-polynesian-chiefdoms-1984|The Evolution of the Polynesian Chiefdoms]]* 1984.)* For Polynesia, Kirch rejects both pure functionalist invention of hierarchy (chiefs as redistributors of dispersed resources, or irrigation managers under population pressure) and single-factor material triggers. **Hereditary chieftainship (*‘ariki*, *mana*, *tapu*, first-fruits)** was already present in Ancestral Polynesian Society; colonizers carried pyramidal structure and differential access. Contact-era stratification spectra (minimal to elaborately stratified) are **transformations** of that baseline under interacting processes: **demography** (important but not mechanical carrying-capacity pressure), **ecological opportunity hierarchies** (wet vs dry paths; island size/isolation; hazard regimes), **political surplus demand and intensification**, **competition/warfare and usurpation** (junior collaterals, *toa* vs *ariki* claims to *mana*), and **symbolic creativity** irreducible to ecology. No single anthropological paradigm explains the sequences; factors are partial and should be synthetic rather than competitive. Empirical core: Tonga, Hawaiʻi, Easter Island diachronic cases (NZ and other sequences used comparatively but not fully treated as case studies). Overlaps Position 2 on agency and reversibility (oppression–revolt cycles; incomplete break with kinship morality) and Position 1 on demography/ecology as real constraints without automatic causation. See [[hereditary-rank]], [[state-formation]], [[chiefly-cycling]], [[hawaiians]].

## Position 4 — Household competition + residual elites (Bogucki 1999)

*(Attributed — [[bogucki-peter|Peter Bogucki]], *[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|The Origins of Human Society]]* 1999, esp. Ch.6. Synthesis position; research horizon late 1990s. Do not adjudicate.)*

| Claim cluster | Bogucki’s stance (as of 1999) |
|---|---|
| **Unit of analysis** | Competing **self-interested households** under risk and uncertainty — not system-level “prime movers” (trade control, irrigation management, craft specialization alone) |
| **Transegalitarian frame** | Adopts Clark & Blake / [[hayden-brian|Hayden]] **[[transegalitarian]]**: beyond forager egalitarian constraints, not yet ossified hereditary hierarchy; leadership not yet fully transformed into power |
| **Aggrandizers necessary but not sufficient** | [[aggrandizer|Aggrandizers]] (Hayden) seek status, clients, wealth; if everyone could succeed as aggrandizer, inequality would not structure |
| **Drop-down / residual elites (load-bearing)** | Synergy with a larger segment **dropping behind** demographically and economically (developmental-cycle failures, debt, risk) — rocket/exhaust metaphor: aggrandizers rise by pushing against those falling; “residual elites” framing rather than pure rising-elite ladder |
| **Household developmental cycle** | Fortes-type expansion → fission → decline makes temporary asymmetries normal; permanence requires cumulative failure across generations |
| **Arenas** | Factions and hamlets as suprahousehold theaters before public-architecture thresholds mark more formal hierarchy |
| **Relation to stages** | Rejects premature “tribe/chiefdom” labels for many Late Neolithic / Formative cases; hierarchical pre-state polities treated as relatively unstable (→ state or collapse) in Ch.7 preview |
| **Europe mechanism example** | Animal traction / [[secondary-products-revolution]] dynamics (cattle loaning) as one regional path to generational downward spiral |

**Relation to other positions:** Closest to Position 2 (agency) and Hayden competitive-feasting, but shifts emphasis from cosmological manipulation or feasting alone to **household economy + residual drop-down**. Explicitly rejects single-factor materialist prime movers (overlaps Position 1’s agriculture-surplus correlation only as background condition, not mechanism). Complements Kirch multi-process by offering a general Holocene agrarian template rather than a Polynesian ancestral-rank baseline. See [[household-archaeology]], [[aggrandizer]], [[transegalitarian]], [[pleistocene-band-society]] (breakdown of band norms as incubator).

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides accept that hereditary inequality appears repeatedly and independently in the
archaeological record, that it is correlated (imperfectly) with agriculture and
sedentism, and that chiefly societies "cycle." The materialist reads the correlation as
near-causation; Flannery and Marcus read the many exceptions and reversals as proof that
human decisions, not material triggers alone, do the work. Kirch’s Polynesian process
position accepts ancestral rank *before* the ethnographic endpoints and treats material
and agentive factors as jointly necessary partial causes. Bogucki accepts agriculture-linked
household reorganization as enabling but insists the *mechanism* is competitive household
success/failure under risk, not automatic surplus or a single managerial technology.
