# Consensus vs Conflict (Two Faces of Society) Debate

## Positions

### Integration / consensus (sociological "Utopian")

Order is conceived primarily as value consensus and normative integration. Differences of interest exist but are subordinated to shared norms. In mid-century form this image is associated with structural-functionalism (Parsons and related programs — primary texts not yet fully ingested here). Conflict tends to be treated as deviance, strain, or system failure rather than as a normal product of structure.

### Conflict / coercion (sociological "Rationalist")

Order is conceived primarily as the precarious reconciliation of opposed interests by constraint and domination. Agreements of value are real but secondary coatings. The classical carrier is the conflict tradition from [[marx-karl|Marx]] ([[historical-materialism]], [[class-struggle]]) through elite theory ([[circulation-of-elites]]) into postwar [[conflict-theory]].

### Dual-heuristic (Dahrendorf)

[[dahrendorf-ralf|Dahrendorf]] (*[[dahrendorf-class-class-conflict-1959|Class and Class Conflict]]*, Ch. V) argues society is Janus-headed: both faces are valid *heuristics* generating different propositions; sociology errs when it treats only one as real. His research program develops the coercion face systematically (authority, quasi-groups, interest groups) without denying the integration face. See [[two-faces-of-society]].

### Synthetic stratification (Lenski)

[[lenski-gerhard|Lenski]] (*[[lenski-power-and-privilege-1966|Power and Privilege]]*, 1966) maps modern functionalism to the long **conservative thesis** on inequality and conflict theory to the **radical antithesis**, then offers an “emerging synthesis” ([[ecological-evolutionary-theory]]): need-based sharing for producer survival plus power over surplus; coercion emphasized where surplus is large, consensus/utility more where it is small; Ch. 13 scores three issues conservative, two radical, three mixed by societal type. Distinct from Dahrendorf’s dual *heuristics*: Lenski builds one comparative theory with type-variable weightings rather than two equal analytic faces.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

All sides grant that both norms and power exist in modern societies. They disagree on *primacy*, on whether conflict is endemic or residual, and on which image should organize research programs.

## Resolution status

`open` — the dualism remains a standard teaching and theory-construction frame; later multi-dimensional and practice theories partly dissolve it without a field-wide verdict. No wiki-voice adjudication.
