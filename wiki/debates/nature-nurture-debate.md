# Nature–Nurture Debate

The long-running dispute over how far human differences — in body, mind, temperament, and
achievement — are set by inherited constitution ("nature") versus conditions of
development, culture, and history ("nurture"). It runs through anthropology, biology,
psychology, and sociology and recurs in new idioms every generation; it is **open**, and
the sophisticated modern position holds the original either/or framing to be
ill-posed.

## Positions

**Hereditarian.** Group and individual differences reflect a largely fixed inherited
constitution. In its classical-era form (Galton, and the race scientists who read the
cephalic index and later the IQ score as markers of innate racial capacity) this licensed
ranking populations and the eugenic policy that followed. See [[scientific-racism]].

**Environmentalist.** The same differences reflect the conditions under which people
develop — nutrition, upbringing, schooling, culture, history. [[boas-franz|Boas]]
advanced the strongest early empirical version: if head form itself — the supposed
bedrock racial marker — shifts in the American-born children of immigrants
([[boas-changes-in-bodily-form-study]]), then traits far more plausibly plastic (mind,
temperament, "intelligence" as measured) cannot be assumed innate. Boas also argued that
intelligence-test differences between groups tracked cultural exposure and schooling, not
capacity (citing Klineberg, Garth against Brigham's hereditarian reading).

**Interactionist.** Heredity and environment are not separable, additively-combined
causes: a trait is the outcome of a developmental process in which both are constitutive.
This is the position most working scientists now hold, and it dissolves rather than
settles the original question.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

That both heredity and environment influence human traits is not in dispute; what divides
the positions is **which differences, at what scale, are being explained**, and whether
"heritability" within a population licenses any claim about differences *between*
populations (it does not, by itself — a standing technical point). Boas's own care is
instructive: he distinguished the **genetic type** from its **physiological/ecotype**
expression, insisted pigmentation showed *no* environmental plasticity even as head form
did, and refused to name a mechanism.

## Historiography and Recurrence

The debate is not one episode but a series: the classical-era race-science and eugenics
form; the culture-and-personality environmentalism of the Boasians; the postwar
consensus; the sociobiology wars of the 1970s (E. O. Wilson and critics) and the
hereditarian-IQ controversies (Jensen 1969; *The Bell Curve* 1994). Boas's immigrant
study sits at the debate's origin as the founding empirical challenge to hereditarian
typology — and its own contested reanalysis (Sparks & Jantz 2002 vs. Gravlee et al. 2003)
shows the dispute reopening even over its charter evidence. Related: the
[[race-and-culture-debate]] and the [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]].
