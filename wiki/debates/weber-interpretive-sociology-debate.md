# What Kind of Thinker Was Weber? The *Economy and Society* Translation and Reception Debate

A dispute over the identity of [[weber-max|Max Weber]]'s project — is he first a sociologist and founder of an "[[interpretive-sociology|interpretive sociology]]," or a political economist whose Anglophone reputation was reshaped by translation? The dispute is inseparable from the textual history of *[[weber-economy-society-1921|Economy and Society]]*, and Keith Tribe's 2019 translation of the 1921 instalment is the occasion for its most recent statement.

## Why the Text Makes the Debate

The book known as *Economy and Society* is a posthumous composite. Only "Part I" (the four chapters translated by Tribe) was drafted in 1919–20 and proofed by Weber; the rest was assembled by **Marianne Weber** from pre-1914 drafts and published in 1922. Talcott **Parsons** translated Part I as *The Theory of Social and Economic Organization* (1947); **Roth and Wittich**'s 1968 *Economy and Society: An Outline of Interpretive Sociology* (based on Winckelmann's 1956 German edition) carried the composite whole into English. Because Weber left no plan and none of his manuscripts survive, *how* the text is presented substantially determines what "Weber" is taken to have argued.

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

All parties accept: Part I is the only portion Weber finished; Marianne Weber added the "Part I" label and the 1922 subtitle; Parsons converted Weber's large-font-statement / small-font-"casuistry" layout into continuous narrative with interpolated subheadings drawn from Marianne Weber's summary; Weber himself called his contribution "a sociology" from November 1913; and Weber described his own discipline for most of his career as political economy / *Sozialökonomik*. What each side *makes* of these facts differs.

## Positions

**A. Weber the interpretive sociologist (Parsons; the postwar canon).** Parsons's translation and long introduction organized Weber's chapters as "Weber's Methodology of Social Science," "Weber's Economic Sociology," and "The Institutionalization of Authority," presenting Weber as the theorist of subjectively meaningful action at the centre of a sociological canon with Marx and Durkheim. Winckelmann's 1956 subtitle *Grundriß der verstehenden Soziologie* and the 1968 English subtitle *An Outline of Interpretive Sociology* institutionalized the label. On this view Weber founds the interpretive-sociological tradition.

**B. Weber the political economist; "interpretive sociology" as reception artefact (Hennis; Tribe).** Tribe argues Parsons's translation "tends to conceal what Weber was doing." His case: (1) Parsons elaborated Weber's compressed neutral terms, blurring the conceptual structure — rendering *[[domination-herrschaft|Herrschaft]]* as "authority" and *Verband* as "corporate group" hides that Herrschaft is the organizing principle of every organisation; (2) Parsons emphasized structure over *process*, against Weber's own preference for *Vergesellschaftung* over *Gesellschaft* and *Wirtschaften* over *Wirtschaft*; (3) calling Weber "primarily a sociologist" ignores that he identified as a political economist and organized the *Grundriss der Sozialökonomik* handbook for a decade; (4) Parsons omits that handbook context; (5) discarding Weber's page layout obscures his didactic structure. Wilhelm Hennis's denial that Weber was ever "a sociologist" of the American kind reconnects him to classical political-economic questions of *Lebensführung*. A telling detail: the Parsons/Henderson translation was commissioned through **Friedrich Hayek's** circle of émigré-economist translations, not by American sociology — placing Weber, in 1930s Britain, among economists.

**C. The "late sociology" periodization (Schluchter).** Wolfgang Schluchter treats Chapter 1's apparatus as Weber's *late* interpretive sociology, distinct from an "earlier" phase epitomized by the [[protestant-ethic-thesis|Protestant Ethic]] essays. Tribe reads this as conceding the dubious premise of an "early Weberian sociology," and prefers to trace continuity through *Lebensführung* from the Protestant Ethic onward.

A related political sub-dispute: **Wolfgang Mommsen** (*Max Weber und die deutsche Politik*, 1959) resisted the Americanized "founding father" image and reconnected Weber to German conservative-liberal politics; **Winckelmann**'s editorial reordering (shown by the MWG editors to rest on a compositional thesis "without foundation") had reinforced the international-social-science reading.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-08)

This wiki records the dispute without adjudicating. On the wiki's own terms, Weber's Part-I text supports *both* readings: it is unambiguously a work of conceptual **sociology** (Ch. 1 defines the discipline) *and* the third section of an economics handbook whose theme was modern capitalism. The prudent editorial line, followed across the Weber pages here, is to attribute the "interpretive sociology" label to the Parsons–Winckelmann reception rather than assert it in wiki voice, and to use Tribe's renderings ("rule," "organisation," "sociation") while noting the Parsons alternatives. `resolution_status: open`.
