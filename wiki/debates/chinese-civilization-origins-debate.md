# Origins of Chinese Civilization: Monocentric vs. Multi-Regional

How, and from where, did Neolithic farming and then civilization arise in China? Over the
20th century the answer moved through four successive positions. The dispute is recorded here
as positions in their strongest form, not adjudicated in wiki voice. It is unusual in that one
scholar — [[chang-kwang-chih|K. C. Chang]] — argued the third position forcefully in 1963 and
then, as evidence accumulated, moved toward the fourth himself.

## Position 1 — Western diffusion ("ex oriente lux")

J. G. Andersson, who discovered [[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]] painted pottery in 1921, related
it to the Anau and Tripolye painted-pottery cultures of Central Asia and southeastern Europe and
surveyed eastern Kansu (Gansu) as the presumed **corridor linking West and East**. On this
reading, painted pottery — and perhaps agriculture and civilization themselves — reached North
China by stimulus diffusion from the Near East, with the Kansu sequence treated as an early,
ancestral stage. Later exponents (Carl W. Bishop) extended the diffusionist reading to Chinese
civilization generally.

## Position 2 — The "Two Culture Theory"

Following the 1928 discovery of [[lungshan-culture|Lungshan]] black pottery at Ch'eng-tzu-yai,
many scholars (Li Chi, Cheng Te-kun, William Watson) held **Yangshao (western, painted-pottery,
possibly Western-derived) and Lungshan (eastern, black-pottery, indigenous)** to be two separate,
geographically exclusive cultures — sometimes correlated with the historian Fu Ssu-nien's
Eastern-Yih / Western-Hsia ethnic scheme.

## Position 3 — North China nuclear-area monocentrism (Chang 1963)

Chang's [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|1963]] synthesis argued against both predecessors.
On his account:

- Farming arose **independently in a single North China [[nuclear-area|nuclear area]]** at the
  confluence of the Huanghe, Fenhe, and Weishui rivers; the East-vs-West-origin question he judged
  "purely academic" given inconclusive evidence, and he rebutted the Kansu corridor specifically —
  the Kansu Yangshao is a *derivative offshoot* of the nuclear area, and the Kansu "eneolithic"
  painted-pottery traditions (Hsin-tien, Ssu-wa, Sha-ching) are **late**, contemporary with
  Shang–Chou, not early. Chang called Andersson's Ch'i-chia-before-Yangshao ordering "completely
  fallacious" and his absolute dates "completely meaningless."
- Yangshao and Lungshan are **two developmental stages of one sequence**, not two cultures of
  different origin — dissolving the Two Culture Theory.
- The [[lungshan-culture|Lungshanoid]] horizon **expanded "explosively" outward** from the nuclear
  area, carrying farming into the eastern lowlands, Manchuria, and South China, and one nuclear-area
  polity became the [[shang-civilization|Shang]]. [[zhou-civilization|Chou]], Eastern Chou, and
  Ch'in/Han conquest were successive further "waves" radiating north-China civilization to the
  peripheries (Ch'u, Wu-Yüeh, Szechwan, Yunnan). Chang explicitly rejected R. Heine-Geldern's
  derivation of the southern Dongson/Tien bronze cultures from Western Asia.

Chang was careful that this was **cultural radiation and elite acculturation, not wholesale
population replacement**, and that local traditions persisted strongly in every region. He also
insisted no single cause — irrigation (contra Steward), technology, ecology — explained the growth,
and closed the book anticipating "the day when new data… will compel me to rewrite this volume
completely anew."

## Position 4 — Regional interaction sphere / multi-regional origins

As regional discoveries multiplied after 1963 — early rice-farming Neolithic cultures of the
Yangtze, and northern and eastern centers such as Hongshan and Liangzhu — the single-nuclear-area
model became untenable. Chang himself recast early China as a set of regional cultures bound in a
mutually stimulating **"interaction sphere,"** and later Chinese archaeology consolidated a broadly
**multi-regional (polycentric)** picture in which the Yellow River core was one of several partly
independent centers.

The position was formalized by **Su Bingqi's "regional systems and local cultural series"**
(*quxi leixing*, early 1980s), which divided the area later encompassed by historical China into
**six relatively stable regional cultural blocs** formed after 10,000 BP, each locally developing and
interacting with the others; **Yan Wenming's "flower and petals" model** (1987) is a competing
multiregional formulation that still privileges the Central Plain as the structural center.
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen (2012)]] give this position its current standard
statement: sociopolitical evolution was **multicentric**, shaped by competition and factionalism
among many contemporaneous polities and by technologies (wheat, sheep/goat/cattle, bronze
metallurgy, the horse-chariot) introduced from western Eurasia. They stress this is **cultural and
technological interaction, not population or language identity** (four-way non-identity), and note
that the origin of Chinese **bronze metallurgy** is itself a live sub-dispute — independent
indigenous invention versus diffusion from the Eurasian steppe (via Qijia/Siba and Xinjiang), with
the authors judging steppe influence "dominant" for the northwest frontier while leaving the
question open. On the Central Plain's weight they are careful: *"We have no intention to assert a
Central Plain-centric perspective… but it is difficult to deny that a core area of sociopolitical
complexity first emerged in the Central Plain."* The related dispute over whether the earliest
Central Plain state ([[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]]) should be named "Xia" is recorded separately at
[[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]].

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

All parties accept the Yangshao and Lungshan/Longshan assemblages, the Erh-li-kang and Anyang Shang
sequences, and the general Neolithic→Bronze Age trajectory. They differ on **whether one hearth
radiated outward (monocentric) or several centers developed in parallel and interacted
(polycentric)**, and on how much weight to give diffusion versus independent local development. The
Position-1 Western-diffusion reading is now generally abandoned; the live modern contrast is
Position 3 (in its "interaction sphere" descendant) versus Position 4.

## Curator's note (2026-07-10)

Chang's 1963 monocentrism is best read as a **historically important, since-superseded** framework
whose evidentiary base (pre-radiocarbon, PRC-report synthesis) it outran within two decades — a case
where the same author documents the supersession. The multi-regional consensus is the mainstream
today, though the relative weight of the Yellow River core remains actively discussed. No wiki-voice
verdict is entered.
