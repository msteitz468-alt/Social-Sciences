# The Reproduction-Schemas Debate

The dispute over what [[marx-karl|Marx]]'s [[reproduction-schemas]] (*[[marx-capital-volume-2-1885|Capital, Volume 2]]*, Part Three) prove about capitalism's viability: whether expanded reproduction can proceed in equilibrium, whether the accumulated portion of surplus-value can be realized within a closed capitalist system, and whether the schemas ground a theory of crisis, imperialism, or final breakdown. The material below is drawn from Ernest Mandel's introduction to the Penguin edition (1978) — a partisan participant's survey; positions are recorded as Mandel reports them, pending primary ingests.

Context: Chapter 21 (expanded reproduction) is an unfinished draft. Per Mandel, the schemas' first serious applications came from Russian Marxists — Tugan-Baranovsky (1894/1901) and Bulgakov (1897); Lenin's 1893 manuscript on the market question circulated only in 1937.

## Positions

**Rosa Luxemburg (*The Accumulation of Capital*, 1913).** The capitalized portion of surplus-value cannot be realized by sales to workers (whose purchasing power derives from capital), to other capitalists (the class cannot enrich itself by internal sales), or to "third persons" whose income derives from surplus-value. Realization therefore requires **non-capitalist markets**; capitalism, by destroying the non-capitalist milieu it depends on, undermines its own conditions of growth — its "absolute limit." This grounds her theory of imperialism. ([[luxemburg-rosa]])

**Contemporary critics (1913).** Bauer (*Die Neue Zeit*), Pannekoek (*Bremer Bürgerzeitung*), Eckstein (*Vorwärts*) reviewed Luxemburg critically. Bauer, with Bukharin, derived proportionality conditions for expanded reproduction and developed Marx's implicit solution to the "unsaleable surplus" problem — periodic transfer of part of Department II's surplus-value to Department I where Department I's organic composition is persistently higher.

**"Neo-harmonicists" (Mandel's label).** Tugan-Baranovsky — "the father of pure disproportionism" per Mandel, and first to add a third department (luxuries/gold) — held crises arise purely from disproportion between branches, so proportionate growth is in principle possible. Hilferding (*Das Finanzkapital*, 1909): "a general cartel regulating total social production and thereby overcoming crises is, in principle, economically imaginable." Bukharin's "state capitalism" variant made a general cartel capable of abolishing crisis-anarchy. Mandel argues these readings mistake Marx's point — that market forces never guarantee harmonious growth.

**Breakdown theory (Zusammenbruchstheorie).** Grossman (1929) converted the falling rate of profit into the sole, mechanical cause of capitalism's final collapse, denying realization/disproportionality problems lie at the root of crises.

**Mandel's adjudication (1978 — a position, not a verdict).** Luxemburg's question is legitimate and unanswered by the schemas alone, but her solution fails at three levels: she poses a "many capitals" problem inside the "capital in general" abstraction; mathematical models (Lange, Koshimura, and others) show full realization is theoretically possible even with rising organic composition; yet she is "fundamentally correct" historically that capitalism was born in and continuously plunders a non-capitalist milieu. Against Grossman, Mandel holds crises of over-production are the mechanism by which the falling rate of profit is periodically overcome via devalorization. He credits Rosdolsky (*The Making of Marx's Capital*, 1977) with the thorough critique of the neo-harmonicist reading.

## Shared ground

All sides accept the two-department apparatus and the simple-reproduction condition I(v+s) = IIc as Marx's; the dispute concerns expanded reproduction, whose chapter Marx left unfinished. Marx's own text supplies ammunition to several sides: he rejects underconsumptionist crisis explanations as "pure tautology" (Ch. 20), yet a marginal note (Ch. 16, inserted by Engels "for future elaboration") states that realization is restricted by the consumption limits of a society whose majority "are always poor and must always remain poor."
