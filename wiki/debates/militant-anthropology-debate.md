# Militant vs. Objective Anthropology

## The Dispute

Should anthropology be a morally and politically **engaged** practice — the anthropologist as *companheira*
and witness, taking the side of the oppressed — or does its authority depend on an **objective, scientific**
stance that a moral-political agenda corrupts? The dispute crystallized in a paired 1995 *Current
Anthropology* exchange between [[scheper-hughes-nancy|Nancy Scheper-Hughes]] and the cognitive
anthropologist Roy D'Andrade, but it was already enacted in *[[scheper-hughes-death-without-weeping-1992|Death
Without Weeping]]* (1992), whose method is avowed **[[militant-anthropology]]**. It is an **ethical** dispute
about the discipline's purpose and the sources of its authority.

## Positions

**Scheper-Hughes — the primacy of the ethical.** In "The Primacy of the Ethical: Propositions for a Militant
Anthropology," she argues that ethics is prior to and enabling of anthropological understanding (a Levinasian
claim), that neutrality in the face of suffering is itself a political choice usually favoring the powerful,
and that the ethnographer should be a witness and *companheira* who "accompanies" the studied community. She
grants the poor agency — and hence accountability — refusing to reduce them to victims, but insists that
"seeing, listening, touching, recording" can be "acts of solidarity" and "sites of resistance."

**D'Andrade — moral models corrupt objective knowledge.** In "Moral Models in Anthropology," D'Andrade
argues that anthropology's value rests on producing objective, empirically-testable knowledge, and that
substituting a "moral model" (which classifies the world into good and evil and licenses denunciation) for an
"objective model" undermines the discipline's ability to explain and to be believed. On this view militant
anthropology risks bias, distortion, and the loss of the scientific authority that gives anthropological
critique any force.

## Shared Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides accept that anthropologists study human suffering and that fieldwork entails relationships and
responsibilities to those studied. They divide on whether moral-political commitment *enhances* understanding
by granting access and insight (Scheper-Hughes) or *degrades* it by compromising objectivity (D'Andrade) —
and, beneath that, on whether "objectivity" is an achievable and desirable ideal or itself an ideological
posture.
