# The "Killer Ape" / Innate-Aggression Debate

Whether humans carry a biologically innate, evolutionarily inherited drive toward
aggression and killing — rooted in a predatory "killer ape" transition in the hominin
past — or whether the strongest, most deeply selected human trait is instead
group cooperation, with war and large-scale violence products of culture (agriculture,
materialism, ideology) rather than genetics. As documented in
*[[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]* (Leakey & Lewin, 1977), which frames the debate
as central to interpreting the hominin fossil and archaeological record and to its
implications for understanding modern warfare.

## Position: Innate Aggression / "Killer Ape" (Dart, Ardrey, Lorenz)

*(Position, strongest form, as characterized in the source.)* Humans possess an
innate, biologically programmed instinct for aggression and territoriality, inherited
from an animal — specifically, an australopithecine "killer ape" — evolutionary past,
and at some point human ancestors "gave up being vegetarian ape-like creatures and
became killers... with a taste... for each other."

- **[[dart-raymond|Raymond Dart]]** originated the scientific version of the thesis
  (his "predatory transition from ape to man" reading of the australopithecine fossil
  and bone-tool record) and is quoted describing human history's "blood-bespattered,
  slaughter-gutted archives," arguing humans bear "this mark of Cain that separates man
  dietetically from his anthropoid relatives and allies him rather with the deadliest
  of carnivore[s]." Dart's **osteodontokeratic culture** hypothesis — that
  Australopithecus used worked bone, tooth, and horn implements (jaws as saws, limb
  bones as clubs) at Makapansgat — is presented as underpinning his reading that the
  australopithecine era "was rife with predation and cannibalism."
- **[[ardrey-robert|Robert Ardrey]]**, a playwright-turned-popularizer, assimilated the
  killer-ape thesis into wide popular currency through *African Genesis*, *The
  Territorial Imperative*, *The Social Contract*, and *The Hunting Hypothesis*
  (alongside Desmond Morris's *The Naked Ape*).
- **[[lorenz-konrad|Konrad Lorenz]]**, Nobel-laureate founder of modern ethology, argued
  in *On Aggression* that humans carry "an inescapable legacy of territoriality and
  aggression, instincts which must be ventilated lest they spill over." Lorenz is
  quoted: "There is evidence that the first inventors of pebble tools — the African
  australopithecines — promptly used their weapons not only to kill game, but fellow
  members of their species as well. Peking Man... used it to roast his brother." Lorenz
  proposed competitive sport as a "safety valve" for accumulated aggression, and
  attributed early-human territorial stress partly to "the counter-pressures of hostile
  neighboring hordes."
- **E. O. Wilson**'s *Sociobiology: The New Synthesis* (1975) is noted as adjacent
  background: an attempted synthesis of humanity's place in the animal kingdom that
  some ("radical scientists," per the source, unnamed) characterized as "just another,
  more sophisticated, form of Social Darwinism," naturalizing the existing social
  order — a controversy the source records without adjudicating.

## Position: Cooperation / Food-Sharing Thesis (Leakey & Lewin)

*(Position, strongest form, as stated by the authors.)* Humans are not innately
aggressive. Territoriality and aggression across the animal kingdom generally are
flexible, ritualized, resource-triggered behaviors rather than fixed instincts
requiring periodic release. The most deeply selected human trait is instead **group
cooperation**, forged over "at least three million years" through hunting-gathering,
organized hunting, and above all [[food-sharing|food sharing]] (see
[[food-sharing-hypothesis]]). The authors state flatly: "It is fiction — dangerous
fiction," of the killer-ape thesis, arguing it conveniently absolves society of
responsibility for addressing social ills, and: "We emphatically reject this
conventional wisdom." They add that engaging in hunting as part of human evolutionary
history does not imply "killing is in our genes." Large-scale war became *possible*
only once the deeply cooperative instinct was culturally redirected: it required
agricultural surplus, materialism ("psycho-materialism"), and political/ideological
manipulation — "those seeds were planted when, ten thousand years ago, our ancestors...
began to be farmers" — summarized in the authors' closing formula: "we should not look
to our genes for the seeds of war."

Supporting evidence marshaled by the authors:

- **Ritualized animal territoriality.** [WIKI, per source] Across much of the animal
  kingdom — stickleback, kittiwake, gibbon — territorial disputes are resolved by
  largely non-injurious, ritualized display rather than lethal combat, presented as the
  behavioral norm against which a fixed "killer instinct" reading is measured.
  Lorenz's neighboring-hordes-hostility claim is countered directly: the authors argue
  it is "an image more dramatically evocative than it is based in fact," and that
  neighboring foraging bands were more likely linked by kinship than by hostility.
  Lorenz's "sport as safety valve" claim is likewise countered with the observation
  that research shows competitive sport correlates with *more*, not less, violence and
  warlike behavior.
- **Cannibalism reinterpreted.** Archaeological cannibalism evidence (e.g., the
  ~500,000-year-old Zhoukoudian/Choukoutien skulls, cited by Dart-tradition readings as
  savagery) is argued to be more plausibly **endocannibalism** — a ritual of respect and
  continuity — than evidence of innate bloodlust. The authors cite a cross-cultural
  survey of 54 South American tribes: 16 practiced endocannibalism (14 of these
  hunter-gatherer societies) and 38 practiced exocannibalism (only 6 of these
  non-agricultural), used to argue that hostile, "othering" exocannibalism correlates
  with agricultural rather than hunter-gatherer life.
- **Ethnographic counter-cases** (flagged explicitly as ethnographic analogy, not
  direct evidence of the deep past): the **Ifaluk** (Polynesia), where children are
  taught ritualized, non-injurious conflict management, and the **Kurelu** (New
  Guinea), whose adults engage in ritualized, largely non-lethal arrow combat — offered
  as evidence that even where conflict occurs, cultural mechanisms channel it away from
  lethality.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides in the source agree that early hominins made and used tools, that some tool
use was directed at animal prey (and, per the disputed osteodontokeratic and
Zhoukoudian evidence, possibly at conspecifics in some circumstances), and that modern
humans are capable of organized, large-scale lethal violence. The dispute is over
**mechanism and inevitability**: the Dart/Ardrey/Lorenz position reads the capacity for
violence as evidence of an inherited, biologically fixed drive requiring periodic
release; the Leakey & Lewin position reads the same broad fossil, archaeological, and
comparative-behavioral record as evidence of a flexible, largely ritualized aggression
system layered onto a much stronger, more deeply selected cooperative disposition, with
large-scale war explained as a post-agricultural cultural product rather than a genetic
inheritance. Both positions draw on the same categories of evidence (tool-marked bone
assemblages, comparative ethology, forager ethnography) and interpret them oppositely —
a dispute over inference from shared evidence, not over the existence of the evidence
itself.
