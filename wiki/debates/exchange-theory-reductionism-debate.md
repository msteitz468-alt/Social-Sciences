# Reductionism vs. Emergence in Exchange Theory (Homans–Blau)

A foundational methodological dispute *within* [[social-exchange-theory]], between its two founders, over whether social exchange (and social structure generally) can be explained by the psychological principles governing individuals, or has emergent properties that cannot. It overlaps the wider [[methodological-individualism-debate]] and [[structure-vs-agency-debate]], but is sharpened by the fact that both parties share a theory of exchange and disagree only about its foundations.

## Position 1 — Psychological reductionism / methodological individualism ([[homans-george-c|Homans]])

The general propositions of sociology are, ultimately, propositions of behavioral psychology; social behavior is the exchange of rewards and punishments explicable by reinforcement principles operating on individuals. Homans preferred the label "methodological individualist" and argued (in "Bringing Men Back In," his 1964 ASA presidential address) that functional and structural sociology had failed to produce explanatory propositions, which must bottom out in statements about individual behavior. On this view there is nothing in a group that is not, in principle, derivable from facts about its members.

## Position 2 — Emergentism ([[blau-peter-m|Blau]])

Social exchange is an irreducibly *social* process. Blau: the theory's task is "to explain social life in terms of exchange principles by analyzing the reciprocal processes composing exchange, not to explain why individuals participate in certain exchange relations in terms of... underlying psychological principles" — "In sociology, not only the explicandum... but also the explicans... is social." Against Homans, "social structures (and, indeed, all structures composed of subunits) have emergent properties that cannot be understood on the basis of the properties of the subunits." The joint relation produced by two exchanging parties is not contained in either party's psychology (Blau's analogy: a triangle's properties are not contained in its lines and points). Blau nonetheless *admires* Homans's attempt at rigorous deductive theory; the quarrel is over the level of explanation, not the value of systematic theory.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both accept: that association can be analyzed as exchange of rewards; that reciprocity and imbalance generate differentiation; that economics supplies useful concepts (marginal utility). They divide on what an *explanation* of these facts must ultimately cite — individual reinforcement (Homans) or emergent social process (Blau). The concrete flashpoints in Blau's text are narrow but telling: he disputes Homans's collapse of "following advice" and "following orders" into one category of behavioral control (only the latter is power); rejects Homans's claim that asking a service acknowledges the server's superiority (not if it is paid for); and revises his own earlier stress on the supplier's *intent* toward the structural fact of who profits in the transaction.

## Relation to adjacent disputes

- Connects to [[methodological-individualism-debate]] (Homans's individualism vs. holist/emergentist alternatives) and to the [[structure-vs-agency-debate]].
- Distinct from the intra-Blau **micro–macro** question: in 1986 Blau conceded that his own attempt to *build* macrosociology on microsociological exchange had failed, concluding the two levels "require different approaches and conceptual schemes" that are complementary, not foundational — a partial concession to the autonomy of the macro level that neither vindicates Homans's reductionism nor abandons Blau's emergentism.

## Current standing

Open. The philosophical question (are there genuinely emergent social properties?) remains live across the social sciences. Within exchange theory proper, Emerson's power-dependence reformulation and the experimental exchange-network program shifted attention from the reductionism question to the analysis of exchange *relations* and network position, leaving the Homans–Blau foundational dispute unresolved rather than settled.
