# The Nature/Culture and Gender-Universality Debate

The dispute set off by [[ortner-female-to-male-1972|Sherry Ortner's]] 1972/74 essay: whether the [[female-subordination|secondary status of women]] is a genuine cross-cultural *universal* at all, and — even if it is — whether the [[nature-culture-distinction|nature/culture]] opposition Ortner used to explain it is a legitimate cross-cultural analytic category or a Western folk category illegitimately projected onto other societies.

*(Positions recorded in their strongest form; not adjudicated in wiki voice.)*

## The Provoking Thesis

[[ortner-sherry|Ortner]] argued that woman is everywhere symbolized as **closer to nature**, man as identified with **culture**; since culture subordinates nature, woman is universally devalued (see [[ortner-female-to-male-1972]] for the full argument, with its three grounds — body, [[domestic-public-dichotomy|domestic role]], psyche — and the "intermediate/mediating/ambiguous" reading of woman's position). The same program is framed volume-wide in *[[rosaldo-lamphere-woman-culture-society-1974|Woman, Culture, and Society]]* (1974), where [[rosaldo-michelle|Rosaldo's]] domestic/public model and [[chodorow-nancy|Chodorow's]] mothering/personality account join Ortner as the three theoretical pillars of the early universalist phase.

## Position 1 — Universalist–symbolic (early Ortner)

Local variables explain *variation* in women's status but not the *universal* fact of subordination; explanation must therefore appeal to other universals of the human condition. The nature/culture opposition, asserted (Ortner claimed) at some level by every culture through [[ritual-pollution|ritual and pollution]] beliefs, supplies the mechanism. Burden of proof lies on those who would produce a genuinely egalitarian or matriarchal counter-case.

## Position 2 — Constructionist–particularist (the critics)

[[strathern-marilyn|Marilyn Strathern]] and Carol MacCormack (*Nature, Culture and Gender*, 1980) argued that "nature," "culture," and their opposition are historically specific Western concepts; many societies (e.g. Hagen, per Strathern) do not think with a nature/culture contrast, so a universal explanation resting on it is incoherent. Strathern's *[[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-1988|The Gender of the Gift]]* (1988; primary-ingested) elaborates the Melanesian alternative: [[dividual]] persons, gift sociality without society/individual hierarchy, and gender as categorical differentiation of relations rather than a nature-bound female identity ([[strathern-gender-of-the-gift-study]]). Jane Collier and Sylvia Yanagisako (1987) pressed further: "gender" and the [[domestic-public-dichotomy|domestic/public]] split are themselves culturally variable, so there may be no stable cross-cultural *explanandum*. [[rosaldo-michelle|Michelle Rosaldo]] (1980) turned against the search for universal origins, including her own earlier frame.

## Position 3 — Revised Ortner (1996)

In *Making Gender* and the essay "So, Is Female to Male as Nature Is to Culture?", Ortner conceded the historicity of the categories and the force of the ethnographic counter-cases, but maintained that **male dominance is genuinely pervasive** across cultures even if not a strict logical universal — reframing the thesis as a strong, historically produced *tendency* rather than a symbolic law.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

All parties accept that (a) in the vast majority of documented societies, the highest public/ritual authority is male-controlled, and (b) female symbolism is cross-culturally variable and often polarized. The universalists read (a) as a universal to be explained by a deep mechanism; the constructionists read the *same* record as showing that the categories used to describe it are the analyst's, and that apparent universality is an artifact of Western framing.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-09)

The field's center of gravity moved decisively toward Position 2 after 1980 — the nature/culture universal is no longer defended in its strong form — while Ortner's revised Position 3 (pervasive-but-not-universal male dominance) remains a live, respectable stance. The essay endures as a landmark precisely because its refutation reorganized the field. Marked `partially-resolved`: the strong symbolic-universal claim is effectively abandoned; whether male dominance is a near-universal empirical tendency remains open.
