## The Dispute

Whether the early states of mainland Southeast Asia — Funan, Chenla/Zhenla, Dvaravati,
Champa, and ultimately [[angkor]] — were essentially products of **Indian** civilising
influence, or indigenous developments that selectively adopted Indian religion, script,
statecraft, and prestige goods. The dispute is fundamental to how
[[southeast-asian-state-formation]] and the [[mandala]] concept are framed. Recorded from
Higham (1989, 2002).

## Positions

**Indianisation (the "Indianised states" tradition).** Georges Cœdès and Paul Wheatley
framed the early polities as *états hindouisés* — states created or decisively shaped by
Indian civilisation, whose Sanskrit inscriptions, Hindu-Buddhist cults, *-varman* royal
titles, and Indian calendar mark a transformative external input. In its strongest older
form (which Higham reports and rejects) Southeast Asia absorbed Indian culture "like
blotting paper absorbs water." Higham (2002) notes that a "superficial glance" at
inscriptions and Chinese documents, combined with assumptions of prehistoric poverty,
underwrote this view before Iron Age ranking was fully documented.

**Indigenous development (the indigenist position).** O. W. Wolters (1982), Michael
Vickery, and Higham himself hold that local [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]] were already developing
social complexity before sustained Indian contact, and that Indian elements were adopted
**selectively** by indigenous elites as tools in pre-existing status competition. In
Higham's formulation (1989), Indianisation was "one of several variables with adaptive
potential for change in the local system," not the cause of civilisation.

Higham (2002) **hardens** this position with two further moves:

1. **Iron Age agency.** Rather than "supinely accepting foreign traders," late prehistoric
   Southeast Asians were "actively engaged" in maritime exchange linking the mainland with
   the islands, India, and China; ocean voyaging may help explain Malagasy settlement.
   Sites such as [[ban-don-ta-phet]] and the full-millennium sequence at [[noen-u-loke]]
   document ranking, iron, and exotic glass/carnelian/agate **before** classical
   Sanskritized states.
2. **Epigraphic reweighting (after Vickery).** Higham reports that attention to
   **Khmer-language** (not only Sanskrit) inscriptions scales back the Indianisation
   narrative: Indic veneer over indigenous political process (pon/mratan titles, labour
   control, factional overlordship) is the preferred reading of Chenla and early Angkor.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides accept the material record of Indian contact — Sanskrit inscriptions, Hindu and
Buddhist iconography, Indian-derived beads and ornaments, the *devarāja*/linga cult,
Roman and Indian trade goods at Oc Eo and related delta sites, canal systems at
[[angkor-borei]]. They divide on **causal weight**: whether these mark the importation of
a state form, or the local appropriation of prestige elements onto an indigenous
trajectory of chiefdom formation. Higham marshals pre- and peri-contact ranking evidence
([[ban-na-di]], [[non-nok-tha]], Mun–Chi moated sites, [[noen-u-loke]], [[dong-son-culture]])
and stresses that structurally similar [[mandala|mandalas]] arose among Khmer/Mon
([[austroasiatic-languages]]) and Cham ([[austronesian-expansion]]) speakers alike —
pointing to a shared indigenous process rather than a single imported template. All early
Southeast Asian states are, in Higham's terms, **secondary** states (contact with other
states played a part), but secondary does not mean "created by India."

**Four-way non-identity.** Chinese unitary labels (Funan, Chenla, Linyi/Champa, Dvaravati)
often overstate political unity relative to inscriptions and archaeology; polity name ≠
ethnicity ≠ language ≠ material culture.
