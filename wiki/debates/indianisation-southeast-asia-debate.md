## The Dispute

Whether the early states of mainland Southeast Asia — Funan, Zhenla, Dvaravati, Champa,
and ultimately [[angkor]] — were essentially products of **Indian** civilising influence,
or indigenous developments that selectively adopted Indian religion, script, statecraft,
and prestige goods. The dispute is fundamental to how [[southeast-asian-state-formation]]
and the [[mandala]] concept are framed. As recorded by Higham (1989).

## Positions

**Indianisation (the "Indianised states" tradition).** Georges Cœdès and Paul Wheatley
framed the early polities as *états hindouisés* — states created or decisively shaped by
Indian civilisation, whose Sanskrit inscriptions, Hindu-Buddhist cults, *-varman* royal
titles, and Indian calendar mark a transformative external input. In its strongest older
form (which Higham reports and rejects) Southeast Asia absorbed Indian culture "like
blotting paper absorbs water."

**Indigenous development (the indigenist position).** O. W. Wolters (1982), Michael
Vickery, and Higham himself hold that local chiefdoms were already developing social
complexity before sustained Indian contact, and that Indian elements were adopted
**selectively** by indigenous elites as tools in pre-existing status competition. In
Higham's formulation, Indianisation was "one of several variables with adaptive potential
for change in the local system," not the cause of civilisation; he treats the period
500 BCE – 800 CE as a **continuum** with the Indian presence "but one of several
interacting variables," a reading he states explicitly differs from Wheatley's.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides accept the material record of Indian contact — Sanskrit inscriptions, Hindu and
Buddhist iconography, Indian-derived beads and ornaments, the *devarāja*/linga cult, Roman
and Indian trade goods at Oc Eo. They divide on **causal weight**: whether these mark the
importation of a state form, or the local appropriation of prestige elements onto an
indigenous trajectory of [[chiefdom]] formation. Higham marshals the pre-contact evidence
for local ranking ([[ban-na-di]], [[non-nok-tha]], the Mun–Chi moated sites) to argue that
centralisation was underway before Indian elements appear, and stresses that structurally
similar mandalas arose among Khmer, Mon ([[austroasiatic-languages]]), and Cham
([[austronesian-expansion]]) speakers alike — pointing to a shared indigenous process
rather than a single imported template.

## Curator's Note

*(2026-07-10)* The indigenist position is now the mainstream in Southeast Asian
archaeology; the "Indianised states" framing survives mainly as a historiographic
starting point. This entry records the dispute as Higham (1989) presents it and should be
enriched as further sources are ingested.
