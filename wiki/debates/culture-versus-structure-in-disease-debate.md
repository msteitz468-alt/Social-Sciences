# Culture vs. Structure in Explaining Disease

## The Dispute

When the poor fall sick and die at higher rates — of [[tuberculosis]], of [[hiv-aids|HIV/AIDS]] — and when
their treatment "fails," what carries the primary causal weight: their *culture* and individual agency
(beliefs, "noncompliance," sexual practices, folk healing), or the *structures* of poverty, racism,
gender inequality, and unequal access to care? The dispute is methodological and interpretive rather than
factual: both sides largely agree on the epidemiological patterns; they disagree about which explanatory
register should carry the analysis, and about how much the "cultural" factors actually bear on disease
transmission and outcome. [[farmer-paul|Paul Farmer]] stages the debate throughout
*[[farmer-infections-inequalities-1999|Infections and Inequalities]]* (1999), arguing hard for the
structural pole while (in principle) recording both. Because Farmer is a partisan of one side, the
positions below are stated in their strongest form, per this wiki's protocol.

## Position A — Culturalist / Patient-Agency Pole

That local culture, belief, and individual behavior are central to explaining disease patterns and
treatment failure, and are the appropriate levers for intervention. As stated by named studies Farmer
cites:

- **TB "noncompliance."** A 1993 review by three pulmonologists called patient non-compliance "the most
  serious problem hampering tuberculosis treatment and control," determined by patients' "personal
  characteristics... beliefs and attitudes"; a *Chest* editorial called it "the most serious remaining
  problem in the control of tuberculosis in the United States."
- **Cultural default.** An anthropologist (Weise) attributed a Haitian TB program's failure to "the
  clinic's lack of knowledge about the local culture"; a South African study attributed Xhosa patients'
  high default to "deep-seated mystical beliefs" (witchcraft etiology, preference for a diviner), with no
  mention of poverty or apartheid; a Honduras study explained noncompliance through patients' "great lack
  of education" and guilt.
- **"Exotic" AIDS.** Early AIDS commentary attributed Haitian HIV to voodoo ritual, animal sacrifice, and
  "necromantic zombiism" (a 1986 *JAMA* piece), and framed international-research ethics as an "American
  values vs. African realities" cultural divide (a 1997 *New York Times* op-ed defending
  placebo-controlled antiretroviral trials).
- **Behavioral risk.** Studies locating HIV risk in women's "low self-esteem," partner number, or
  knowledge deficits, recommending "culturally sensitive education" or psychotherapy.

## Position B — Structural-Violence / Political-Economy Pole

That the operative causes are structural — poverty, inaccessible or erratic drug supply, inadequate
health infrastructure, gender and racial inequality, political violence — and that culturalist accounts
misattribute these to belief or agency, shifting blame onto the sick poor
([[immodest-claims-of-causality|immodest claims of causality]]). As stated by Farmer and allied work:

- **The [[do-kay|Do Kay]] TB comparison.** Patients with equal sorcery beliefs but *unequal* financial and
  social support had cure rates of 100% vs. ~48%; sorcery belief showed no relation to compliance —
  isolating access, not culture, as the cause.
- **Belief does not predict default.** Rubel and Garro found high compliance among migrant Mexican
  farmworkers who attributed their symptoms to *susto* and denied the TB diagnosis; a San Francisco chest
  clinic cut its missed-appointment rate from 34% to 6% purely by changing clinic hours and location —
  "the social and cultural characteristics of the user population were not altered."
- **Data vs. conclusion.** Barnhoorn and Adriaanse (India) found the strongest compliance predictors were
  three *socioeconomic* variables, yet reframed the remedy as "educational interventions" — a
  self-contradiction Farmer flags.
- **Deeper political economy.** Randall Packard argues even apartheid is a "symptom" of deeper
  industrial-capitalist transformation; the access finding (Chaisson et al.) shows race/sex survival gaps
  in AIDS *disappear* when access barriers are removed.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both poles accept that TB and HIV morbidity and mortality are steeply patterned by poverty. They differ
on (a) whether "cultural" factors (ritual, belief, sexual custom) have demonstrable relevance to actual
transmission and outcome, and (b) which register should organize the explanation and thus the
intervention. Farmer's own position (attributed) is *not* that culture is irrelevant — he holds its
weight varies by setting, potentially greater in wealthy societies and lesser where the binding
constraint is destitution — but that its causal weight has been systematically overstated in ways that
"unburden policy" of the duty to provide access.

## Resolution Status

`open`. The debate recurs across [[medical-anthropology]] and global health. Farmer's demonstrations
(the Do Kay and Peru cure results) shifted the practical argument on MDR-TB treatment, but the underlying
methodological dispute — how to weight culture against structure, and how to study agency without
exaggerating it — remains live. No adjudication is offered here; see [[immodest-claims-of-causality]],
[[structural-violence]], and [[biosocial-analysis]] for the analytic terms.
