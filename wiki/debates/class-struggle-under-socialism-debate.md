# Class Struggle Under Socialism Debate

Does **antagonistic class struggle** — especially between proletariat and bourgeoisie, including on the **ideological front** — continue as a central dynamic **after** a socialist transformation of ownership, or does the consolidation of a socialist system displace that struggle?

This page records positions. It does **not** adjudicate. The only primary source so far feeding the Maoist side in depth is the mass catechism *[[mao-quotations-from-chairman-mao-1966|Quotations from Chairman Mao Tse-tung]]* (1964/1966).

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Mid-20th-century socialist states claimed to have transformed property relations (nationalization, collectivization).
- They continued to run political campaigns, security organs, and ideological education.
- Mao's 1957 texts were delivered after China's main ownership transformation and amid international shocks (including Hungary 1956, which Mao cites).
- How to *name* remaining conflicts (class struggle vs non-class contradictions vs totalitarian repression vs bureaucratic domination) is exactly what is disputed.

## Position 1 — Maoist continuing struggle (strongest form from the anthology)

[[mao-zedong|Mao]] argues (1957, as quoted in *Quotations* ch. 2):

- Although socialist transformation of ownership is "in the main" complete and large-scale turbulent mass class struggles of earlier periods have "in the main come to an end," remnants of landlord and comprador classes remain, a bourgeoisie remains, and petty-bourgeois remoulding has "only just started."
- **"The class struggle is by no means over."** Struggle between proletariat and bourgeoisie, between political forces, and **in the ideological field** "will continue to be long and tortuous and at times will even become very acute."
- "The question of which will win out, socialism or capitalism, is still not really settled."
- Bourgeois and petty-bourgeois ideology will exist "for a long time"; ignoring ideological struggle is a grave mistake.
- **Revisionism** (Right opportunism) is a bourgeois trend "even more dangerous than dogmatism"; it pays lip-service to Marxism while negating its basic principles, the dictatorship of the proletariat, and Party leadership.
- Consolidation of socialism requires struggle on political and ideological fronts, not economic change alone (Propaganda Work Conference, 1957).
- Later note (1963, on cadres' labour): without class struggle, struggle for production, and scientific experiment as "three great revolutionary movements," restoration and a "revisionist" or "fascist" party are possible within decades.

Linked apparatus: [[contradictions-among-the-people]] (some struggles are among the people; enemy struggles remain); [[mass-line]] and rectification as methods; people's democratic dictatorship as suppression of enemy classes.

## Position 2 — Stabilization / "revisionist" foil (as constructed by Mao)

Mao's texts define an opposing line he calls **revisionism**: denial of meaningful differences between socialism and capitalism and between proletarian and bourgeois dictatorship; weakening of Party leadership and socialist transformation; restoration risk from the right. **This page does not yet hold a primary Soviet or post-Stalin text stating that line in its own strongest form.** Until those sources are ingested, treat Position 2 as **Mao's constructed foil**, not as a fair reconstruction of Khrushchev-era or Soviet theory.

## Position 3 — End of ideology / liberal modernization (adjacent)

Postwar Western claims that advanced industrial societies replace total ideologies with pragmatic interest conflict ([[end-of-ideology]]). That tradition would read Maoist continuing-struggle doctrine as evidence *against* end-of-ideology — or as a late total ideology — rather than as correct social analysis. Not argued inside the Little Red Book.

## Position 4 — Critical / Western Marxist (placeholder)

May agree that domination, ideology, and class residues persist after nationalization, while rejecting Mao's Party monopoly, enemy/people dualism, and Cultural Revolution practice. Requires separate sources (e.g. [[western-marxism]], [[ideology]] traditions).

## Evidence each side would need (method note)

| Claim type | What would count |
|---|---|
| Doctrinal | Primary program texts (this anthology serves Maoist side) |
| Sociological | Stratification, income, bureaucratic class studies in socialist states |
| Historical | Campaign outcomes, violence, restoration or reform paths (China after 1978; USSR) |
| Comparative | Other post-revolutionary regimes' conflict patterns |
