# Ancient DNA, Consent, and Repatriation Ethics

Who may authorize the sampling and genetic study of human remains — living
descendants, source communities, tribal governments, museums, states, or
individual donors — and under what standard remains should be studied or returned.
The ancient-DNA revolution sharpened the dispute by making destructive sampling of
skeletons scientifically valuable while often severing any demonstrable link
between ancient individuals and present peoples. Drawn from
[[reich-who-we-are-2018|Reich (2018)]], chs. 7 and 12; positions stated in their
strongest form without adjudication.

## Position A — Community consent and Indigenous sovereignty

Genetic research on a group is legitimate only with **community consultation and
consent**, not merely individual donors' signatures — because harms and meanings
fall on the collective. Grounded in a history of extraction: the **Karitiana**
(Brazil) were sampled in 1996 amid unfulfilled healthcare promises; the
**Havasupai** (Arizona) diabetes samples were reused for population-history and
other studies without consent for those purposes, yielding a lawsuit, sample
return, and a ~$700,000 settlement (as of Reich 2018); the **Navajo Nation**
declared a 2002 moratorium on genetic research. Anthropologist **Kim TallBear**
(quoted in *Nature*): tribes resist "a scientific worldview politically shoved
down their throat," while retaining genuine interest in science. Henrietta Lacks
is invoked as a parallel of biological material used without consent.

## Position B — Individual informed consent plus community engagement

A "middle ground" (Reich's stated preference): honour individual donors'
informed consent while consulting communities, rather than requiring collective
veto. Reich reports the withdrawal of Navajo samples (given with 1993 individual
consent) from his 2012 study as personally distressing, and worries that the
Karitiana/Havasupai/Navajo episodes have made researchers too wary to study
Native American genetics at all. **Eske Willerslev**'s practice — approaching
Indigenous communities directly after obtaining DNA (Aboriginal Australian,
Anzick, Kennewick) — is offered as an innovative, goodwill-generating model,
though it is contested among archaeologists and museum professionals.

## Position C — Scientific access to the deep past

That very ancient remains, with no traceable connection to living groups, are a
shared human heritage whose study "promotes understanding" and should not be
foreclosed. Reich frames the counsel he received from an Orthodox rabbi (2016):
graves are sacrosanct, but may be opened where there is "potential to promote
understanding, to break down barriers between people." Reich also argues ancient
remains are "real people whose physical integrity we should perhaps only violate
if we have good reasons" — a scientist's version of restraint, not open access.

## Position D — Repatriation beyond demonstrable connection

That remains should be returned to culturally affiliated peoples even absent a
demonstrated biological/cultural link — a standard Reich observes is expanding
past NAGPRA's letter. Cases: **Kennewick Man** (~8,500 y, Washington State),
initially withheld from tribes after morphological analysis suggested Pacific-Rim
affinities, then shown by Willerslev's 2015 ancient DNA to share the Native
American lineage; and the ~10,600-year-old **Spirit Cave** remains, returned
(2016) by the U.S. Bureau of Land Management to the Fallon Paiute-Shoshone despite
a NAGPRA officer noting the decision exceeded the law's documentation
requirements. Anthropologist Dennis O'Rourke (in *Nature*) called Spirit Cave a
model for engaging Native groups in study-and-reburial decisions.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

NAGPRA (1990) requires federally funded institutions to return Native American
remains where a cultural or biological connection to present peoples exists — all
sides accept this; they divide on whether the standard is too permissive of
science (Positions A/D) or too restrictive of it (Positions B/C), and on whether
consent inheres in individuals or communities. A methodological fact reshapes the
stakes: **[[ancient-dna]] "trumps" morphology** for population assignment (tens of
thousands of genome positions vs. a few skeletal traits, per Reich), so genetic
findings increasingly override the morphological judgments on which earlier
repatriation decisions rested — sometimes supporting tribal claims (Kennewick),
sometimes complicating specific-descent claims (Reich's critique that the
Kennewick genome paper sampled only the Colville and did not share its data).

## Additional Case Material (attributed, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]] adds textbook-level framing of the
NAGPRA/Kennewick episode consistent with the positions above. NAGPRA (1990, brute
legislative fact) requires federally funded U.S. museums and universities to inventory
and offer to return Native American human remains, sacred/ceremonial objects, and
"cultural patrimony" to associated tribes. On **Kennewick Man**: remains found in 1996 on
the Columbia River bank at Kennewick, Washington, were radiocarbon-dated to over 8,000
years old; some academics described "Caucasoid" traits, making the remains a pawn in
single- vs. multiple-migration debates (attributed, contested interpretation). The
Umatilla and other Native groups sought repatriation on religious grounds regardless of
the scientific date; a 2004 court ruling rejected the claim, finding "kinship" with the
modern Umatilla unproven (brute fact); the remains are now studied at the Burke Museum.
Johnson's account converges with Reich's (2018) discussion already on this page (see
Position D) that ancient-DNA evidence later shifted the balance of the Kennewick dispute.
