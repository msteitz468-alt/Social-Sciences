# Man the Hunter vs. Woman the Gatherer

A dispute over the role of hunting versus gathering in human evolution, and — inseparably —
over androcentrism in anthropology. The *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]*
volume (1968) states the "Man the Hunter" pole in its strongest form; the "Woman the
Gatherer" reply crystallized in the mid-1970s.

*(Positions recorded in strongest form; not adjudicated in wiki voice.)*

## Position A — Man the Hunter

*(Washburn & Lancaster 1968; Laughlin 1968; see [[hunting-hypothesis]].)* Hunting was "the
master behavior pattern of the human species," the adaptation that shaped human intellect,
emotions, and social life. Male cooperation in the hunt is the root of the
[[sexual-division-of-labor|sexual division of labour]], male social bonding, and war; the
family originated as the reciprocity of male hunting and female gathering; human psychology,
including the pleasure of killing, is a product of the hunting adaptation.

## Position B — Woman the Gatherer

*(Sally Slocum 1975; Nancy Tanner & Adrienne Zihlman 1976; developed after 1968; anticipated
in [[rosaldo-lamphere-woman-culture-society-1974|Rosaldo & Lamphere's 1974 introduction]], which
prefers Linton's "Woman the Gatherer" reconstruction and notes Tanner & Zihlman's work in
progress.)* If, as the *Man the Hunter* volume's own data show, **gathering supplies most
calories** in most foraging societies (see [[original-affluent-society]],
[[lee-what-hunters-do-study|Lee]]), then women's gathering, food-sharing, tool-carrying
(slings, containers), and social strategies are at least as plausible engines of hominization
as male hunting. The hunting thesis, on this view, projects a mid-century masculine ideal onto
human nature and exemplifies "male bias in anthropology."

## Dissent already present at the symposium

The "Man the Hunter" pole was contested from within the 1966 meeting, not only by later
feminists:
- **[[steward-julian-h|Steward]]** denied hunting is "a primary diagnostic characteristic of
  social types" or "even a single causal factor," and treated the family as culturally
  contingent and "weakening," against Washburn & Lancaster's more biological account.
- **Aggression.** Turnbull challenged the aggression claim ("there is an almost total lack of
  aggression" among the [[mbuti|Mbuti]]); Schneider retorted that if the aggression thesis were
  right, ethnographers should see it among living hunters. Gardner proposed a reconciliation
  (non-aggression in "refuge areas," competition in "homogeneous intercultural environments");
  Hamburg reframed aggression as an evolutionary "carry-over problem," now maladaptive.
- **Gender asymmetry in the data.** Meggitt noted earlier ethnography gave male hunting "more
  than five times the space" of women's larger dietary contribution.

## Archaeological reception — Johnson (2011), attributed

*(Attributed to feminist archaeologists as reported by Matthew Johnson; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]] and [[gender-relations-theory]].)* Johnson's textbook account reports that feminist archaeologists argue against assuming rigid, universal binary divisions of labor (domestic/public, hunting/gathering) in past societies without direct supporting evidence, noting that "all archaeology is gendered" — that traditional archaeological literature implicitly values male-coded craft specialization (e.g., flintknapping) over female-coded food preparation and processing. This is presented by Johnson as a downstream, archaeology-specific extension of the "Woman the Gatherer" corrective into method and interpretive practice, distinct from but continuous with the primary 1968–1976 exchange recorded above.

## An intermediate position — *Origins* (Leakey & Lewin 1977)

*(Attributed; see [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]], [[food-sharing-hypothesis]].)*
[[leakey-richard|Leakey]] & Lewin occupy an unstable middle ground. With Position B (and
[[isaac-glynn|Isaac]]) they demote hunting from prime mover to secondary factor, make
[[food-sharing|food-sharing]] the human-making adaptation, and note that gathering supplies
most calories — endorsing "gatherer-hunter" over "hunter-gatherer." Yet with Position A they
still root the [[sexual-division-of-labor|sexual division of labour]] in a male-hunting /
female-gathering bargain and argue that male social dominance "appears to have become very
common" as "a direct result of the division of labor between hunting and gathering" — adding
that women's status is "inversely proportional to the amount of meat hunted." They flag this
last as an attributed, contestable interpretation not to be equated with "social justice
today." The position thus adopts the gathering-centred *subsistence* correction while
retaining the hunting-centred *social* narrative that Position B most sharply attacks.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides accept the subsistence data (gathering supplies most calories in most foraging
cases) and that both sexes' activities mattered in human evolution. They disagree over which
activity was *formative* and over whether the hunting thesis's psychological claims (innate
male aggression, the family as a hunting bargain) are science or ideology.

## Current standing

`partially-resolved`. Current biological anthropology largely rejects the strong
"Man the Hunter" claims (hunting as sole engine, innate male aggression) and incorporates the
"Woman the Gatherer" corrective — cooperative provisioning by both sexes — into mainstream
accounts. The episode is a standard case in the history of the discipline's confrontation with
its own androcentrism. Related: [[hunting-hypothesis]], [[sexual-division-of-labor]],
[[washburn-sherwood]].

**Third pole — cooking (Wrangham 2009).** *[[wrangham-catching-fire-2009|Catching Fire]]* does not simply pick Hunter or Gatherer: it accepts meat’s early role, stresses gathered staples and women’s processing labor, and argues **cooking** is the missing condition that makes either long male hunts or household food exchange viable. Domestic cooking’s near-universal female assignment is then linked to pair-bonds and patriarchy ([[cooking-and-pair-bonding]]). Treat as a separate evolutionary-social program in dialogue with this debate, fully recorded on [[cooking-hypothesis-debate]] — not as adjudication of Positions A/B above.

**Archaeological extension — *Engendering Archaeology* (1991).** Position B’s corrective enters prehistoric method via [[feminist-archaeology]]: Watson & Kennedy (Eastern Woodlands horticulture) argue women as intentional plant domesticators against textbook sex-linked DoL and “shaman cultivator” innovation-to-men stories; Jackson (“Woman the Gatherer” banner) models Western Mono acorn production and bedrock mortars as women’s property structuring mobility and residence; Gero extends the critique to **Man-the-Toolmaker** ([[genderlithics]]). Source: [[gero-conkey-engendering-archaeology-1991]]. Not adjudication of A/B — densification of archaeological operationalizations of Position B–adjacent claims.


## Archaeological densification — Gilchrist (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gilchrist-roberta|Gilchrist]], *[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gender and Archaeology]]* ch.2.)* Gilchrist recapitulates the Man the Hunter lineage (Darwinian sexual selection, baboon models, Washburn-stream hunting as master adaptation) against Woman the Gatherer replies (Linton; Zihlman) and primatology’s late receptivity to female-centred models (Fedigan). She treats the debate as a textbook case of [[androcentrism]] in knowledge production. Archaeological application she highlights: Watson & Kennedy on Eastern Woodlands horticulture/maize — women gatherers as intentional acclimatisers of maize — versus Prentice’s high-status (male) innovator model; Gilchrist presents Watson & Kennedy as empirically grounded female-agency reconstruction resisting androcentric default.
