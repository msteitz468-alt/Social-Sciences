# Relative Autonomy of the State Debate

## Shared ground

How far can the modern [[the-state|state]] act **independently** of the economically dominant class? Positions differ on whether autonomy is exceptional (Bonapartist conjunctures), normal (capitalist state form), illusory (instrumentalism), or grounded in non-class sources of power (Weberian and elite theories). Each position below in strongest form; no wiki-voice verdict.

## Positions

### 1. Conjunctural autonomy — Marx, *Eighteenth Brumaire* (1852)

[[marx-karl|Marx]] argues that under earlier regimes bureaucracy prepared or served bourgeois class rule; “Not before the advent of the second Bonaparte does the government seem to have made itself fully independent.” The parliamentary bourgeoisie “longed to be rid of its own political rule”; each overturn perfected the state machine. **Qualification internal to the same text:** “the French Government does not float in the air” — Bonaparte represents the allotment [[peasantry-as-class|peasantry]]; [[lumpenproletariat|slum-proletariat]] and army are immediate supports. See [[bonapartism]], [[relative-autonomy-of-the-state]], [[marx-eighteenth-brumaire-study]].

### 2. Instrumental / committee formula — *Communist Manifesto* (1848)

Marx and Engels write that “the executive of the modern State is but a committee for managing the common affairs of the whole bourgeoisie” ([[bourgeoisie]]; [[marx-engels-communist-manifesto-1848]]). Read as denying substantial autonomy. The *Brumaire* is often treated as complication or deepening of this line rather than simple negation — **how** the two texts relate is itself disputed among later Marxists (secondary literature not yet ingested).

### 3. General relative autonomy (later school — stub)

Twentieth-century Marxist state theory (e.g. Poulantzas — not yet ingested) theorizes relative autonomy as a structural feature of the capitalist state, not only a Bonapartist emergency. Recorded as a **position family** awaiting primary sources.

### 4. Weberian and elite contrasts

[[weber-max|Weber]] defines the state by the [[monopoly-of-violence|monopoly of legitimate force]] and analyzes [[bureaucracy]] and [[legitimate-domination]] as following “quite different laws” from economic class reduction (*[[weber-politics-as-a-vocation-1919|Politics as a Vocation]]*; analogy of separation from means of administration/production accepted without economic reduction). [[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]]’s elite circulation offers another non-class-primary account. These are **contrasting research programmes**, not internal footnotes to Marx.

### 5. Skocpolian potential autonomy — Skocpol (1979)

[[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]] argues (attributed, *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]*) for a stronger claim than the Marxist school: the state is "a set of administrative, policing, and military organizations headed... by an executive authority," with interests of its own (order, revenue, war-making) and *potential autonomy* even against the entire dominant class. She reads the 1970s "relative autonomy" theorists (Miliband, Poulantzas, Anderson, Therborn, Offe) as stopping short — still tying the state functionally to reproducing the mode of production, or treating it as "a materialized concentration of... class relations" (Poulantzas). Her lineage is Weberian–Hintzean rather than Marxist. See the concept page [[state-autonomy]] and [[structural-theory-of-revolution]]. Whether this is genuinely distinct from the Weberian contrast (position 4) or a radicalization of it is itself part of the dispute.
