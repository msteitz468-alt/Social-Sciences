# Is the Working Class Still the Revolutionary Subject? (Marcuse's Integration Thesis)

A theoretical dispute internal to the Marxist tradition over whether the industrial proletariat remains the agent of revolutionary transformation under advanced ("late") capitalism. [[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]'s *[[marcuse-one-dimensional-man-1964|One-Dimensional Man]]* (1964) states the negative case in its strongest postwar form; it is a defining question of [[critical-theory|Frankfurt-School critical theory]] and of the [[historical-materialism|Marxist]] tradition it revises. Recorded as positions; not adjudicated here.

## Position A — The classical Marxist proletariat

As [[marx-karl|Marx]] and Engels argue (attributed; see [[historical-materialism]]), the proletariat is the "self-conscious, independent movement of the immense majority" and capitalism's "grave-digger": the one class whose emancipation requires abolishing class society as such. Marcuse himself restates the thesis he means to revise: "according to Marx, the proletariat is the liberating historical force only as revolutionary force; the determinate negation of capitalism occurs if and when the proletariat has become conscious of itself and of the conditions... which make up its society." On this view the integration of workers is contingent and reversible — a matter of false consciousness, reformist leadership, or the labour aristocracy — not a structural end of the proletariat's revolutionary vocation.

## Position B — Marcuse's integration/containment thesis

Marcuse argues (attributed) that advanced industrial society has structurally integrated the working class: mechanization reduces the manual-labour basis of the Marxian proletarian concept; the occupational shift toward white-collar and non-production work changes the labourer's consciousness toward "social and cultural integration"; and capitalist "domination" is transfigured into impersonal "administration," obscuring the target of opposition. "This notion was valid, and still is valid, where the laborers were... the living denial and indictment of the established society. However, where these classes have become a prop of the established way of life, their ascent to control would prolong this way in a different setting." Critical theory is therefore "thrown back to a high level of abstraction... In the absence of demonstrable agents and agencies of social change." Marcuse names critical theory's "greatest weakness" as "its inability to demonstrate the liberating tendencies within the established society," and locates only a "chance" of refusal in the "substratum of the outcasts and outsiders, the exploited and persecuted of other races and other colors" who "exist outside the democratic process" — "their opposition is revolutionary even if their consciousness is not" (see [[the-great-refusal]]).

## Position C — The peasantry as the actual revolutionary class (Skocpol, from the historical record)

Cutting across the Marcuse debate is an empirical challenge from comparative-historical sociology: in the great social revolutions that actually occurred, the decisive insurrectionary class was not the industrial proletariat at all but the **peasantry**. [[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]] argues (attributed, *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]*, 1979) that "peasant revolts against landlords were a necessary ingredient in all three Revolutions [France, Russia, China], whereas successful revolts by urban workers were not." Modern social revolutions have occurred in agrarian countries caught behind more-developed competitors — not, as Marx expected, in advanced industrial ones. This reframes the question: the issue is less whether the proletariat *remains* revolutionary than whether it was ever, historically, the agent of the classic revolutions. Skocpol treats the revolutionary agency of any class as *structurally* conditioned (solidarity, autonomy, state breakdown), not as a matter of consciousness — see [[peasantry-as-class]], [[social-revolution]], and [[structural-theory-of-revolution]].

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides accept the observable postwar facts Marcuse cites — rising real wages, the incorporation of unions into bargaining and the "welfare-warfare state," the parliamentary accommodation of Western communist and social-democratic parties, the growth of white-collar strata. They divide on what the facts *mean*: Position A reads them as a conjunctural, ideologically induced quiescence compatible with the proletariat's continuing structural centrality; Position B reads them as a structural transformation that ends the proletariat's role as the "living contradiction" and forces the search for a new historical subject.

## Curator's note

*(2026-07-08)* The debate is genuinely open and recurrent: 1968's student and Third-World movements were read by Marcuse's sympathizers as vindicating the "substratum," while orthodox Marxists and later analysts (E. P. Thompson, and the post-Marxism of Laclau and Mouffe from the other direction) contested both the writing-off of the working class and the coherence of the marginal substratum as an agent.
