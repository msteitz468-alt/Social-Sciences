# Pirahã Recursion Debate

Whether the grammar of **[[piraha|Pirahã]]** contains **[[recursion]]** (structural embedding), and — if it does not — what that implies for [[chomsky-noam|Chomsky]]'s [[universal-grammar|Universal Grammar]] and for the relation between **culture and grammar**. Positions are stated below in their strongest form from the ingested apparatus ([[everett-dont-sleep-2008|Everett 2008]]); the wiki does **not** adjudicate. Because nearly all Pirahã data come from a single fieldworker ([[everett-daniel|Everett]]), the dispute is simultaneously **empirical** (what Pirahã grammar contains), **theoretical** (what a counterexample would prove), and **methodological** (the reliability of single-observer corpora).

## Position 1 — Everett: Pirahã lacks recursion, and culture explains it

[[everett-daniel|Everett]] argues Pirahã sentences show no embedding: where English nests clauses, Pirahã uses juxtaposed independent sentences (parataxis). His load-bearing example is Kóhoi's three-sentence utterance about nails ("Bring back some nails. Dan bought those very nails. They are the same"), which he reads as three sentences, not one with an embedded relative clause. He offers diagnostics — no phrase-within-phrase, no embedding via intonation (a three-year project with Van Valin), no "longest sentence," single-modifier and single-possessor constraints, no coordination or disjunction. He attributes the absence to a **cultural cause**, the [[immediacy-of-experience|immediacy of experience principle]]: "It doesn't want it; it doesn't allow it because of a cultural principle." Crucially he holds that recursion **does** appear in Pirahã *discourse* and in general cognition — "a nonfinite language with a finite grammar" — and that recursion originates in the brain's general powers, not a language module.

## Position 2 — Everett: the challenge to Universal Grammar

Everett frames the case against **Hauser, Chomsky & Fitch (2002)**, which made recursion the plausibly-unique core of the human language faculty. His falsification argument: "if recursion is … the core faculty of the human language capacity, and if recursion is absent in one or more languages, then the Chomskyan proposal is falsified." He further argues (attributed) that grammar is not a distinct genetic module and that linguistics belongs with anthropology (Sapir) rather than psychology.

## Position 3 — Roberts: iteration entails recursion

Cambridge linguist **Ian Roberts**, debating Everett on BBC radio's *Material World*, argued Pirahã must have recursion because it permits adding or repeating material after a sentence — "iteration is a form of recursion." Everett rebuts with a reductio (iterated dog-barking would then be recursive, so either non-humans have recursion or iteration is not recursion).

## Position 4 — Chomsky and defenders: a counterexample would not refute UG

Everett reports [[chomsky-noam|Chomsky]] denying that languages with Pirahã's described properties exist ("Pirahã is pretty much like any other language"), and defenders replying that recursion is a **capacity the brain makes available that need not be used** in every language. Everett also reports a **redefinition** of recursion by some Chomskyans after his challenge (which he rejects, quoting "If at first you don't succeed, redefine success"). Everett's counter: an unused core property is hard to reconcile with recursion being *the* defining trait.

## Position 5 — Technical critics: the data/analysis are wrong (not yet ingested)

A formal linguistics literature after 2008 (e.g. Nevins, Pesetsky & Rodrigues 2009) reanalyzes the Pirahã data and disputes both the no-recursion claim and its bearing on UG. These sources are **not yet ingested**; the position is recorded as existing, not detailed, to avoid asserting uningested specifics.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

All parties agree the Pirahã corpus is overwhelmingly **Everett's**. To Everett this is deep, decades-long access; to critics it is an unreplicated single-observer base for an extraordinary claim. All agree Everett's English glosses can add embedding absent in the original — for Everett a caution he flags himself, for critics a symptom of the analysis's fragility. Everett himself calls the culture→grammar route "circuitous" and the recursion question "far from settled."
