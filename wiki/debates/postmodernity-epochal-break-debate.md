# Is Postmodernity an Epochal Break or a Phase of Capitalism?

A theoretical dispute over the status of "the postmodern": whether the cultural, aesthetic,
and epistemic changes gathered under that label mark a genuine epochal break — a new
condition of knowledge succeeding modernity — or are the *cultural expression* of an ongoing
transformation *within* capitalism. This wiki holds the debate principally through
[[harvey-david|David Harvey]]'s *[[harvey-condition-postmodernity-1989|The Condition of
Postmodernity]]* (1989), which is one of the sharpest statements of the second position;
the [[postmodernism]] concept page holds a separate, archaeology-framed account of the term.

## Positions

*(Each stated in its strongest form; no adjudication in wiki voice.)*

**Position A — A genuine break / new condition (Lyotard, Baudrillard).** Lyotard's *The
Postmodern Condition* (1979) diagnoses "incredulity toward metanarratives" — a real,
epochal loss of faith in the totalizing frameworks (Enlightenment progress, Marxism,
science) that legitimated modern knowledge, associated with the arrival of a
"postindustrial," information-based society (drawing on Bell and Touraine). Baudrillard
radicalizes this: image and sign (the "simulacrum") have displaced production as
capitalism's organizing principle, so that Marx's theory of commodity production is
"outdated."

**Position B — The cultural logic of late capitalism (Jameson, Harvey).** Fredric Jameson
reads postmodernism as "the cultural logic of late capitalism" (1984, following Mandel):
not an autonomous break but the aesthetic register of a new stage of capital.
[[harvey-david|Harvey]] gives this its most developed political-economic form: postmodernity
is "a historical-geographical condition," the experiential registration of the
[[fordism|Fordism]]→[[flexible-accumulation|flexible accumulation]] transition, mediated by
an intensified round of [[time-space-compression|time-space compression]] after the crisis
of overaccumulation that peaked in 1973. Harvey's key move: the postmodern celebration of
place, difference, and "local determinisms" against totalizing space *recurs* a
response-current already present within modernism itself (Sitte's place-affirming urbanism,
craft revival, geopolitics) reacting to an *earlier* (1847–1918) wave of compression — so it
is a second instance of a repeating pattern, "by no means new," and "certainly within the
grasp of historical materialist enquiry, even capable of theorization by way of the
meta-narrative of capitalist development that Marx proposed." Harvey directly rebuts
Baudrillard, holding "there are in fact no serious difficulties in extending Marx's theory
of commodity production" to image-commodities.

**Position C — A real but bounded shift in sensibility (Huyssen).** Andreas Huyssen (1984),
whom Harvey quotes approvingly, describes postmodernism as "a slowly emerging cultural
transformation in Western societies, a change in sensibility" that is real and worth taking
seriously but explicitly "not … a wholesale paradigm shift of the cultural, social, and
economic orders" — a middle position that grants novelty without conceding an epoch.

**Position D — Radicalised/high modernity; postmodernity misnamed (Giddens 1990).** *(Attributed —
[[giddens-anthony]], *[[giddens-consequences-of-modernity-1990|The Consequences of Modernity]]*.)*
Giddens argued we are **not** entering post-modernity as then labelled; we are moving into a phase
in which modernity’s consequences are more **radicalised and universalised**. Disorientation comes
from living in a runaway ([[juggernaut-of-modernity|juggernaut]]) world of events only partly under
control — not from the collapse of epistemology alone. He distinguished **aesthetic post-modernism**
from claims about a new social order; treated Lyotard’s evaporating grand narratives as continuous
with modernity’s own reflexivity rather than a successor epoch; and reserved "post-modern" for a
*possible future institutional order* (post-scarcity, polyarchy, demilitarisation, planetary care)
sketched under [[utopian-realism]] — **not** a description of the present. Table 2 contrasts PM vs
[[radicalised-modernity|radicalised modernity (RM)]]. Parallel sibling program: Beck’s
[[reflexive-modernization]] / [[risk-society]] (second modernity, not postmodern exit).

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

All sides agree that a marked cultural shift — toward pastiche, fragmentation, ephemerality,
skepticism of grand narratives, and the foregrounding of image and difference — became
salient in Western societies from the late 1960s–1970s, and that it coincided with economic
restructuring (deindustrialization, financialization, flexible labour). They divide on
**causation and depth**: Position A reads the cultural change as marking a break *at the
level of the social/economic order itself* (a postindustrial or sign-based society);
Positions B and C hold the economic order remains capitalist, with B insisting the cultural
change is *generated by* capitalism's own transformation and C leaving the linkage looser.
Position D holds the institutional order remains *modern* (capitalism, industrialism, surveillance,
military power — intensified and globalised), so "postmodernity" as present-tense epoch-label is a
misdiagnosis even if a different future order is conceivable.

## Stakes

The dispute is politically loaded. Harvey argues that treating postmodernity as an epochal
break disarms critique: it licenses a "triumph of aesthetics over ethics," dissolves class
analysis into "language games," and — taken to the limit — cannot in principle rule out the
place-and-myth politics that let Heidegger accommodate Nazism (a danger Harvey traces
through both modernist and postmodernist thought). His closing call is to renew historical
materialism and the Enlightenment project rather than abandon them. Defenders of Position A
reply that the metanarratives Harvey would renew are precisely what postmodern critique
exposes as "secretly terroristic" (Eagleton's gloss on Lyotard), and that a totalizing
political economy subordinates the difference, gender, and otherness that the postmodern
turn made visible.
