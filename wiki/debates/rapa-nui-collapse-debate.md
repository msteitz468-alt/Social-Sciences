## Positions

**Position 1 — Ecocide / societal collapse (Bahn and Flenley 1992; Flenley and Bahn 2002; popularized by Jared Diamond 2005).** [[rapanui|Rapa Nui]] is presented as a parable and case study for planetary overexploitation of resources. Diamond's *Collapse* (2005:118, quoted) calls it "a society that destroyed itself by overexploiting its own resources" — an "ecocide" in which deforestation, driven by clearance and fire for gardening and by Pacific rat (*Rattus exulans*) predation on palm seeds and seedlings, triggered a cascading societal breakdown, including demographic catastrophe, well before European contact in 1722.

**Position 2 — Revisionist, no precontact collapse (Hunt and Lipo 2011, *The Statues That Walked*).** A near-opposite account: precontact population never exceeded roughly 3,000; horticulture was highly productive and society largely peaceable; "collapse," in the sense of demographic catastrophe, occurred only after European contact, via introduced disease and slave raiding — not through precontact self-destruction.

**Position 3 — Intermediate / gradual transformation (Kirch 2017).** Kirch declines to fully adjudicate between the extreme scenarios, stating that "what really happened... lies somewhere between these extreme scenarios." His own synthesis documents a genuine deforestation, seabird-decline, and environmental-degradation sequence, together with a real transition to endemic intertribal warfare (the Huri Moai Period, circa AD 1500–1722), while rejecting a total-societal-"collapse" narrative. He cites Mulrooney (2012, 2013) for a gradualist reading of "economic, social, political, and religious transformations" rather than catastrophic collapse.

## Shared Factual Ground

All parties agree on a common evidentiary core: the island's original palm forest (dominated by *Paschalococos disperta*, related to the Chilean wine palm) was deforested; seabird populations, originally at least 25 species recorded at Anakena, crashed sharply; warfare and the proliferation of obsidian spearheads (*mata'a*) are archaeologically attested in the terminal precontact period; and statue quarrying at Rano Raraku ceased before European contact, with the transition appearing gradual rather than abrupt (older statues were incorporated into later temple foundations rather than abandoned outright). Where the positions diverge is in how this shared record is framed: as self-inflicted "ecocide" and societal collapse (Diamond), as an adaptive, non-catastrophic transformation with the demographic disaster deferred to the postcontact period (Hunt and Lipo, the position Kirch leans toward), or as an intermediate reading that accepts real ecological and political crisis without endorsing a total-collapse narrative (Kirch's own stated position).

One attributed interpretation of the island's religious history bears on the debate: Grant McCall (1980:38–39, quoted) interprets the birdman (*tangata manu*) cult of Makemake, centered at Orongo, as an adaptive religious innovation responding to ecological crisis — replacing multiple-ancestor lineage worship with a single god and an achieved, ordeal-elected leadership role in place of hereditary kingship.
