# Social Origin of Categories Debate

Dispute over whether (and how) **logical categories** and the **classificatory function** originate in **social organization** — opened most sharply by [[durkheim-mauss-primitive-classification-study|Durkheim & Mauss 1903]] and pressed by [[needham-rodney|Needham]]'s 1963 critique; continuous with the categories thesis of *[[durkheim-elementary-forms-study|Elementary Forms]]* (1912) and with broader [[sociology-of-knowledge]] debates. **No adjudication in wiki voice** — positions in strongest form.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Complex cultural classification systems exist and vary (Australian moiety/totem schemes, Zuñi regional systems, Chinese correlative cosmology, etc., as reported in period sources).
- The 1903 essay and *Elementary Forms* assert social genesis of categories/classification; critics dispute the inference, not that the ethnography reports classifications.
- Needham (1963) corrected extensive bibliographic errors in the French original and rechecked quotations against sources.

## Position 1 — Durkheim & Mauss (1903)

**Holders:** [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]], [[mauss-marcel|Mauss]] (*Année Sociologique* VI).

**Strongest form:** The individual mind does not spontaneously produce hierarchical, totalizing classification. The first logical categories were social categories; the first classes of things were classes of men; moieties were the first genera, clans the first species. Society is not only the *model* but supplies the *divisions* of the system. Logical hierarchy is another aspect of social hierarchy; unity of knowledge is the unity of the collectivity extended to the universe. How things are sorted among classes is affective (collective sentiment); sociocentrism places the group at the center of schemes of nature. Scientific classification is the progressive weakening of social affectivity, retaining the cadre of hierarchical grouping. Same method should open time, space, cause, substance, reasoning to sociology.

**Evidence they offer:** Morphological correspondences in Australian, Zuñi/Sioux, and (more abstractly) Chinese systems drawn from published ethnography/sinology.

## Position 2 — Needham (1963 Introduction)

**Holder:** [[needham-rodney|Rodney Needham]], editor-translator of the English *Primitive Classification*.

**Strongest form:** The essay is theoretically significant but **logically fallacious and methodologically unsound** as a causal demonstration. Formal correspondence between society and classification fails on their own cases (negative instances, patched anomalies, unevidenced evolution). Sentiment is introduced without evidence. There is no logical necessity that society causes classification; common principles or reverse causation are open. Worse, the project confuses **content of collective representations** with **cognitive faculties** (Gehlke/Dennes): deriving the category of space from social groups already spatially perceived is circular. **Residual value:** the analytical notion of [[symbolic-classification|classification]] for sociology; intensive comparison; structural concordance (especially in prescriptive-alliance systems) without D&M's formal-causal claim; research charter for inventory of categories (Mauss).

## Position 3 — *Elementary Forms* refinement (1912)

**Holder:** Durkheim (solo monograph).

**Strongest form:** The categories of the understanding (time, space, class, causality, force, totality, etc.) are [[collective-representations|collective representations]] with social — and specifically religious — origins; society is the reality expressed in sacred force and ritual [[collective-effervescence|effervescence]]. Australian totemism is the experimental elementary case. (Study page: [[durkheim-elementary-forms-study]].) Commentators (Gehlke, Alpert, Needham) read 1903 as initial formulation of this program.

**Relation to Position 1:** Same research program, religious mechanism more developed, still social-origin of categories; inherits ethnographic and causal disputes.

## Position 4 — Circularity / faculty critique (Gehlke, Dennes, via Needham)

**Strongest form:** If "mind" means cognitive faculties, it is absurd to say categories *originate* in social organization — spatial, quantitative, and class categories are presupposed in recognizing social groups as a usable model. If "mind" means culture-variable ideas only, comparative ethnography cannot establish the origin of the *faculty* of classification.

## Position 5 — Reverse / common-principle alternatives (Needham; Lévi-Strauss line as Needham narrates)

**Strongest form:** Concordance of symbolic and social orders, where it exists, may reflect shared structural principles (e.g. dyadic relation) rather than society causing classification; classification may organize social arrangements; Lévi-Strauss on sentiment as empty explanation and on *pensée sauvage* as classificatory thought in its own right (primary Lévi-Strauss not ingested here — position as reported by Needham).

## Resolution status

`open` — No field consensus that the 1903 causal thesis is established; the research object (symbolic classification) and the weaker claim that knowledge is socially located remain active in [[sociology-of-knowledge]] and anthropology. Promotion of the strong causal claim to wiki voice is not warranted.
