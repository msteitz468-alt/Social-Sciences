## Background — the one point of consensus

As of Possehl's 2002 synthesis, "in spite of many claims to the contrary, the writing
system of the Indus Civilization remains undeciphered" — no proposed decipherment can be
independently demonstrated to be correct, chiefly because no bilingual text (an Indus-
script "Rosetta Stone") has been found. This absence of a validated reading is the one
point on which the field converges; everything past it is contested interpretation. The
corpus (per Mahadevan's 1977 concordance, ~2,900 texts, possibly ~3,100 by 2002 with new
finds from Harappa, Dholavira, and Rakhigarhi) is overwhelmingly short — most inscriptions
run one line, the longest known runs to 26 characters — which itself limits what any
decipherment can be tested against. Near-consensus exists (via M. Zvelebil's 1970/1990
synthesis) on several structural points: the script is read predominantly right-to-left
(occasionally boustrophedon); it admits suffixes but not prefixes, infixes, or
inflectional endings; and, with a sign inventory estimated at roughly 150–540 signs
depending on the counting scheme (all counts land "in the range of a few hundred," ruling
out both a ~26-sign alphabet and a many-thousand-sign logographic system like Chinese),
it is generally termed **logosyllabic**.

## Positions

### Position 1 — Undeciphered, but plausibly encodes a real language: Dravidian / Proto-Elamo-Dravidian (Parpola and the Finnish team; Mahadevan; McAlpin)

The leading working hypothesis, held by the Finnish decipherment team (led by Asko
Parpola) and by Iravatham Mahadevan, is that the script renders a real spoken language,
most likely an early form of Dravidian, or David McAlpin's proposed deeper "Proto-Elamo-
Dravidian" family (McAlpin 1981) linking Dravidian to the also-undeciphered Elamite of the
Iranian plateau. This position rests partly on a structural argument: the script's
suffixation-only morphology fits an agglutinative language type such as Dravidian and is
held to rule out Sanskrit or another inflectional Indo-European language as the encoded
tongue. Mahadevan's own contribution is treated by the field (and by Possehl) as
methodologically sound independent of any specific rebus reading: his statistical,
positional-frequency analysis of sign distribution is a rigorous structural-linguistics
result, separable from — and more secure than — any claim about which language or rebus
values the signs represent. Munda has also been proposed as a rival candidate language
family within this broader "it is a real language, plausibly regional to South Asia" camp.

### Position 2 — Rival rebus-based decipherments, mutually incompatible (Fairservis, S. R. Rao, K. Rao, Barua, Sankarananda, Hrozny, Chakravorty, Priyanka, and others)

A long series of individual scholars have proposed specific rebus (sound-value)
decipherments, each internally confident and each incompatible with the others. The
"Proto-Śiva"/Mahāyogi seal alone has been read, by different decipherers, as "tribute to
god Kueya" (Hrozny), "aquatic birds covering waterways" (Sankarananda), "mountain-
worshipped one" in Sanskrit (Barua), "Man of the Star (Śiva)" (the Finnish team), "Satta
Kosika" (Chakravorty), a Sanskrit phrase "tri Maha mahisa" (Priyanka), and "An-il the
Ruler…" (Fairservis) — seven irreconcilable readings of one short inscription. Proposed
language affiliations across the whole rebus literature range even wider, including
Sumerian, Egyptian, and (rejected by Possehl himself as "almost comical" given the ~2,300-
year and ~21,000 km gap) Rapa Nui/Easter Island script. The sheer diversity and mutual
incompatibility of these readings is treated within the field — and by Possehl, who
narrates this position without endorsing any entry in it — as evidence that no rebus
decipherment yet proposed has been validated, not as a set of competing valid options.

### Position 3 — Marshall's original position (1931): probably more than one language, explicitly not Sanskrit

The earliest recorded scholarly position, John [[marshall-john|Marshall]]'s in his 1931
synthesis, held that the script likely rendered more than one language across the
civilization's territory, that there was "no reason for connecting it in any way with
Sanskrit," and that Harappan speech was "possibly… Dravidic" given the sheer size of the
area implying more than one language community. Marshall's position anticipates the later
Dravidian-affiliation camp (Position 1) while explicitly and early rejecting a Sanskrit or
Indo-European affiliation for the script.

### Position 4 — Use-contexts without validated reading (Kenoyer 1998)

[[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] (1998) treats the script as **genuinely undeciphered** and rejects premature rebus claims (including over-confidence in any single Parpola-style reading) until a bilingual or independent validation appears. His distinctive move is to shift research emphasis from **sign-list decipherment** to **archaeological contexts of use**: seals, tokens, pottery marks, and inscribed tools as instruments of trade control, ownership, and ritual integration within cities. He dates the script's widespread adoption to urban consolidation around 2600 BCE (with earlier abstract marks and potters' marks treated as non-writing precursors), estimates a logosyllabic inventory on the order of ~400–450 signs, and notes that most of the known corpus comes from Mohenjo-daro and Harappa, with HARP finds refining seal-carving and inscription sequences at Harappa. On language affiliation he does not claim a solved Dravidian or Indo-European identity; four-way non-identity applies. This position is compatible with "undeciphered but linguistic" (Position 1) while remaining agnostic on specific rebus values — and is orthogonal to the later Farmer–Sproat–Witzel non-writing thesis (post-1998). Source: [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998]]; see also [[indus-script]], [[parpola-asko]].

## A later development, outside this source's scope

The Farmer–Sproat–Witzel argument that the Indus symbol system is **not writing at all** —
that it is a non-linguistic symbol system rather than a script encoding a spoken language —
was published in 2004 and post-dates Possehl's 2002 synthesis entirely. It does not appear
in, and cannot be attributed to, the source ingested here; it is noted only as a later
development in the field, to be filed under its own attribution once a source discussing
it directly is ingested.

## Four-way non-identity (mandatory)

This debate concerns what **language**, if any, the Indus script encodes — a linguistic
question. It is a separate question from (a) what **ethnic or self-identified group**
produced or used the script, (b) any **genetic population** history claim about the
Indus region's inhabitants, and (c) whether "the Indus Civilization" as an archaeological
culture is coextensive with any of the above. A structural argument that the script's
grammar fits Dravidian typology is not evidence about the ethnicity or genetic ancestry
of the people who wrote it; "Dravidian script" (if true) does not entail "Dravidian
people" without a wholly separate argument. This same caution attaches to the recurring
proposal that the Chanhu-daro seal 13 or other iconography shows early Indo-European
speakers already present in the Mature Harappan Greater Indus region (F. R. Allchin's
hypothesis) — Allchin himself explicitly hedged this as a "premature attempt" to read
Indo-Aryan presence from a single seal, and it bears on population/migration timing, not
on the separate, undecided question of what language the Indus script itself records.
