# The Kalahari Debate

A dispute over whether the **Dobe [[juhoansi|!Kung]]** — the most intensively documented
foraging population in anthropology — were a substantially autonomous foraging society or a
marginalized people long enmeshed in herding, trade, and colonial economies. Because the
!Kung anchor the [[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]] and are widely used
as a foraging reference case, the stakes reach the whole project of using living foragers to
model the past ([[ethnographic-analogy]]).

*(Positions recorded; not adjudicated in wiki voice.)*

## Origins in *Man the Hunter* (1968)

The debate's roots are in the *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* volume
itself. [[lee-what-hunters-do-study|Lee's]] Dobe study presented the !Kung as a secure,
leisured foraging economy, and the volume's editors treated the !Kung, Netsilik, and Arunta
as "classic" cases while cautioning they are marginal-environment survivors, not
representative of the habitats foragers once occupied. Two threads of dissent surfaced at the
symposium:

- **Affluence vs. hardship.** In the "Does Hunting Bring Happiness?" exchange, Lorna Marshall
  qualified the affluence picture — reporting Nyae Nyae !Kung as "all thin" and "constantly
  express[ing] concern and anxiety about food," and calling "affluent society" "a bon mot"
  that "does not add to the understanding." Balikci ([[netsilik|Netsilik]]) and Williams
  (Birhor) reported real hardship, showing the affluence thesis "would not apply in all
  cases."
- **Pristine vs. degraded foragers.** Lathrap's chapter argued Amazonian "hunters" were
  degraded ex-agriculturalists ("devolution"), and [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] held
  that recent foragers are often "regressive rather than primitive" (see [[primitiveness]]) —
  the general form of the worry that would later be pressed against the !Kung specifically.

## Position A — Isolationist / foraging tradition

*(Lee, Solway, Yellen.)* The Dobe !Kung of the 1960s were substantially self-sufficient
foragers: subsistence rested on wild foods, the group weathered the 1963–66 drought without
famine relief while pastoralist neighbours did not, and the ethnographic and archaeological
records support long continuity of a foraging way of life. On this view the Dobe are a
legitimate (if carefully qualified) reference case for forager economics and sociality.

### Lee 1979 densification (primary-ingested)

*[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* elaborates Position A with full apparatus rather than a
symposium chapter. *(Attributed — Lee 1979.)*

- **Demography and dual structure:** Resident foraging camps *and* cattle-post client groups
  are both documented; Lee does not claim every San household was an independent forager, but
  treats majority hunting-and-gathering camps as real and measurable ([[lee-kung-san-study]]).
- **Contact without erasure:** Ch.3 traces trade goods and black/white contact from the
  1870s–80s and permanent black settlement from the mid-1920s, yet argues exclusive foraging
  characterized the area into the early twentieth century and remained central into the 1960s.
- **[[nore|n!ore]] land tenure:** Stewardship by *k"awsi* and reciprocal access are presented as
  indigenous spatial institutions, not mere residuals of dispossession.
- **[[foraging-mode-of-production]]:** Tools private, food shared, land collective/stewarded;
  mafisa and farming appear as *contradictions* entering that system (Ch.14), not as proof it
  never existed.
- **Nutrition vs cash:** Lee reads large seasonal weight losses under craft/cash involvement
  as failure of mercantile entanglement, not of wild-food economies — a direct pushback against
  using stress data to collapse the foraging case.
- **Violence and courts:** Homicide series and Tswana *kgotla* show external law mattered by
  mid-century, but also that internal fission, leveling, and (rarely) collective sanction
  operated without a state.

## Position B — Revisionist / enmeshment

*(Edwin Wilmsen and others, developed after 1968, esp. *Land Filled with Flies*, 1989.)* The
!Kung / Zhu were not isolated foragers but a **rural underclass** integrated for centuries into
regional herding, trade (including in ivory and skins), and, latterly, colonial and apartheid
labour economies; their "foraging" of the mid-twentieth century reflected impoverishment and
marginalization, not an untouched Paleolithic survival. On this view, using the Dobe as a
Pleistocene analogue is category error.

### Wilmsen 1989 densification (primary-ingested)

*[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Land Filled with Flies]]* is the monographic spine of
Position B. Study object: [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]]; hub:
[[hubs/studies/wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]]; thinker: [[wilmsen-edwin-n]].
*(Attributed — Wilmsen 1989; not an adjudication.)*

- **Genre and program:** Explicitly *not* a classic ethnography; restores San-speakers to a
  multi-ethnic political economy (Zhu + Mbanderu primary field association at CaeCae from 1973;
  regional archaeology and archives). Wolf-style relational totality and critique of forager
  mode-of-production models (Meillassoux/Lee/Sahlins lineage).
- **Category as class:** Bushman/Basarwa/San treated as colonial-administrative and class
  categories more than stable ethnic essences; "San" polite substitution changes little in practice.
- **Deep integration:** Iron Age pastoral/settlement synthesis (western sandveld loci incl.
  Nqoma and related sequences) plus nineteenth-century cattle/ivory trade corridors; San as
  herders, traders, raiders, clients, tributaries. Remoteness as historical product of
  merchant-capital and colonial labor regimes, not eternal isolation.
- **Underclass consolidation:** Cattle-post clearances, reenserfment episodes, German–Herero war
  refugee/labor effects, mine-labor substitution that left San at posts; Dobe-area mid-century
  demography read as **43–49% Bantu-speakers** among ~900 persons — barely half Zhu at the peak
  "isolated Bushmen" moment.
- **Tenure and class within Zhu:** [[nore|nqore/n!ore]] and [[hxaro|haro/hxaro]] inside stratified
  entitlement; CaeCae 1980 — **66%** of Zhu households with no cattle/goats, **11%** holding
  **~64%** of Zhu cattle; *nqorekau*/*xhaiha* surplus extraction; class characteristics argued
  as inherent in historically formed Zhu relations, not only recent external impositions.
- **Energetics vs year-round political economy:** July kcal can **agree** with Lee (~2,140/2,355
  vs Wilmsen ~2,294) while forager monthly means range ~**1,408–3,114** kcal; year-round
  affluence inference rejected.
- **Physiology:** Seasonal weight amplitude **~3%** (1968–69) vs **~6%** (1975–76);
  independent/pastoral Zhu **~10% heavier** than foragers; weight-for-height ~75% vs ~83% of
  local Bantu; Lee's craft/store-food remedy for weight loss rejected as mis-specifying underclass
  budgets. Bodies read as class inscription, not desert genetic destiny alone.
- **Policy close:** Fascination with a fixed forager image obscures present class conditions;
  Remote Area / equal-opportunity politics over category ethnography.

### Lee 2013 densification — App. B oral history (*Dobe Ju/'hoansi* 4th ed.)

*[[lee-dobe-juhoansi-2013|The Dobe Ju/'hoansi]]* Appendix B ("The Kalahari Debate: Ju/'hoan Images
of the Colonial Encounter") is Lee's teaching-volume statement of Position A with **Ju elders'
own historical testimony** — material he argues neither side had systematically collected until
his 1986–87 / 1995 / 1997 interview program on *n//a k aishe* ("first time," pre-1870s target).
*(Attributed — Lee 2013 App. B; not an adjudication.)*

- **Revisionist package as Lee summarizes it:** centuries of regional trade domination; possibility
  Ju were cattle-keepers or servants; egalitarianism as outcast adaptation (Wilmsen 1989;
  Wilmsen & Denbow 1990) — see Position B.
- **Oral-history program:** elders from five major Ju settlement areas; named interlocutors
  include Kumsa, !Goshe /Ti!kais, Cho/ana N!ae–/Kunta (as Lee presents them).
- **Archaeology as Lee cites it:** sparse Iron Age goods; concerted bone search 1978–92 almost
  lacking pre-late-19th cattle/Black settlement inland (Smith–Lee and related work in his
  presentation); glass beads and micro-retouched bottle glass as contact markers; discarded ivory
  noted in some accounts.
- **Ethnohistory:** explorer accounts (incl. Müller 1912 and Lee's five propositions about late
  contact); hxaro, pottery, and iron in Ju memory without equating that to permanent herder
  domination.
- **Method claim:** "ask the Ju" is required alongside archaeology and ethnohistory; oral images
  surprised both camps in Lee's telling.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides accept that the Dobe !Kung had contact with Bantu pastoralists (documented from the
1880s in Lee; claimed deeper by revisionists) and outside economies; they disagree on **how
much** that contact structured !Kung society and subsistence, and therefore on whether the
Dobe can stand as a foraging reference case. Solway & Lee's "Foragers, Genuine or Spurious?"
(1990) is the isolationist reply to Wilmsen; App. B is the oral-history densification of the
isolationist toolkit.

## Handbook reception (*Oxford Handbook of African Archaeology*, 2013)

*(Attributed — handbook authors summarizing the debate’s archaeological stakes; *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013|Mitchell & Lane 2013]]*; as of ~2012. Does not adjudicate.)*

- **Wadley (Ch. 25):** Revisionists (Wilmsen; Wilmsen & Denbow 1990) challenge portrayals of Kalahari foragers as autonomous or pristine communities — “an issue of particular relevance when pastoral and farming communities entered the African stage.” Ethnographic analogy contributed to forager/farmer polarization that archaeology has helped counter by showing interaction variety. Wobst’s “tyranny of the ethnographic record” and the risk of treating living hunter-gatherers as “human fossils” frame the analogy problem for MSA/LSA interpretation.
- **Mitchell (southern Holocene chapters):** Documents exchange of iron, pottery, livestock for beads and “invisible” products (Thukela, Maloti-Drakensberg, possibly Tsodilo specularite); high Khoisan mtDNA in South African Nguni speakers fits female San–male Bantu hypergamy; **Wilmsen/Denbow dependency readings are theoretically and empirically contestable** (Mitchell’s attributed position — not a vault verdict). Warns against treating the LSA as “Khoisan history” via a few semi-arid San groups generalized across time and biomes.
- **MacEachern (Ch. 5):** Genetic focus on San/Pygmy groups can reinscribe them as fossilized Pleistocene remnants; non-adaptive markers do not make carriers “less modern” — reflexivity that parallels revisionist critiques of pristine-forager imagery without equating genetics with the Kalahari debate’s ethnohistoric poles.
- **Sadr (Ch. 44):** Pre-Bantu LSA livestock and thin-walled pottery in southern Africa complicate any model that places all regional change after Iron Age farmer arrival — relevant background for how long multi-economy entanglements have been possible.
