# The Kalahari Debate

A dispute over whether the **Dobe [[juhoansi|!Kung]]** — the most intensively documented
foraging population in anthropology — were a substantially autonomous foraging society or a
marginalized people long enmeshed in herding, trade, and colonial economies. Because the
!Kung anchor the [[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]] and are widely used
as a foraging reference case, the stakes reach the whole project of using living foragers to
model the past ([[ethnographic-analogy]]).

*(Positions recorded; not adjudicated in wiki voice.)*

## Origins in *Man the Hunter* (1968)

The debate's roots are in the *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]* volume
itself. [[lee-what-hunters-do-study|Lee's]] Dobe study presented the !Kung as a secure,
leisured foraging economy, and the volume's editors treated the !Kung, Netsilik, and Arunta
as "classic" cases while cautioning they are marginal-environment survivors, not
representative of the habitats foragers once occupied. Two threads of dissent surfaced at the
symposium:

- **Affluence vs. hardship.** In the "Does Hunting Bring Happiness?" exchange, Lorna Marshall
  qualified the affluence picture — reporting Nyae Nyae !Kung as "all thin" and "constantly
  express[ing] concern and anxiety about food," and calling "affluent society" "a bon mot"
  that "does not add to the understanding." Balikci ([[netsilik|Netsilik]]) and Williams
  (Birhor) reported real hardship, showing the affluence thesis "would not apply in all
  cases."
- **Pristine vs. degraded foragers.** Lathrap's chapter argued Amazonian "hunters" were
  degraded ex-agriculturalists ("devolution"), and [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] held
  that recent foragers are often "regressive rather than primitive" (see [[primitiveness]]) —
  the general form of the worry that would later be pressed against the !Kung specifically.

## Position A — Isolationist / foraging tradition

*(Lee, Solway, Yellen.)* The Dobe !Kung of the 1960s were substantially self-sufficient
foragers: subsistence rested on wild foods, the group weathered the 1963–66 drought without
famine relief while pastoralist neighbours did not, and the ethnographic and archaeological
records support long continuity of a foraging way of life. On this view the Dobe are a
legitimate (if carefully qualified) reference case for forager economics and sociality.

## Position B — Revisionist / enmeshment

*(Edwin Wilmsen and others, developed after 1968, esp. *Land Filled with Flies*, 1989.)* The
!Kung were not isolated foragers but a **rural underclass** integrated for centuries into
regional herding, trade (including in ivory and skins), and, latterly, colonial and apartheid
labour economies; their "foraging" of the 1960s reflected impoverishment and marginalization,
not an untouched Paleolithic survival. On this view, using the Dobe as a Pleistocene analogue
is category error.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides accept that the Dobe !Kung had contact with Bantu pastoralists (documented from the
1880s) and outside economies; they disagree on **how much** that contact structured !Kung
society and subsistence, and therefore on whether the Dobe can stand as a foraging reference
case. Solway & Lee's "Foragers, Genuine or Spurious?" (1990) is the isolationist reply to
Wilmsen.
