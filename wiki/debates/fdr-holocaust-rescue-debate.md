# American Refugee Policy and the Holocaust

Did the United States — its government, public, and Jewish leadership — do what it could for Europe's Jews between 1933 and 1945, and how much did domestic antisemitism shape the answer?

## Shared factual ground

These facts are documented across the literature (per Dinnerstein 1994): the Wagner–Rogers Bill (1939) to admit 20,000 refugee children failed amid strong public opposition and without Roosevelt's endorsement; 1938 polling found 77% of Americans opposed admitting more Jewish refugees from Germany; in January 1944 Treasury Secretary Henry Morgenthau Jr. confronted Roosevelt with evidence of governmental indifference, after which Roosevelt created the War Refugee Board — while having subordinates delete mention of Jews from the announcement — and authorized a single temporary refugee camp (Fort Ontario, NY) whose residents were to be returned to Europe after the war. Some American Jews themselves were ambivalent about refugee admission, fearing backlash; the DAR, American Legion, and VFW called in 1944–45 for total postwar immigration suspensions.

## Positions

**Feingold** argued "Roosevelt's anti-refugee policy was based on a broad popular consensus" — the constraint was societal antisemitism and isolationism, not presidential indifference alone.

**Wyman** argued that "many Americans were prejudiced against Jews and were unlikely to support measures to help them... antisemitism had been a significant determinant of America's ungenerous response" — a harsher verdict on both administration and public.

**Dinnerstein** frames the 1970s–80s indictments of FDR and of American Jewish leadership as partly anachronistic: critics judge wartime actors with post-Holocaust hindsight, and the productive question is what they realistically could have done. He also records contemporaries' bleaker readings — Barbara Tuchman's remark (attributed) that the Gentile world "would fundamentally have welcomed the final solution."

## Status

Open. The dispute is over weighting (public opinion vs. presidential agency vs. bureaucratic obstruction) rather than over the documentary record itself.
