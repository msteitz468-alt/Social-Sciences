# Celticity Debate

## Shared Factual Ground

The following are not seriously disputed as bare historical facts (though their interpretation is):

- Greco-Roman writers used ethnonyms (*Keltoi*, *Celtae*, *Galli*, *Galatae*) for various northern and western European communities and for Galatians in Asia Minor (Cunliffe 1997, Ch. 1; R1-007, R1-086).
- A Celtic branch of Indo-European is real and reconstructible; Continental epigraphy and Insular medieval literatures attest it (see [[celtic-languages]]; R1-048–R1-050).
- [[hallstatt-culture]] and [[la-tene-culture]] are conventional material-culture constructs with type-sites ([[hallstatt]], [[la-tene]]), seriation history, and real assemblage patterning (R1-068–R1-072).
- In 1871 scholars matched La Tène-type Champagne/Neuchâtel assemblages to Marzabotto burials and treated them as the material culture of historically described Celts (R1-036, R1-070).
- Modern Celtic national and cultural identities exist on the Atlantic façade and beyond (R5-040).
- Cunliffe 1997 (and later scholarship) notes incomplete spatial and temporal overlap between Celtic speech and La Tène material culture (R1-060, R3-058, R5-042).

**What is disputed** is how these layers map onto each other: whether “the Celts” name one historical people expanding largely by migration; whether the term is a modern scholarly (or classical) construct with little emic unity; how Iberia, Britain, and Ireland fit continental packages; and what role nationalism and present politics play in the image.

This page records positions; it does **not** adjudicate. Primary densification source for Position C: [[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]]; for Position D: [[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]] ([[cunliffe-barry]]). Related: [[primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate]], [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[celts]], [[celtic-languages]].

## Position A — Culture-historical Celtic package

**Strongest form:** Language + La Tène (and often Hallstatt) material culture + classical *Keltoi*/Galli form one historical people or people-complex expanding largely by migration and invasion. Archaeology after 1871 “identified” the historic Celts; invasionist models (Hawkes’s hillfort waves; Bosch-Gimpera’s “Two Celtic Waves in Spain,” 1939; Rhys’s Goidelic then Brythonic waves) explained cultural change as folk movement, modelling on classical Celtic migrations and modern imperial expansion (R1-036–R1-039, R1-056, R1-070–R1-071). La Tène culture was regarded as “the archaeological expression of the Celts” (R1-071). Simple invasionist theories remained common until the 1960s and persist in popular literature (R1-039).

**Empirical programme (as historicized by Cunliffe):** cemetery and settlement archaeology across Champagne, Switzerland, Bohemia, the Po Valley; Déchelette’s pan-European La Tène synthesis; equation of material horizons with Livy/Polybius migration narratives.

**Weaknesses opponents stress:** circular language–migration–material reasoning (R1-038); paucity of La Tène material where Celts are historically attested (Greece, Asia Minor) (R1-037); Iberian Celtiberian speech and ethnonyms without La Tène package (R3-058); Britain/Ireland never called “Celtic” by ancient authors (R3-079).

## Position B — Sceptical Celticity

**Strongest form:** “Celtic” as a single archaeological culture or ancient ethnic identity is largely a modern scholarly (and classical Mediterranean) construct. Classical ethnonyms are external labels serving “enemy without,” soft-primitivist, and conquest-justifying agendas. Equating art style or cemetery type with ethnicity is circular. The term is so abused by nationalism, tourism, and pan-European myth-making as to be useless for serious analysis except as a history of reception. Cunliffe names this pole the “politically correct view” that the term is so abused as to be useless except for book sales, and notes Chapman 1992 *The Celts: The Construction of a Myth* as the most challenging work on validity of the concept (R1-046, R5-048). Extreme scepticism can extend to denying any useful content to “Celtic” beyond modern identity politics.

**Empirical programme:** source criticism of classical ethnography; constructivist ethnicity theory; deconstruction of nineteenth-century Celtomania and nationalist archaeology; insistence on regional archaeological sequences without pan-Celtic ethnic labels.

**Weaknesses opponents stress:** erases real comparative linguistic unity; discards classical testimony of named peoples and movements; leaves no vocabulary for genuine long-distance similarities in art, warfare, and social forms that specialists still need to discuss.

## Position C — Cunliffe 1997 partial-overlap synthesis

**Strongest form (as Cunliffe states it):** Classical sources are biased but indispensable; Celtic languages are real; Hallstatt/La Tène packages are real; **overlaps are partial and historical**. There were peoples known as Celts whose movements and behaviour are reflected in contemporary sources; they spoke a language still spoken/taught on western fringes; some communities developed unique art and distinctive material culture that spread through Europe; in Ireland echoes survive via written versions of oral tradition — “These threads are real and are worthy of our attention” (R1-046). Both New Celtomania (pan-European past comforting amid ethnic division) and terminological nihilism are sterile in extremity (R1-046).

**Key methodological moves (attributed):**

1. **Uncouple language and La Tène.** Celtic speech earlier and wider than La Tène; defining Celt by language uncouples the ethnonym from the archaeological culture (R1-060). The two common definitions — language and La Tène material culture/art — are different attributes, not coterminous (R5-042).
2. **Iberia as decisive non-identity case.** Classical Celtiberians + Celtic speech + social resemblances without La Tène type → La Tène must be dissociated from “Celtic” (R3-058). Classical authors equated temperate and Iberian “Celts” via language, valour, and hospitality, not material package (R3-074).
3. **Insular ethnonym gap.** No ancient author called Britain/Ireland inhabitants Celts; language established by sixth century BCE on Cunliffe’s reading; Pytheas does not treat Pretani as Celtic (R1-005, R3-079).
4. **Migration and local development both.** Historic migrations 400–200 BCE (Italy, Greece, Galatia) are real (R1-007, R5-045); Hallstatt C Atlantic distributions are not invasions (R4-012); Atlantic Celticization via long exchange is preferred over invasion *or* pure in-situ IE evolution as sole story (R3-081, R4-018).
5. **Classical vision as durable representation.** Enemy-without and worthy-opponent tropes; Poseidonian soft primitivism; Caesar/Tacitus agendas; once created, the classical vision remains in subsequent observers’ consciousness (R1-009–R1-013, R1-016–R1-021, R1-087).
6. **Nationalism and presentism.** Each generation gets the archaeology it deserves; Napoleon III, EC/Venice 1991, and post-1970 economic/world-systems Celticisms are political as well as scholarly (R1-043–R1-045). Celtomania was a necessary stage, yet romantic nonsense persists (R1-029).
7. **Self-ascription for modern Celts.** “Perhaps the only real definition of a Celt, now as in the past, is that a Celt is a person who believes him or herself to be Celtic” (R5-040). Ancient international self-conscious Celticness remains doubtful despite multi-tribe coalitions (Vercingetorix; third-century eastern hordes) (R5-041).
8. **Celt/German divide.** Rhine boundary is a convenient Roman construct; doubtful significant ethnic divide before mid-first century BCE; if a single characteristic is required, only language — and place-name maps are chronologically ill-focused (R5-025).
9. **Source-use ethic.** All historical sources biased; chronological and regional variation forbids universalized “Celtic society”; still usable if critically read (R2-076, R3-035).

**Periodization he offers for Celtic-speaking communities:** formative LBA c.1300–750 → chiefdoms 750–450 under Mediterranean impingement → systems-collapse and diaspora 450–200 (classical barbarian stereotype + “Celtic” art/material for archaeologists) → Roman absorption until Atlantic fringe alone remains free (R5-047 of 1997 densification).

## Position D — Cunliffe 2013 Atlantic Celtic / continuity densification

**Relation to Position C.** Not a repudiation of the 1997 partial-overlap synthesis, but a **Britain-and-Ireland-centred densification** in [[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]] that (a) hardens the Atlantic case for **Celtic language origin**, (b) restates what survives after the invasion “house of cards” collapses, and (c) folds religious specialists ([[druids]]) and La Tène art diffusion into a continuity-first model of the islands. Distinguish **Cunliffe 1997** from **Cunliffe 2013** when citing; do not merge into one frozen “Cunliffe view.”

**Strongest form (as Cunliffe 2013 states it):**

1. **Deconstruct the Celtic-invasion myth, retain three threads.** After rejecting the elaborated eastern-origin/invasion model, keep: (i) Celtic dialects over much of western Europe including probably Ireland and Britain in the later first millennium BCE; (ii) historic fourth–second-century BCE migrations of Celtic-speaking groups from eastern France/southern Germany into Italy and east into Balkans/Asia Minor; (iii) La Tène material culture and Celtic art as mobility of ideas/values **without necessary ethnic or linguistic identity** (R5-033).
2. **No classical or archaeological evidence of large migrant bands moving northwards or westwards** from the La Tène elite homelands into Brittany, Britain, or Ireland — though that belief remained popular until comparatively recently (R7-003). La Tène art reached north-western France, Britain, and Ireland by **elite adoption**, gifts/trade, and possibly travelling craftsmen, not mass migration (R7-004).
3. **Atlantic origin of Celtic language as working hypothesis.** Celtic may have emerged in Atlantic-facing Europe as a maritime lingua franca; phylogenetic separation from Italic sometime 4000–2500 BCE (contended absolute dates) is compatible with mature Celtic in SW Iberia by the 7th c. BCE if Tartessian is Celtic as Cunliffe reports (R5-037–R5-043, R5-049). If correct, Britain and Ireland speak Celtic dialects **by 2000 BCE** (model-dependent). See [[celtic-languages]] for the full Atlantian/Beaker model.
4. **Classical ethnonym geography is western.** Earliest Greek sources (Hecataeus, Herodotus, Ephorus) place Celts from Massalia to the Atlantic including much of Iberia — not as an eastern people who only later reached the west (R5-040). This undercuts the early-modern myth that required imagined westward sailings for which no classical source exists (R5-030).
5. **Language history ≠ ethnonym ≠ art style.** Post-1871 equation of La Tène ornament with ethnic Celts, and of British/Irish “Celtic art” with 5th–4th c. Gaulish migration, is “grossly oversimplified” (R5-032). Historic eastward Celtic-band movements explain some Danube–Anatolia place-name concentrations without licensing reverse invasions of the islands.
6. **Religious system may share western roots.** Because Celtic language may be an indigenous Atlantic-zone development and significant first-millennium incursions are denied, Cunliffe (2013) holds it a distinct possibility that the religious system of the Druids developed in the west; Caesar’s report that Druidic doctrine was invented in Britain and exported to Gaul may even be correct (R7-039). Roots of mature first-century BCE practice lie deep in prehistory (R7-040). Filed also on [[druids]].
7. **Historiographic spine of the myth.** Pezron 1703 maximally elaborates eastern-origin expansion; Lhuyd 1707 names the languages Celtic and installs dual-wave then Q-then-P invasion models influential for centuries; archaeologists fitted material culture to those models; the myths are still reiterated in some quarters after field archaeologists abandoned them (R1-029, R1-030, R5-030, R5-031, R5-072). Romantic forgeries (Ossian; Iolo Morganwg’s Gorsedd) feed later reception, not Iron Age evidence (R1-034). Camden 1586 already treated Britons as a Gaulish branch by language, customs, and religion (R1-026).

**Empirical programme (2013-specific):** Atlantic maritime connectivity archaeology; Sims-Williams/Oppenheimer Celtic place-name frequencies; phylogenetic experiments (as reported); Tartessian corpus reconsideration (as reported); elite-network transmission of La Tène art into Britain/Ireland; Arras culture as micro-case of emulation vs small-group immigration; classical source criticism of ethnonym geography and of Druid ethnography.

**Weaknesses opponents stress (record, do not adjudicate):** Atlantic Celtic origin over-reads connectivity as language; Tartessian-as-Celtic is highly contested among philologists; phylogenetic absolute dates remain unstable; “by 2000 BCE” speech is model-dependent; elite-adoption accounts of La Tène may underweight small-scale mobility; Britain-origin of Druidism remains hearsay Caesar recorded.

### The 2001 origin of "Atlantic Celtic" (*Facing the Ocean*)

Position D did not begin in 2013. In [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|*Facing the
Ocean*]] (2001) Cunliffe first proposed the term **"Atlantic Celtic"** — to
distinguish a maritime lingua franca of the Atlantic façade from the language of
the classically attested migratory Celts — arguing that a Celtic-related common
tongue developed gradually over roughly four millennia of Atlantic seaways
contact, reaching distinctive form in the Late Bronze Age when sea-lane
communication was most intense. He claimed the archaeological and linguistic
evidence "support each other without being dependent," and suggested regional
variation within the family (Lepontic, Celtiberian, "early"-trait Goidelic vs.
"developed" Brittonic/Gaulish) may reflect regional interaction patterns rather
than a strict east-to-west spread. The 2001 statement is explicitly hedged in
the source ("no doubt", "might have been") and predates the Koch Tartessian
claims that *Britain Begins* later reports; it also restates the invasionism
critique in its sharpest form — "no classical writer ever referred to the
inhabitants of Britain and Ireland as Celts," and the "thick black arrows" of
invasion maps as a self-reinforcing artifact of circular reasoning
(Cunliffe 2001). Cite the 2001, 1997, and 2013 statements separately.

## Additional position notes (within or adjacent to C / D)

- **Proto-Celtic Atlantic substratum models** (Spanish current work as Cunliffe 1997 reports): Atlantic Bronze Age substratum developing into historical Celtiberian by absorbing Mediterranean-fringe influences — parallel to La Tène emergence process, not La Tène identity (R3-067 of 1997 densification). Position D’s Atlantic lingua-franca model is a **Britain Begins 2013** extension of this family of arguments, not identical to the Spanish Celtiberian programme.
- **Vernacular literature as Iron Age window (Jackson 1964) vs Dark Age composition (Mallory; Cunliffe’s caution):** not a separate full position here, but a sub-dispute on evidence quality for Celtic social reconstruction (R1-064–R1-066 of 1997 densification).

## Stakes

- Nationalist appropriations of Iron Age pasts (France, Britain, Ireland, Belgium, Iberia, Brittany) and museum labelling (R1-031–R1-035, R1-042).
- Whether British/Irish Iron Age is “Celtic” in the Continental sense (ethnonym absent; language present) (R3-079).
- Whether Iberia counts as Celtic without La Tène (R3-058).
- IE and steppe arguments that recruit “Celts” as a migration package without separating language, material culture, and people ([[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]).
- Pan-European political uses (Venice 1991; EC-era world-systems Celticism) (R1-043, R1-044).
- Tourism, revival, and emotional Atlantic Celticism vs scholarly construct critique (R5-040, R5-041).

## Current State

**Open.** Late-twentieth and twenty-first-century Celtic studies remain polarized between refined culture-history, Cunliffe-style partial-overlap synthesis (1997), Atlantic-origin continuity densification (Cunliffe 2013), and strong constructivist scepticism. aDNA (post-Cunliffe 1997; only lightly and cautiously handled in *Britain Begins*) has added a fourth evidence class; any genetic claim must be date-stamped from genetic sources and still obey four-way non-identity. This wiki records positions with attribution; promotion of any equation to wiki voice requires independent multi-class convergence documented elsewhere. Distinguish [[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]] (Position C) from [[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]] (Position D).

## Historiography of the dispute

The debate is as old as the construct. Cunliffe’s 1997 narrative arc — classical vision → oblivion/awakening → Celtomania → archaeological equation (1871–72) → invasionism → domesticated *celticus domesticus* → economic/world-systems Celt → late-century challenge — is the backbone of how this wiki currently historicizes the dispute (R1-001–R1-047, R1-087, R5-041–R5-048 of the 1997 densification). [[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]] retells the Pezron–Lhuyd–post-1871 spine with Britain-focused stakes and advances the Atlantic Celtic working hypothesis as the positive alternative after the invasion house of cards falls (R5-030–R5-033, R5-049, R5-072). Further densification when Chapman, Collis, James, Koch, and later critical monographs are ingested.
