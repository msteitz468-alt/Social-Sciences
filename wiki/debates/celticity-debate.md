# Celticity Debate

## Shared Factual Ground

The following are not seriously disputed as bare historical facts (though their interpretation is):

- Greco-Roman writers used ethnonyms (*Keltoi*, *Celtae*, *Galli*, *Galatae*) for various northern and western European communities and for Galatians in Asia Minor (Cunliffe 1997, Ch. 1; R1-007, R1-086).
- A Celtic branch of Indo-European is real and reconstructible; Continental epigraphy and Insular medieval literatures attest it (see [[celtic-languages]]; R1-048–R1-050).
- [[hallstatt-culture]] and [[la-tene-culture]] are conventional material-culture constructs with type-sites ([[hallstatt]], [[la-tene]]), seriation history, and real assemblage patterning (R1-068–R1-072).
- In 1871 scholars matched La Tène-type Champagne/Neuchâtel assemblages to Marzabotto burials and treated them as the material culture of historically described Celts (R1-036, R1-070).
- Modern Celtic national and cultural identities exist on the Atlantic façade and beyond (R5-040).
- Cunliffe 1997 (and later scholarship) notes incomplete spatial and temporal overlap between Celtic speech and La Tène material culture (R1-060, R3-058, R5-042).

**What is disputed** is how these layers map onto each other: whether “the Celts” name one historical people expanding largely by migration; whether the term is a modern scholarly (or classical) construct with little emic unity; how Iberia, Britain, and Ireland fit continental packages; and what role nationalism and present politics play in the image.

This page records positions; it does **not** adjudicate. Primary densification source for Position C: [[cunliffe-ancient-celts-1997]] ([[cunliffe-barry]]). Related: [[primordialism-constructivism-ethnicity-debate]], [[nationalism-and-archaeology]], [[celts]], [[celtic-languages]].

## Position A — Culture-historical Celtic package

**Strongest form:** Language + La Tène (and often Hallstatt) material culture + classical *Keltoi*/Galli form one historical people or people-complex expanding largely by migration and invasion. Archaeology after 1871 “identified” the historic Celts; invasionist models (Hawkes’s hillfort waves; Bosch-Gimpera’s “Two Celtic Waves in Spain,” 1939; Rhys’s Goidelic then Brythonic waves) explained cultural change as folk movement, modelling on classical Celtic migrations and modern imperial expansion (R1-036–R1-039, R1-056, R1-070–R1-071). La Tène culture was regarded as “the archaeological expression of the Celts” (R1-071). Simple invasionist theories remained common until the 1960s and persist in popular literature (R1-039).

**Empirical programme (as historicized by Cunliffe):** cemetery and settlement archaeology across Champagne, Switzerland, Bohemia, the Po Valley; Déchelette’s pan-European La Tène synthesis; equation of material horizons with Livy/Polybius migration narratives.

**Weaknesses opponents stress:** circular language–migration–material reasoning (R1-038); paucity of La Tène material where Celts are historically attested (Greece, Asia Minor) (R1-037); Iberian Celtiberian speech and ethnonyms without La Tène package (R3-058); Britain/Ireland never called “Celtic” by ancient authors (R3-079).

## Position B — Sceptical Celticity

**Strongest form:** “Celtic” as a single archaeological culture or ancient ethnic identity is largely a modern scholarly (and classical Mediterranean) construct. Classical ethnonyms are external labels serving “enemy without,” soft-primitivist, and conquest-justifying agendas. Equating art style or cemetery type with ethnicity is circular. The term is so abused by nationalism, tourism, and pan-European myth-making as to be useless for serious analysis except as a history of reception. Cunliffe names this pole the “politically correct view” that the term is so abused as to be useless except for book sales, and notes Chapman 1992 *The Celts: The Construction of a Myth* as the most challenging work on validity of the concept (R1-046, R5-048). Extreme scepticism can extend to denying any useful content to “Celtic” beyond modern identity politics.

**Empirical programme:** source criticism of classical ethnography; constructivist ethnicity theory; deconstruction of nineteenth-century Celtomania and nationalist archaeology; insistence on regional archaeological sequences without pan-Celtic ethnic labels.

**Weaknesses opponents stress:** erases real comparative linguistic unity; discards classical testimony of named peoples and movements; leaves no vocabulary for genuine long-distance similarities in art, warfare, and social forms that specialists still need to discuss.

## Position C — Cunliffe 1997 partial-overlap synthesis

**Strongest form (as Cunliffe states it):** Classical sources are biased but indispensable; Celtic languages are real; Hallstatt/La Tène packages are real; **overlaps are partial and historical**. There were peoples known as Celts whose movements and behaviour are reflected in contemporary sources; they spoke a language still spoken/taught on western fringes; some communities developed unique art and distinctive material culture that spread through Europe; in Ireland echoes survive via written versions of oral tradition — “These threads are real and are worthy of our attention” (R1-046). Both New Celtomania (pan-European past comforting amid ethnic division) and terminological nihilism are sterile in extremity (R1-046).

**Key methodological moves (attributed):**

1. **Uncouple language and La Tène.** Celtic speech earlier and wider than La Tène; defining Celt by language uncouples the ethnonym from the archaeological culture (R1-060). The two common definitions — language and La Tène material culture/art — are different attributes, not coterminous (R5-042).
2. **Iberia as decisive non-identity case.** Classical Celtiberians + Celtic speech + social resemblances without La Tène type → La Tène must be dissociated from “Celtic” (R3-058). Classical authors equated temperate and Iberian “Celts” via language, valour, and hospitality, not material package (R3-074).
3. **Insular ethnonym gap.** No ancient author called Britain/Ireland inhabitants Celts; language established by sixth century BCE on Cunliffe’s reading; Pytheas does not treat Pretani as Celtic (R1-005, R3-079).
4. **Migration and local development both.** Historic migrations 400–200 BCE (Italy, Greece, Galatia) are real (R1-007, R5-045); Hallstatt C Atlantic distributions are not invasions (R4-012); Atlantic Celticization via long exchange is preferred over invasion *or* pure in-situ IE evolution as sole story (R3-081, R4-018).
5. **Classical vision as durable representation.** Enemy-without and worthy-opponent tropes; Poseidonian soft primitivism; Caesar/Tacitus agendas; once created, the classical vision remains in subsequent observers’ consciousness (R1-009–R1-013, R1-016–R1-021, R1-087).
6. **Nationalism and presentism.** Each generation gets the archaeology it deserves; Napoleon III, EC/Venice 1991, and post-1970 economic/world-systems Celticisms are political as well as scholarly (R1-043–R1-045). Celtomania was a necessary stage, yet romantic nonsense persists (R1-029).
7. **Self-ascription for modern Celts.** “Perhaps the only real definition of a Celt, now as in the past, is that a Celt is a person who believes him or herself to be Celtic” (R5-040). Ancient international self-conscious Celticness remains doubtful despite multi-tribe coalitions (Vercingetorix; third-century eastern hordes) (R5-041).
8. **Celt/German divide.** Rhine boundary is a convenient Roman construct; doubtful significant ethnic divide before mid-first century BCE; if a single characteristic is required, only language — and place-name maps are chronologically ill-focused (R5-025).
9. **Source-use ethic.** All historical sources biased; chronological and regional variation forbids universalized “Celtic society”; still usable if critically read (R2-076, R3-035).

**Periodization he offers for Celtic-speaking communities:** formative LBA c.1300–750 → chiefdoms 750–450 under Mediterranean impingement → systems-collapse and diaspora 450–200 (classical barbarian stereotype + “Celtic” art/material for archaeologists) → Roman absorption until Atlantic fringe alone remains free (R5-047).

## Additional position notes (within or adjacent to C)

- **Proto-Celtic Atlantic substratum models** (Spanish current work as Cunliffe reports): Atlantic Bronze Age substratum developing into historical Celtiberian by absorbing Mediterranean-fringe influences — parallel to La Tène emergence process, not La Tène identity (R3-067).
- **Vernacular literature as Iron Age window (Jackson 1964) vs Dark Age composition (Mallory; Cunliffe’s caution):** not a separate full position here, but a sub-dispute on evidence quality for Celtic social reconstruction (R1-064–R1-066).

## Stakes

- Nationalist appropriations of Iron Age pasts (France, Britain, Ireland, Belgium, Iberia, Brittany) and museum labelling (R1-031–R1-035, R1-042).
- Whether British/Irish Iron Age is “Celtic” in the Continental sense (ethnonym absent; language present) (R3-079).
- Whether Iberia counts as Celtic without La Tène (R3-058).
- IE and steppe arguments that recruit “Celts” as a migration package without separating language, material culture, and people ([[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]).
- Pan-European political uses (Venice 1991; EC-era world-systems Celticism) (R1-043, R1-044).
- Tourism, revival, and emotional Atlantic Celticism vs scholarly construct critique (R5-040, R5-041).

## Current State

**Open.** Late-twentieth and twenty-first-century Celtic studies remain polarized between refined culture-history, Cunliffe-style partial-overlap synthesis, and strong constructivist scepticism. aDNA (post-Cunliffe 1997) has added a fourth evidence class Cunliffe did not use; any genetic claim must be date-stamped from genetic sources and still obey four-way non-identity. This wiki records positions with attribution; promotion of any equation to wiki voice requires independent multi-class convergence documented elsewhere.

## Historiography of the dispute

The debate is as old as the construct. Cunliffe’s own narrative arc — classical vision → oblivion/awakening → Celtomania → archaeological equation (1871–72) → invasionism → domesticated *celticus domesticus* → economic/world-systems Celt → late-century challenge — is the backbone of how this wiki currently historicizes the dispute (R1-001–R1-047, R1-087, R5-041–R5-048). Further densification when Chapman, Collis, James, and later critical monographs are ingested.
