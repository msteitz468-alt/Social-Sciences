# The "Saving Muslim Women" Debate

The post-9/11 dispute over whether Western feminist, humanitarian, and military attention to Muslim women's oppression is emancipation or empire. Crystallized by the use of Afghan women's rights to justify the 2001 Afghanistan intervention (Laura Bush's radio address, November 17, 2001: "The fight against terrorism is also a fight for the rights and dignity of women") and by Lila [[abu-lughod-lila|Abu-Lughod]]'s response, the 2002 *American Anthropologist* article expanded into *[[abu-lughod-do-muslim-women-2013|Do Muslim Women Need Saving?]]* (2013). Primary ingested source so far: Abu-Lughod's own book — the interventionist positions below are recorded largely as she presents them; strengthen from their own texts when ingested.

## Position 1 — Interventionist / rescue feminism

Muslim women suffer distinctive, religiously and culturally rooted oppression (veiling mandates, honor violence, forced marriage), and the morally serious response is advocacy, pressure, and where necessary force. Carriers: Nicholas Kristof and Sheryl WuDunn (*Half the Sky*: gender inequality as "the paramount moral challenge" of the century, on the model of abolition); Kwame Anthony Appiah (*The Honor Code*: moral revolutions via honor-shaming, foot-binding as precedent); Ayaan Hirsi Ali (Islam itself as the problem; emancipation through exit and atheism); Susan Moller Okin ("Is Multiculturalism Bad for Women?"); Women for Afghan Women board member Esther Hyneman ("a bloodbath if we leave Afghanistan"). At its strongest: it refuses relativist paralysis, names real violence, and mobilizes resources and legal reform for victims whom local institutions fail.

## Position 2 — Anthropological / postcolonial critique

The rescue frame is the latest phase of [[gendered-orientalism]]: it culturalizes suffering whose causes are political and economic (war economies, structural adjustment, occupation, Cold War legacies — Deniz Kandiyoti on Afghanistan's "new and more brutal forms of subjugation" produced by a criminal economy; Hirschkind and Mahmood on CIA funding of the mujahideen), erases Western complicity and domestic gender violence (US DOJ one-in-six rape statistic), reproduces the missionary/colonial structure (Spivak's "white men saving brown women from brown men"; Leila Ahmed's "colonial feminism"), and denies Muslim women's [[agency]] and the diversity of their desires ([[mahmood-saba|Mahmood]]: "the desire for freedom and liberation is a historically situated desire"). Abu-Lughod's own position adds: terms like "oppression," "choice," and "freedom" are "blunt instruments"; the productive question is what work the concepts "Muslim women's rights" and "the oppressed Muslim woman" do in the world, and for whom; the alternative to both rescue and relativism is attention to "registers of humanity" and to what particular women say they want. Extends to the [[honor-crime]] category and to critiques of "pulp nonfiction" abuse memoirs.

## Position 3 — Internal reform through Islamic frameworks

Afghan and other Muslim women activists divided at the November 2001 Bonn conference: RAWA (secular-democratic since 1977, opposed both the Taliban and the US bombing) rejected conciliation with Islamic governance, while Fatima Gailani argued Islam had to be reform's starting point. Islamic feminist organizations — Sisters in Islam (Malaysia, NGO 1993), Musawah (launched Kuala Lumpur, February 2009; CEDAW–Muslim family law compatibility research), WISE/Global Muslim Women's Shura Council ("Jihad against Violence," 2009; rights grounded in the six objectives of Shari'a) — pursue equality within religious idioms. Ziba Mir-Hosseini calls Islamic feminism "the unwanted child of political Islam" yet defends it against both patriarchal interpretation and "neo-colonialist hegemonic" feminism. Whether "Islamic feminism" is coherent or an oxymoron is itself an open sub-dispute. Abu-Lughod is sympathetic but asks (2013) whether these initiatives can be understood apart from "global governance," donor funding, and class privilege.

## Notable sub-disputes

- **Abu-Lughod vs. Unni Wikan** over the Fadime Sahindal case (Sweden, 2002): Wikan attributes the killing to immigrant rootedness in old rural cultures; Abu-Lughod charges her with misreading the evidence of racial harassment and media-exposure shame (see [[honor-crime]]).
- **Hyneman vs. Ann Jones** (2010, around the Time Bibi Aysha cover) on whether the Taliban were being singled out among similarly misogynous US-backed factions.
- **Within feminism**: whether "woman" is a coherent global analytic category (liberal universalists vs. third-world/women-of-color feminists).

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides accept that severe violence and legal disability affect many women in Muslim-majority societies (Taliban edicts; documented killings; forced-marriage cases) and that some rescue literature is fabricated (the Norma Khouri hoax). Interventionists read the violence as the phenomenon and the hoaxes as marginal; critics read the hoax-sustaining market as itself the phenomenon and the violence as multi-causal. Neither side, at its serious core, denies the other's base facts — the dispute is over causal attribution (culture/religion vs. politics/economy) and over who may act.
