## Positions

**Position A — American origins (Thor Heyerdahl, 1952, 1961, 1965).** [[heyerdahl-thor|Heyerdahl]], a Norwegian zoologist, argued that Polynesia was settled from the Americas, not from the west. His 1947 Kon-Tiki balsa-raft voyage was staged as a practical demonstration that a South American population could have drifted to Polynesia on prevailing currents and winds. Heyerdahl led the 1955–56 Norwegian Archaeological Expedition to southeastern Polynesia — primarily Rapa Nui, with additional work at Rapa Iti, Ra'ivavae, and Hiva Oa — staffed by four professional archaeologists (E. Ferdon, W. Mulloy, A. Skjølsvold, C. Smith), on the premise that Rapa Nui monumental architecture and statuary (the Rano Raraku quarry, the Ahu Vinapu temple complex) resembled South American (Tiahuanaco/Incan) forms closely enough to indicate a trans-Pacific American origin for Polynesian culture.

**Position B — West-to-east settlement from Island Southeast Asia (Suggs 1960; Golson 1965; the postwar disciplinary consensus).** Robert Suggs, who conducted the first stratigraphic excavations in the Marquesas (discovering the Ha'atuatua dune site, 1956), directly attacked Heyerdahl's reconstruction with a detailed parody pointing out its chronological impossibilities — Heyerdahl's proposed Peruvian voyagers arriving circa AD 380 but using material culture (e.g., Incan forms) that did not exist in South America until after AD 750. Jack Golson, from stratigraphic evidence at Rapa Nui and elsewhere, publicly refuted Heyerdahl's South American-origins claim, holding that the archaeological evidence supported settlement "by ancestral Polynesians from the west, with subsequent internal cultural developments" (Golson 1965:78). This position rests on Austronesian linguistic affiliation, Pacific-wide stratigraphic sequences, and internally coherent cultural development traceable to Island Southeast Asia via the [[lapita-cultural-complex|Lapita cultural complex]] — the same synthesis that resolved the broader the "Problem of Polynesian Origins".

## Shared Factual Ground

Both positions agree Rapa Nui's monumental statuary and temple platforms require explanation and that any settlement-origins claim must be evaluated against controlled stratigraphic and chronological evidence. They disagree entirely on the direction of settlement and on whether architectural resemblance constitutes evidence, given that Heyerdahl's comparanda span incompatible periods within South America itself.

## Resolution

Resolved-by-consensus. Per Patrick Kirch's historiographical assessment (Kirch 2017), "What the Norwegian Expedition failed to do... was convince anyone of the veracity of Heyerdahl's South American origins hypothesis" — the discipline uniformly rejected the American-origins/colonization-direction theory, on the strength of Suggs's and Golson's rebuttals and the broader postwar archaeological/linguistic consensus for a west-to-east settlement route.

## A Separate, Narrower Claim: Some Precontact Polynesia–Americas Contact

This resolution must not be conflated with a distinct and much narrower finding that has re-emerged in recent decades: evidence for *some* precontact contact between Polynesia and the Americas, running in the opposite direction (Polynesian voyagers reaching South America, not American settlers reaching Polynesia). The clearest evidence is the presence of the South American domesticate sweet potato (*Ipomoea batatas*) in Eastern Polynesia before European contact — molecular-genetically confirmed (Roullier et al. 2013) and archaeologically dated (a carbonized sweet potato at the Tangatatau rockshelter, Mangaia, no later than the late 14th century AD). Kirch treats this specific finding of Polynesia–South America contact as "incontrovertible" (his word, attributed) — but this is Kirch's own strong characterization of a single-line-of-evidence claim, not full wiki-voice given the source diversity that convergence normally requires.

Two related sub-claims remain genuinely open, unresolved disputes and should not be read as reviving Heyerdahl's colonization-direction thesis:
- Storey et al. (2007) reported pre-Columbian chicken bones at El Arenal, Chile (calibrated AD 1321–1407), interpreted as evidence of a Polynesian introduction of chicken to South America — disputed by Gongora et al. (2008), defended by Storey et al. (2008). Dispute type: empirical, open.
- Jones and Klar (2005) attributed sewn-plank Channel Islands (California) canoe technology to Polynesian contact — disputed by Anderson (2006) and Arnold (2007). Dispute type: interpretive/empirical, open.

None of these narrower, still-contested contact claims vindicates Heyerdahl's original thesis that Polynesia itself was *settled* from the Americas; that thesis remains rejected by the discipline.
