# Ethnomethodology vs. Conventional Sociology

A standing methodological dispute set off by [[garfinkel-harold|Garfinkel]]'s
*[[garfinkel-studies-ethnomethodology-1967|Studies in Ethnomethodology]]* (1967) over
whether [[ethnomethodology]] supplements conventional (especially quantitative and
[[structural-functionalism|structural-functional]]) sociology or undermines it. Positions
are recorded, not adjudicated.

## Position A — Ethnomethodology specifies method's hidden conditions

On this view, Garfinkel does not reject sociological method but exposes the common-sense
reasoning it silently relies on. The clinic-records studies (Ch. 6–7) argue that coding and
measurement depend ineliminably on coders' membership knowledge and on
[[etcetera-principle|ad hoc "et cetera" practices]], so that statistical "results" become
sociological "findings" only when the analyst supplies an interpretive theory. Far from
nihilism, this is presented as making method's actual workings visible: "logic, methodology,
adequate demonstration" are "glosses for organizational phenomena." Members are practical
methodologists, not "cultural dopes," and studying their methods is a legitimate — indeed
prior — sociological task.

## Position B — Ethnomethodology dissolves explanatory sociology

On this view, by treating every finding as an occasioned, [[indexicality|indexical]]
accomplishment and refusing to offer "correctives" ("They are useless when they are done as
ironies"), ethnomethodology forfeits the ability to explain social patterns or to test
claims against evidence. Its deliberately estranging vocabulary is read as obscurantism; its
demonstrations, being uncontrolled ("aids to a sluggish imagination"), establish nothing
generalizable; and its symmetry between "science" and "divination" is read as corrosive of
sociology's warrant. On this account the program describes the obvious in an unfalsifiable
idiom.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides agree that coders and survey analysts use tacit background knowledge to apply
their instruments, and that Garfinkel's demonstrations reliably provoke the reactions he
reports. They disagree over what follows: Position A reads the tacit work as a phenomenon
method must acknowledge and study; Position B reads it as a practical limitation that
better-specified procedures manage well enough, and denies that acknowledging it requires
abandoning explanatory or quantitative sociology.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-09)

The dispute remains `open`. Its most durable issue — that measurement and coding rest on
interpretive work that cannot be fully formalized — has been partly absorbed by mainstream
methodology (attention to inter-coder reliability, operationalization, and reflexivity),
while ethnomethodology's stronger claim, that this dissolves the objectivity of
sociological findings, has not won general assent. The program's own most-cited empirical
study, the [[garfinkel-agnes-study|Agnes case]], carries an independent evidential
complication (subject deception) that both sides have used.
