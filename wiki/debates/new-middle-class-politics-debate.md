# The Political Destiny of the New Middle Class

Whether the propertyless salaried strata — the [[new-middle-class]] of managers, salaried
professionals, salespeople, and clerks — constitute a distinct political force, and if so of
what kind, was a live question of twentieth-century social theory. [[mills-c-wright|C. Wright
Mills]]'s *[[mills-white-collar-1951|White Collar]]* (1951) is the fullest American intervention.

## Shared ground

All parties accept the structural fact: a large stratum of propertyless, non-manual, salaried
employees grew rapidly through industrial capitalism (in the U.S., ~6% of the labor force in
1870 to ~25% in 1940), too numerous to ignore and too heterogeneous to classify easily. The
dispute is over what follows politically. All sides also accept that the stratum's own
subjective consciousness must be distinguished from its objective position — a Weberian
methodological point Mills insists on against those who infer "no classes in America" from the
mere absence of class-consciousness.

## Positions (strongest form each)

**Proletarian absorption (classical Marxist).** Because the salaried employee is propertyless,
his interests objectively converge with the wage-worker's; rationalization and deskilling make
white-collar work "more factory-like"; in time he will recognize this and join the proletariat.
Held, in varying forms, by Marxist party statisticians and theorists (Kautsky, Corey, and
others) who first "discovered" the stratum as a recruitment problem.

**Independent / balance class.** The new middle class is large and pivotal enough to form an
autonomous political force, or at least to act as the balance-wheel that stabilizes capitalist
democracy between big business and organized labor. Associated with liberal and technocratic
theorists (e.g. Alfred Bingham); Mills also engages the "indispensability" version (Burnham,
Lasswell) that technical necessity confers political power.

**Bourgeois shock troops / fascist susceptibility.** The status-conscious salaried stratum,
threatened by proletarianization yet refusing a worker identity, is mobilized against labor and,
in economic crisis, is peculiarly vulnerable to fascism. The Weimar analysis (Lederer, Speier,
Kracauer; and the Gurland–Kirchheimer–Neumann account of Nazism's capture of German small-business
and salaried frustration) that Mills absorbed through [[gerth-hans|Gerth]] and cites as a
cautionary comparison for the American case.

**Rearguard (Mills's position).** Mills rejects all three. The new middle class is a **"dependent
variable"** in the structure of power: it "will not go proletarian" (no political proletariat
exists in America, and propertylessness has coincided with rising living standards, not
immiseration); it "will not go middle class" (no unified middle-class policy is possible given
internal material contradictions); it will not form an independent bloc (no unity, leadership, or
opportunity); and it will not be a balance-wheel (no will to choose before a winner is apparent).
It is "a chorus, too afraid to grumble, too hysterical in their applause," following prestige in
the short run and power in the long run "since prestige is determined by power." Mills's closing
image: "on the political market-place of American society, the new middle classes are up for
sale... So far, nobody has made a serious bid." He rejects both "a pure and automatic democracy
of numbers" and "a magic leap from occupational function to political power."

## Relation to other disputes

Bears on the "managerial revolution" question (whether managers displaced owners as a ruling
class — Mills says no; see [[managerial-demiurge]]), on [[social-class-weberian|Weberian vs.
Marxian class analysis]], and on the [[political-indifference]] Mills places at the center of
postwar politics.

## Current status

**Open.** No stratum-wide political identity of the kind the classical theories predicted
emerged; Mills's "rearguard" diagnosis has worn better than the proletarianization thesis, but
the category itself dissolved into finer distinctions (professional-managerial class, precarious
service labor) that reframed rather than resolved the question. Curator's note (2026-07-08): the
debate is recorded here without adjudication beyond noting which predictions the twentieth
century did not bear out.
