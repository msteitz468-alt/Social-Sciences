# Unthinkable Haitian Revolution Debate

Dispute over how the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]] entered (or failed to enter) Western and world-historical consciousness: as [[unthinkable-history|unthinkable]] in its time, as a later **non-event**, or as a known but marginalized event. Seeded and densified from [[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]] 1995; opposing/complicating positions require further ingests.

## Position 1 — Trouillot (unthinkable + formulas of silence)

*(Attributed — *[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]*, Ch.3.)*

1. **Ontology, not empirics:** The claim that enslaved Africans could not envision or strategize freedom rested on an implicit organization of the world (degrees of humanity; scientific racism already in Enlightenment landscapes), shared widely among whites and many non-white plantation owners. Variations never included a successful revolutionary Black state.
2. **Unthinkable as it happened:** Contemporaries could read news only through ready-made categories incompatible with a slave revolution. Planter discourse (La Barre 1790: revolt “impossible”) is emblematic; no plan for general insurrection.
3. **Formulas after the fact:** [[formulas-of-silence|Erasure]] (generalists) and **banalization** (specialists) continue the same formal tropes into professional historiography — yellow fever as victor; French intrigue as necessary and sufficient; Age of Revolutions silence; Haiti as pretext/litmus test rather than history for its own sake.
4. **Structural, not conspiracy:** Effective silencing is structural continuity with Renaissance/Enlightenment ontological order, not a plot. Even “sound” post-1940s scholarship may not have broken those iron bonds.
5. **Method:** “How does one write a history of the impossible?” Issue is epistemological more than simple presentist ideology.

C.L.R. James (*Black Jacobins*) appears as counter-discourse with limited structural impact (Trouillot).

## Position 2 — Complicating / opposing strands (stub)

Not yet filled from primary counter-sources in this vault. Anticipated lines (placeholders only — **not** wiki-voice verdicts):

- Black Atlantic and Haitian narrators who *did* think and archive the revolution.
- Abolitionist and diplomatic networks that registered it as real (even when hostile).
- Historians who treat silence as later imperial convenience or archive inaccessibility more than cognitive impossibility.
- Critiques that “unthinkable” overstates Western consensus or flattens specialist gains since James/Fouchard/etc.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

Independence of Haiti in 1804 and the prior mass slave uprising are not in dispute. The dispute is about **cognitive-political frames**, **historiographic practice**, and **degrees/kinds of silencing** — not whether the revolution occurred ([[historicity-1-and-2|historicity 1]]).
