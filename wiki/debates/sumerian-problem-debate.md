# The Sumerian Problem: Origin of the Sumerians

## Statement of the Dispute

Where did Sumerian-speakers, attested in southern Mesopotamia from the late Uruk
period (writing appears c. 3300 BCE), come from? Because Sumerian is a language
isolate with no known relative, the usual comparative-linguistic homeland arguments
available for Indo-European or Semitic languages are unavailable, leaving the question
open to archaeological and circumstantial argument alone (see [[sumerians]] for the
language's typological status).

## Position 1: Migration During the Uruk Period

Sumerian-speakers arrived in Mesopotamia during the Uruk period (mid-4th millennium
BCE), into an already-populated land whose earlier (Ubaid-period and before)
inhabitants spoke a different language or languages, attested only by a residue of
non-Sumerian, non-Semitic place- and personal-names.

## Position 2: Already Present Since Ubaid (or Earlier)

Sumerian, or its linguistic ancestor, was already spoken in southern Mesopotamia by
Ubaid times at the latest — i.e., the "Sumerians" may have "always" been in Iraq, with
no discrete migration event to identify. [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] states
his own leaning toward this position, while explicitly framing it as his personal view
within an unresolved dispute, not as settled fact.

## Evidence and Arguments

Roux marshals several arguments against Position 1: the first appearance of Sumerian
writing at the *end* of the Uruk period does not prove the language was not spoken
earlier (writing systems routinely postdate the languages they record); non-Sumerian/
non-Semitic place-names do not necessarily indicate a displaced prior population; the
Uruk-period change in pottery style most likely reflects the adoption of mass
production rather than an invading population; and postwar excavations across Turkey,
Iran, Baluchistan, Afghanistan, and Central Asia found nothing resembling Uruk or
Jemdat Nasr material culture, nor any Sumerian inscriptions, abroad — used as negative
evidence against an external migration route for the Sumerians specifically.

Tracing material continuity backward, Roux links Uruk/Jemdat Nasr material culture to
6th–5th-millennium-BCE antecedents in northern Iraq (the Choga Mami excavations connect
the Samarra culture to the early stages of the Ubaid culture at Eridu and Hajji
Muhammad). He explicitly cautions, however, against equating the Samarrans with either
the Sumerians or the Ubaidians "on the sole basis of their pottery" — flagging the
pots-≠-people trap directly, even while making his own case for continuity.

## Shared Factual Ground

Both positions agree that: Sumerian is a language isolate offering no comparative clue
to an external homeland; Sumerian literature's setting (rivers, marshes, reeds,
tamarisks, palms) reads as indigenous to southern Iraq; Uruk/Jemdat Nasr material
culture shows no archaeological signature of invasion or destruction layer separating
it from the preceding Ubaid culture; and physical-anthropological ("racial") evidence
is useless for adjudicating the question (see [[sumerians]] Identity Cautions).

## Current Status

Open. Roux (1992) — a single-author narrative synthesis now three decades old —
represents the fullest treatment of the debate in the wiki's collection to date; no
independent aDNA or later archaeological-linguistic reassessment has yet been ingested
to adjudicate or update either position. Flag for revisiting when a more recent
Mesopotamian archaeology or historical-linguistics source is ingested.
