# The Sumerian Problem: Origin of the Sumerians

## Statement of the Dispute

Where did Sumerian-speakers, attested in southern Mesopotamia from the late Uruk
period (writing appears c. 3300 BCE), come from? Because Sumerian is a language
isolate with no known relative, the usual comparative-linguistic homeland arguments
available for Indo-European or Semitic languages are unavailable, leaving the question
open to archaeological and circumstantial argument alone (see [[sumerians]] for the
language's typological status).

## Position 1: Migration During the Uruk Period

Sumerian-speakers arrived in Mesopotamia during the Uruk period (mid-4th millennium
BCE), into an already-populated land whose earlier (Ubaid-period and before)
inhabitants spoke a different language or languages, attested only by a residue of
non-Sumerian, non-Semitic place- and personal-names.

## Position 2: Already Present Since Ubaid (or Earlier)

Sumerian, or its linguistic ancestor, was already spoken in southern Mesopotamia by
Ubaid times at the latest — i.e., the "Sumerians" may have "always" been in Iraq, with
no discrete migration event to identify. [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] states
his own leaning toward this position, while explicitly framing it as his personal view
within an unresolved dispute, not as settled fact.

## Evidence and Arguments

Roux marshals several arguments against Position 1: the first appearance of Sumerian
writing at the *end* of the Uruk period does not prove the language was not spoken
earlier (writing systems routinely postdate the languages they record); non-Sumerian/
non-Semitic place-names do not necessarily indicate a displaced prior population; the
Uruk-period change in pottery style most likely reflects the adoption of mass
production rather than an invading population; and postwar excavations across Turkey,
Iran, Baluchistan, Afghanistan, and Central Asia found nothing resembling Uruk or
Jemdat Nasr material culture, nor any Sumerian inscriptions, abroad — used as negative
evidence against an external migration route for the Sumerians specifically.

Tracing material continuity backward, Roux links Uruk/Jemdat Nasr material culture to
6th–5th-millennium-BCE antecedents in northern Iraq (the Choga Mami excavations connect
the Samarra culture to the early stages of the Ubaid culture at Eridu and Hajji
Muhammad). He explicitly cautions, however, against equating the Samarrans with either
the Sumerians or the Ubaidians "on the sole basis of their pottery" — flagging the
pots-≠-people trap directly, even while making his own case for continuity.

## Shared Factual Ground

Both positions agree that: Sumerian is a language isolate offering no comparative clue
to an external homeland; Sumerian literature's setting (rivers, marshes, reeds,
tamarisks, palms) reads as indigenous to southern Iraq; Uruk/Jemdat Nasr material
culture shows no archaeological signature of invasion or destruction layer separating
it from the preceding Ubaid culture; and physical-anthropological ("racial") evidence
is useless for adjudicating the question (see [[sumerians]] Identity Cautions).

## History of the Debate (per Potts 1997)

[[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts (1997, Ch. II)]] supplies the debate's intellectual history, absent from the wiki until now. The Sumerians were essentially unknown in the West until Ernest de Sarzec's excavations at Tello from 1877; Jules Oppert proposed a non-Semitic "Sumerian" people in 1869, confirming Edward Hincks's 1850 suspicion that cuneiform's creators were neither Assyrians nor Babylonians. (The biblical "Shinar," long identified with Sumer, is now derived by Zadok (1984) from "Shanhara," the Kassite-period western name for Babylonia.)

Milestones of Position 1 (later arrival): E. A. Speiser (1930) argued the oldest attested southern toponyms bore non-Sumerian names, evidence of pre-Sumerian occupation. B. Landsberger (1944) elaborated this with far greater rigor: key toponyms (Ur, Uruk, Larsa, Adab, Nippur, Sippar) plus a long list of core occupational and cultural vocabulary (engar "farmer," apin "plough," nagar "carpenter," bahar "potter," etc.) were loanwords from a substrate language whose speakers he called "Proto-Euphrateans." Falkenstein (1959) and Kramer (1963) accepted non-Sumerian priority; Kramer identified the Proto-Euphrateans with the Ubaid population and dated Sumerian arrival to the Late Uruk period (his further Caspian-homeland hypothesis is no longer taken seriously).

Milestones of Position 2 (continuity): Henri Frankfort (*Archaeology and the Sumerian Problem*, 1932) argued backward from the unequivocally Sumerian Early Dynastic through Jamdat Nasr and Uruk iconographic and architectural continuity to Sumerian presence from the start of the Ubaid period ("Since we have found them to be Sumerians in the early dynastic period, we must take them to be Sumerians in the earlier periods also"), a view he maintained in *The Birth of Civilization in the Ancient Near East* (1956). Joan Oates's 1953/1960 analysis of southern Ubaid material argued strongly for ceramic and temple-architecture continuity from early Ubaid into Sumerian times (the Eridu temple sequence), renaming the southern phases Ubaid 1–4. Seton Lloyd's 1936 claim that Frankfort's view commanded majority assent is judged by Potts "much too sweeping"; Moortgat (1945) rejected the craniometric evidence entirely and thought the question unresolvable.

### The settlement-environment strand

Potts also connects the debate to early-settlement geography. Frankfort, Watelin (1931) and Perkins (1949) held the south emerged from marsh/submergence only gradually; Nissen (1988) invoked Ubaid-period sea levels up to 2.75 m above modern to explain sparse early settlement — but Sanlaville's (1989) sea-level curve shows Ubaid-period levels *below* modern, and Oates (1991, 1993) rejects the sea-level explanation. Alluvial burial is the observational culprit: the 1983 Tell Oueili sounding revealed "Ubaid 0" occupation more than 5 m deep, showing settlement substantially precedes the formerly "first" Ubaid 1.

### Potts's synthesis

Potts (1997) argues for a **multi-origin, multilingual original population**: hunter-gatherers bearing the Arabian bifacial tradition entering from the north Arabian desert (identical lithics found at Ur, near Eridu, Tell Oueili, Tello); agriculturalists with Choga Mami Transitional pottery and irrigation knowledge entering from the east; and einkorn-cultivating, Hassuna-pottery-linked groups from the north — co-existing ethnic and linguistic groups with different subsistence strategies. On this reading the Sumerian/non-Sumerian place-name split reflects original diversity rather than a datable Sumerian immigration event.

## Current Status

Open. Potts (1997) now provides the fullest treatment in the collection, including the
debate's full historiography (Oppert → Speiser → Frankfort → Landsberger →
Falkenstein/Kramer → Oates) and his own multi-origin synthesis; Roux (1992) supplies
the continuity-leaning narrative treatment. No independent aDNA or later
archaeological-linguistic reassessment has yet been ingested to adjudicate or update
either position. Flag for revisiting when a more recent Mesopotamian archaeology or
historical-linguistics source is ingested.
