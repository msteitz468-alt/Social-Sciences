# The Mesopotamian Temple-State Debate

## Statement of the Dispute

How much of the early Sumerian (third-millennium BCE) economy did the temple institution own and control? Was the Sumerian city-state, in effect, a "temple-city" — a theocracy in which the god (through the temple's staff) owned all the land and employed the entire population — or was the temple one economic actor among several coexisting sectors (private landholding, communal/household land, and, increasingly, the palace)?

The dispute is historiographic as much as evidential: the underlying documentary base — chiefly the Presargonic archive of the Bau temple at Girsu (Lagash) — has not changed since the early twentieth century; what has changed is how far scholars are willing to generalize a single, large, but partial temple archive into a model of the whole economy.

## Position A — The Temple-City / Temple-State Model (Deimel; Falkenstein)

Associated chiefly with **Father Anton Deimel**'s early-twentieth-century analysis of the Bau temple archive at Girsu, and (in a more qualified form) **Adam Falkenstein**. On this reading, the temple — as the earthly household of the city's patron deity — owned essentially all agricultural land in the city-state and the whole population were its dependents, receiving subsistence allocations, rations, or crop-share tenancies from the temple in exchange for labor and produce. Deimel's own estimate, cited by Postgate, put the land controlled by the Bau temple at Girsu (end of the Presargonic period) at 200–300 sq km. The model treats the Sumerian city-state as, in effect, a **theocracy**: the extreme formulation (quoted by Postgate from V. Gordon Childe, *What Happened in History*, rev. edn 1954) held that at Lagash the temple owned all the land and employed the entire population, with the gods figuring as representatives of clans owning farmland created by "social labour." On this view, the whole economy operated through temple-centered **redistribution**.

## Position B — The Revisionist Calibration (Diakonoff; Postgate 1994)

Associated with **I. M. Diakonoff**'s emphasis on the extended family and local commune as an independent, non-temple sector of landholding, and endorsed in a moderate form by **J. N. Postgate** (1994). On this view the temple was a major institutional landholder and redistributive hub, but by no means the whole economy: private and communal/household land, and (increasingly from the mid-third millennium onward) a distinct palace sector, coexisted alongside it. Postgate states the corrective bluntly: "This extreme view is now discredited. We cannot any longer maintain that because the temple collected commodities and distributed them to its dependants the entire economy operated through 'redistribution', or that the priests controlled all agricultural production and commercial activity" (as of Postgate 1994). He further notes that "the accepted opinion that the temples were the state has been drastically revised" since the 1920s studies, even though the *range* of economic activities recorded in the surviving texts "remains unchanged" — that is, the facts are stable; the interpretive framework has changed. Postgate frames the wider economy through a tripartite exchange typology (reciprocal, redistributive, commercial) explicitly against a pure-redistribution reading: the three modes "are not mutually exclusive," and "one of the complexities of early Mesopotamian society is precisely the co-existence of these three modes and their variations" (as of Postgate 1994). On this reading, the Girsu-Bau archive is a genuine but partial and administratively self-selecting record (a single temple's internal accounts, disproportionately well preserved because of how and where it was excavated), and Postgate explicitly notes that the temples of Lagash are no longer thought to have controlled the *entire* territory of the city-state — a revision of Deimel's stronger claim.

## Position C — Nissen's Independent Skepticism

**Hans Nissen**'s skepticism is a related but distinct position: doubt about the antiquity and uniformity of southern temple economic dominance itself, rather than (as with Diakonoff) an emphasis on a rival non-temple sector. Postgate (1994) reports that skepticism and presents Diakonoff's household/commune emphasis and Nissen's doubts as both "live, unresolved positions" that he **explicitly declines to adjudicate**, citing insufficient evidentiary control — the long-run balance between temple strength, private-sector strength, and free vs. semi-free labor being, in Postgate's assessment, heavily confounded by archival survival bias (as of Postgate 1994).

Nissen’s **own** arguments in *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988|The Early History of the Ancient Near East]]* (1988) go well beyond that second-hand report. The following is Position C in its strongest form as Nissen stated it (attributed throughout; no wiki-voice verdict).

### C1 — Demotion of “temple” / “palace” labels for early public buildings

For Late Uruk [[uruk|Uruk]], Nissen argued that excavators’ names — monumental buildings called “temples,” a square-plan building “Palace E” — outrun the evidence. Given ignorance of the political leadership system and lack of internal functional proof, such structures should only be designated **“public buildings.”** The same ground plan and fireplace type appear as centers of residential complexes at Habuba and related Syrian sites, so the form is a **standard building unit** whose function depends on position, size, and unknown characteristics. Cult buildings in Eanna remain certain from cult-related tablets; warehouses and administrative buildings are implied by tablet and sealing rubbish but not securely identified. Architecture therefore cannot prove a chronological **temple-then-palace** succession: criteria for the labels are lacking, early material is fragmentary, and Late Uruk “temple”/“palace” designations are **arbitrary**.

### C2 — Classical temple-state hypothesis and its weaknesses

Nissen reconstructed the former thesis: the whole city, inhabitants, and land as property of the supreme city god, administered by high priest/priestess; titles **en** (later **ensi**) as “priest-ruler” with strong god-relationship vs **lugal** (“great man”) as military commander/“king”; support drawn from late appearance of non-temple monumental buildings labeled “palaces” and Sumerian *e-gal* (“great house”) vs *e* (“house/temple”). He found two weaknesses: (1) architecture cannot prove temple-before-palace succession; (2) the *en*/*lugal* difference may be **regional** without a strong functional split. The whole concept rests on **few ED III Girsu texts** applied uncritically to other regions and earlier phases. A Jamdet Nasr economic text where *en* and *lugal* appear together in a nonconspicuous position, plus the professions list’s first entry as likeliest highest title (neither *en* nor *lugal*), further undermines projecting later high-title meanings backward. The king-vs-high-priest contrast “can no longer be sustained,” though late ED still shows plural leadership forms, at least one designatable as “theocratic.”

### C3 — Urukagina and the “temple city” as late ED III particularist reaction

On the Girsu (*e-mi* / Bau) corpus that anchors Position A, Nissen reported that under Lugalanda the complex was *e-mi* (“house of the woman”) directed by the ruler’s wife (e.g. Baranamtara); under **Urukagina** the same complex appears as the economic unit of goddess **Baba**. The Reform texts confirm Ningirsu / Baba / Shulshagana as lords of ruler’s, woman’s, and prince’s houses and fields; the **standard** reading is restoration of god-owned estates usurped by the ruler’s family. Nissen’s **own** reading reverses the deep-time prototype: against centralizing aspirations, the late-ED-III Girsu idea that city, inhabitants, and lands were city-god property is **overemphasized particularism** — a rigid **“temple city”** created as a **reaction to centralization** in the **last phase of ED III**. The Reform texts’ “old order” is a legitimizing fiction of tradition; generalizing this form as prototype for the whole country and early periods is **“inadmissible.”** The temple city did not long survive, as the centralized-state trajectory was already advanced. Shortly afterward Lugalzaggesi’s conquests (and temple destructions lamented by Urukagina) can be read, in Nissen’s hypothesis, as strategic attack on temples as centers of resistance to central plans — if Lugalzaggesi rejected temple-city ideology (attributed hypothesis, not settled fact).

### C4 — Dual cult centralization at Uruk without temple-state economy

Independently of the Girsu archive, Nissen treated Uruk’s Late Uruk **dual cult layout** (western high terrace / later Kullaba vs Eanna on flat ground) and its end-Late-Uruk / Jamdet Nasr **unification into a single central cult area** as a major religious-political reorganization — possibly political consolidation — with tremendous effect on city life. Eanna as a very large economic unit controlled agriculture, husbandry, crafts, trade, raw-material delivery, building, festivals, and sacrifices **at the Uruk center**, but he stressed that **almost all examples come only from Eanna**, that countryside organization may have been totally different, and that this is **no subject for generalizations** about a temple-dominated national economy. Political centralization is argued from labor control (terrace labor; wall building), dual-to-single cult reorganization, and related measures — while he repeatedly stresses ignorance of leadership form and resists reading early “temples” as proven temple-state institutions. See [[uruk]] (Nissen densification).

### Reception note (how Position C is reported)

Postgate’s 1994 summary captures only the **antiquity/uniformity** half of Nissen’s skepticism. Nissen 1988 supplies the architectural demotion of labels, the Girsu-as-late-reaction thesis, and the Uruk dual-cult centralization argument. Related later strands in the same book (Naram-Sin “god of Akkad” claiming city-god rights; Gudea’s inscriptions as saturated “temple city” ideology as antithesis of Akkad central rule) treat temple-city ideology as a recurrent **particularist** resource in center–local conflict, not as the pristine constitution of third-millennium Babylonia. None of the above adjudicates Positions A–C; each is left in attributed form.

## Shared Factual Ground

All parties agree that the Bau temple archive from Presargonic Girsu is real, large, and detailed, and that it documents extensive temple landholding, staff allocations, and redistribution on a substantial scale. The dispute is over generalization: how far a single, well-preserved institutional archive licenses a model of the *whole* Sumerian economy, and how much weight to give the comparatively sparse and unevenly distributed documentation for private, communal, and (later) palace sectors.

## Further Support (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] independently reinforces Position B for the specific Lagash case that anchors this debate. Discussing the Early Dynastic Lagash temple archive, he states: "it used to be thought that temples were completely dominant in Early Dynastic society... Lagash was once described as a temple-state, where the gods owned all land and property. Today, most scholars reject this idea, acknowledging the fact that other sectors of society were important participants in economic life but simply remain undocumented." This is a third, independent textbook-level endorsement of the revisionist calibration — consistent with Roux and Postgate — and adds the explicit observation that the older model's undoing rests partly on absence of documentation for other sectors, not positive proof those sectors were small. (Attributed: Van De Mieroop 2016.)
