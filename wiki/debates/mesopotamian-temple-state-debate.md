# The Mesopotamian Temple-State Debate

## Statement of the Dispute

How much of the early Sumerian (third-millennium BCE) economy did the temple institution own and control? Was the Sumerian city-state, in effect, a "temple-city" — a theocracy in which the god (through the temple's staff) owned all the land and employed the entire population — or was the temple one economic actor among several coexisting sectors (private landholding, communal/household land, and, increasingly, the palace)?

The dispute is historiographic as much as evidential: the underlying documentary base — chiefly the Presargonic archive of the Bau temple at Girsu (Lagash) — has not changed since the early twentieth century; what has changed is how far scholars are willing to generalize a single, large, but partial temple archive into a model of the whole economy.

## Position A — The Temple-City / Temple-State Model (Deimel; Falkenstein)

Associated chiefly with **Father Anton Deimel**'s early-twentieth-century analysis of the Bau temple archive at Girsu, and (in a more qualified form) **Adam Falkenstein**. On this reading, the temple — as the earthly household of the city's patron deity — owned essentially all agricultural land in the city-state and the whole population were its dependents, receiving subsistence allocations, rations, or crop-share tenancies from the temple in exchange for labor and produce. Deimel's own estimate, cited by Postgate, put the land controlled by the Bau temple at Girsu (end of the Presargonic period) at 200–300 sq km. The model treats the Sumerian city-state as, in effect, a **theocracy**: the extreme formulation (quoted by Postgate from V. Gordon Childe, *What Happened in History*, rev. edn 1954) held that at Lagash the temple owned all the land and employed the entire population, with the gods figuring as representatives of clans owning farmland created by "social labour." On this view, the whole economy operated through temple-centered **redistribution**.

## Position B — The Revisionist Calibration (Diakonoff; Postgate 1994)

Associated with **I. M. Diakonoff**'s emphasis on the extended family and local commune as an independent, non-temple sector of landholding, and endorsed in a moderate form by **J. N. Postgate** (1994). On this view the temple was a major institutional landholder and redistributive hub, but by no means the whole economy: private and communal/household land, and (increasingly from the mid-third millennium onward) a distinct palace sector, coexisted alongside it. Postgate states the corrective bluntly: "This extreme view is now discredited. We cannot any longer maintain that because the temple collected commodities and distributed them to its dependants the entire economy operated through 'redistribution', or that the priests controlled all agricultural production and commercial activity" (as of Postgate 1994). He further notes that "the accepted opinion that the temples were the state has been drastically revised" since the 1920s studies, even though the *range* of economic activities recorded in the surviving texts "remains unchanged" — that is, the facts are stable; the interpretive framework has changed. Postgate frames the wider economy through a tripartite exchange typology (reciprocal, redistributive, commercial) explicitly against a pure-redistribution reading: the three modes "are not mutually exclusive," and "one of the complexities of early Mesopotamian society is precisely the co-existence of these three modes and their variations" (as of Postgate 1994). On this reading, the Girsu-Bau archive is a genuine but partial and administratively self-selecting record (a single temple's internal accounts, disproportionately well preserved because of how and where it was excavated), and Postgate explicitly notes that the temples of Lagash are no longer thought to have controlled the *entire* territory of the city-state — a revision of Deimel's stronger claim.

## Position C — Nissen's Independent Skepticism

**Hans Nissen**'s skepticism, as reported by Postgate, is presented as a related but distinct position: doubt about the antiquity and uniformity of southern temple economic dominance itself, rather than (as with Diakonoff) an emphasis on a rival non-temple sector. Postgate presents Diakonoff's household/commune emphasis and Nissen's skepticism about the antiquity of temple dominance as both "live, unresolved positions" that he **explicitly declines to adjudicate**, citing insufficient evidentiary control — the long-run balance between temple strength, private-sector strength, and free vs. semi-free labor being, in Postgate's assessment, heavily confounded by archival survival bias (as of Postgate 1994).

## Shared Factual Ground

All parties agree that the Bau temple archive from Presargonic Girsu is real, large, and detailed, and that it documents extensive temple landholding, staff allocations, and redistribution on a substantial scale. The dispute is over generalization: how far a single, well-preserved institutional archive licenses a model of the *whole* Sumerian economy, and how much weight to give the comparatively sparse and unevenly distributed documentation for private, communal, and (later) palace sectors.

## Further Support (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] independently reinforces Position B for the specific Lagash case that anchors this debate. Discussing the Early Dynastic Lagash temple archive, he states: "it used to be thought that temples were completely dominant in Early Dynastic society... Lagash was once described as a temple-state, where the gods owned all land and property. Today, most scholars reject this idea, acknowledging the fact that other sectors of society were important participants in economic life but simply remain undocumented." This is a third, independent textbook-level endorsement of the revisionist calibration — consistent with Roux and Postgate — and adds the explicit observation that the older model's undoing rests partly on absence of documentation for other sectors, not positive proof those sectors were small. (Attributed: Van De Mieroop 2016.)

## Curator's Note

*(Dated 2026-07-10.)* [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]], [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]], and now [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop (2016)]] all endorse the revisionist calibration (Position B); the strong temple-state/theocracy model associated with Deimel and the mid-century consensus it fed is now considered largely superseded, though it remains historiographically important as the starting point against which Diakonoff's, Postgate's, and Van De Mieroop's revisions are defined. Nissen's further skepticism (Position C) has not, per Postgate's own account, been adjudicated by the field. This note records the curator's reading of relative current standing; it is not a wiki-voice verdict, and the positions above remain stated in their own strongest form.
