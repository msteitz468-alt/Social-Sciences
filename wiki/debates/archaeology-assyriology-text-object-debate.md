# Text vs. Object: Archaeology and Assyriology in Mesopotamian Studies

Mesopotamia is unusual among archaeological fields in possessing a vast contemporary
**written record** — hundreds of thousands of cuneiform tablets — alongside its material
remains. This produces a standing methodological dispute over the relative epistemic status
of texts and objects, and over whether archaeology is an independent evidence class or a
"handmaiden" supplying context for the documents. Recorded here as positions; **not
adjudicated**. The dispute directly shapes how the whole discipline of
[[mesopotamian-archaeology|Mesopotamian archaeology]] defines its object.

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

Both sides accept that Mesopotamia yields both rich text corpora and rich material remains,
and that much surviving cuneiform is **unprovenanced** or poorly recorded (proto-cuneiform
tablets from Jemdet Nasr lost most contextual value to inadequate excavation; Old Babylonian
tablets at Ur had catalogue numbers inked on only after baking, losing find-context), while
controlled excavations often yield sparse text. Where the text-primacy side reads this as
archaeology's inherent limitation, the parity side reads it as a symmetrical interpretive
problem afflicting both evidence classes.

## Position 1 — Text Primacy (Bottéro)

*(Attributed — Jean Bottéro 1992, 2000; milder form in Oppenheim 1977.)* Written documents
give "precise, detailed, and analytical" knowledge, against the "hazy" and "speechless"
record of archaeology; cuneiform texts are "the surest, the most complete, the most
indispensable sources" for Mesopotamian history. On this view the archaeological record is
best understood as supplying background and confirmation for a history whose spine is textual.

## Position 2 — Parity (Van De Mieroop, Moreland, Morris, Matthews)

*(Attributed — Van De Mieroop 1999; Moreland 2001; Morris 1997;
[[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews]] 2003.)* Objects and texts are **both**
socially produced, multiply readable, and equally difficult to interpret; "objectivity is an
elusive ideal" (Van De Mieroop), and "both categories of evidence … are generated by actors
manipulating shared but contested cultural expectations" (Morris). Archaeology should not be
a "text-controlled laboratory" or handmaiden to history. Matthews presses that even Bottéro
concedes interpretive uncertainty ("the code that would enable us to decipher … escapes us"),
undercutting the strong text-primacy claim; following [[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]], he holds
that **texts are themselves archaeological artefacts** with meaningful contexts and
associations beyond their content, and that improved excavation-context publication of
cuneiform finds is essential.

## The Integrative Middle

Both sides point to studies that **combine** the evidence classes rather than rank them:
Elizabeth Stone's correlation of excavated Old Babylonian house plans at
[[nippur|Nippur]] with cuneiform property texts and ethnographic analogy (critiqued by
Postgate); Richard Zettler's reconstruction of the Ur III Inanna temple administration from
sealed clay artefacts plus tablets plus architecture. Matthews treats these as the productive
path — neither discipline subordinate to the other.

## Resolution Status

`open`. The dispute is less about facts than about the **default weighting** of two evidence
classes and the disciplinary self-image that follows — whether Mesopotamian archaeology
narrates its own past or services Assyriology's. It connects to the wiki's Evidence-Class
Framework (textual/archival vs. material evidence, each with its own failure modes) and to the
[[uruk-world-system-debate]], where a *purely archaeological* phenomenon (the Uruk expansion)
is argued to resist the analogies imported from text-based history.
