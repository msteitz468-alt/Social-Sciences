# The Original Affluent Society Debate

Whether [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s **[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]** thesis — that hunter-gatherers enjoyed material sufficiency and abundant leisure by limiting their wants — is empirically sound, and whether the foraging societies it rests on can stand for the Paleolithic condition at all. The thesis, launched in 1968 and elaborated in *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]* (1972), became a fixture of anthropology; its afterlife has been a running argument over the numbers and the samples behind them.

## Position 1 — Foragers were affluent ([[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]; the *Man the Hunter* milieu)

*(Attributed.)* Affluence is a want/means ratio, not a quantity of goods; foragers meet their finite wants with intermittent labor (the Arnhem Land 4–5 hr/day and Dobe !Kung ~2 hr/day figures) and choose leisure over surplus once satisfied. Non-accumulation is rational under mobility, not a sign of poverty; "poverty is the invention of civilization." Agriculture likely *increased* per-capita labor. See [[original-affluent-society]].

## Position 2 — The affluence figures undercount labor and ignore hardship

*(Attributed — largely post-1972, not ingested.)* Critics (e.g. David Kaplan, "The Darker Side of the Original Affluent Society," 2000; work-effort re-examinations) argue that the celebrated short "work" days counted only food *acquisition*, excluding food processing, tool manufacture and repair, water- and firewood-collection, and childcare — which, added in, substantially raise total working time. They further point to seasonal hunger, nutritional stress, high infant and child mortality, and physically demanding conditions as inconsistent with any ordinary sense of "affluence." On this reading the thesis romanticized foraging by a selective accounting.

## Position 3 — The reference foragers were not pristine (the Kalahari revisionists)

*(Attributed — post-1972, not ingested.)* Edwin Wilmsen (*Land Filled with Flies*, 1989) and others argued that the Dobe !Kung / Ju/'hoansi were not an isolated survival of the Paleolithic but a people long integrated into — and marginalized by — regional trade, herding, and colonial economies; their "foraging" was in part the poverty of a rural underclass. If so, the Dobe cannot serve as a window onto ancestral hunter-gatherer life. [[lee-richard-b|Lee]] and others contested this "revisionist" reading, defending the integrity of the Dobe data — the core of the wider **Kalahari debate**.

## Shared ground, framed per side

All sides accept the basic time-allocation observations as *data*; they divide on what those data were *of* (acquisition only vs total subsistence labor), on how to weigh health and mortality against leisure, and on whether the documented foragers are representative. Notably, **Sahlins anticipated both lines of attack**: he cautioned that surviving foragers are "displaced persons" in marginal refuges of possibly unrepresentative productivity, and that some foragers (the industrious [[murngin|Murngin]]) contradict the leisure pattern, so that forager ethnography may record "incomplete cultures."

The thesis remains a live reference point precisely because no side has fully dislodged it.

## Position 4 — Scott 2017: archaeological reappraisal of foragers vs farmers (Sahlins-adjacent)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017. Not a restatement of Sahlins’s want/means economics; a Near Eastern–focused synthesis mobilizing paleopathology, ethnography, and the “original affluent society” afterlife against civilizational progress narratives.)*

**Strongest form of the position:**

- Contrary to folklore of starving foragers, hunters and gatherers “have never looked so good” in diet, health, and leisure; agriculturalists “have never looked so bad” on the same measures; the shift to agriculture carried at least as many costs as benefits (Introduction).
- Farming was far more onerous than hunting/gathering (except possibly flood-recession agriculture); Boserup and others: foragers have no reason to shift to agriculture unless forced by population pressure or coercion (Introduction; Ch.2). Even contemporary hunter-gatherers in resource-poor environments still spend only ~half their time in subsistence labor (Ch.3).
- Hunter-forager life as multi-tempo, multi-web activity and encyclopedic ethnobotanical knowledge (“entire library of almanacs” in oral tradition) vs fixed-field cereal farming’s confinement to one starch plant’s annual metronome and sparse almanac-style knowledge — late Neolithic as “deskilling” (Ch.2).
- Osteology/paleopathology: coeval hunter-gatherers several inches taller; high sedentism + agricultural reliance correlates with nutritional stress, iron-deficiency anemia (especially reproductive-age women on cereals), enamel hypoplasia, infectious pathologies; ~40–50% infant mortality among most early agriculturalists (Ch.3).
- Sedentism and the multi-species *[[domus-complex|domus]]* generated zoonoses and crowding diseases as a “civilizational effect” — epidemiological costs layered on top of drudgery (Ch.3). See [[agricultural-revolution]].

**How it sits relative to Positions 1–3:** Scott **extends** Position 1’s forager-favorable comparison into deep archaeological time and health/disease, without re-deriving Sahlins’s formal leisure-time arithmetic or want/means ratio. He does **not** resolve Position 2’s work-time accounting debate or Position 3’s Kalahari representativeness problem; his reference frame is mainly Fertile Crescent / Mesopotamian prehistory plus generalizing ethnography. Treat as **Sahlins-adjacent reception**, not as Sahlins himself.
