# The Original Affluent Society Debate

Whether [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]'s **[[original-affluent-society|original affluent society]]** thesis — that hunter-gatherers enjoyed material sufficiency and abundant leisure by limiting their wants — is empirically sound, and whether the foraging societies it rests on can stand for the Paleolithic condition at all. The thesis, launched in 1968 and elaborated in *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]* (1972), became a fixture of anthropology; its afterlife has been a running argument over the numbers and the samples behind them.

## Position 1 — Foragers were affluent ([[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]; the *Man the Hunter* milieu; Lee's Dobe program)

*(Attributed.)* Affluence is a want/means ratio, not a quantity of goods; foragers meet their finite wants with intermittent labor (the Arnhem Land 4–5 hr/day and Dobe !Kung short-week figures) and choose leisure over surplus once satisfied. Non-accumulation is rational under mobility, not a sign of poverty; "poverty is the invention of civilization." Agriculture likely *increased* per-capita labor. See [[original-affluent-society]].

### Lee 1979 densification of Position 1 (primary numbers)

*(Attributed — [[lee-richard-b|Lee]], *[[lee-kung-san-1979|The !Kung San]]* 1979; study [[lee-kung-san-study]]; fieldwork diary July–Aug 1964 at Dobe, midwinter dry season of a serious drought year when Herero/Tswana crops had failed.)* Recalculated vs earlier publications (Lee 1968a, 1969a):

| Measure | Lee 1979 figure |
|---|---|
| Subsistence work week (adults, truck-adjusted) | **~2.4 days**/week (unadjusted 2.2) |
| Camp diet | **2,355 Cal** + **96.3 g protein**/person/day |
| Surplus over estimated requirement (~1,975 Cal) | **~380 Cal**/person/day |
| Plant vs meat calories | gathering **~70%** / hunting **~30%** |
| Full work week (subsistence + tools + housework) | men **44.5 hrs**, women **40.1**, avg **~42.3** |
| Seasonal adult weight loss (1967–9 anthropometry) | **~1–2%** July→January (vs 4–6.5% agricultural "hungry season") |
| Draper /Du/da multi-season check | overall **2.3-day** subsistence week |

Lee's interpretive claims *(attributed)*: modest subsistence input challenges the "constant struggle for existence" image even under drought; January low is not a hungry season by African agricultural standards; children continue growing while adults absorb more stress; food supply with simple tools is sufficient; researchers had skewed samples toward harsh cases. He notes the 1964 diary is a continuous block on one group and "cannot be considered representative of !Kung groups in general or even of the Dobe group at other times of the year." Cross-link: [[lee-what-hunters-do-study]] (1968 figures superseded/refined by 1979 recalculation).

## Position 2 — The affluence figures undercount labor and ignore hardship

*(Attributed — largely post-1972; Kaplan 2000 not yet ingested.)* Critics (e.g. David Kaplan, "The Darker Side of the Original Affluent Society," 2000; work-effort re-examinations) argue that the celebrated short "work" days counted only food *acquisition*, excluding food processing, tool manufacture and repair, water- and firewood-collection, and childcare — which, added in, substantially raise total working time. They further point to seasonal hunger, nutritional stress, high infant and child mortality, and physically demanding conditions as inconsistent with any ordinary sense of "affluence." On this reading the thesis romanticized foraging by a selective accounting.

**How Lee 1979 already speaks to this critique *(attributed, not adjudicating)*:** Table 9.12 adds manufacture/maintenance and housework to subsistence and reports a **~42-hour** average week — closer to a full industrial work week than the pure 2.4-day subsistence figure Sahlins popularized. Housework alone: ~2.2 hrs/day men, ~3.2 women; mongongo cracking ≥1 hr/day for most people. Childcare (60–80% by the child's own mother) is acknowledged as a major further category that, Lee holds, redresses male subsistence surplus without making women an exploited class. Seasonal weight data are his main empirical reply on "hunger." Position 2 can still argue that ~42 hours plus childcare, mobility costs, and morbidity undercut "affluence," or that the drought-year / single-camp design overstates security — record those as critical positions, not wiki-voice verdicts.

## Position 3 — The reference foragers were not pristine (the Kalahari revisionists)

*(Attributed — [[wilmsen-edwin-n|Wilmsen]], *[[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-1989|Land Filled with Flies]]* 1989; study [[wilmsen-land-filled-with-flies-study]].)* Zhu / San-speakers were not an isolated Paleolithic survival but a people long integrated into — and marginalized by — regional trade, herding, and colonial economies; mid-20th-c. "foraging" is in large part the poverty of a rural underclass. If so, the Dobe/CaeCae case cannot serve as a clean window onto ancestral hunter-gatherer life. Full Position B densification: [[kalahari-debate]].

**Wilmsen 1989 physiology and accounting densification *(attributed)*:**

- July camp calories can **agree** with Lee (~2,140 / revised 2,355 vs Wilmsen ~2,294) while **year-round** forager monthly means range ~**1,408–3,114** kcal/person/day — year-round affluence inference rejected.
- Seasonal mean weight amplitude **~3%** (1968–69 Lee/Howell series reanalyzed) vs **~6%** (1975–76 Wilmsen); independent/pastoral Zhu **~10% heavier** than foragers at all times; forager drought losses ~2× pastoral/independent.
- Weight-for-height ~**75%** of local Bantu for foragers vs **~83%** for independent/pastoral Zhu; food-relief rapidly raises mean weights.
- Lee's R144 store-food remedy for seasonal weight loss rejected: foragers spent little cash on food despite larger shortfalls; shortfall read as structural underclass deprivation.
- Counter-authorities mobilized: Lee's own 1965 lean-month "precarious conditions"; Marshall 1968 "all thin"; Harpending & Wandsnider; Tobias; Konner & Shostak on limits of abundance language.

**Lee 1979 materials already in the clash *(attributed)*:** Lee revises his own 1963 "pristine full-time forager" impression (most men had herded cattle; many planted in good rainfall years; mafisa clientage widespread) while arguing early Dobe contact was with adjacent precapitalist formations rather than European commercial frontiers, and that the foraging mode remained viable into the 1970s. On Wilmsen's larger mid-1970s weight loss vs Lee's ~**1.5%** in 1968–9, Lee rejects wild-resource failure and attributes the difference to **rapid sedentarization and cash-economy introduction** (craft manufacture diverting hours; store foods not closing the deficit) — "a failure of the cash economy, not a failure of the hunting and gathering economy" (Ch.15). Regional 1967–9 pattern: greater stress at concentrated /Xai/xai than at multi-hole western N!umsi. **Both primary texts are now in the vault** — record the clash; do not equate Lee's self-revision with Wilmsen's full underclass case.

## Shared ground, framed per side

All sides accept the basic time-allocation observations as *data*; they divide on what those data were *of* (acquisition only vs total subsistence labor), on how to weigh health and mortality against leisure, and on whether the documented foragers are representative. Notably, **Sahlins anticipated both lines of attack**: he cautioned that surviving foragers are "displaced persons" in marginal refuges of possibly unrepresentative productivity, and that some foragers (the industrious [[murngin|Murngin]]) contradict the leisure pattern, so that forager ethnography may record "incomplete cultures." Lee 1979 adds shared factual ground on recalculated calories, the three-category work week, modest seasonality in 1967–9, and open acknowledgment of contact, drought context, and diary non-representativeness — while the parties still disagree on *interpretation* of those facts.

## Position 4 — Scott 2017: archaeological reappraisal of foragers vs farmers (Sahlins-adjacent)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017. Not a restatement of Sahlins’s want/means economics; a Near Eastern–focused synthesis mobilizing paleopathology, ethnography, and the “original affluent society” afterlife against civilizational progress narratives.)*

**Strongest form of the position:**

- Contrary to folklore of starving foragers, hunters and gatherers “have never looked so good” in diet, health, and leisure; agriculturalists “have never looked so bad” on the same measures; the shift to agriculture carried at least as many costs as benefits (Introduction).
- Farming was far more onerous than hunting/gathering (except possibly flood-recession agriculture); Boserup and others: foragers have no reason to shift to agriculture unless forced by population pressure or coercion (Introduction; Ch.2). Even contemporary hunter-gatherers in resource-poor environments still spend only ~half their time in subsistence labor (Ch.3).
- Hunter-forager life as multi-tempo, multi-web activity and encyclopedic ethnobotanical knowledge (“entire library of almanacs” in oral tradition) vs fixed-field cereal farming’s confinement to one starch plant’s annual metronome and sparse almanac-style knowledge — late Neolithic as “deskilling” (Ch.2).
- Osteology/paleopathology: coeval hunter-gatherers several inches taller; high sedentism + agricultural reliance correlates with nutritional stress, iron-deficiency anemia (especially reproductive-age women on cereals), enamel hypoplasia, infectious pathologies; ~40–50% infant mortality among most early agriculturalists (Ch.3).
- Sedentism and the multi-species *[[domus-complex|domus]]* generated zoonoses and crowding diseases as a “civilizational effect” — epidemiological costs layered on top of drudgery (Ch.3). See [[agricultural-revolution]].

**How it sits relative to Positions 1–3:** Scott **extends** Position 1’s forager-favorable comparison into deep archaeological time and health/disease, without re-deriving Sahlins’s formal leisure-time arithmetic or want/means ratio. He does **not** resolve Position 2’s work-time accounting debate or Position 3’s Kalahari representativeness problem; his reference frame is mainly Fertile Crescent / Mesopotamian prehistory plus generalizing ethnography. Treat as **Sahlins-adjacent reception**, not as Sahlins himself.
