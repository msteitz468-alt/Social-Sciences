# CSR and Interpretivism Debate

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

Both sides study "religion" as a major domain of human culture. Both acknowledge that religious meanings are culturally situated. Dispute centers on whether **cognitive-causal explanation** is legitimate, necessary, or reductive relative to **interpretive understanding**.

## Position 1 — CSR explanatory program

Holders (as White 2021 presents them): [[lawson-e-thomas]], [[mccauley-robert-n]], [[boyer-pascal]], [[whitehouse-harvey]], [[barrett-justin-l]], [[guthrie-stewart]], [[sperber-dan]], [[white-claire]].

Claim in strongest form: culture-alone and pure-interpretation models leave unexplained the cross-cultural recurrence of religious forms; minds actively process cultural inputs; scientific methods (experiment, developmental comparison, multi-level evolution) can explain constraints on religious thought and practice without settling theological truth claims. CSR *complements* interpretation rather than replacing it (McCauley per White).

## Position 2 — Interpretive / anti-reductionist

Holders (as CSR's foils in White): Geertzian symbolic anthropology; Eliadean history of religions; strong cultural relativism/determinism; critics who fear reductionism "explains away" the sacred.

Claim in strongest form: religion is a system of meanings that must be understood from within; reducing it to cognitive by-products or adaptive functions misses what believers care about and risks ethnocentric scientism; personal and thick-descriptive accounts are not optional extras but the proper object.

## Stakes

Method training in religious studies and anthropology; legitimacy of evolutionary psychology in humanistic fields; public debates about whether science disproves religion (both serious sides often reject that leap).

## Status

**Open.** Institutional coexistence (CSR journals and RS departments; White's own tenure-track RS appointment). Empirical CSR findings (theological incorrectness, mixed MCI/HADD results) refine but do not dissolve the methodological dispute.
