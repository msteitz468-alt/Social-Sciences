# Does Flexible Capitalism Corrode Character?

Whether the "new capitalism" of [[flexibility|flexible]], short-term, networked work — post-Fordist production, reengineering, contingent employment (see [[flexible-employment]]) — erodes the personal qualities [[sennett-richard|Richard Sennett]] groups under [[character]]: loyalty, mutual commitment, durable purpose, and the ability to make a coherent narrative of one's working life. Sennett's *[[sennett-corrosion-of-character-1998|The Corrosion of Character]]* (1998) is the sharpest statement of the affirmative; the dispute is partly substantive (do these effects occur, and how widely?) and partly methodological (can an essayistic, interview-based study establish them?).

## Positions

**1. The corrosion thesis (Sennett).** "'No long term' is a principle which corrodes trust, loyalty, and mutual commitment." Flexible capitalism, by rewarding discontinuity and detachment, "divorces will from behavior" and denies workers the temporal continuity out of which [[character]] is built. The teamwork ethic offers "power without authority"; risk becomes a daily necessity that "eats away at your sense of character"; the culminating claim (attributed): "a regime which provides human beings no deep reasons to care about one another cannot long preserve its legitimacy." The evidence is [[participant-observation|ethnographic]] — Enrico and Rico, the computerized Boston bakery, Rose, the downsized IBM programmers, Davos.

**2. The empirical/optimist critique.** Flexibility's consequences are heterogeneous: for some workers (Piore & Sabel's flexible-specialization craft workers; professionals with genuine flextime) it enlarges autonomy and opportunity. On this view Sennett selects distressing cases and reads a uniform corrosion into a varied record, and treats a historically brief, unionized, largely male-breadwinner stability as if it were a natural baseline — a **nostalgia** charge. Labor-market data (job creation, rising credentials) are marshalled against the mood of "apprehension" Sennett foregrounds.

**3. The methodological critique.** Even sympathetic readers question whether a small set of vivid vignettes, however well-observed, can support macro claims about "capitalism" and "character." The unit-of-analysis leap from a handful of interviews to a civilizational diagnosis is the recurring objection; Sennett's defenders reply that the qualitative, meaning-level evidence class reaches exactly what survey and cliometric approaches render invisible, and that he offers his cases as illuminating, not statistically representative.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

All sides accept the broad structural facts: the spread of contingent and non-standard work, delayering and reengineering of firms, rising income inequality across the advanced economies, and the erosion of long-tenure "organization man" careers. They disagree over the *interpretation* of these facts at the level of the person — whether they amount to a corrosion of character or a more mixed reshaping of working lives whose winners and losers must be counted case by case.

## Current Standing

Open. *The Corrosion of Character* remains a touchstone precisely because it poses a question quantitative labor sociology finds hard to answer on its own terms; the empirical and interpretive disputes it provokes are live. The debate connects to broader disputes over [[reflexive-modernization|reflexive modernity]] and the individualization of work (cf. [[individualization-death-of-class-debate]]) and over the [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate|qualitative–quantitative divide]] in method.
