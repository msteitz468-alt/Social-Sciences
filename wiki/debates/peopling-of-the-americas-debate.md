## Shared Factual Ground

There is overwhelming scholarly consensus, dating in essence to the Jesuit naturalist
José de Acosta (1590), that humans first reached an uninhabited Americas from Asia via
the Bering region (Reich 2018). Beyond that starting point, the number of founding
migrations, their routes, their timing, and their correspondence to linguistic
groupings remain actively disputed and have been substantially revised by ancient DNA
evidence since the 2010s, as reported in [[reich-who-we-are-2018]].

## Position: Clovis-First

Clovis-style stone spear points — first identified at Folsom and Clovis, New Mexico in
the 1920s–1930s and since found at hundreds of North American sites, some embedded in
bison and mammoth remains — were long read as evidence that the makers of Clovis
technology, entering south of the ice sheets via an ice-free corridor roughly 13,000
years ago, were the first humans in the Americas and the ancestors of all Native
Americans. This "Clovis First" model held the status of the standard model of American
prehistory for decades (as of Reich 2018).

**Mid-century textbook form (Clark 1961).** *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* presents a Bering-route first peopling during a late-glacial interstadial (not glacial maximum), with Palaeoindian big-game hunters on the High Plains by the tenth millennium B.C. (then-available C-14): Folsom with extinct bison; Sandia and Clovis as older or functionally distinct projectile traditions (mammoth vs bison); fluted points as a New World innovation with only weak Old World parallels; expansion east, into the Desert West, and south toward Magellan (attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 9). Clark's "Palaeoindians" is a culture-historical label, not a genetic population. Absolute dates are uncalibrated and predate Monte Verde / pre-Clovis consensus revisions.

## Position: Pre-Clovis and the Coastal Route

Excavations at Monte Verde, Chile, published in 1997, produced butchered mastodon
bones, wooden structures, knotted string, hearths, and stone tools stylistically
unlike Clovis technology, radiocarbon dated to roughly 14,000 years ago — before the
ice-free corridor is thought to have opened, directly undermining Clovis-First. A group
of archaeologists who had rejected earlier pre-Clovis claims visited the site in 1997
and left persuaded of its validity (as of Reich 2018). The Paisley Caves, Oregon,
independently yielded ancient human feces in undisturbed soil layers dated to roughly
14,000 years ago and containing human mitochondrial DNA, likewise supporting
pre-ice-free-corridor occupation. Geological work in the 1990s established that parts
of the Pacific coast of Canada were ice-free after 16,000 years ago, motivating a
"coastal route" hypothesis for early coastwise migration south; no archaeological sites
survive to test the route directly, since the relevant coastline is now submerged by
sea-level rise.

**Mid-1990s synthesis form (Gamble 1994).** *[[gamble-timewalkers-1994|Timewalkers]]* treats New World peopling as three geographical problems — Beringian paleocontinent; penetration south of the ice; post-Wisconsin Arctic — and records: no serious Pliocene or Middle Pleistocene support; extensive colonization after 12,000 B.P. undisputed; Hoffecker, Powers & Goebel review placing significant Beringian occupation ~12–11 ka (Gamble concurs); majority favor overland ice-free corridor vs Fladmark coastal route (Hopkins argued against coastal productivity 25–14 ka); Clovis widespread after 12 ka, Folsom transition ~10.7–10.5 ka; Meadowcroft as unambiguous >3,000 years pre-Clovis in Pennsylvania; Monte Verde 13 ka peat component (huts, mastodon, expedient stone) and a still-provisional 33 ka locality; multi-wave lithic-homeland models (Kozlowski & Bandi; Dikov) judged sketchy; Gamble's own opinion — all after 40 ka and, for him, after 12 ka; Paul Martin overkill/blitzkrieg as a rapid-colonization POSITION that pre-Clovis would undermine (attributed throughout, Gamble 1994 ch. 10). **Horizon:** pre-aDNA and pre-1997 Monte Verde consensus conference; do not treat as current genetic consensus.

## Position: Morphology-Based "Paleoamerican" Model (Neves)

Physical anthropologist Walter Neves argued that a set of early American skeletons —
exemplified by the roughly 11,500-year-old Luzia skeleton from Lapa Vermelha, Brazil,
and 55 skulls from Lagoa Santa, Brazil (10,000+ years old) — show craniofacial
affinities to Australian and New Guinean populations rather than to East Asians or
modern Native Americans, implying a distinct, pre-Native-American founding population
he termed "Paleoamerican."

Reich (2018) treats Neves's claims as controversial, criticized as resting on selective
site inclusion, and cites the Kennewick Man case (below) as a cautionary parallel in
which morphological population assignment proved wrong once genetic data became
available. He further reports that the genetic Population Y signal (see
[[ancient-north-eurasians]] for the deeper Siberian ancestry it is sometimes compared
against) does not spatially match the sites Neves identified as Paleoamerican — for
example it is absent from tested Pericúes of Baja California and Fuegans of southern
South America, populations Neves's framework would predict to carry it.

## Position: Genetics-Based Single-Lineage and Revised Two-Lineage Models

Reich's 2012 laboratory study concluded that Native Americans from Mesoamerica
southward derive overwhelmingly from a single founding population, termed "First
Americans," that moved south of the ice sheets after roughly 15,000 years ago; a Four
Population Test on 52 populations found 47 of 52 statistically indistinguishable in
their relatedness to Asian populations, with five exceptions concentrated in the
Arctic and Pacific Northwest carrying additional distinct ancestry (as of Reich 2018).
Reich and Lars Fehren-Schmitz additionally reported broad genetic continuity across
millennia between ancient and present-day Native American populations sampled in Peru,
southern Argentina (~8,000 BP), southern Brazil (~10,000 BP), and coastal British
Columbia (~6,000 BP).

This single-lineage model was revised twice. First, Eske Willerslev and colleagues'
2014 whole-genome study of the Clovis-associated Anzick infant (Montana), radiocarbon
dated to just after 13,000 years ago, showed that a deep split among Native American
populations had already occurred by that time — disproving the idea that Clovis
represents the founding population of all Native Americans (as of Reich 2018). Second,
Reich, with Pavel Flegontov and Stephan Schiffels (2017), proposed just two deep
ancestral lineages accounting for nearly all non-Population-Y Native American
ancestry: the First Americans, and a second lineage, associated with small stone tools
and archery technology roughly 5,000 years ago, that founded the Paleo-Eskimos and
contributed to both Na-Dene and Eskimo-Aleut speakers. This same work found that the
Chukchi of northeastern Siberia carry roughly 40% First American ancestry via
back-migration from the Americas into Asia — a reversal of the expected one-way
direction of migration, attributed to Eskimo-Aleut speakers mixing heavily with Native
Americans in North America before some groups migrated back to Siberia (as of Reich
2018).

## Position: Population Y — A Third Lineage in Amazonia

Pontus Skoglund, working in Reich's laboratory, found via the Four Population Test
that some Amazonian populations — most strongly the Suruí, also the Karitiana and the
Ge-language-speaking Xavante — are more closely related to Australasians (Andaman
Islanders, New Guineans, Australians) than to any other world population, at an
estimated minimum proportion of 1–6%. The signal was replicated across independently
collected datasets and is not explained by recent Asian migration or Polynesian
contact; Reich's team named it "Population Y," from the Tupí word "ypykuéra"
("ancestor") (as of Reich 2018). The signal is essentially restricted to Amazonia —
undetected in Mesoamerica, in South America west of the Andes, in the Clovis infant, or
in present-day Algonquin speakers.

Reich hypothesizes Population Y represents an earlier, separate migration into South
America that was largely displaced or absorbed by the later First American expansion,
surviving disproportionately in the harder-to-penetrate Amazon basin. Because
Population Y likely mixed extensively with First-American-related populations en route
through Siberia and North America before reaching Amazonia, Reich models its true
ancestral contribution to groups such as the Suruí as potentially far higher than the
roughly 2% "tracer dye" signal directly observed — as high as 85% by his estimate (as
of Reich 2018).

## Linguistic Classification: Greenberg vs. Campbell

Linguist Joseph Greenberg argued that nearly all Native American languages fall into
just three families — Eskimo-Aleut, Na-Dene, and a large residual "Amerind" category
comprising roughly 90% of languages — corresponding to three migration waves from
Asia. Linguist Lyle Campbell, a self-described "splitter," instead divided Native
American languages into roughly 200 families and in 1986 wrote that Greenberg's
Amerind classification "should be shouted down."

Reich (2018) reports that genetic data broadly corroborate Greenberg's tripartite
clustering: the First American lineage corresponds closely to Greenberg's Amerind
category, even though critics found Greenberg's lexical method — comparing word lists
across a time depth roughly double what linguists generally consider reliable —
methodologically unsound. Reich's lab estimated Eskimo-Aleut speakers carry roughly
60% First American ancestry and some Na-Dene speakers roughly 90%, meaning First
American ancestry dominates even in groups Greenberg classified into linguistically
distinct branches. Separately, linguist Edward Vajda (2008) documented a deeper
connection between Na-Dene languages and the Yeniseian family of central Siberia
(today represented only by Ket), implying a comparatively recent Asian-derived
migration; Reich's 2012 study found the Na-Dene-speaking Chipewyan carry a distinctive
~10% ancestry component not widely shared with other Native Americans, read as genetic
support for this later migration (as of Reich 2018).

## Kennewick Man: Morphology Overturned by Genetics

Kennewick Man, an approximately 8,500-year-old skeleton found on U.S. federal land in
Washington State in 1996, was claimed for repatriation under NAGPRA by five Native
American tribes. Courts found insufficient scientific evidence of Native American
status — based on morphological analysis suggesting Pacific Rim/Pacific Islander
affinities — and allowed continued scientific study. A 2015 ancient DNA study led by
Willerslev overturned the morphological conclusion, confirming that Kennewick Man
derives from the same ancestral lineage as other Native Americans (as of Reich 2018).
Reich argues ancient DNA "trumps" morphological analysis for population assignment
because morphology relies on a small number of variable traits versus genetics' tens
of thousands of independent positions, though he also criticizes the Kennewick genome
paper for sampling only the Colville tribe among the five claimant tribes and for not
sharing the Colville data for independent analysis, arguing the published data do not
compellingly show closer relatedness to the Colville than to South American Native
Americans. The repatriation and research-ethics dimensions of this case, and the
comparable 2016 Spirit Cave repatriation, are treated in
[[ancient-dna-repatriation-ethics-debate]].

## Wenke & Olszewski — *Patterns in Prehistory* (2007)

Mid-2000s textbook survey of the peopling debate **before** the aDNA revisions densified above (Reich 2018 and after). Attribute as of W&O 2007; genetics and some site readings lag later work. Source: [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]] ch. 5.

**Shared starting point.** Scholarly consensus that Native Americans derive principally from Asian ancestors who entered via Beringia/Alaska — anticipated by José de Acosta (c. 1590) and Thomas Jefferson (1781) (attributed, W&O 2007). Why people entered is not treated as a puzzle: budding-off of hunter-forager groups under social friction or resource scarcity is the expected process of range expansion (attributed).

**Clovis and the core dispute.** Clovis (NM, 1932) produced large blade tools with extinct fauna beneath Folsom points; Clovis-style points dated ~11,000 years ago (uncalibrated as used in the chapter) and found widely across North and South America (attributed). As of W&O 2007, the core dispute was whether anyone was in the Americas before Clovis cultures ~11,500 years ago (uncalibrated) — Clovis-first vs pre-Clovis (attributed).

**Routes.** Beringia — a 1,500–3,000 km-wide land bridge of exposed Bering Sea floor during glacial lowstands — was most recently available ~25,000–14,000 years ago; eastern Beringia ~30–14 kya reconstructed as cool-dry steppe-tundra attractive to mammoths, horses, long-horned bison (attributed). Long-dominant position: walk across Beringia then south through an "ice-free corridor" between Canadian ice sheets (attributed). Updated corridor critique (as of 2007): corridor was a late-glacial feature; large parts blocked 30–11.5 kya; after ~18 kya remaining stretches may have been uninhabitable for lack of game; no corridor archaeology older than Clovis (attributed). Coastal/coast-hopping position: watercraft along Beringia's southern coast and NW North American coast at least to Washington/Oregon (possibly farther), exploiting coastal refugia available ≥1,000–2,000 years before the ice-free corridor (attributed). Stanford & Bradley Solutrean/Atlantic hypothesis (Clovis partly from European LGM skin-boat migration citing biface similarities and mtDNA X) is recorded and then rejected by majority critique (Straus et al.): ~5,000 radiocarbon-year gap between Solutrean and Clovis; no deep-water Solutrean adaptation; tool similarities may be independent invention (attributed). **W&O judgment (2007):** despite imperfect evidence, Beringian coast-hopping is the most likely route for first Americans south of the ice sheets; Atlantic Solutrean model is not accepted by most archaeologists (attributed).

**Sites and chronology.** Northeastern Siberia earliest known sites ~≤25 kya; Siberian groups may have shifted south at LGM and re-expanded into Beringia only after ~16 kya (attributed). Monte Verde (Chile; Dillehay): open-air peat-preserved settlement ~12,500 years ago with dwellings, mastodon, plant foods, trade goods; estimate 30–50 residents; deeper layers claimed possibly to ~33 kya (highly consequential if true); Fiedel still questions authenticity/antiquity (as of 2006 pers. comm. to authors) (attributed). Monte Verde at 12.5 kya implies earlier New World entry and possibly semi-sedentary coastal-forest economies challenging big-game-hunter first-American models (attributed). Caverna da Pedra Pintada / Monte Alegre (Amazon; Roosevelt et al.): Paleoindian strata with tropical plant foods, fish, stemmed points — gathering-foraging economy contemporary with Clovis but unlike North American Clovis big-game signature (attributed). Meadowcroft Rockshelter and Cactus Hill (VA) recorded as contested pre-Clovis candidates (attributed). Fiedel's calibration: late Pleistocene 14C dates ~2,000 years too young; Clovis ~13,400–13,000 cal BP; Monte Verde pre-Clovis ~14,400 cal BP; pre-Clovis earlier than ~13,500 cal BP still controversial as a class (as of W&O 2007) (attributed). Whitley & Dorn argue Clovis-first cannot be reconciled with accepted site ages given modeled migration/fertility rates (attributed).

**W&O synthesis of initial peopling (ch. 5 conclusions, as of 2007):** coastal route from Beringia to at least WA/OR underway by ~13,500 years ago; ice-free corridor not feasible before ~11,500 years ago and used later (possibly bidirectional); substantial populations by 11 kya; large-animal extinctions after ~11 kya unlikely mainly human-caused (attributed). Paleoindian visibility is biased toward large stone tools and kill sites; less marked forager sites may be under-recognized (Dixon; Meltzer on SE fluted points as generalized foragers) (attributed). Eastern North America denser fluted-point finds than the West (attributed).

**Megafaunal extinctions (positions).** Martin overkill: rapid post-Pleistocene extinctions driven by human hunting after ~12 kya (attributed). Grayson & Meltzer "requiem for overkill": only mammoth and mastodon known from Clovis kill sites (14 sites); many bird extinctions; humans not primary cause (attributed). Kelly & Todd: Clovis high-mobility hunters with little storage technology; extinction pattern similar without people (attributed).

**Reflexivity link.** W&O use Native American origin-in-North-America beliefs vs Asian-entry archaeology as a case in the relativism problem for post-processualism: if science cannot evaluate competing truth claims, indigenous origin claims would claim equal validity to the archaeological majority view of Asian origins 10–20 kya (attributed, ch. 1). They also note increasing collaborative archaeology with Native Americans generating alternative hypotheses open to scientific evaluation (attributed).

## Fagan — *The Great Journey* (1987 / updated 2004)

Archaeological-synthesis account **pre-dating the aDNA revolution** — attribute as
of Fagan 2004; superseded on genetics/linguistics by the Reich 2018 material above.
Source: [[fagan-great-journey-1987]]. Fagan's value here is the **archaeology and the
history of the controversy**, not molecular data.

**Fagan's position (late chronology).** [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] holds a
cautious "relatively late" chronology: first settlement after the last glacial
maximum (after ~18,000 BP), via northeastern Siberia and [[beringia|the Bering land
bridge]] on foot, with entry south of the ice ~15,000–14,000 BP; no site he accepts
reliably predates ~14,000 BP (attributed). He favors terrestrial over early maritime
crossing (no evidence of skin boats before ~3,000 BP) and **dismisses the Solutrean
(trans-Atlantic) hypothesis** as thin. He treats [[monte-verde]] as the one pre-Clovis
site that "has stood the test of time," while reporting Pedra Furada, Calico Hills,
and the amino-acid-dated California skulls as discredited or overturned early-date
claims — Paul Martin's quip that most pre-Clovis claims "have a shelf life of about
ten years."

**The site-by-site skeptic's method.** Fagan restates four evidentiary criteria
(descended from Holmes and [[hrdlicka-ales|Hrdlička]]): secure stratigraphy, reliable
dating, corroborating multidisciplinary evidence, and artifacts demonstrably human in
primary context. He applies them to reject or suspend most pre-Clovis candidates west
of the Mississippi (Santa Rosa Island, China Lake, Dutton/Lamb Spring, Lewisville) and
in Latin America (Valsequillo, Tlapacoya, El Bosque, Pikimachay, Pedra Furada), while
accepting **Meadowcroft Rockshelter** (secure to ≥12,000 BP; deeper dates disputed in
the Adovasio–Haynes "dead carbon" controversy) and Monte Verde. He sides, unusually
explicitly, with the strict criteria against José Luis Lorenzo's charge of
"pettifogging legal phraseology."

**Routes.** Fagan reports the [[ice-free-corridor]] as at most a late-glacial,
game-poor passage opening ~14,000–11,000 BP, with no corridor site older than Clovis;
he records Knut Fladmark's Pacific **coastal-route** alternative (now-submerged
shorelines) as the live competitor, plus J. Louis Giddings's "settlement-in-place"
view and the Workman vs. MacNeish/Bryan dispute over required technological
sophistication.

**Linguistics/biology (pre-aDNA, superseded).** Fagan reports Greenberg's tripartite
Amerind/Na-Dene/Aleut-Eskimo classification (which by 2004 drew "a torrent of
criticism" he declines to adjudicate), Johanna Nichols's much-earlier coastal-entry
estimate (>20,000 BP), Christy Turner's Sinodont dental model, and Robert Williams's
Gm-allotype three-migration genetics — all now downstream of the ancient-DNA results
above.

## Current Standing

The routes-and-timing question (ice-free corridor vs. coastal route, pre-Clovis
occupation) is resolved toward pre-Clovis coastal entry being at minimum plausible and
Clovis-as-sole-founding-population being false, on the convergent weight of Monte
Verde, Paisley Caves, and the Anzick genome (as of Reich 2018) — hence
`resolution_status: partially-resolved` rather than open. The number and structure of
founding lineages (First Americans, the Paleo-Eskimo-founding second lineage,
Population Y) remains an active, evolving genetic research program rather than a
settled model, and the correspondence between Greenberg's linguistic clusters and
genetic lineages, while broadly supportive, is not treated by Reich as full
vindication of Greenberg's specific comparative method. W&O 2007 already favored coastal entry and treated Clovis-first as contested; post-2010 genetics supersede their molecular and some chronological detail.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed positions — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003, chs. 23–28. **Pre-aDNA horizon:** genetics/linguistics claims are as of 2003 and must not be promoted over Reich 2018+ above. Do not adjudicate.)*

**Shared framing (as of Mithen 2003).** Americas empty of humans at 20,000 BC despite rich game (Hot Springs mammoths) (attributed). Long discourse from Fray Joseph de Acosta (1590) Lost-Tribe/overland northern meeting idea as early Beringia-like speculation (via Meltzer’s history) (attributed).

**Clovis-first textbook form (as of Mithen 2003).** By the 1970s, ¹⁴C for Clovis sites indicated none older than 11,500 BC; with no accepted earlier settlement, Clovis cast as first Americans via Beringia then ice-free corridor between Laurentide and Cordilleran sheets (attributed).

**Pre-Clovis candidates (positions).**

- **Meadowcroft:** Adovasio’s deep sequence claimed millennia pre-Clovis; coal-region contamination critique vs Adovasio re-tests finding no contamination (as of Mithen 1993-era report) — open dispute (attributed).
- **Bluefish Caves / Old Crow “worked bone”:** Cinq-Mars smooth-edged bones claimed >20,000 BC; Mithen remains sceptical — nature more likely workman without seeing bones (personal position).
- **Monte Verde (Dillehay):** MV-II assemblage radiocarbon ~12,500 BC; year-round settlement with coastal trade or visits; 1997 site visit by former critics (Meltzer, Haynes, etc.) largely persuaded; earlier Lynch reworked-artefacts and Dincauze ¹⁴C-misinterpretation critiques noted; Folsom 1927 / Pedra Furada 1993 visit tradition (attributed).
- **Fell’s Cave / Palli Aike (Bird 1934):** fishtail points, horse/sloth/guanaco; 1969 ¹⁴C as early as 11,000 BC — among earliest dated American occupations known before Monte Verde (attributed).

**Routes and languages (as of Mithen 2003).** Coastal-route alternatives to ice-free corridor discussed; linguistic peopling scenarios (Greenberg-type multi-wave vs splitters) judged too incomplete to constrain settlement tightly — Mithen follows Nettle and Goddard/Campbell skepticism (personal inclination). **Genetics in the book are pre-aDNA ancient-genome resolution** — keep subordinate to Reich section above.

**Megafauna concurrent debate.** Paul Martin overkill tied to Clovis arrival; climate/habitat mosaic-collapse (Graham, Lundelius, Guthrie); Meltzer & Mead / Grayson radiocarbon pruning leaves only “ice-age seven” tightly dated 11,000–10,000 BC with Clovis — most extinct taxa lack secure terminal dates (attributed). Not resolved in Mithen.

**Mithen’s inclination (position):** accepts Monte Verde-class pre-Clovis and coastal/complex forager possibilities; remains cautious on extreme early claims and on over-interpreting linguistics. Record only — not wiki-voice verdict.

## Meltzer 2009 densification

*(Attributed throughout to [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|First Peoples in a New World]]* 2009. **Horizon note:** archaeological synthesis at post–Monte Verde, pre–nuclear-aDNA depth. Genetics/linguistics claims below are **as of Meltzer 2009 only** — do not overwrite or demote the Reich 2018 sections above; treat as a parallel dated layer. All chronological figures follow Meltzer’s convention: **radiocarbon years BP, not cal BP**, because late-Pleistocene calibration is multi-valued and uncertain [C-1-030, C-1-031].)*

### Clovis-first historiography (as of Meltzer 2009)

Meltzer reconstructs Clovis-first as a mid-twentieth-century consensus that hardened from contingent research design rather than pure discovery.

- **Prehistory of the orthodoxy.** After centuries of speculation, the 1927 [[folsom-site|Folsom]] in-situ fluted point between extinct-bison ribs demonstrated Ice Age human presence without radiocarbon (C-1-020, C-3-032–037). Search strategy then chased kill sites and extinct fauna → [[clovis-culture|Clovis]]/[[blackwater-draw|Blackwater Draw]] mammoth associations, larger less-refined fluted points, and the name “Paleoindians” (C-1-021, C-3-039–041).
- **Radiocarbon lock-in.** By the early 1960s Folsom ~10,800 BP, Clovis almost 11,500 radiocarbon years BP (C-1-022). [[haynes-c-vance|C. Vance Haynes]] (1964) published a tight Clovis ¹⁴C cluster 11,500–11,000 BP, none >12,000 BP, none with pre-Clovis in suitable underlying sediments; he tied ~12,000 BP opening of the [[ice-free-corridor]] (after ~15 ka closed) to rapid Clovis radiation south of the ice (C-3-043).
- **Model form.** Mid-century consensus as Meltzer summarizes it: corridor opens ~12,000 BP along eastern Rocky flanks; Clovis/Clovis-like artifacts spread across North America in centuries; Tierra del Fuego within ~1,000 years of leaving Alaska; continental toolkit uniformity taken as rapid-dispersal signal, possibly ~500-year traverse (C-1-023–024).
- **Expectations that became “fact.”** Folsom-era kill-site methods created the expectation that Clovis sites were oldest and therefore first Americans — “quickly became fact” (C-1-043). Meltzer’s own framing (as of 2009): in the dozen years before the book, pre-Clovis shifted from hypothesis to “reality” reshaping the field (C-1-004).

### Standards of proof (Hrdlička–Folsom–pre-Clovis bar)

- **Three criteria.** Evolved from [[hrdlicka-ales|Hrdlička]] (1907) and Folsom (1927): (1) undeniable human traces (artifacts or skeletal remains); (2) undisturbed geological deposits in proper stratigraphic position; (3) indisputable radiometric ages (speared bison alone no longer suffice) (C-3-047; C-1-042).
- **Practical failure modes.** Nature mimics artifacts (geofacts); geology can be mixed (Steno hazards; Taber child intrusion); dating methods beyond ¹⁴C often ambiguous (C-3-049–054). “Pre-Clovis credibility decay curve”: sites burst with fanfare then collapse (C-1-032). Krieger 1964 list ~50 sites; MacNeish 1976 ~35 almost completely different; Morlan 1988 ~5 — high attrition breeds cynicism, but Meltzer insists site failure ≠ proof humans absent pre-Clovis (C-3-046).
- **Jackie Robinson Rule.** A pre-Clovis site must be unimpeachable enough to convince skeptics with room to spare (C-3-060). Meltzer: extraordinary claims require extraordinary proof (Pendejo “fingerprint the crew” quip) (C-1-002). Criteria not unfair: Clovis already met them; Folsom met them under coarser methods (C-3-058).
- **Standards met (as of 2009).** Meltzer holds the bar was finally cleared at [[monte-verde|Monte Verde]] in 1997 (C-1-042).

### Pre-Clovis resolution: Monte Verde (and limbo contenders)

**[[monte-verde|Monte Verde]] (Dillehay).** Waterlogged peat sealed MVII after abandonment; wood, hut planks, mastodon bone/ivory/meat/hide, juncus string on stakes, footprints, plants, ~715 stone pieces; multi-lab ¹⁴C 11,790 ± 200 to 13,565 ± 250 BP, average put at ~12,500 BP (C-1-038, C-3-069–073). Architecture: wishbone work hut + rectangular “row house”; plant economy with coastal seaweed and Mapuche ethnobotanic parallels (analogy, not identity) (C-3-070–071). Optional older MVI ~33 ka not basis of consensus (C-3-074).

**1997 visit and after.** January 1997 expert visit (proponents and skeptics including Dincauze and Haynes; Meltzer participant) left convinced of pre-Clovis antiquity; *American Antiquity* statement drafted by [[grayson-donald|Grayson]] & Meltzer (C-1-039, C-3-078). Haynes later second thoughts (only six “unequivocal” artifacts) treated by Meltzer as red herring — wood, bone, ivory, meat, strings, exotic plants remain (C-3-080). Fiedel *Discovering Archaeology* critique and 1999 Clovis-and-Beyond ambush dynamics recorded (C-3-081). 2008 seaweed/algae ¹⁴C affirmation: long-time skeptic Sandweiss said results “remove any lingering doubts” (C-3-082).

**Geographic/chronological paradox.** Only ~1,000 years older than Clovis but ~16,000 km from Beringia and non-Clovis in character — forces earlier departure, route rethink, and questions about missing intermediate sites (C-1-040, C-3-083).

**Other contenders (as of 2009).**
- **[[meadowcroft|Meadowcroft]]** ([[adovasio-james|Adovasio]]): meticulous dig; lower Stratum IIa six dates 12,800–16,175 BP; Adovasio conservative human presence ~14,250 BP; unfluted Miller lanceolate as possible fluted-point precursor; Haynes coal-contamination critique vs Adovasio multi-lab/stratigraphic rebuttal; limbo without comprehensive final report (C-3-061–065, C-6-007).
- **Pedra Furada:** older “artifacts” as free-fall geofacts under cliff (Meltzer site visit with Adovasio & Dillehay); TL failed to confirm hearths (C-3-066–068).
- **Calico, Old Crow, Tule Springs, Taber, Valsequillo, Toluquilla footprints:** failed or unresolved under criteria (C-3-044, 050, 052, 055–056, 059).
- **North American post-Monte Verde contenders** (Paisley Cave aDNA coprolites “promising methods”) — breaching the barrier does not upgrade all prior claims (C-3-090–092).

### Routes: corridor vs coast (as of Meltzer 2009)

- **Corridor blocked when it mattered for Monte Verde.** Laurentide + Cordilleran obstructed interior from before ~28,000 until after ~12,000 BP — first Americans could not reach southern Chile ~500 years before corridor opened via interior alone (C-3-084). No pre-Clovis sites in corridor region; few Clovis-age; most <10,500 BP (C-3-085).
- **Coastal shift.** After Monte Verde, attention shifted to a coastal entry available before corridor opening (C-1-041). Occam favors walking coastal route over open-ocean boating (Australia analogy limited); Fedje-type coastal searches; drowned coastlines and isostatic exceptions complicate tests (C-3-086–087, C-2-032, C-5-030).
- **Dual-route possibility.** Clovis after 11,500 BP; Monte Verde earlier; coastal pre-Clovis and/or corridor-timed Clovis both live in Meltzer’s Figure 38 scenarios (C-5-028, C-5-085). Interior GIS least-cost can prefer walking; coastal genetics simulations cannot prove routes — “proof is in the ground” (C-5-033–036, C-5-088).
- **Siberian staging (as of 2009).** *H. sapiens* central Siberia ~35 ka BP; north/east expansion after ~27 ka; LGM stall; significant western Beringia maybe only well past 14,000 BP (C-5-015). Goebel: Siberian UP toolkit broadly comparable (not identical) to early American classes (C-5-016). Nenana re-dated ~11,300 BP — roughly contemporaneous with Clovis, not a clear precursor; no fluted points (C-5-019–020). Alaska/corridor fluted points with secure ages <10,500 BP — too young for southbound Clovis; fluting likely invented south of ice and spread including northward “backwash” (C-6-006).

### Solutrean rejection (as of Meltzer 2009)

POSITION — Bradley & Stanford: Solutreans rafted skin boats across North Atlantic → Clovis (“Iberia, not Siberia”), boosted by Kennewick “Caucasoid” and haplogroup-X rhetoric (C-5-008). Sandia earlier Solutrean-link claim collapsed as integrity/hoax problem (C-5-005–007).

Meltzer / Straus / Goebel critique:
- **Time gap:** Solutreans gone from Europe ≥~17,000 BP — ~6,000 radiocarbon years before Clovis (C-5-009).
- **Convergence:** red ocher, bifaces, overshot flaking claimed as exclusive link — burden is non-convergence; not met (C-5-010–011).
- **Missing Solutrean inventory in Clovis/pre-Clovis:** cave/portable art, beveled antler points, atlatls, bone needles, perforated ornaments, backed bladelets, true burins, Solutrean point — would require total cultural (and genetic/dental/linguistic) amnesia (C-5-012, C-5-087).
- **Status:** few if any specialists take Solutrean colonization seriously as of 2009; “Internet immortality” among fringe audiences (C-5-014). Norse remain first Europeans at L’Anse aux Meadows ~AD 1000 (C-5-013). Haplogroup X founding lineage via Asian (Altai) branch — not Solutrean Europeans (C-4-036).

### Genetics and linguistics as of 2009 only

*(Parallel layer — superseded in detail by Reich 2018+ above; keep both dated.)*

**Linguistics.** Historic ~1,000 American Indian languages; if one ancestral tongue, some linguists floated ~50,000 years (“wrong ballpark” for Clovis) (C-1-037). Greenberg three-migration (Eskimo-Aleut, Na-Dene, Amerind) vs Campbell “should be shouted down” / ~150–200 families (C-4-008–016). Traditional labor-intensive method vs Greenberg mass comparison critiques (chance matches, African fallacy, megalocomparison) (C-4-017–018). Meltzer: genes do not map cleanly onto Greenberg families (Hunley & Long: pervasive exchange across linguistic boundaries; Aleuts genetically closer to some “Amerind” groups than to Eskimos) (C-4-037). Four-way non-identity: language ≠ genes ≠ archaeology ≠ people (C-4-075, C-5-056).

**Dentition (Turner).** Sinodont vs Sundadont; three American dental groups ≈ three migrations; dentochronology ~14 ka Amerind–NE Asian split — non-independence when teeth pooled by language, Paleoindian tooth scarcity, Szathmary critiques (C-4-020–028, C-5-025). Classical markers inadequate for tripartite model (C-4-029).

**mtDNA / NRY (as of Meltzer 2009).** Founding mtDNA A–D (~98% studied Native Americans; frequencies ~40/32/17/7%) plus X (~3% NE US, pre-contact, Altai-related) — five founders (C-4-033–036). Early three-migration mtDNA patterns dissolved with more sampling; Merriwether single-migration rationale widely held (C-4-034–035). Molecular ages scatter (early ~20–41 ka A/C/D/X, younger B; Tamm et al. A–D generally <20 ka BP) — clock dates lineage divergence, not necessarily migration (C-4-031, C-4-039, C-1-036). NRY founders C and Q; ages ~18–14 ka; lower diversity than Asia → bottleneck; R largely post-1492 (C-4-041–042). Effective founding size estimates diverge: Hey ~70 effective (~200–350 census) vs Kitchen ~1,000–5,400 effective (C-4-043). Tamm et al. **Beringian standstill** then swift expansion (haplogroup C and related) (C-4-048, C-5-049). aDNA: China Lake haplogroup M (~5 ka BP); On Your Knees Cave new D haplotype ~10,300 BP (C-4-045). Crania: Meltzer calls picture “very confused” (Brace two-types, Neves Paleoamerican, Jantz & Owsley, Steele & Powell) (C-4-054–058) — morphology not reliable population assignment once later aDNA available (Reich layer above).

**Migration count.** Genetics vs linguistics disagree; Schurr: multiple close expansions may be genetically undetectable; Schroeder: common founding population ≠ migration count (C-5-040–043). Minimum archaeological: Paleoindian + Paleoarctic as two Pleistocene pulses; pre-Clovis complexes lack coherent inter-American links (MV ≠ Clovis) (C-5-044–045). Clovis as mere diffusion among pre-existing population rejected by Meltzer (C-5-048). Archaeology gives minimum age; genetics maximum age brackets (C-5-051, C-5-093).

### Clovis tempo and colonization framing (bridge to culture page)

Rapid radiation ≤~500 radiocarbon years (perhaps ~1,000 calendar years under early YD plateaus) among fastest prehistoric expansions (C-6-027). Net expansion ~10–20 km/year (C-5-060). Meltzer leans “Bold Transient Explorers” (Beaton) over Cautious Estate Settlers — archaeological density too low for estate settlement; caches as outbound supply depots on unknown landscapes; Clovis points as continent-wide style zone / social glue (C-5-078–084). Empty-continent adaptive challenge and landscape-learning tempos (200–1,000 years) frame Ch. 7 (C-5-057–075).

### Meltzer’s standing synthesis (position, not wiki voice)

As of 2009 Meltzer treats pre-Clovis (Monte Verde-class) as established reality; favors coastal entry for earliest peopling while allowing later corridor use; rejects Solutrean; treats genetics as constraining Asian origin and bottlenecked founders without settling exact pulse count; insists archaeological proof remains primary for routes and timing.
