# Mead–Freeman Debate (Samoa)

Standing dispute over the accuracy and implications of [[mead-margaret|Margaret Mead]]'s *[[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-1928|Coming of Age in Samoa]]* (1928) — whether her portrait of easy adolescence and relative sexual freedom in 1920s Manu'a was ethnographically sound, and what that means for culture-versus-biology claims about [[adolescence]].

## Positions

### Position A — Mead's Samoa as valid natural experiment

Mead argued, from nine months on Ta'ū among ~fifty girls, that adolescent conflict is not biologically inevitable and that Samoan socialization produced a smoother youth. [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s foreword read the result as confirming that much ascribed to human nature is response to civilization. Later Mead (1973 preface) insisted the book is about **1926–28** Manu'a and America, and described anonymization practices.

### Position B — Freeman's challenge

Derek Freeman (works not yet primary-ingested) argued that Mead was misled about Samoan sexual mores and adolescent experience, emphasizing virginity ideals, aggression, and informant joking, and linking alleged error to cultural-determinist bias. Treat full detail as **pending primary ingest** of Freeman; do not invent chapter-level Freeman claims here.

### Position C — Historicizing / both-sides limits

Many later commentators treat the controversy as overdetermined by American culture-war stakes (1980s sociobiology and sex debates), note that both Mead and Freeman wrote argumentative books for broad audiences, and urge reading each ethnography as **dated and positioned** rather than as a single true Samoa.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Mead did fieldwork on Ta'ū, Manu'a, ~1925–26; published 1928; sample of adolescent girls in three villages.
- The book was a bestseller and shaped public and disciplinary talk about adolescence.
- A major public controversy erupted decades later around Freeman's critique.
- Samoan communities and scholars have their own stakes in representation (Mead 1973 notes embarrassment and pride among later Samoan readers).

## Resolution status

**Open** as a packaged culture-war debate; **partially re-specified** in professional anthropology toward: (1) date-stamped reading of Mead; (2) multi-source Samoan ethnography; (3) refusal of single-case settlement of nature–nurture. No wiki-voice verdict.
