# Mead–Freeman Debate (Samoa)

The most public controversy in the history of anthropology: whether [[mead-margaret|Margaret
Mead]]'s *[[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-1928|Coming of Age in Samoa]]* (1928) — a portrait of easy
adolescence and relative sexual freedom in 1920s Manu'a — was ethnographically sound, and what
that means for culture-versus-biology claims about [[adolescence]]. Both principal texts are now
ingested: Mead's ethnography and [[freeman-derek|Derek Freeman]]'s refutation
*[[freeman-margaret-mead-and-samoa-1983|Margaret Mead and Samoa]]* (1983). No verdict is entered
in wiki voice.

## Positions

### Position A — Mead's Samoa as valid natural experiment

Mead argued, from nine months on Ta'ū among ~25 principal adolescent-girl informants, that
adolescent conflict is not biologically inevitable: her girls differed from pre-pubertal sisters
"chiefly in bodily changes," and Samoan casualness, multi-adult households, diffuse authority,
few conflicting standards, and relatively free commoner-girl premarital sexuality (with *taupou*
virginity a rank exception) produced a smoother youth. [[boas-franz|Boas]]'s foreword read the
result as confirming that "much of what we ascribe to human nature is no more than a reaction to
the restraints put upon us by our civilisation." Later Mead (1969, 1973) date-stamped the book
to 1925–26 Manu'a, conceded a "serious problem" reconciling it with other records — offering a
"temporary felicitous relaxation" or a young-woman's-vantage explanation — but refused to revise
the 1928 text. Details: [[mead-coming-of-age-samoa-study]].

### Position B — Freeman's refutation

*(All attributed to Freeman, [[freeman-margaret-mead-and-samoa-1983|1983]]; framed by him as
Popperian "error elimination," targeting the "negative instance" logic, not Mead personally.)*
Freeman marshals his own Western Samoa fieldwork (1940–43, 1965–68, 1981), 19th-century
missionary/traveler sources, and colonial court and police records to argue Mead misdescribed
Samoa on nearly every dimension:

- **Rank and competition**: an intensely rank-conscious *matai* society (Holmes: rank "the focal
  point of Samoan culture") with fierce title competition, oratorical contest (*fa'atau*),
  club-fighting, and competitive gift-giving — against Mead's "casual," "competition completely
  impossible" society.
- **Aggression and warfare**: high assault and homicide rates (his 1960s statistics vs NZ,
  Australia, US) and destructive historical warfare (the A'ana war of 1830–31; head-taking) —
  against Mead's "slight and spasmodic" war and "most peaceful" people.
- **Religion, punishment, childrearing**: developed pagan religion and intense Christianity;
  severe corporal punishment (documented to age 18 months); early strong maternal attachment
  followed by harsh discipline — against Mead's "underdeveloped" religion and casual, diffuse
  family.
- **Character — "the mask"**: an outward decorum (*fa'aaloalo*) over suppressed rage (*musu*),
  with elevated adolescent suicide — against Mead's "no strong passions."
- **Sex and adolescence** (the crux): a Western-Polynesian **cult of virginity** (the *taupou*
  and ceremonial defloration), his 1967 survey finding 73% of comparable girls virgins, and high
  rates of *moetotolo* and forcible **rape** ("completely foreign to the Samoan mind," per Mead)
  — arguing Samoan adolescence is not "the age of maximum ease."

Freeman's account of *how* Mead erred (Ch. 19): a half-hour Boas briefing and no preparatory
ethnography; ~10 weeks' language study; residence with the American Holt family, not a Samoan
household; reliance on adolescent-girl informants; and a prior doctrinal commitment that led her
to see "life through the eyes" of that group. On the **"hoaxing" (*tau fa'ase'e*) explanation** —
that her informants teased her with false tales — the 1983 text presents it as a *Samoan* account
(via Gerber and his own informants), **explicitly hedged**: "we cannot, in the absence of detailed
corroborative evidence, be sure about the truth of this Samoan claim," and "of no scientific
significance" to his refutation. (Freeman hardened it into a thesis only in his 1999 *Fateful
Hoaxing*, not ingested.)

### Position C — Historicizing / both-sides limits

Many commentators treat the controversy as overdetermined by American culture-war stakes (1980s
sociobiology and sex debates); note that **both** wrote argumentative books for broad audiences;
and stress that Freeman's evidence is largely **Western** Samoa (Upolu/Sa'anapu) decades after
Mead's **American** Samoa (Manu'a, 1925–26) — a comparability gap Freeman bridges by borrowing
Keesing's and Gray's cultural-continuity claims rather than establishing the 1925–1940 interval
independently. Restudies cut both ways: Holmes's 1954 Manu'a restudy found much "at variance"
with Mead yet judged her reliability "remarkably high." The professional resolution has moved
toward date-stamped, multi-source Samoan ethnography and refusal of single-case settlement of
nature–nurture.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Mead did fieldwork on Ta'ū, Manu'a (American Samoa), 1925–26; published 1928; principal sample
  ~25 adolescent girls across three villages.
- Freeman did fieldwork in Western Samoa (a different polity) over 1940–1981; his statistical and
  historical evidence is real but drawn mostly from criminal records, mission-era sources, and a
  later period.
- The 1920s were politically turbulent (the Mau movement) — Freeman uses this against Mead's
  "temporary relaxation" defense; it equally complicates reading any single moment as timeless.
- Both books shaped public understanding: Mead's of adolescence and sexuality, Freeman's of
  anthropology's scientific status.
- Samoan communities and scholars have their own stakes in representation (some, per Shore,
  reacting to Mead with "she lied"; a reading Freeman himself rejected).

## Resolution status

**Open** as a packaged culture-war debate; **partially re-specified** in professional
anthropology toward (1) date-stamped reading of Mead; (2) multi-source Samoan ethnography;
(3) refusal of single-case settlement of nature–nurture. No wiki-voice verdict.
