# Civilizing Process Debate

Open dispute over the claims, scope, and implications of [[elias-norbert|Elias]]'s [[civilizing-process]] program (*[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]*, 1939). Positions below in strongest form; **no adjudication in wiki voice**.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Elias published a two-volume German study in 1939 linking manners-book series to European state and court history.
- Weber's monopoly-of-force definition is an explicit reference point Elias process-ualizes.
- Reception in English-language sociology accelerated decades after first publication; Elias's 1968 Postscript addresses reception and method.
- Twentieth-century mass violence is historically real; sides disagree on what it implies for the theory.

## Positions

### 1. Elias program (strongest form)

Long-run Western upper-class standards of conduct and thresholds of shame/repugnance shifted in a patterned way; this psychical change is bound to sociogenesis of monopolies of force and tax and to courtization; "civilization" is a technical process concept, not a moral eulogy; reverse or decivilizing processes are conceivable; unplanned figurational dynamics explain outcomes no one designed.

### 2. Teleology / smuggled progress

Despite technical disclaimers, the directional language and shame-threshold "advance" function as a progress story isomorphic to older evolutionary civilizational rankings.

### 3. Eurocentrism / colonial entanglement

The core evidence is Western secular upper classes; generalizing to "civilization" as such is illegitimate; the watchword "civilization" historically legitimated colonial and crusading violence (a point Elias himself notes as echo — critics say the theory does not metabolize that fact).

### 4. Violence anomaly

Industrial war, genocide, and bureaucratic mass killing in the twentieth century show that "pacification" and self-constraint do not yield a net civilizing outcome, or that any such process is fragile and reversible in ways that gut the original narrative's weight.

### 5. Source bias

Manners manuals are prescriptive elite texts; they may track ideology of manners more than practice; bourgeois and subaltern bodies, and women's experience, are poorly captured.

## Resolution status

**Open.** Program continues; critiques are standard in reception literature. Further primary critiques (e.g. Duerr and respondents) to be filed when ingested.

### 6. Bauman: the civilizing process redeploys rather than eliminates violence

[[bauman-zygmunt|Zygmunt Bauman]] (*[[bauman-modernity-holocaust-1989|Modernity and the Holocaust]]*, 1989) argues the Eliasian narrative underwrites an "etiological myth": modern civilization did not remove violence but concentrated it in the state's monopoly and hid it from daily life, leaving pacified populations *defenceless* before centralized coercion. On this reading the Holocaust is not a breakdown of the civilizing process but one of its possibilities. See [[holocaust-and-modernity-debate]].
