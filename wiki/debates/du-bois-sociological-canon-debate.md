# Du Bois's Place in the Sociological Canon

A historiographic dispute over whether — and why — [[du-bois-w-e-b|W. E. B.
Du Bois]]'s pioneering empirical sociology, above all
[[du-bois-philadelphia-negro-study|*The Philadelphia Negro*]] (1899), was
marginalized in the standard genealogy of the discipline that instead credits the
[[chicago-school|Chicago School]] with founding scientific urban sociology.

## Position A — "The Scholar Denied"

Du Bois conducted the first empirical, statistically grounded community study in
American sociology a full generation before the Chicago School, pioneering the
survey-plus-mapping method later credited to others. His near-total exclusion from
the canon reflects the racial structure of the discipline — segregation of Black
scholars into under-resourced Black institutions and white sociology's refusal to
cite them — not the quality of the work. Du Bois's methods "lay dormant" only
because the field looked away. Associated with Aldon Morris's *The Scholar Denied*
(2015) and the reassessment in Katz & Sugrue (1998); Elijah Anderson (2024)
stresses the work's continued neglect on syllabi despite its explanatory power.

## Position B — Institutional Location

Without denying Du Bois's priority or brilliance, this position emphasizes that
disciplines are built by institutions, networks, journals, and training lineages —
which coalesced around Chicago in the 1910s–20s, not around Du Bois's Atlanta
program. On this reading the canon tracks institution-building rather than sheer
priority, and Du Bois's marginal position followed from his exclusion from those
networks as much as from active erasure.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides accept that *The Philadelphia Negro* preceded Chicago urban sociology,
was methodologically pioneering, and was little cited by mainstream sociology for
decades before a late-20th-century revival. They disagree on the **weight of cause**
(active racial erasure vs. institutional location) and on what "founding" a
discipline should mean (priority of work vs. building of networks).
