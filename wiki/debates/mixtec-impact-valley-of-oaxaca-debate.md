# The Mixtec Impact on the Postclassic Valley of Oaxaca

At issue: does **Monte Albán V** (the Late Postclassic Valley of Oaxaca) represent a substantial [[mixtec-culture|Mixtec]] cultural and demographic presence — invasion, in-migration, or ethnic replacement of the [[zapotec-civilization|Zapotec]] — or is it fundamentally continuous Zapotec culture with only modest, marriage-mediated Mixtec influence? The dispute is staged within [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983) itself, and both sides note it has been unusually politically charged since [[caso-alfonso|Alfonso Caso]]'s 1932 excavation of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]]'s Tomb 7, with mutual accusations of findings tailored to ethnic-nationalist agendas.

## Position 1 — Monte Albán V as a Mixtec cultural province ([[paddock-john|John Paddock]], building on Caso and [[bernal-ignacio|Bernal]])

Vestiges of Monte Albán IV Zapotec culture persisted only marginally; Monte Albán V is essentially a province of Mixtec culture, brought into the valley by Mixtec migrants, differing from Coixtlahuaca or Yanhuitlan only as normal regional variation. Paddock marshals ~11 categories of evidence from his [[yagul|Yagul]] and [[mitla|Mitla]] excavations: the disappearance of Classic Zapotec urn deities in favor of Mixtec-style stone penates and effigy vessels; seated "cajete-on-head" burials paralleled in the Mixteca; Mixtec polychrome and "Cacique Burnished" gray ware; distinctive sahumador lids shared with Coixtlahuaca; "Mixtec wall construction"; and Caso's linkage of Zaachila Tomb 1 reliefs to the Yanhuitlan dynasty.

## Position 2 — Local Zapotec continuity with limited Mixtec presence ([[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] & [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]], Topic 80)

The editors reject both polar readings. They accept a documented Mixtec presence in the western valley (Cuilapan/Zaachila), originating in strategic marriage alliances between Zapotec royalty of Zaachila and Mixtec royalty of Yanhuitlan-Almoloyas, with most in-migrating Mixtecs likely *tay situndayu* (tenant-servants) working land granted to a royal marriage partner — not a mass invasion. Postclassic Oaxacan warfare, they argue, was "cacique versus cacique, not nation versus nation." They deny that G3M gray ware, the step-fret (*greca*), or intermarried royal lineages can be read as markers of "the Mixtec people" versus "the Zapotec people" as bounded ethnic-political blocs, tracing several supposed Mixtec traits to Classic Monte Albán III Zapotec precedents.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side
Both sides agree that: Mixtec-style polychrome ceramics, penates, and certain architectural motifs appear in the Late Postclassic valley; royal Zapotec–Mixtec intermarriage is documented ethnohistorically; and the western valley (Cuilapan/Zaachila) shows the strongest Mixtec signal. They disagree on whether these signal population replacement (Paddock) or elite alliance atop demographic continuity (Flannery & Marcus).

## Curator's Note (2026-07-15)
This debate is the volume's clearest worked example of the four-way non-identity rule: it is fundamentally a dispute about whether pottery, architecture, and burial style track a *people*.
