# Structure vs. Agency Debate

## Shared ground

Social scientists disagree about how to weight **structural conditions** (class positions, institutions, material constraints, inherited tradition) against **agency** (individual and collective action, choice, leadership). The dispute is theoretical and recurrent across eras; this page records positions as they enter the wiki from primary sources. No adjudication in wiki voice.

## Positions (strongest form each)


### Eliasian figuration / unplanned order — Elias, *The Civilizing Process* (1939/1968)

[[elias-norbert|Elias]] argues that plans and actions of individuals interweave into an order *sui generis* stronger than any single will: civilization and state monopolies are largely unplanned yet ordered outcomes of [[figuration|figurations]] of interdependent people (*[[elias-civilizing-process-1939|The Civilizing Process]]*; 1968 Postscript). He rejects *homo clausus* (closed independent personality) as both bad psychology and bad epistemology, replacing it with open personalities in figurations. Structure is the compelling web of interdependence; agency is real but never absolute; long-run outcomes need not be intended. Distinct from Bourdieusian habitus/field and from Giddensian structuration (not yet ingested as primary). See [[civilizing-process]], [[figuration]], [[structure-vs-agency-debate]] positions list.


### 1. Constrained agency — Marx, *Eighteenth Brumaire* (1852)

[[marx-karl|Marx]] argues: “Man makes his own history, but he does not make it out of the whole cloth… out of such [conditions] as he finds close at hand. The tradition of all past generations weighs like an alp upon the brain of the living” ([[men-make-their-own-history]]). In the same work he treats the coup’s success as “a necessary, unavoidable result of the development that had preceded,” not mere surprise or clever secrecy — while still narrating Bonaparte’s tactics, parliamentary failures, and class choices. **Agency within structure**; tradition as cognitive-political weight ([[historical-costume]]).

### 2. Structural primacy (as charged against Marxism / as internal hard reading)

A hard materialist reading holds that economic structures and class forces determine political outcomes; personal will and ideology are secondary. Critics of Marxism often ascribe this view to Marx; the *Brumaire* formula is frequently cited *against* that ascription. Recorded here as a **position in the debate**, not as the wiki’s verdict on Marx.

### 3. Strong voluntarism / conspiracy or great-man explanations

Accounts that treat the coup (or revolutions generally) as the product of a few swindlers’ plot, or of pure democratic failure of nerve, without structural class analysis. Marx polemicizes against mere “surprise” as explanation of how thirty-six millions fell to “three swindlers,” while granting contingency of tactics.

### 4. Durkheimian social constraint — *Rules*, *Suicide*

[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] defines [[social-fact|social facts]] as external to the individual and endowed with coercive power; “every time a social phenomenon is directly explained by a psychological phenomenon, the explanation is false” (*[[durkheim-rules-of-sociological-method-1895|Rules]]* Ch. V, attributed). Association produces a psychical individuality of a **new kind**; individuals are necessary but not sufficient. The 1901 Preface softens reification charges (social life = representations; no substantial soul) without surrendering exteriority/constraint. Practical payoff: reform targets structures and secondary groups, not exhortation alone (*Suicide*). Closely related but distinct pole: [[methodological-individualism-debate]].

### 5. Dual character of society — Berger & Luckmann (1966)

[[berger-peter-l|Berger]] and [[luckmann-thomas|Luckmann]] (*[[berger-luckmann-social-construction-1966|The Social Construction of Reality]]*) argue that Durkheim’s “consider social facts as things” and Weber’s focus on subjective meaning-complexes of action are **not contradictory**: society possesses objective facticity *and* is built up by activity expressing subjective meaning; that dual character makes society’s reality *sui generis*. Formula: “Society is a human product. Society is an objective reality. Man is a social product” ([[externalization-objectivation-internalization]]). Central question: how subjective meanings become objective facticities. Socialization inducts individuals into a world they did not make; they nonetheless (collectively) produce and maintain it. **Mutual constitution** without dissolving either pole. See [[social-construction-of-reality]]; [[hubs/theory/social-construction-of-reality]].

### 6. Joint action and interpretive process — Blumer / symbolic interactionism (1969)

[[blumer-herbert|Blumer]] (*[[blumer-symbolic-interactionism-1969|Symbolic Interactionism]]*) reconstructs [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]] against the "structural conception of human society" that treats social system, roles, and norms as self-operating determinants of action. **Society**, he argues, is "a vast number of occurring joint actions," not a system equilibrium; **[[joint-action|joint action]]** is the fitting together of diverse participants' acts through interpretation and definition. Structure (roles, status, bureaucracy, codes) is **not denied** — "such a position would be ridiculous" — but its importance "does not lie in an alleged determination of action nor in an alleged existence as parts of a self-operating societal system"; it enters only as taken into account in the process that forms joint actions. Interaction is "between people and not between roles." Alignment need not spring from shared values (compromise, duress, mutual use, necessity also fit acts together). Method commitment: study action from the actor's standpoint. See [[symbolic-interactionism]]; [[hubs/theory/symbolic-interactionism]].

### 7. Skocpolian structuralism in revolution studies — Skocpol (1979)

[[skocpol-theda|Theda Skocpol]]'s *[[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-1979|States and Social Revolutions]]* (1979) states the hard structural position for macro-comparative sociology (attributed): social revolutions are explained by structural conjunctures — old-regime state breakdown under international pressure plus autonomous peasant insurrection — "beyond the deliberate control of avowed revolutionaries." Her epigram, borrowed from Wendell Phillips: "Revolutions are not made; they come." Actors, ideologies, and vanguard organizations exploit crises they do not create. The strongest counter-position is the **agency-and-ideology critique** (William H. Sewell Jr., 1985; Michael Burawoy, 1989): that ideology and cultural creativity are constitutive of revolutions and their outcomes, not epiphenomenal — to which Skocpol replied by admitting ideology as "cultural idiom" while defending the structural frame. See [[structural-theory-of-revolution]] and the [[skocpol-states-social-revolutions-study|study page]].

### 8. Habitus, practical sense, and strategy — Bourdieu, *The Logic of Practice* (1980/1990)

*(Strongest form of the position as Bourdieu states it in *[[bourdieu-logic-of-practice-1990|The Logic of Practice]]*; not a wiki-voice verdict. See [[practice-theory]], [[habitus]], [[practical-sense]], [[strategy-vs-rule]].)*

[[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]] argues that the social sciences are trapped in a **false choice** between **objectivism** (social physics: structures, statistical distributions, mechanical models of reciprocity or kinship that treat agents as executants of rules or unconscious programs) and **subjectivism** (social phenomenology, rational-actor finalism, or pure existential freedom that treats practices as transparent projects of a consciousness without inertia). Both, he holds, are incomplete; both project the theorist's relation to the world into the practice analyzed (**intellectualism** / "intellectualocentrism").

**Third path via practice.** He claims one must return to practice as the site of the dialectic of *opus operatum* and *modus operandi* — objectified products of historical practice and incorporated products ([[habitus]]); structures and dispositions. Habitus is presented as **spontaneity without consciousness or will**, opposed both to mechanical necessity of things without history and to reflexive freedom of subjects "without inertia." The concept aims, in his words, to transcend antinomies of determinism and freedom, conditioning and creativity, consciousness and the unconscious, individual and society.

**Strategy, not rule.** Regularity of practice, on this position, is not produced by agents following juridical or ethical **rules** (legalism), nor by mysterious cerebral/social mechanisms that make the model into a real governor of action (structuralist "unconscious"). Sliding from a model that *fits* regularity to a rule that *guides* behaviour is, he argues, the commonest scientific error. Analysis should move from **rule to strategy** — regulated improvisation by practical sense under temporal pressure — without collapsing into calculating rational-actor anthropology. Even where parallel-cousin marriage becomes a compelling obligation (as in extreme [[kabyle]] cases he documents), he holds there is no need to appeal to ethical or juridical rules to account for practices oriented toward material and symbolic interests. See [[strategy-vs-rule]].

**Time and uncertainty.** Practices such as [[gift-exchange]] and the joust of honour are, for agents, irreversible sequences of relatively unpredictable acts; the "cycle of reciprocity" exists only for the omniscient spectator. Reintroducing uncertainty is reintroducing time (rhythm, orientation, irreversibility), substituting the dialectic of strategies for the mechanics of the model — **without** falling into imaginary anthropology of the rational actor.

**Neither automata nor pure calculators.** Agents, on Bourdieu's strongest formulation, are neither automata moved by obscure mechanisms (objectivist/Lévi-Straussian cycle reduced to obligatory interlocking) nor pure calculating subjects. Practical sense does not burden itself with rules, principles, or calculations excluded by the urgency of action. Matrimonial and reproductive "strategies" have their principle neither in calculating reason nor in mechanical economic determination alone, but in dispositions that make objectively calculable demands of an economy of practices experienced as duty or irresistible feeling.

**Objectivism as necessary but incomplete stage.** Objectivism is a necessary stage in breaking with primary experience and constructing objective relationships, but leads to error when it hypostatizes those relations as realities already constituted outside the history of the group. Social science must reintroduce primary representations into the full definition of the object: individuals and groups are defined not only by what they are but by what they are reputed to be. [[misrecognition]] of objective truth (of exchange, class, kinship exploitation) is part of the full truth of those relations.

**Founding *nomos* in ordinary practice.** The fundamental separation (*nomos*) one is tempted to locate in an initial act of constitution is, he argues, instituted in each ordinary act of ordinary practice (e.g. sexual division of labour) — unconscious, collective, continuous creation as basis of durability and transcendence with respect to individual consciousnesses.

**Empirical matrix.** Kabyle agrarian, matrimonial, honor, gift, and house practice (*Logic*, Book II and Appendix) is the principal ethnographic illustration in this source; Béarn inheritance material illustrates strategy vs legalism on European ground. Structuralist kinship taxonomies that treat parallel-cousin marriage as "scandal" are challenged as artefacts of anthropological categories rather than of practice itself.

### 9. Coleman's rational-choice / methodological-individualist position

[[coleman-james|Coleman]]'s *[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|Foundations of
Social Theory]]* (1990) occupies the **agency/individualist pole** — but a chastened
one. Explanation should descend to the purposive actions of individual actors and
compose system behaviour back up (the *internal analysis of system behavior*; see
[[methodological-individualism]], [[rational-choice-theory]]); social structures —
[[corporate-actor|corporate actors]], [[social-norm|norms]], [[trust]] — are
emergent, often unintended products of transfers of control. Coleman insists this is
"a variant of methodological individualism" in which interaction yields "emergent
phenomena at the system level," and that the downward **macro→micro** arrow (structure
shaping situations and interests) is as important as the upward one — so his position
is a *dualism of levels* (see [[micro-macro-problem|Coleman's boat]]), not atomism.
It is the mirror image of Bourdieu's third path: where Bourdieu dissolves the
objectivism/subjectivism antinomy into practical sense and habitus and explicitly
rejects "rational-actor anthropology," Coleman embraces purposive rational action as
the microfoundation and locates the real theoretical work in the composition step
that habitus is meant to bypass. The two are the wiki's principal opposed answers to
the same question of how micro produces macro.

### 10. Duality of structure — Giddens, *The Constitution of Society* (1984)

*(Strongest form as Giddens states it in *[[giddens-constitution-of-society-1984|The Constitution of Society]]*; not a wiki-voice verdict. See [[structuration-theory]], [[duality-of-structure]], [[hubs/theory/structuration-theory]].)*

[[giddens-anthony|Giddens]] reconceptualizes the subject/object (structure/agency) dualism as a **duality**: structure is **both medium and outcome** of the practices it recursively organizes. Structure is [[rules-and-resources|rules and resources]] (allocative and authoritative), existing as memory traces and as instantiated in action — not an external shell and not a plastic creation of sovereign subjects. Agents are [[knowledgeability|knowledgeable]] ([[practical-consciousness]] and [[discursive-consciousness]]); [[routinization]] sustains [[ontological-security]] and is a prime expression of duality in daily life. Structure is always **enabling and constraining**. Micro/macro dualism is replaced by [[social-and-system-integration|social integration]] (co-presence) and system integration (extended time-space). Method: analysis of strategic conduct and institutional analysis as brackets, each rounded out by duality; [[double-hermeneutic]] binds social science to lay frames of meaning.

**Distinct from** Bourdieusian habitus/field (parallel Critical Turn third path; different apparatus), Eliasian figuration, Berger & Luckmann's three-moment dialectic, and Coleman's rational-choice composition. Giddens explicitly refuses to choose between “structural sociology” and strong methodological individualism (*Constitution* Ch. 4 Critical Notes).

### 11. Change internal to structure — Sewell, "A Theory of Structure" (1992)

*(Strongest form as [[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] states it in *[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]* / AJS 1992; not a wiki-voice verdict. See [[transposable-schemas]], [[agency]].)*

[[sewell-william-jr|William H. Sewell Jr.]] argues that structure and agency are mutually constitutive and that **change is generated from within the ordinary operation of structures**, not introduced from outside. Building on but reconstructing Giddens's [[duality-of-structure|duality]], he defines structure as mutually sustaining **[[transposable-schemas|schemas (virtual) and resources (actual)]]** and derives [[agency]] directly from them: agency is "the capacity to transpose and extend schemas to new contexts" and to remobilize polysemous resources under new schemas. Five features make structures transformable — multiplicity, transposability of schemas, unpredictability of resource accumulation, polysemy of resources, intersection of structures. Against the structural pole (and explicitly against [[habitus|Bourdieu's habitus]], which he reads as "agent-proof"), he insists structures are "at risk … in all of the social encounters they shape"; against a drift into idealism, he insists resources have independent, material weight. Distinct from Giddens (whom he faults for an incoherent "virtual" structure), from Bourdieu (determinism), and from Coleman/rational choice (he denies calculation can explain the transformation of the very frameworks within which actors calculate). Agency is universal, unevenly distributed, and collective as well as individual (he disputes Barry Hindess on the univocal application of "agent" to collectives).

## Archaeological uptake — Johnson (2011), attributed

*(Attributed to Matthew Johnson's textbook account of postprocessual archaeology's borrowings; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]] and [[post-processual-archaeology]].)* Some postprocessual archaeologists borrowed [[giddens-anthony|Giddens]]'s [[structuration-theory|structuration theory]] — the recursive relationship in which people manipulate social rules creatively, thereby reinforcing or transforming structure — and [[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]'s [[practice-theory|practice theory]], itself a reaction against orthodox structuralist anthropology's passive-actor model, to argue for active, creative social agents in the archaeological record. [[hodder-ian|Hodder]]'s own move in this direction is reported (attributed, per Johnson) as arising from ethnoarchaeological fieldwork among the Nuba: an unpublished field observation of a Nuba woman deliberately dumping polluting hearth-ash outside the compound, where men could see, rather than inside as convention required, illustrating **agency as rule-manipulation** rather than passive rule-following. See [[agency]], [[hodder-ian]].

**Densified — Hodder & Hutson, *Reading the Past* (2003).** *([[hodder-reading-past-2004|attributed]], chs. 5–6, ingested 2026-07-10.)* Hodder & Hutson distinguish **three senses of "agency"** in archaeology: (1) *anti-humanist* (agency works *through* individuals — Foucault, Butler's "citationality," where the norm-resisting subject is itself produced by the norm); (2) *impact-based* ("power over" — the effect of action, with intention simply assumed = outcome); and (3) *intentional action*, their favoured sense (illustrated by the passer-by who "acts" by catching a president's bullet but only intended to cross the road). Their **four-step model of action** — positioning/assessment, formation of intention, doing, and evaluation of impact — situates agency between [[habitus]] and event, insisting "group action is a form of individual agency" and that there can be "no free will" in the sense of acting outside society. They critique John C. Barrett's agency work for over-attending to the *conditions* for action at the expense of intention, and (ch. 6) argue the "individual" was used "abundantly and uncritically as a synonym for agency" in earlier editions — replacing it with a relational, [[embodiment|embodied]], culturally variable **personhood** (the self's boundary "not isomorphic with the biological body").
