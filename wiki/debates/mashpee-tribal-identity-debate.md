# The Mashpee Tribal-Identity Dispute

Whether the Indian community of Mashpee, Massachusetts constituted a continuously
existing tribe — litigated in *Mashpee Tribe v. New Seabury et al.* (41 days of
testimony, fall 1977, before Judge Walter J. Skinner) and recorded here from
[[clifford-james|Clifford]]'s account ([[clifford-predicament-of-culture-1988]], ch. 12).
The courtroom conflict doubles as a scholarly dispute — "a struggle between history and
anthropology" (Clifford) — about what culture, tribe, and identity are. Society page:
[[mashpee-wampanoag]].

## Position — "History I" (defense; after Hutchins)

There never was a genuine Indian tribe in Mashpee. The community was a colonial-era
creation of disparate Indian survivors and other minorities: Richard Bourne's 1666
"praying Indian" plantation, Christian conversion, district status in 1763, the 1834–70
transition to full township, Revolutionary War service, and the near-disappearance of
the "Indian" census category 1905–60 mark a progressive, voluntary movement into full
citizenship — tribal status, where it existed, was repeatedly relinquished. Chief
evidentiary base: the documentary archive (Hutchins 1979; his trial testimony).
Sociologist Jean Guillemin, for the defense, concluded from pretrial depositions and
documents that Mashpee never had a distinct culture, contrasting the Micmac (whom she
had studied firsthand); her "anthropology by deposition" was attacked on cross.

## Position — "History II" (plaintiffs; after Axtell, Campisi, Sturtevant, Deloria)

The Mashpee maintained a continuous if flexible and often informal Indian identity
across three centuries: syncretic Christianity with an indigenous cast
(Massachusett-language preaching; resistance to imposed missionaries), collective land
tenure functioning as a de facto reservation, intermarriage that absorbed outsiders
into an Indian core, and periodic revivals (the 1833 Mashpee "Declaration of
Independence," the 1920s Wampanoag Nation, the 1970s Tribal Council). Campisi's
five-part tribe definition (ascriptive membership, kinship network, consciousness of
kind, homeland, political leadership) fit Mashpee; Sturtevant distinguished
acculturation from assimilation and offered the "emerging tribe" model (Seminole
precedent); Deloria testified that Indian usage of "tribe" simply means "a group of
people living pretty much in the same place who know who their relatives are."

## Verdict and Clifford's reading

The jury found tribal existence in 1834 and 1842 but not 1790, 1869, 1870, or 1976,
and no continuity; Skinner dismissed the suit under the *Montoya* (1901) definition
(upheld on appeal). Clifford argues (attributed) that three Western categories
prejudged the outcome — cultural wholeness, literate-over-oral epistemology, and
narrative continuity — and that both attorneys' summations shared "a linear teleology"
that "ruled out the possibility of a group existing discontinuously... being both
Indian and American." His own stated (personal) conclusion: organized Indian life had
gone on in Mashpee for 350 years. The 1987 Gay Head BIA recognition reversal, aided by
Campisi, is the recorded aftermath as of the source.
