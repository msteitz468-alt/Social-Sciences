# Binford–Bordes Mousterian Debate

Dispute over how to give **meaning** to variability among Mousterian (Middle Paleolithic / Neanderthal-associated, ~125,000–30,000 ya in Binford’s framing) assemblages: cultural/ethnic units versus functional/activity differentiation. *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* (1983) is dedicated to Bordes’ memory and recounts the debate as a **methodological** crisis about the nature of culture, not merely Neanderthal behavior.

## Positions

### Culture-historical / Bordes

François Bordes developed quantitative tool-type graphs and recognized **four basic Mousterian assemblage types** (with few ambiguous cases):

1. **Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition (MAT)** — hand axes; moderate scrapers; many denticulates/notches; high backed knives; low cumulative graph.
2. **Typical Mousterian** — roughly equal tool proportions (diagonal graph); few hand axes; fewer backed knives than MAT.
3. **Denticulate Mousterian** — few scrapers, many notches/denticulates; hand axes rare/absent.
4. **Charentian Group** — scraper-dominated high convex graph; **Quina** (transverse scrapers, rare Levallois) and **Ferrassie** (side scrapers, frequent Levallois).

Bordes treated flake-production technique as largely raw-material conditioned (little culture-historical use). Application at Combe Grenal and related sequences showed **alternating industries**, **parallel phyla** (types coexisting over long spans), and **“tenacious cultures”** relatively unchanged despite contact. Binford notes Bordes’ pattern recognition was integrated into an older organic **tree-of-life** model of culture (assemblages as cultural species).

### Functional / Binford (with Sally R. Binford 1966)

[[binford-lewis|Lewis R. Binford]] (with [[binford-sally-r|Sally R. Binford]], 1966) suggested conditions that, if true in the past, could produce Bordes’ patterning via **different activities at separate locations**, with technology flexible enough that the same activities could use different tools at different sites — not necessarily four ethnic peoples. He treated the controversy as questioning **basic ideas about the nature of culture**, not merely filling data gaps: Bordes’ methods were accepted as sophisticated, so the pattern seemed “self-evident” and did not immediately scramble for new methods the way early-man site-content disputes did.

Nunamiut ethnoarchaeology (scale: few families covering roughly Dordogne-sized space; heterogeneous site types from one group; site complexes; intense locales more diverse, not more similar) makes four coexisting ethnic toolkit-groups hard to sustain — but Binford states these experiences **do not prove** his Mousterian functional model; they show Palaeolithic conventions inadequate for documented land-use diversity.

## Stringer & Gamble 1993 Engagement (attributed)

*Source: [[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993|In Search of the Neanderthals]]* ch. 7 (and linked transitional discussion). Date-stamp: **as of Stringer & Gamble 1993**.

### Binfords' settlement model (as S&G present it)

Lewis & Sally Binford proposed a regional settlement model for limited [[mousterian|Mousterian]] assemblage variability versus Bordes: **base camps** (food preparation/consumption; tool manufacture/repair), **work camps** (obtain food/raw materials), plus overnight stops under uneven, seasonal resources. Functional assignment of tool types and factor analysis of European and Middle Eastern sites: base camps identified with two of Bordes' five Mousterian groups; assemblages dominated by **denticulates and side scrapers** interpreted as work-camp activities (attributed via S&G 1993).

### Reduction and raw-material alternatives

S&G synthesize Harold **Dibble** (scraper types as continuous utilization/resharpening: straight single-sided → convex → transverse as retouch intensifies) and Michael **Barton** (Spanish MP: two basic edge classes — expedient notches/denticulates vs points/scrapers varying with use intensity). They reject a simple "mental template" blueprint; reduction sequences were dynamic and tied to survival decisions (attributed, 1993).

**Dibble & Rolland** remodel Bordes's five SW French assemblage types as driven more by **raw-material distance** than five cultural traditions: near sources → **expedient** technology (less finished tools, made and discarded when needed); distant sources → **curated** technology (more retouched/recycled implements reflecting transport cost). Kuhn's Italian Pontinian work (Guattari vs Sant'Agostino) further complicates pure distance models: retouch frequency can track **activity types** more than flint-source distance (via S&G 1993).

### Explicit rejection of Bordes's five tribes

S&G "finally dispense with a model that long dominated the interpretation of Neanderthals: Francois Bordes' theory of the five distinct, sedentary Neanderthal tribes" lasting millennia at very low densities, each using one cave at a time with little inter-group contact ("men living beyond the river who make hand axes"). Sitting in one spot exploiting only the immediate countryside would be a route to extinction; temperate/glacial survival depends on **mobility**. They invoke Binford's comparison of modern forager territories (e.g. Inuit family annual travel) versus the small Dordogne MP site cluster (attributed, S&G 1993).

### Landscape and planning depth

S&G also channel Binford on **planning depth** as key to different archaeological records of Ancients vs Moderns, and Simek's reading of Grotte Vaufrey VIII via Binford: MP foragers frequently moved residential base camps, whereas UP peoples operated from more permanent home bases (attributed via S&G 1993). This extends the debate from assemblage facies alone into settlement systems — while S&G's own Ancient/Modern contrast (social networks, style, colonization) goes beyond Binford's functional Mousterian model.

### Châtelperronian side-note

On transitional industries, S&G report Bordes holding that [[chatelperronian|Châtelperronian]] points and style derived from late **Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition B**, against intrusion readings based on prepared blade cores — a related Bordes culture-historical commitment at the MP–UP boundary (attributed, S&G 1993).

## Stakes

Whether Paleolithic systematics can be read as culture history of “peoples”; whether ethnoarchaeological land-use complexity refutes ethnic facies models; limits of typology without [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range]] bridging; Americanist culture concept vs French accommodation of the record.

## Resolution History

`partially-resolved`: functional, landscape, and site-structure perspectives are mainstream; facies, identity, and social-signaling readings remain live. No single adjudication; ethnoarchaeology did not close the case. As of Stringer & Gamble 1993, the five-sedentary-tribes version of Bordes is treated as discarded for Neanderthal landscape interpretation, without ending all debate over what assemblage structure means.

## Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein's synthesis partly recasts the dispute as a **chronological** rather than purely
ethnic-vs-functional question. He reports that revised TL and ESR dating plus climatic
correlation of the Combe-Grenal and Le Moustier sequences support a **temporal succession**
(Ferrassie → Quina → Mousterian of Acheulean Tradition), undermining Bordes's claim that the
facies had no chronological order and represented coexisting "tribes" — implying much French
Mousterian variability "reflects temporal change within a single tradition rather than the
coexistence of several separate traditions." He notes that use-wear (microwear) analysis found
**no strong correspondence between Bordes's morphological tool types and function** (most tools,
regardless of type, were used on wood), which cuts against a simple type=activity reading too.
Klein therefore treats the Binford activity-variant model as **plausible but not conclusively
demonstrated** — "the strongest argument for the activity variant model remains its
plausibility." In the Levant, typological change (Tabun types D→C→B) is more clearly time-linked
than in Europe. For Klein the deeper point is that all Mousterian/Middle Stone Age variability is
minor next to the gulf separating it from the [[upper-paleolithic-revolution|Upper Paleolithic]] —
which he reads as a cognitive threshold (see [[mousterian]], [[levallois]],
[[behavioral-modernity]]).

## Gamble — *The Palaeolithic Societies of Europe* (1999)

*(Attributed — [[gamble-clive]], [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]]; **as of Gamble 1999**. No adjudication.)*

### Framing: tribes as anti-social-explanation episode

Gamble (Ch.1) frames the Binfords’ critique of Bordes’s **five Mousterian tribes** of southwest France (defined by different lithic assemblages) as a deliberate move **away from culture history and social explanation** toward middle-range/taphonomic clarity about the record. His own project seeks to restore **social** analysis of Palaeolithic life without returning to ethnic facies-as-peoples (position).

### Synthesis of the expanded debate

The original Bordes/Binford debate is now (as of his 1999 synthesis, following Mellars 1996) extended to settlement systems, mobility, raw material, *chaîne opératoire*, cultural transmission/cognition ([[mithen-steven|Mithen]]), and chronology. Bordes’s view that five Neanderthal tribes made five different lithic assemblages is presented as the culture-historical pole; for **Quina**, Turq concludes it is **not a culture** in Bordes’s sense but a combination of time-economizing procedures, good **local** raw material, and specialized activities. Bordes’s *évolution buisonnante* still describes overall variability (attributed).

### Quina, transfers, and reduction models

- **Quina** (mostly Pleniglacial OIS 4): scraper-dominated; Levallois index <10%; provisioning territories 5–10 km; artifacts not landscape-transported unlike Levallois assemblages; contrasts with MTA (OIS 3) territories ~15–20 km (Turq via Gamble).
- Turq’s local Quina raw materials **contradict** Rolland & Dibble’s model that Quina = distant materials / intensive retouch of imports; once distances are scaled, that model’s explanatory power declines (Gamble position).
- Reuse/resharpening evidence does not measure curation intensity alone but mobility/land-use pattern (Binford forager/collector + Kuhn via Gamble); high Quina retouch on local high-grade stone undermines Dibble’s import-conservation story for transverse scrapers (attributed).
- **SW succession** (Mellars; Rolland; Turq): Quina always above Ferrassie; MTA A/B always latest when present (Combe Grenal) — chronological patterning that makes climate–resource–assemblage association more plausible and undercuts pure parallel-phyla coexistence without time order (attributed; aligns with Klein’s chronological recasting above without equating the authors).

### Social reading without ethnic tribes

Gamble relocates Mousterian meaning from “which people?” to **network scale**: intimate/effective networks and landscape of habit; Levallois as mobile flexible elements; agency and gesture in *chaîne opératoire* (Boëda vs static Bordes classification). See [[mousterian-industry]], [[neanderthals]]. Functional, reduction, chronological, and network positions remain live side by side — this page records, does not pick a winner.
