# Lukács and the Dialectics of Nature Debate

A standing dispute over whether Marxist dialectic applies in the same sense to **nature** as to **history**, crystallised by [[lukacs-georg|Lukács]]’s *[[lukacs-history-and-class-consciousness-1923|History and Class Consciousness]]* (1923) and the Comintern-era attacks that followed. Closely entangled with charges of idealism, the status of Engels, and the theory of [[reification]] (which critics largely ignored while fighting over nature).

## Positions

### 1. Lukács 1923 (strongest form)

In “What is Orthodox Marxism?” Lukács argues that Engels’s influential *Anti-Dühring* never adequately treats the interaction central to **revolutionary** dialectic: the dialectical relation between **subject and object** in the historical process. Without that interaction, dialectics ceases to be revolutionary: the object remains untouched, thought remains contemplative, and the central problem — **to change reality** — drops out. Related notes and the reification essay distinguish knowledge of nature from historical knowledge in which theory and practice become dialectical with reference to each other. The proletariat as potential identical subject–object of history is the socio-historical resolution of bourgeois thought’s antinomies — a thesis **internal** to the social dialectic.

### 2. Engelsian / Comintern orthodoxy (Rudas, Deborin, et al.)

Dialectical materialism is a general ontology: the same dialectic governs nature, history, and thought (Engels: science of general laws of motion in external world and in thought). Separating historical dialectic or restricting Marxism to social philosophy is **idealism**, severs proletarian science from natural science, and opens the door to voluntarism. Deborin’s 1924 attack in *Pod Znamenem Marxisma* and Rudas’s critiques exemplify this line (as reconstructed in the HCC apparatus / Löwy).

### 3. Löwy 2023 (historiographical reframing)

Michael Löwy’s centenary preface argues that discussion of *HCC* was side-tracked into a “byzantine, metaphysical dispute over the dialectics of nature.” Lukács “did not deny” nature’s dialectic; he distinguished “merely objective dialectics of nature” from historical dialectic including the subject. The book’s heart is “proletarian revolution against capitalist [[reification]].” Notably, reification theory is **absent** from Rudas/Deborin’s agenda and from Lukács’s own *Tailism and the Dialectic* reply — evidence that the nature fight displaced the book’s distinctive contribution.

### 4. Lukács 1967 self-criticism

Authorising republication, Lukács retracts the book’s tendency to treat Marxism exclusively as theory of society and to claim “nature is a societal category” — a strike “at the very roots of Marxian ontology.” He also revises his polemic against Engels on experiment and industry (wrong to call experiment pure contemplation; wrong to deny industry is praxis). He continues to criticise aspects of reflection theory debates but no longer defends the 1923 package as is. Earlier 1957 letter had already listed oscillation between materialist reflection and Hegelian subject–object identity (implying negation of dialectics in nature) among reasons to forbid republication.

## Shared factual ground

- *HCC* published 1923 (Malik-Verlag); English Merlin 1971 / Verso reprints with 1967 preface.
- Immediate critiques from social-democratic (Kautsky) and communist (Zinoviev; Rudas; Deborin 1924) quarters.
- *Tailism and the Dialectic* (*Chvostismus und Dialektik*, c. 1925) discovered in former Lenin Institute archives; defends *HCC* against Rudas/Deborin; unpublished in its time.
- French 1960 republication proceeded against Lukács’s stated wishes; German 1967 authorised with critical preface.
- The nature issue is **one** front among several (also: imputed consciousness, party, totality vs economics).
