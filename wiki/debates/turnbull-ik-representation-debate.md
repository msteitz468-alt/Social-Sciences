# Turnbull, the Ik, and the ethics of representation

A standing ethical, methodological, and empirical dispute over [[turnbull-colin-m|Colin Turnbull]]'s *[[turnbull-mountain-people-1972|The Mountain People]]* (1972) — his ethnography of the [[ik|Ik]] of northern Uganda under mid-1960s famine — and over what popular anthropology may claim about a living people under extreme stress. Related framing: [[crisis-of-representation]]; analogous case structure: [[chagnon-tierney-yanomami-ethics-debate]].

## Positions

### Position A — Turnbull and sympathetic readers

*(Strongest form.)* Turnbull argued he had watched a once-viable hunting society collapse after settlement, exclusion from **Kidepo National Park** hunting grounds, drought, and starvation. Family, love, ritual, and law, he claimed, had become luxuries the Ik could no longer afford; individuals survived by discarding the weak. The book is offered as clinical honesty under horrible conditions and as a general lesson: "our much vaunted human values are not inherent in humanity at all, but are associated only with a particular form of survival called society." His relocation advice (military round-up; remote mountains; **random units of about ten**, kinship immaterial; hope isolation continues until ~2,000 die out) is framed, on this reading, as desperate remedy for a people "beyond saving as a society" — reintroducing them as individuals into still-social host communities. He insisted more charitable readings neither lived the facts nor faced them hungry.

### Position B — Empirical and methodological critics

*(Strongest form.)* Turnbull overstated lovelessness and undercounted residual sharing, ritual, political structure, and art (his own pages document *nyot* bonds, di verdicts, Lolim's priesthood, cattle-camp institutions, cave gourds, and exceptional carers such as Kauar and Nangoli's family). Third-choice entry, low enthusiasm, personal suffering, and "contamination" shaped perception; named cases were selected for horror; the mid-1960s famine was misread as the essence of Ik society. On this view the book fails as ethnography of a people even if some famine behaviours occurred. Administrative readers who called his report "extreme" and invoked African family devotion exemplify an early pushback (as he himself records).

### Position C — Ethics-of-representation critics

*(Strongest form.)* The book packaged a living minority for a metropolitan audience as a people without love, compared conditions to **concentration camps** and named **Treblinka**, used **Belsen/Buchenwald** similes for the bodies of the starving, and recommended measures that would end the Ik as a people and language community. That combination is an ethical failure of representation whether or not particular field incidents happened — a landmark case for post-[[writing-culture|Writing Culture]] and Indigenous-rights critiques of ethnographic authority. The leap from local famine to a prophecy about Western technological society multiplies the harm by universalising a contested portrait.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Turnbull (with Joseph Towles) conducted fieldwork among the Ik in northern Uganda between **1964 and 1967**; *The Mountain People* was published in **1972**.
- The region experienced severe **drought and food shortage** in the mid-1960s; **Kidepo** had been constituted as a protected area affecting former hunting access.
- Turnbull reported to government and proposed **radical relocation/dispersal**; officials, as he records, judged the advice extreme and unlikely to be adopted; he believed a Napore move was attempted and that people walked back.
- The book was widely read outside anthropology and provoked strong professional reaction.
- Turnbull's text itself records **exceptions and residual institutions** that any fair reading of the dispute must include.

## Load-bearing passages (attributed, from the 1972 text)

On love as luxury: Turnbull concludes that if love is absent among the Ik as he defines it, then "for all humanity love is not a necessity at all, but a luxury, or an illusion."

On camps: conditions "in some ways comparable" to WWII concentration camps; "it was rare for man to shed so much of his 'humanity,' though it happened, at Treblinka and elsewhere."

On dispersal: "men, women and children could be rounded up at random and should be dispersed… in small units of about ten. Age, sex or kinship was immaterial."

On extinction hope: "I am hopeful that their isolation will remain as complete as in the past, until they die out completely."

On human nature: "The Ik teach us that our much vaunted human values are not inherent in humanity at all, but are associated only with a particular form of survival called society."
