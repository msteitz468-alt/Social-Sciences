# The Toltec–Chich'en Itza Connection Debate

Architectural and iconographic similarities between Toltec [[sites/tula]] and the Maya
site of [[sites/chichen-itza]] have been debated for over a century, hampered by weak
independent chronology at Chich'en Itza despite decades of Carnegie Institution and
Mexican government research (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Shared Factual Ground

Chich'en Itza has two overlapping architectural components: a native Maya "Puuc" style
(dated to the end of the Epiclassic, concentrated in the site's southern part) and a
component closely linked to Tula. Richard Diehl is quoted by Coe & Koontz: "despite the
1,100 km that separate them, these two communities were more alike than any other two
archaeological sites in Pre-Columbian America." C. Kristan-Graham catalogs the shared
traits: feathered serpent columns, atlantid figures, chacmools, cipactli (crocodile
day-sign) glyphs, processional-relief banquettes, colonnaded halls, similarly attired
profile figures, and feathered-serpent/composite-creature reliefs; both sites also place
a similarly shaped tzompantli east of their main ball court, and both use a shared,
little-studied non-Maya glyphic system for personal names. All positions below accept
this factual base; they disagree on the mechanism that produced it.

## Position: Toltec Invasion/Conquest (Charnay 1885; qualified)

Désiré Charnay first proposed a Toltec military conquest of Yucatan in 1885, based on
the architectural and sculptural similarities noted above. Coe & Koontz argue it is more
parsimonious (invoking Occam's razor) that Feathered Serpent imagery and other non-Maya
traits (tzompantli, colonnaded hall) were appropriated directly from Toltec Tula, rather
than transmitted from Chich'en Itza back to Tula "as some scholars have asserted" —
noting the geography and Tula's ties to northwest Mexico favor a Tula-to-Maya direction.
They consider it "unlikely" that Tula was conquered by Chich'en Itza rather than the
reverse. However, they note the scarcity of Tula-specific ceramic types at Chich'en Itza
and the cohabitation of Puuc and Toltec traits neither rule out nor clearly support an
actual military invasion.

## Position: Putún-Maya Intermediary (J. Eric S. Thompson)

Thompson proposed that the non-Maya traits reached Chich'en Itza not directly from Tula
but via the "highly Mexicanized" Putún Maya of the Gulf Coast, themselves presumed
influenced by the Toltecs at an unknown point. Coe & Koontz describe the hypothesis as
untestable given the poor archaeological definition of "Putún" (sometimes conflated
with the Olmeca-Xicallanca).

## Position: Shared Political-Economic System / "Zuyuá" (López Austin & López Luján)

Alfredo López Austin and Leonardo López Luján argue that Toltec traits at both cities
reflect a shared political-economic system cemented by a shared esoteric
language/tradition ("Zuyuá," still recited in Colonial-period Yucatan) rather than
conquest — a system that allowed both cities to project power over trade routes
previously controlled by local elites, without direct military subjugation.

## Position: Shared Quetzalcoatl Cult Network (Ringle & Bey)

William Ringle and George Bey define archaeological evidence for a shared
Quetzalcoatl-based cult uniting Tula and Chich'en Itza in which neither city originated
the relevant symbolism or practices — both cities instead derived their imagery from an
underlying network of Epiclassic-period cities that Tula and Chich'en Itza subsequently
supplanted.

## Current Standing

Coe & Koontz summarize that explanatory models have shifted generally from
invasion/influence toward appropriation/trade, but state firmly that resolution awaits
better chronological control and further excavation at both Tula and Chich'en Itza. No
position has been adjudicated on this page; each is recorded in its strongest form.
