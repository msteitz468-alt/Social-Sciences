# The Authorship and Reconstruction of *Mind, Self, and Society*

Record of the source-reliability problem attaching to the most-cited text in American social psychology. **This page does not adjudicate substance** — it documents how the 1934 book was made and what follows for reading it. The evidentiary basis is Daniel Huebner's source-critical appendix to the 2015 Definitive Edition, itself part of the ingested source.

## Shared factual ground

[[mead-george-herbert|George Herbert Mead]] died in 1931 having published no monograph. *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]* (1934) was compiled by **Charles W. Morris** after Mead's death. On Huebner's documentation, the following are established facts, not interpretations:

- The book's core is a stenographer's transcript of Mead's **winter 1928** "Advanced Social Psychology" course (produced by Walter Theodore Lillie), plus undergraduate **Robert Page's** rewritten notes of the **1930** offering, supplemented by other students' notes (Shelburg, Queen, Osborne, Bloom, Sutherland), Morris's own 1924–25 course notes, and Mead manuscript fragments — materials spanning **1912–1931**.
- Morris originally mislabeled the transcripts as **1927**; scholarship since corrects them to **1928** (fixed in the Definitive Edition).
- Morris **edited pervasively**: correcting grammar, removing repetitions and indexicals ("I said yesterday"), shifting grammatical person, and **silently interleaving different course-years within single chapters**, sometimes reordering content out of lecture sequence.
- Morris **composed original sentences with no source**, some written "as if... in Mead's voice." The signature line **"Our behaviorism is a social behaviorism"** appears nowhere in the transcripts and was added by Morris; "social behaviorism" is throughout an editorial term Mead did not use.
- Distinctive doctrine is traceable to specific note-sets: the **triadic** account of meaning and the first appearance of the **"generalized other"** come from **Page's 1930 notes**; the heavy use of the word "social" is a feature of Page's rewriting (his sixth most common word, "more common even than 'is'").
- 46 recorded student Q&A exchanges are "more often than not" omitted; source usage was uneven (one Feb. 29, 1928 transcript feeds four chapters; the March 1 transcript is nearly discarded); some note-sets (Osborne's) are lost.
- Morris's introduction wrongly denied Mead studied "at Leipzig with Wundt"; **Joas (1985)** showed Mead did study with Wundt at Leipzig in winter 1888–89.

## The positions

**Position A — traditional reading.** For most of the book's history it was read straightforwardly as Mead's system: the four-part architecture (Mind / Self / Society), the chapter arguments, and the quotable formulations were cited as "Mead." This reading underwrote the canonization of the text by [[symbolic-interactionism|symbolic interactionism]] and its entry into classical sociological theory.

**Position B — source-critical reading (Huebner, Joas).** The book is "the editorial work done by Charles Morris," and should be used with the appendix's tools "for the reevaluation and conscientious use of *Mind, Self, and Society*." The four-part structure and chapter divisions are Morris's; some load-bearing terms and sentences are editorial or student-specific; quotation should distinguish Mead's own published papers and manuscripts (the securer witnesses) from the reconstructed lecture text. Huebner further judges some interventions to have introduced conceptual **ambiguity** (e.g., "universe of discourse" vs. "universal discourse") rather than merely tidying prose.

## Curator's note (2026-07-08)

The two positions are not symmetric claims about the same object: Position B is a documented account of the text's construction, and it is not seriously contested that Morris shaped the book heavily. What remains genuinely open is *interpretive* — how much the reconstruction **distorts** Mead versus faithfully systematizing a thinker who "was always engaged in building" a system he never wrote down (Morris's own phrase). This wiki therefore treats all quotation from the main text of *Mind, Self, and Society* as **reconstructed lecture material** in attributed voice, flags editorially-originated terms (notably "social behaviorism"), and prefers Mead's own papers and the Supplementary Essays where authorial fidelity matters. `resolution_status: partially-resolved` — the provenance facts are settled; the interpretive weight of the mediation is not.
