# Unilinear Cultural Evolution vs. Historical Particularism

The foundational theoretical dispute of anthropology: whether human cultures develop through a single universal sequence of stages (savagery → barbarism → civilization) driven by the [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|uniform human mind]], or whether each culture is a particular historical product to be explained by its own diffusion, contact, and local history. Positions are recorded here in their strongest form; the wiki does not adjudicate.

## Position 1 — Unilinear cultural evolutionism

Held by [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]], [[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]], [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]], and [[frazer-james-george|Frazer]]. Culture everywhere ascends one developmental ladder; because minds are uniform ([[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]), similar conditions independently produce similar forms, and present-day "lower" societies approximate the earlier stages of "higher" ones. The [[comparative-method]] arrays customs across societies to reconstruct the universal sequence, and [[survivals]] evidence vanished stages. Tylor's *[[tylor-primitive-culture-1871|Primitive Culture]]* and Morgan's *Ancient Society* are the canonical statements for culture stages; Frazer's *[[frazer-golden-bough-1922|Golden Bough]]* is the canonical statement for the **intellectualist** branch — magic → religion → science as successive "theories of thought," plus [[sympathetic-magic]], [[divine-king]], and [[dying-and-rising-god]] as comparative types (see [[intellectualist-theory-of-magic-and-religion]], [[frazer-golden-bough-study]]). See [[social-evolutionism]].

## Position 2 — Boasian historical particularism

[[boas-franz|Franz Boas]] ("The Limitations of the Comparative Method," 1896; *[[boas-mind-of-primitive-man-1938|The Mind of Primitive Man]]*, 1911/1938) argued the comparative method's key inference is invalid: **similar cultural traits do not imply parallel independent development**. The same form can arise from diffusion, borrowing, or different historical paths; identical outward traits may have wholly different meanings and histories; categories like totemism or soul are often incomparable across cases. Cultures must be reconstructed from their own historical and geographical context (inner growth + neighbors), not slotted into a universal ladder. *Mind* Ch. 10 dismantles unilinear sequences for agriculture/herding and for maternal→paternal family with distributional evidence; Ch. 9 defines culture as structured totality rather than a grade of Civilization. Boas retained [[psychic-unity-of-mankind|psychic unity]] but turned it *against* evolutionism: shared mental processes do not entail shared stage sequences. Program page: [[historical-particularism]] · [[hubs/theory/historical-particularism]]. This position discredited unilinear evolutionism as the discipline's mainstream by the 1920s.

**Boas vs. Tylor on the origin of religious concepts ("The Idea of the Future Life among Primitive Tribes," 1922, in *[[boas-race-language-culture-1940|Race, Language and Culture]]*, pp. 596–607).** Boas directly critiques [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s *Primitive Culture* treatment of soul and immortality concepts as products of "speculative thought, of reasoning that leads to a consistent view of the world," unfolding in a fixed evolutionary sequence "regardless of race and historical affiliations." Against this rationalist, intellectualist reading, Boas states: "We are, at present, more inclined to consider the growth of ideas, not as a result of rational processes, but rather as an involuntary growth, and their interpretation as the outcome of rationalization." His own positive account derives "soul" concepts from two convergent psychological sources — the "life-soul" (an objectification of what distinguishes a living from a dead body) and the "memory-image soul" (the persisting mental image of the dead in the minds of the living) — illustrated across Chinook, Eskimo, and Bantu Vandau material. Boas also cautions against a purely psychological reading where diffusion is demonstrably at work, citing circumscribed African sacred-grove ancestral-soul beliefs as historically diffused rather than independently invented — a Galton's-problem-adjacent methodological point. This essay is a clean instance of the founding-era-vs.-Boasian rupture: Boas rejects Tylor's unilinear intellectualist account in favor of an unconscious-psychological-plus-historical (diffusionist) one, reacted_against: [[tylor-edward-burnett]] / [[social-evolutionism]].

**Lowie’s social-organization application:** *[[lowie-primitive-society-1920|Primitive Society]]* (1920) is the major American working-out of particularism for kinship, associations, property, rank, government, and justice — “mottled diversity,” family-prior-to-sib, Schurtz-recovered associations without Schurtz’s ladder, diffusion as axe to historical laws, and the “mock-law of social evolution” / [[shreds-and-patches]] close (Sapir: present temper of American anthropology). Study: [[lowie-primitive-society-study]].

## Position 3 — Functionalist rejection of conjectural history

Malinowski and Radcliffe-Brown rejected the whole enterprise of reconstructing origins and stages as unverifiable "conjectural history," and attacked [[survivals]] specifically: a persisting custom has a **present function**, so explaining it as a vestige of an earlier stage explains nothing about why it endures now. Frazer's *Golden Bough* was a principal target: Malinowski (Trobriand fieldwork) argued magic manages anxiety where technique fails, not that it is failed physics; the armchair catalogue of "parallels" was treated as methodologically indefensible once intensive fieldwork became the discipline's signature.

**Stocking's earlier internal crisis, before Boas (attributed — [[stocking-after-tylor-1995|Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995]]).** Stocking argues the paradigmatic unity of classical evolutionism was "from the beginning somewhat problematic," fracturing from within well before the Boasian external critique reached Britain — offering the careers of [[fison-lorimer|Fison]], [[howitt-a-w|Howitt]], and [[codrington-robert-henry|Codrington]] as case studies. Fison, whose *Kamilaroi and Kurnai* (1880) had supplied central evidence for Morgan's evolutionary family sequence, later explicitly repudiated his own signature Kurnai theory ("it accounts for all the facts, but it is wrong in spite of that") and grew broadly skeptical of theoretical speculation. Codrington, though a nominal correspondent within the evolutionist network, worked from an underlying philological "ethnological" orientation and used his [[mana]] data to rebut [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s own [[animism]] theory. See [[social-evolutionism]] for the fuller account, including Tylor's pivotal 1888 "adhesions" paper and the Galton's-problem episode ([[comparative-method]]).

**Stocking's complication of Position 3.** *(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1987.)* Stocking documents that functionalism's own founders remained more indebted to evolutionary assumption than their "revolution" rhetoric suggested. [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] himself said he had "all his life accepted the hypothesis of social evolution as formulated by Spencer as a useful working hypothesis in the study of human society," and modeled his program for a "Natural Science of Society" directly on Tylor's earlier ambition for a science of culture. [[rivers-w-h-r|W. H. R. Rivers]]'s celebrated 1911 "conversion" from evolutionism to diffusionism — a widely-noted defection by a leading kinship evolutionist (see [[social-evolutionism]], [[evolutionism-diffusionism-debate]]) — likewise retained core evolutionary features on Stocking's reading: it stayed staunchly determinist, kept a diachronic orientation, and reconstructed history just as "conjecturally" as the evolutionists it displaced. Stocking uses this continuity to argue the evolutionist/diffusionist/functionalist sequence was less a series of clean epistemic ruptures than the discipline's own origin myths suggest.

## Position 4 — Neo-evolutionism (multilinear / universal)

Leslie White and [[steward-julian-h|Julian Steward]] (mid-20th c.) revived evolutionary explanation while abandoning Victorian unilinearity. Two sub-positions must be kept distinct:

**4a. White — [[universal-evolution]] / [[culturology]].** *[[white-evolution-of-culture-1959|The Evolution of Culture]]* (1959) is White's book-length statement. Culture is an extrasomatic continuum dependent on [[symboling]]; it advances as energy harnessed per capita per year increases and/or as tool efficiency increases ([[energy-law-of-cultural-evolution]]); human-energy "primitive" systems give way after the [[agricultural-revolution|Agricultural Revolution]] to civil society organized by property and the [[state-church]]. White **rejects the label "neoevolutionism"**: he holds the *principle* continuous with Tylor 1881. He also holds that no particular society must recapitulate every stage (diffusion shortcuts; Bantu Stone→Iron without Bronze does not kill culture-as-a-whole sequences). Carneiro and Brown (2007 foreword) treat White as primary resuscitator of evolutionary anthropology against Boasian "barefoot empiricism" and Laufer's denunciation of cultural evolution. Deep analysis: [[hubs/theory/universal-evolution]] · [[hubs/thinkers/white-leslie-a]].

**4b. Steward — [[multilinear-evolution]] / [[cultural-ecology]].** Steward's *[[steward-theory-of-culture-change-1955|Theory of Culture Change]]* (1955) formulated multilinear evolution as a distinct third path: not a single ladder for all societies, and not only White/Childe-style universal laws that explain that culture changes without explaining particular forms. Multilinear evolution, Steward held, is an **empirical methodology** for limited parallels of form, function, and sequence under similar conditions — paired with **[[cultural-ecology]]** (creative adaptation via technology–environment–behavior and the [[cultural-core]]) and a taxonomy of [[culture-type|culture types]] at comparable [[levels-of-sociocultural-integration]]. He argued Boasian [[culture-area]] particularism and relativism underplay independent functional regularities; unilinear schemes overclaim universality. Program pages: [[cultural-ecology]] · [[multilinear-evolution]] · [[hubs/theory/cultural-ecology]].

**4c. Sahlins & Service — *[[sahlins-service-evolution-culture-1960|Evolution and Culture]]* (1960) synthesis (primary).** The Michigan neo-evolutionary charter volume (White Foreword; joint Introduction; essays by Sahlins, Thomas G. Harding, David Kaplan, Service) proposes that mid-century confusion over unilinear / multilinear / universal and history vs evolution is resolved by recognizing **two aspects of one process** ([[general-and-specific-evolution]]):

- **Specific evolution** — adaptive diversification “along many lines”; phylogenetic taxonomy; relative advance (Stewardian multilinear ecology as primary affinity).
- **General evolution** — successive higher levels of all-round development; stage taxonomy without phylogeny; absolute criteria (energy, organization, dominance range) (Whitean universal program as primary affinity).

White’s Foreword equates specific≈multilinear and general≈unilinear and insists both are fully evolution. The volume disavows the label “neoevolutionism” (plain old evolutionism continuous with Tylor’s twofold view). Linked principles: Harding on adaptation as orienting process of *specific* evolution (creative + conservative; natural + superorganic environments); Kaplan’s [[law-of-cultural-dominance]] (energy return per unit labor; specific vs general dominance); Service’s [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]] (specific progress inversely related to general potential; phylogenetic and local discontinuity; Veblen/Trotsky). Recorded as the principal mid-century *synthesis* position within Position 4, not a third school.

## Johnson (2011) on directionality and the postprocessual critique (attributed)

*(Johnson 2011, Ch.9 — see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)* Johnson argues
there is no *a priori* reason cultural evolution must run simple-to-complex, must be
adaptive, must be gradual, or must be morally progressive: societies can move toward
simplicity, as in the Classic Maya collapse or the abandonment of Chaco Canyon.
Directionality is a contingent finding, not a logical requirement of "evolution."

Postprocessual critics — Shanks and Tilley (1987) and Pluciennik (2005), as Johnson
reports them — argue that function, adaptation, and evolution have no genuine
explanatory role for the social and should be abandoned or reduced to descriptive
vocabulary. Their charges: cultural-evolutionary schemes "flatten out" historical
particularity; ignore indigenous self-understanding; downplay contingency (the
teleology charge); ignore diffusion and contact; and erase individual agency (a
critique Johnson notes runs parallel to Habermas-style [[critical-theory|critical-theory]]
critique of systems thinking as an ideology of control — see [[ideology]]). Johnson
further notes the moral and political stakes for indigenous peoples subjected to
comparative ethnography, who have often experienced the "flattening out" of
evolutionary schemas as suppression of their traditions' particularity — implicitly
framing non-Western modes of life as outmoded relics to be superseded by "modern"
Western ways.

**Johnson's own thesis (explicitly first-person, "my response," "in my view").**
Despite overt rejection by many scholars, Johnson argues cultural-evolutionary
assumptions remain embedded implicitly in archaeological practice: in periodization
terminology (archaic/formative/classic/post-classic; Stone/Bronze/Iron Age); in
phenomenological interpretations of prehistoric landscapes, which rely on implicit
ethnographic-comparative assumptions about social organization; and in any causal "why"
question that traces a chain of explanation back through a feudalism-to-capitalism
narrative. This is recorded here as Johnson's own authorial position within the
debate, not adjudicated as wiki-voice fact.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

All sides accept that cultures change over time and that cross-cultural regularities exist. They divide on the **explanation** of similarity: parallel independent invention up a common ladder (Position 1) versus diffusion/local history (Position 2) versus present function (Position 3) versus ecological adaptation along multiple lines (Position 4b) or thermodynamic laws of culture-as-a-whole (Position 4a).

## Current standing

The unilinear ladder and its savage/barbarian/civilized ranking are abandoned. Boas's critique of the naïve comparative method is broadly accepted. Evolutionary questions returned in multilinear, ecological, Whitean-energetic, and (later) Darwinian-cultural-evolution forms that share Tylor's interest in long-run change while rejecting his single scale and its ethnocentric hierarchy. White's master energy law and culturology brand are largely **absorbed** into other programs; Steward's multilinear/ecological toolkit left a clearer methods legacy; archaeologists became major standard-bearers of evolutionary questions (Carneiro & Brown 2007).

## Position 5 — Carneiro’s critical-history rehabilitation (2003)

*(Attributed — [[carneiro-robert-l|Carneiro]], *[[carneiro-evolutionism-cultural-anthropology-2003|Evolutionism in Cultural Anthropology]]*, 2003.)* Not a new evolutionary law-set so much as a **historiographic and theoretical counter-position** inside neo-evolutionism:

1. **Classicals misread.** The charge that Spencer, Tylor, and Morgan held that all cultures must pass identical stages is largely false; classicals recognized diffusion, stage-skipping, and differential rates. True without exception (Carneiro): a society at Stage B passed through Stage A; false: every society at Stage A must become Stage B.
2. **Spencerian vs Darwinian “evolution.”** On Sahlins’s [[general-and-specific-evolution]] distinction, Carneiro prefers Spencer: reserve “evolution” for increasing complexity; do not call rise *and* fall of Rome equally “evolution.”
3. **Uni- and multilinear complementary** (with White): political evolution shows strong unilinearity (band → village → chiefdom → state); many low-distance traits vary in order. Unilinear ≠ rectilinear.
4. **Anti-evolutionism periodized:** Boasian backlash + British diffusionism + functionalist ban on “conjectural history” produced a “barren landscape”; White, Steward, Childe, Sahlins–Service, and processual archaeology restored the program; later postmodern/post-processual currents and residual British hostility left “islands.”
5. **Determinants debate is the live front:** Service’s pluralism (“Down with prime-movers!”) vs Harris’s [[cultural-materialism]] vs Carneiro’s own ranking — **population pressure** primary for chiefdoms/states, paired with **warfare** under [[circumscription|environmental/social circumscription]]; ideology and agency secondary or co-present but not equal.
6. **Normative close:** cultural evolutionism is “too valid and fruitful… not to remain a central tenet of anthropology”; core task = band→state transformation and its causes.

Recorded as a **position**, not a wiki-voice verdict on who won the debate. See [[social-evolutionism]], [[circumscription]], [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]].
