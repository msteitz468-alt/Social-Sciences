# Doing Difference Debate

Live theoretical dispute sparked by [[west-candace|West]] and [[fenstermaker-sarah|Fenstermaker]]'s 1995 extension of [[doing-gender]] to **[[doing-difference]]** — race, class, and gender as simultaneous interactional accomplishments — and the *Gender & Society* symposium that answered them. Primary dossier: Chapters 4–5 (and Ch. 11 revisit) of [[fenstermaker-west-doing-gender-doing-difference-2002]].

No adjudication below — each position in its strongest form.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- West and Zimmerman's 1987 "Doing Gender" is a major reference point in U.S. gender sociology ([[west-zimmerman-doing-gender-study]])
- The 1995 paper deliberately generalizes the ethnomethodological apparatus beyond gender
- A formal symposium and author reply were published; the debate did not end the program's use
- Authors explicitly disclaim **equivalence of oppressions** while asserting a shared *mechanism* of categorical accomplishment (their framing)

## Position A — West & Fenstermaker (program extension + reply)

West and Fenstermaker argue that mathematical metaphors (additive/multiplicative models of oppression) and residual individual-focused gender concepts cannot capture **simultaneity**. They propose that race and class, like gender, are ongoing **situated accomplishments**: persons are held accountable to normative conceptions attached to race category and class category, and these accountabilities can operate at once (e.g., Black men and police stops; Patricia Williams's boutique buzzer; Baby M / class display; Thomas–Hill hearings).

Key program claims (attributed):

- Difference *qua* difference is not the problem; processes that produce categorical difference make systems of oppression possible
- Interaction is not apart from producing structural divisions (camera-lens metaphor in the reply)
- Accomplishments rest on history, institutions, and structure without being disembodied forces
- Resistance is as ubiquitous as doing difference; change can occur by confounding categorization and via social movements
- Modest aims: not a hierarchy of oppressions; future empirical agenda

Ch. 11 later restates misapprehensions they reject: process ≠ outcome; not Butlerian "inequality as performance"; constructionism does not erase power or confine analysis to face-to-face chat.

## Position B1 — Patricia Hill Collins

[[collins-patricia-hill|Collins]] argues race/class/gender studies emerged with social movements against racism, patriarchy, and class exploitation and needs models linking interlocking structures and [[intersectionality]] (see [[black-feminist-thought]] and the [[matrix-of-domination]]). In her strongest form: "Doing Difference" **erases** race/class/gender as systems of power, decontextualizes inclusivity language, retains an additive gender-first structure, and offers **difference without power** — a postmodern depoliticization whose outcome is "a difference that didn't make any difference."

## Position B2 — Lionel A. Maldonado

Maldonado presses that social constructionist accounts need **historical and macro constraints**. Strongest form: without specifying *whose* construction and under what material/historical limits, "construction" risks treating reality as monolithic voluntary manufacture.

## Position B3 — Dana Y. Takagi

Takagi distinguishes **metaphors** from **mechanisms**. Strongest form: simultaneity language can be after-the-fact labeling rather than an explanatory mechanism; even the mathematical language feminist theory uses may itself be part of how difference is accomplished; the field needs vision, not mere technique.

## Position B4 — Barrie Thorne

Thorne credits the "doing" imagery and **accountability as connecting mechanism**. Strongest form of critique: one may create/inhabit racial categories in interaction yet not **transform or destroy** them; the framework risks a functionalist tilt (reproduction over rupture); Butler on performance/parody and Connell on historical gender orders supply wider resources than "doing difference" alone.

## Position B5 — Lynn Weber

Weber grants strengths: simultaneity and anti-essentialism in interaction. Strongest critique: exclusive face-to-face focus **obscures power**, structure, and resistance communities; examples such as Black driver/police encounters can be read as showing **no change possible** under the frame; the authors' metaphor critique is overstated because they offer "the thing itself," not only a metaphor.

## Position B6 — Howard Winant

[[winant-howard|Winant]] reads the paper as extending ethnomethodology toward **hegemony** and contradictory identity, bursting Schutzian lifeworld bounds. Strongest critique: **structure is too thin** — analysis needs pre-existing structure in dominance / a hegemony synthesis; neither classic ethnomethodology nor conceded autonomy of actors adequately explains **oppositional difference and change**.

## Dorothy Smith (adjacent, not symposium)

In the 2002 foreword, Smith records partial agreement while remaining **unconvinced that power is fully drawn into the theory's circumference** — including Collins's issues — treating that reservation as a sign of productive dialogue rather than dismissal.

## Resolution status

**Open.** The program continues in empirical applications; intersectional and structural critics continue; Ch. 11 of the 2002 volume revisits prospects without claiming settlement. Related open agenda (authors): micro/macro as *relationship* to research; simultaneous differences and doing sexuality; lasting institutional change.
