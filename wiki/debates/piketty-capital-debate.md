# Piketty Capital Debate

Standing dispute cluster around *[[piketty-capital-twenty-first-century-2014|Capital in the Twenty-First Century]]* — empirical series, mechanisms ([[r-greater-than-g]], [[capital-income-ratio]]), and policy ([[progressive-global-wealth-tax]], [[social-state]], [[progressive-taxation]]). Multi-type (empirical + theoretical + policy); frontmatter uses closest single enum `theoretical`.

> **Voice rule:** Positions are recorded in strongest form. No wiki-voice adjudication. Piketty’s own text supplies Position A in detail; Positions B–D remain partly schematic until secondary critiques are ingested.

## Positions

### Position A — Piketty research program (from the book)

**Empirical.** Multi-century series (income tax; estate tax; national wealth accounts) show U-shaped capital/income ratios in Europe and Japan (β ~6–7 pre-1914 → 2–3 mid-century → 5–6 early twenty-first century); US more stable; global β ~500% today. Capital share α follows a shallower U-curve. Top income shares: US top decile of national income back to ~45–50% by 2000s (from 30–35% mid-century); France compression due almost entirely to fall of top capital incomes, not wage hierarchy. Wealth: Europe 1910 top 10% ~90% of wealth → ~60% today with a [[patrimonial-middle-class]]; bottom 50% still ~5%. Inheritance flow France ~20–25% of NI (nineteenth century) → ~4–5% (1950s) → ~15% (2010). Supermanager explosion mainly Anglo-Saxon since 1980.

**Mechanism.** No natural spontaneous process permanently prevents inegalitarian forces. When r significantly exceeds g, inherited wealth grows faster than output and wages—“the past tends to devour the future”; entrepreneur tends to become rentier. r > g is a historical regularity (not pure logical necessity), unrelated to market *imperfection*—more perfect capital markets make r > g more likely. Second law β = s/g: slow growth (especially demographic) plus high saving produces high capital intensity. Elasticity of substitution capital/labor over long run >1 (estimate 1.3–1.6) so rising β can raise α. Differential returns by portfolio size amplify top divergence. Twentieth-century compression was shock- and policy-driven (wars, depression, inflation, progressive tax, nationalizations), not Kuznets’s tranquil advanced-stage equality.

**Normative / policy (explicit POSITION).** Inequality not bad per se if justified by “common utility” (1789 Declaration). Democracy can regain control while preserving openness: modernize the social state; rethink progressive income tax (including confiscatory top rates on indecent labor incomes); implement progressive annual capital tax—ideally global, practically regional (Europe)—plus automatic international bank-data sharing. Capital tax purpose is primarily *regulatory* (stop indefinite wealth-share rise; financial transparency), not social-state financing. Ranked debt tools: exceptional progressive capital tax > inflation > prolonged austerity. Protectionism and capital controls are inferior substitutes. Economics as political economy / social science, not sealed mathematical science.

**Strongest form:** Without democratic regulation of capital, twenty-first-century low growth and high private wealth reopen patrimonial dynamics incompatible with meritocratic democracy; free markets alone will not equalize.

### Position B — Measurement and data critics

Challenge asset valuation (market vs book; Tobin’s Q; housing bubbles), what counts as capital (human capital exclusion; durables; pure land), national-accounts bridges, inheritance-flow construction (gifts, life insurance, μ), underreporting corrections, and some published series (including post-publication spreadsheet debates not yet ingested). Strongest form: core descriptive claims about β rebound, r levels, or top shares fail or shrink under alternative constructions so the narrative of returning patrimonial capitalism is overstated.

Piketty’s own caveats usable by this side: imperfect incomplete data; French tax returns understate capital income; WTID has not systematically applied underreporting corrections; US inheritance poorly measured; non-rich-world wealth series thin before 1990; conclusions “tenuous” and open to debate (Conclusion).

### Position C — Theory and mechanism critics

Accept parts of the description but deny that r > g is the master mechanism of inequality. Emphases may include: elasticity of substitution ≤1 so rising β lowers α; saving rates and life-cycle behavior; human capital and education–technology race as primary labor-inequality story; institutions and politics without the capital-stock law; demography alone without r; Solow/neoclassical balanced growth as already containing β = s/g without inegalitarian implication. Strongest form: inequality dynamics require a different causal model; r > g is an accounting tendency or historical coincidence, not a theory of distribution.

Internal book materials this side can cite: Piketty’s rejection of pure Cobb-Douglas stability; his admission that r > g is contingent on shocks and policy; Cambridge capital controversy history he himself narrates; Goldin–Katz skill race he partly endorses for mid-distribution wages.

### Position D — Policy critics

Even if diagnosis partly holds, a global progressive wealth tax is politically utopian, administratively unworkable, or economically harmful (capital flight, investment deterrence, valuation nightmares). Prefer national instruments, growth, education, inheritance reform only, financial regulation without wealth tax, or inflation/austerity for debt. Strongest form: the book’s Part Four is advocacy detached from feasible political economy; regional capital tax without full political union will fail.

Piketty concedes global tax is utopian and offers it as reference standard; still ranks it above protectionism and pure capitalization of pensions.

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

- Large historical swings in top income and wealth shares in several rich countries are widely recognized in the inequality literature the book synthesizes.
- Progressive income taxes and world wars temporally coincide with mid-century compression—causal weight disputed.
- Post-1980 rise in US top income shares is documented in WTID-style series; composition (labor vs capital) and causes disputed.
- Capital/income ratios in Europe are higher now than mid-twentieth century in standard national-balance-sheet work—magnitude and welfare meaning disputed.
- Full secondary critique literature (Acemoglu/Robinson, Summers, Mankiw, FT/Giles spreadsheet episode, elasticity debates, etc.) **not fully ingested**—Positions B–D above are scaffolds for later filling from those sources.
