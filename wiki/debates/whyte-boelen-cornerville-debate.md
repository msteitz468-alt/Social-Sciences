# Whyte vs. Boelen: the Cornerville restudy controversy

A dispute (surfacing publicly in 1992, ~50 years after publication) over the
reliability and ethics of [[whyte-street-corner-society-study|*Street Corner
Society*]]. It is filed as `source-reliability` — the core question is how the
account was *produced* (language competence, informant verification, theoretical
framing), not a competing reading of shared evidence — though it carries ethical
and epistemological dimensions. See the study's [[hubs/studies/whyte-street-corner-society-study|Studies Hub]]
analysis for the fuller afterlife.

## Occasion

Two 1991–92 publications made [[whyte-street-corner-society-1943|SCS]] "a new
focus of scholarly attention" (Whyte, 4th ed. appendix): *Reframing
Organizational Culture* (Frost et al., 1991), which treated SCS as an "exemplar
of organizational culture research," and the **April 1992 issue of the
*Journal of Contemporary Ethnography* (JCE), entirely devoted to SCS**. The JCE
issue paired W. A. Marianne Boelen's critique with Whyte's rejoinder, an essay
by [[cornerville|Cornerville]] insider Angelo Ralph Orlandella ("Sam Franco"),
and postfoundational commentaries by Arthur Vidich, Laurel Richardson, and
Norman K. Denzin.

## Positions (each in its strongest form)

**Boelen — the account is unreliable and unethical.** Boelen revisited
Cornerville and interviewed surviving figures decades after the fieldwork. Per
Whyte's summary of her charges (his characterization, attributed): she held that
(1) Whyte "did not recognize that the custom of young men hanging on street
corners was brought over from Italy"; (2) "the common language among corner
gangs was Italian, and that [Whyte's] comprehension of that language was
deficient so that [he] did not fully understand what was going on"; (3) Whyte
"distorted [his] interpretations in order to bring them in line with the Chicago
School of Sociology"; and (4) he committed an "ethical cardinal sin by not
taking his manuscript back to the field and checking the data and contents with
the subjects." She reported that Doc's sons believed Whyte had exploited Doc and
should have shared royalties.

**Whyte — contemporaneous fieldwork outranks late recollection.** Whyte's
rejoinder (attributed): his "three and a half years of field work, backed up by
voluminous notes typed shortly after the events or interviews, would be a more
accurate guide to the realities of Cornerville in the late 1930s than the
recollections of selected informants thirty to forty-five years later." On the
Chicago charge, he noted he "had completed a first draft of SCS before [he]
entered graduate work at the University of Chicago," thought of himself during
the 1936–40 fieldwork as "a student of social anthropology" reading under
[[whyte-william-foote|Conrad Arensberg]]'s guidance, had "not read any of the
University of Chicago urban studies," and at his thesis examination "had to
resist the efforts of Louis Wirth and Herbert Blumer to put SCS into the social
dis-organizational framework." On ethics: he "had never heard of such an
obligation" as member-checking, knew of "no code of professional ethics" then
requiring it, and asked how one would feed findings back "with a community of
twenty thousand people." He noted Doc had read the manuscript and discussed
criticisms, and that revealing the measured status ranking to the Nortons "would
have embarrassed Doc and upset his followers." On exploitation, he acknowledged
"I gained more from our relationship than he did," but held that royalties were
negligible until after 1955, that he had been guided by "the principle of
interpersonal reciprocity," and that Doc "still greeted me as a friend" in 1953.

**Postfoundational critics — the realist frame is the problem.** Richardson and
Denzin declined to adjudicate factual accuracy because, in Richardson's words
(quoted by Whyte), the work is now read "in a radically different context…
'postfoundational'… doubt that any discourse has a privileged place, any text an
authoritative 'corner' on the truth." Denzin labeled Whyte a "positivist-social
realist." Riley (glossing Geertz) held that "cultural descriptions, filtered
through the ethnographer, are actually second or third order fictions." Whyte
answered that he had "not yet given up" the commitment to a science of society,
while conceding the objective/subjective distinction "is not as clear as I once
thought" — selection of what to observe "depends upon some implicit or explicit
theory… in large part subjective," but specifying assumptions and methods lets
others "verify or challenge our conclusions."

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides agree that Whyte was a non-Italian outsider dependent on a key
sponsor (Doc), that the published analysis was single-authored despite Doc's and
"Sam Franco"'s collaborative input, and that no return-to-subjects verification
occurred. Boelen frames these as fatal to the account's validity and ethics;
Whyte frames the sponsor relationship and contemporaneous notes as the account's
strength and treats member-checking as an anachronistic standard. Orlandella, an
actual Cornerville participant, endorsed the account's substance — a datum Boelen's
"selected informants" framing must accommodate.

## Curator's note (2026-07-08)

Not adjudicated here. The language-competence charge is the most consequential
reliability claim and is not resolvable from the ingested text alone (Whyte does
not quantify his Italian). The ethics charge is partly a periodization dispute —
member-checking and informant compensation are contemporary norms projected back
onto 1930s practice — which is itself a live methodological question, not a
settled verdict against Whyte. The postfoundational strand talks past the
reliability question rather than resolving it. This is a productive standing case
for teaching how ethnographic authority, [[participant-observation|participant-observation]]
ethics, and restudy evidence interact; it belongs open.
