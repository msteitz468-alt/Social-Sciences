# Hunting versus scavenging in early hominid subsistence

How did early *Homo* (and australopithecines) obtain animal tissue, and what does the Plio-Pleistocene bone record at sites like [[olduvai-gorge|Olduvai Gorge]] actually document? The dispute is both empirical (what agent produced the assemblages) and interpretive (what social life the pattern implies). It is a live case of the four-way non-identity problem in miniature — tools, bones, and behavior are not the same thing, and their association is not their integration.

## Position 1 — Man-the-hunter / home-base model

Associated with [[isaac-glynn|Glynn Isaac]] (the [[food-sharing-hypothesis|food-sharing hypothesis]]) and, in popular form, Richard Leakey and Roger Lewin. In its strongest form: concentrations of stone tools and heterogeneous animal bone at Olduvai are **[[home-base|home bases]]** to which hominids transported food from kills; transport beyond an individual's needs implies **food sharing**, division of labor, and a central-place social organization — the behavioral threshold distinguishing hominids from apes (see [[hunting-hypothesis]]). The model drew ethnographic warrant from Lee and DeVore's work on the Dobe [[juhoansi|!Kung]].

## Position 2 — Scavenging model

Stated in strongest form by [[binford-lewis|Binford]] in *[[binford-bones-1981|Bones]]* (1981): the anatomical-part composition at Olduvai fits hominids **scavenging marrow bones of low food utility** from carcasses already exploited by other predator-scavengers. Percussion tools dominate and cutting tools are near-absent; there is "no evidence of carrying food home," no base camps, and food sharing is unsupported. The famous sites "carry little specific information about hominid behavior" because they are ravaged palimpsests, not living floors. Binford's warrant is [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range research]]: actualistic wolf-den and [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] butchery control data, not ethnographic analogy.

## Position 3 — Mixed / early-access model

The position most of the field converged toward (attributed). Cut-mark and tooth-mark studies at FLK Zinj (Bunn; Potts and Shipman, from 1981; Bunn and Kroll 1986) found **both** stone-tool cut marks and extensive carnivore damage — a multi-agent history. The productive question became not hunting *or* scavenging but the **timing of hominid access**: early access to fleshed carcasses (implying hunting or aggressive/confrontational scavenging) versus passive scavenging of defleshed remains. Cut-mark placement, tooth-mark/cut-mark overprinting order, and skeletal-part profiles became the discriminating evidence.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

All parties accept that Olduvai assemblages are conjoint tool-and-bone accumulations, that carnivores were active agents at the sites, and that anatomical-part frequencies are non-random. They divide on whether that patterning is behaviorally hominid (Position 1), overwhelmingly carnivore-then-marrow-scavenger (Position 2), or a resolvable mix whose signal is the sequence of access (Position 3). Position 1's ethnographic analogy is, for Positions 2–3, exactly the unwarranted inference at issue.

## Curator's note (2026-07-15)

The methodological core of Binford's intervention — that assemblage formation must be established by independent actualistic method before behavior is read off association — is now consensus and reshaped the field. His specific pure-scavenging conclusion is generally regarded as a productive overstatement: it forced development of the taphonomic cut-mark methods that then supported a mixed, early-access picture. The debate is thus **partially resolved** — settled on method, open on the hunting/early-access details. See [[binford-bones-study]] for the study analyzed as a research act.
