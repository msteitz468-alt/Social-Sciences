# Can contract and self-interest found social order? (Durkheim vs. Spencer)

A foundational classical-era dispute over whether modern industrial society is held together by the free exchange of self-interested individuals or by a moral and legal order those individuals did not create. Positions are stated in their strongest form; the wiki does not adjudicate.

## Position A — Spencerian contractualism

[[spencer-herbert|Herbert Spencer]] held (attributed) that as societies pass from the **militant** to the **[[industrial-type-of-society|industrial]]** type, coercive regulation recedes and cooperation becomes spontaneous. The normal form of the industrial bond is the **free contract**; the sphere of positive social/state control shrinks toward a minimal negative role (preventing mutual interference), and the *system of particular contracts* becomes the real basis linking individuals. Spencer rejected Rousseau's social-contract-as-origin-of-society, but held that ongoing voluntary exchange, not an imposed moral order, sustains an advanced society. (See [[differentiation-and-integration]].)

## Position B — Durkheimian social realism

[[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]] countered (attributed) that a society resting only on "the spontaneous accord of individual interests" would be inherently unstable — "where interest is the only ruling force each individual finds himself in a state of war with every other," since "there is nothing less constant than interest." Contract cannot be foundational because **[[non-contractual-elements-of-contract|not everything in the contract is contractual]]**: every contract presupposes a socially and traditionally supplied body of regulation (contract law, custom, professional morality) that the parties did not author. The bond of modern society is **[[organic-solidarity]]**, a *moral* fact generated by the division of labor and indexed by *growing* **[[restitutive-law|restitutive and administrative law]]**.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both agree modern societies are increasingly differentiated and interdependent, and both use an organic/biological analogy. They divide on what the growth of law shows:

- **Spencer** predicted positive/state control would *shrink* under industrialism (evidence: an England–France comparison of state functions).
- **Durkheim** marshaled the *growth* of restitutive, domestic, and administrative law as decisive counter-evidence, and turned Spencer's own "sympathetic nervous system" analogy against him (the sympathetic system still regulates the viscera, so economic life implies a regulating social organ, not its absence). Durkheim also charged Spencer's method with anecdotalism: "some few examples thrown together in helter-skelter fashion do not constitute proof."

## Current standing

`resolution_status: open`. The dispute recurs wherever sociology confronts economics and rational-choice theory: Durkheim's "non-contractual element in contract" became a standard argument (via Parsons) for the social embedding of markets, while contractual/individualist foundations for order persist in economics and rational-choice sociology. Neither position has been retired; the debate is a live template rather than a settled question.
