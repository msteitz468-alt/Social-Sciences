# Communicative Competence Debate (Hymes–Chomsky)

A Critical-Turn dispute over **what knowledge a theory of language is about**, and whether social appropriateness is internal to linguistics or residual "performance." Primary source on the Hymes side for this wiki: *[[hymes-foundations-in-sociolinguistics-1974|Foundations in Sociolinguistics]]* (1974). Related program: [[ethnography-of-speaking]]; concept: [[communicative-competence]].

## Positions

### Position A — Linguistic competence (Chomsky and generative program)

In its strongest form: the object of linguistic theory is the **competence** of an **ideal speaker-hearer** in a **perfectly homogeneous speech community**, unaffected by memory limits, distractions, or nonlinguistic beliefs. **Performance** is the imperfect use of that competence. Explanatory adequacy seeks universal properties of the language faculty (human nature). Variation, style, and social meaning are not the core object — they belong to performance or to other disciplines.

Hymes reconstructs this position as methodologically powerful for formal grammar and historically progressive relative to corpus-bound structuralism, yet as closing off the social realities of language when elevated to the whole of linguistic theory (*Foundations* Chs. 3–4, 10).

### Position B — Communicative competence (Hymes)

In its strongest form: a theory of language adequate to human life must account for the knowledge that enables members of a [[speech-community|speech community]] to **conduct and interpret speech** — including when to speak, when not to, in what key, with what genre, toward what ends. **[[communicative-competence|Communicative competence]]** includes grammatical knowledge but is not exhausted by it. Creativity requires **appropriateness**, not only well-formed novelty. **Performance** should be reclaimed as **accomplishment** (and as the site of genre, style, and responsibility), not as residue.

Four aspects of competence Hymes lists: systemic potential, appropriateness, occurrence, feasibility. Explanatory adequacy has a **complementary** direction: from what is common to all languages toward what particular communities have made of their means of speech (sociocultural, comparative, evolutionary in a cultural sense) (*Foundations* Ch. 10).

### Adjacent positions (not reduced to A/B)

- **Socially realistic linguistics** (Hymes's middle orientation): Labovian variation and validity challenges from community data — extends/challenges formal linguistics without always recasting foundations.
- **Socially constituted linguistics** (Hymes's radical orientation): social function gives form to how linguistic features are encountered; start from social functions, reverse isolation of pure referential structure.
- **Folklore/performance studies:** performance as positive structured competence (Abrahams, Ben-Amos, Bauman et al., as Hymes cites) — allies of Position B.
- **Speech-act philosophy (Austin/Searle):** shared interest in acts; Hymes holds acts still require ethnography and are not reducible to performative deep structures.

## Shared factual ground

- Both distinguish **knowledge** from **behavior**.
- Both treat some idealized capacity as necessary for theory (neither is pure behaviorism; Hymes rejects Skinnerian functionalism of "mands").
- Both can accept that formal grammar is a real analytic object — the dispute is whether it is **the** object of a theory of language in social life.
- Historical fact: generative grammar and ethnography of speaking/sociolinguistics institutionalized partly **in parallel**, not as a single settled paradigm.

## Status

`resolution_status: open`. The fields largely **diverged**: formal linguistics retained competence/performance in a generative sense; linguistic anthropology, interactional sociolinguistics, and much applied linguistics adopted communicative competence and ethnographic units ([[speech-event]], [[speaking-model]]). Convergence appears locally (interface pragmatics, variationist formal work) without dissolving the foundational disagreement Hymes named.

## Reflexivity

Hymes frames the Chomskyan ideal speaker-hearer canon as risking **intellectual imperialism** when exported into education (homogeneous competence as standard) while presenting his own program as liberatory (égalité of speech as social achievement, not postulate). Both programs carry institutional politics of departments, funding, and "science" status — record as production context, not as adjudication.
