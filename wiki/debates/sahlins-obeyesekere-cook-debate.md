# Sahlins–Obeyesekere Debate (Captain Cook in Hawaii)

## Shared ground

Captain James Cook arrived in Hawaii in 1778–79, was received with elaborate ceremony, departed, returned unexpectedly after a mast broke, and was killed in a beach confrontation on 14 February 1779. The dispute is over how to interpret the Hawaiians' reception and killing of Cook — and, through it, over how anthropologists should represent the reasoning of culturally distant actors. No adjudication in wiki voice. Sahlins's side is now **primary-ingested** via *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985, chs. 1 and 4); the page also draws on [[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Sewell's *Logics of History*]], which discusses the case at length. Obeyesekere's *The Apotheosis of Captain Cook* (1992) and Sahlins's *How "Natives" Think* (1995) remain outstanding.

## Position 1 — Sahlins (cultural interpretation)

[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (attributed, *Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities*, 1981; *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*, 1985) argues that Hawaiians received Cook through their own cultural categories: his advent coincided with the **Makahiki** festival celebrating the annual return by sea of the fertility god **Lono**, and Cook — anchoring at Kealakekua ("The-Path-of-the-God") after a sunwise circuit of Hawaiʻi Island that shadowed the ritual right-circuit of the Lono image — was received at the Hikiau temple as the god ("O Lono"), in "the classic Frazerian mode of the dying god." His unexpected return **out of season** after a broken foremast (Feb. 1779) made him *hors cadre*, a structural anomaly and threat to King Kalaniʻōpuʻu, precipitating his death in a "ritual transformation" from venerated god to sacrificial victim. Sahlins reads the whole encounter as a **[[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]** — priests, chiefs, and the British each following their received inclinations — exemplifying his thesis that cultural categories applied to novel circumstances both reproduce and transform the cultural order (see [[hawaiians]]). *Islands of History* also mounts a subsidiary, distinct **historiographic dispute** over the identity of Cook's killer, which Sahlins argues on ritual/status grounds points to the warrior-kinsman Nuha (Kanuha) rather than a commoner.

## Position 2 — Obeyesekere (colonial myth / practical rationality)

Gananath Obeyesekere (*The Apotheosis of Captain Cook*, 1992) argues that the "Cook was a god" story is itself a **European myth** — a Western trope about how "natives" deify white newcomers — projected onto the Hawaiians. On his account Hawaiians treated Cook pragmatically, as a chief and potential ally, reasoning in ways continuous with ordinary practical rationality rather than enacting a mythic script; the deification narrative reflects imperialist mythmaking more than Hawaiian thought.

## Sahlins's reply

Sahlins (*How "Natives" Think: About Captain Cook, For Example*, 1995) responded at length, defending the ethnohistorical evidence for the Lono identification and arguing that Obeyesekere's "practical rationality" universalizes a Western commonsense while itself patronizing Hawaiian culture.
