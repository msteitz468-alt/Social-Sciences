# Sahlins–Obeyesekere Debate (Captain Cook in Hawaii)

## Shared ground

Captain James Cook arrived in Hawaii in 1778–79, was received with elaborate ceremony, departed, returned unexpectedly after a mast broke, and was killed in a beach confrontation on 14 February 1779. The dispute is over how to interpret the Hawaiians' reception and killing of Cook — and, through it, over how anthropologists should represent the reasoning of culturally distant actors. No adjudication in wiki voice. Sahlins's side is now **primary-ingested** via *[[sahlins-historical-metaphors-1981|Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities]]* (1981, the original statement) and *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]* (1985, chs. 1 and 4); the page also draws on [[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Sewell's *Logics of History*]], which discusses the case at length. Obeyesekere's *The Apotheosis of Captain Cook* (1992) and Sahlins's *How "Natives" Think* (1995) remain outstanding.

## Position 1 — Sahlins (cultural interpretation)

[[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]] (attributed, *Historical Metaphors and Mythical Realities*, 1981; *[[sahlins-islands-of-history-1985|Islands of History]]*, 1985) argues that Hawaiians received Cook through their own cultural categories: his advent coincided with the **Makahiki** festival celebrating the annual return by sea of the fertility god **Lono**, and Cook — anchoring at Kealakekua ("The-Path-of-the-God") after a sunwise circuit of Hawaiʻi Island that shadowed the ritual right-circuit of the Lono image — was received at the Hikiau temple as the god ("O Lono"), in "the classic Frazerian mode of the dying god." His unexpected return **out of season** after a broken foremast (Feb. 1779) made him *hors cadre*, a structural anomaly and threat to King Kalaniʻōpuʻu, precipitating his death in a "ritual transformation" from venerated god to sacrificial victim. Sahlins reads the whole encounter as a **[[structure-of-the-conjuncture]]** — priests, chiefs, and the British each following their received inclinations — exemplifying his thesis that cultural categories applied to novel circumstances both reproduce and transform the cultural order (see [[hawaiians]]). *Islands of History* also mounts a subsidiary, distinct **historiographic dispute** over the identity of Cook's killer, which Sahlins argues on ritual/status grounds points to the warrior-kinsman Nuha (Kanuha) rather than a commoner.

### The 1981 evidential backbone — and Sahlins's own cautions

*(From *Historical Metaphors*, 1981, attributed.)* The empirical core of Position 1 is a **calendrical correlation**: Sahlins matches the Hawaiian lunar Makahiki calendar day by day against the Julian dates of Cook's movements, claiming this is possible "with considerable assurance" from ritual practices noted by date in the voyage chronicles (while conceding uncertainty about lunar–solar intercalation). Ritual correspondences claimed: Cook's clockwise circumnavigation shadowing the Lono image's right circuit; landfall at Kealakekua, seat of the Lono priests; the *hānaipū* rite performed over Cook (posture of the cross-piece Lono image, coconut-oil anointment distinctive to Lono). The killing: "not premeditated by the Hawaiians. But neither was it an accident, structurally speaking. It was the Makahiki in an historical form." Notably, the 1981 text itself records two cautions later central to the controversy: **(a)** the "Lono"/"Orono" designation does not appear in the record of the first (Kauai, 1778) visit — King's journal ties the name to Hawaiʻi Island; and **(b)** Sahlins reports Hawaiian ethnologists' consensus that the explicit myth of Lono's annual return from Kahiki is *not* ancient but a late-18th/early-19th-century development — "not the charter of the Makahiki ceremony so much as it is the explication of Cook's advent and integration in the ceremony." He also analogizes Cook/Kalaniʻōpuʻu to Cortés/Moctezuma (Quetzalcoatl vs. Huitzilopochtli) — a comparative-mythology claim itself contested in the later literature. Post-mortem evidence he adduces: Lono priests bringing part of Cook's body to the ships asking when Lono "would come again"; the bones later carried in the Makahiki procession; the Kealakekua high priest's 1793 statement to Puget identifying Kamehameha's accompanying god by Cook's name.

## Position 2 — Obeyesekere (colonial myth / practical rationality)

Gananath Obeyesekere (*The Apotheosis of Captain Cook*, 1992) argues that the "Cook was a god" story is itself a **European myth** — a Western trope about how "natives" deify white newcomers — projected onto the Hawaiians. On his account Hawaiians treated Cook pragmatically, as a chief and potential ally, reasoning in ways continuous with ordinary practical rationality rather than enacting a mythic script; the deification narrative reflects imperialist mythmaking more than Hawaiian thought.

## Sahlins's reply

Sahlins (*How "Natives" Think: About Captain Cook, For Example*, 1995) responded at length, defending the ethnohistorical evidence for the Lono identification and arguing that Obeyesekere's "practical rationality" universalizes a Western commonsense while itself patronizing Hawaiian culture.
