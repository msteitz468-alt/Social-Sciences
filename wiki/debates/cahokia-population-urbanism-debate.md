# Cahokia: Population Scale and Urbanism

Was [[cahokia]] a city — the only pre-Columbian city north of Mexico — or a ceremonial center serving a modest, dispersed population? The dispute runs through the entire history of Cahokia archaeology and remains open. Each position stated in its strongest form; no adjudication.

## Positions

**1. Ceremonial center, sparse population (Griffin, mid-20th century).** James B. Griffin (Michigan), the most prominent Eastern Woodlands archaeologist of his era, held that Cahokia's 120 pyramids constituted "a ritual center where a few inhabitants lived for brief periods of time in insubstantial temporary housing" (as paraphrased by Fowler, via Pauketat 2009). Grounded in the absence, at that time, of excavated dense residential remains.

**2. Dense city (Wittry, Fowler, Hall, 1960s+; maximalist form: Pauketat).** Warren Wittry's 1960–61 Tract 15A/15B salvage excavations found dense Old Cahokia (900–1050) housing beneath New Cahokia construction, a bastioned compound, and the Woodhenge post circles; Melvin Fowler's mapping and Mound 72 program added planned mortuary theater. [[pauketat-timothy|Pauketat]] extends this to the strongest form: a planned "big bang" foundation ~1050 CE, 10,000–16,000 residents in New Cahokia proper plus 20,000–30,000 in a 50-mile hinterland, comparable to Mesopotamian city-states — "ancient America's one true city north of Mexico" ([[pauketat-cahokia-2009]]). Emerson's "replacement hypothesis" (abrupt ~1050 rural reorganization into farmsteads-plus-temples) and Susan Alt's evidence of large-scale immigration (Halliday-site Varney-style pottery locally made by immigrants from southeast Missouri) support the rapid-transformation reading.

**3. Dispersed low-density norm (FAI-270-era skeptics, late 1970s–80s).** Charles Bareis, James Porter, and students argued the dense Tract 15A/15B findings might be anomalous, with lower-density rural settlement the norm, and that the Woodhenge was "not necessarily that startling." Pauketat characterizes this position (his framing — he is a party to the dispute) as consistent with "a romantic view of American Indians as ecologically sensitive beings who would never have built a city."

**4. Demographic minimalism (Milner).** George Milner ([[milner-moundbuilders-2021]]) puts peak population on the order of **~3,000** (possibly 2–3× that), with mound labor within the capacity of a few thousand people working under two weeks a year — no urban mega-population required. Fagan (1991) illustrates the unit-of-analysis problem: ~30,000 (regional) vs. as few as ~500 (walled central precinct, citing Muller).

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

More than 100 (Pauketat: 120+, with ~200 across the wider complex) mounds; Monks Mound at ~100 ft and >600,000 m³; a leveled Grand Plaza; a bastioned palisade; the Mound 72 mass burials; rapid construction activity around 1050 CE (radiocarbon); regional depopulation by ~1350–1400. Minimalists read the same monuments as achievable by modest populations over time; maximalists read house-count extrapolations and the abruptness of the ~1050 horizon as demanding an urban-scale, in-migrating population.

## The dispute's mechanics (Pauketat 2007 layer)

*(Attributed — [[pauketat-chiefdoms-delusions-2007]]; Pauketat is a party.)* The estimate history he reconstructs: Griffin's near-vacant center → post-1960/61 house exposures pushing estimates to 25,000–40,000 (Gregg 1975) → 1960s–70s "Ramey state" claims (Gibbon, O'Brien, Conrad) built on Fowler's four-tiered settlement hierarchy → FAI-270 "third-generation synthesis" rejecting the state reading for "no more than a linked series of simple chiefdoms" with a weak three-tier hierarchy (Milner 1996) → Milner's (1998) "low thousands." Pauketat locates the crux in the **Lohmann-phase duration** (Milner's lengthening from 50 to 100 years halves the rates) and the then-unknown **Richland complex**; his corrected figures are 10,000–16,000 (Cahokia proper) / 25,000–50,000 (region). Notable cross-positions: Muller (1997), a minimalist, concedes Cahokia is exceptional with "possible 'statelike' organization"; Yoffee (pers. comm. 2005, per Pauketat) "stands ready to accept that Cahokia was a city" while doubting it centered an archaic state, agreeing with Monica Smith that cities "need to be uncoupled from the necessary presence of states." The vocabulary of the wider dispute: "downsizers" vs. "exaggerationalists" (Muller's terms; Yoffee, Fish & Milner call southeastern archaeologists "to a man, downsizers"). Pauketat also reports the debate has turned political — citation refusals and blocked access to collections between the factions (Mehrer/Milner/Muller vs. Emerson/Kehoe/Pauketat) — and critiques Yoffee's Rule itself as "an arbitrary yardstick" whose "you" (scholarly consensus) changed sides on Cahokia twice; Lekson's counter-corollary: "If you can argue about whether a case is or is not a state, then it's really interesting."

## Status

Open. The wiki records Milner's ~3,000, Pauketat's 10–16k + hinterland, and the textbook 30–40k figures side by side on [[cahokia]] — different estimates and partly different units of analysis; do not silently reconcile.
