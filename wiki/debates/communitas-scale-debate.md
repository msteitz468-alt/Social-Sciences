# Do liminality and communitas apply beyond small-scale societies?

The most persistent dispute over [[turner-victor|Victor Turner]]'s
*[[turner-ritual-process-1969|The Ritual Process]]* concerns the *scale* at which his
concepts hold. Turner extended **[[liminality]]** and **[[communitas]]** — defined from
[[ndembu]] tribal ritual — to monasticism, millenarian movements, the medieval Franciscans,
the Bengali Sahajiya, and the 1960s counterculture. Critics charged that the concepts break
down outside the small-scale settings in which they were forged. Turner recorded and answered
the charge himself in the 1977 Foreword.

## Position A — The concepts are general (Turner)

*(Attributed to Turner.)* Structure and communitas are "immortal antagonists" constituting the
human condition; every "living, relatively well-bonded human group" alternates between fixed and
"floating" worlds. Communitas' boundaries are "ideally coterminous with those of the human
species," so the concept is not restricted to tribal societies. In the 1977 Foreword Turner
notes the book had been "cited repeatedly by scholars in such diverse fields as history, the
history of religions, English literature, political science, theology, and drama," and answers
the overgeneralization charge with the adage "the proof of the pudding is in the eating."

Turner does, however, concede a real difference at scale: modern communitas-seeking groups
(hippie and "digger" communities, universal ideological movements) "have not yet developed a
structure capable of maintaining social and economic order over long periods of time" — tribal
communitas is the *complement* of tribal structure in a way modern communitas is not.

## Position B — The concepts overgeneralize (critics)

*(Attributed.)* The charge, as Turner reports it: liminality and communitas "may adequately
describe or account for social and cultural processes and phenomena found in preliterate
societies, but have limited use in explaining sociocultural systems of much greater scale and
complexity." On this view the terms become metaphors when lifted out of ritual seclusion —
"liminality" loosens into a synonym for any in-between condition, and "communitas" into a name
for any warm fellow-feeling, losing the specific processual and structural content that gave
them purchase in the Ndembu case.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

Both sides agree that Turner *did* extend the concepts far beyond their tribal origin, and that
modern communitas movements lack the durable structural complement of tribal ritual. They differ
on whether that difference is a *limit* on the concepts (Position B) or simply a *variation* the
structure/communitas dialectic already predicts (Position A).

## Curator's Note (2026-07-09)

The concepts' migration into ritual studies, performance studies, history, and theology is a
historical fact independent of the dispute; the live question is whether that breadth reflects
analytic power or terminological dilution. No consensus resolution; recorded as **open**.
