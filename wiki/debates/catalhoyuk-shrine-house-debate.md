# Çatalhöyük shrine vs house debate

Dispute over whether elaborately decorated buildings at [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] were specialized **cult rooms (“shrines”)** distinct from ordinary houses, or whether decoration, burial, and daily practice form a **continuum** that the 1960s dualism misdescribes.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Dense mudbrick settlement with roof access; under-floor burial; wall-paintings and plaster installations in many buildings (material record of both excavation programs).
- Mellaart excavated a limited area he called a religious quarter (1961–65); Hodder-project teams reopened and expanded recording (from 1993).
- Classification criteria and sampling frame differ: trait lists vs microstratigraphic activity sequences.

## Position A — Mellaart dualism (strong form)

[[mellaart-james|James Mellaart]] (*[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967|1967]]*) classed **≥40 of 139** living rooms (Levels II–X) as cult rooms: more elaborate decoration, often largest buildings, but same plan as houses. Recognition criteria include elaborate ritual paintings, plaster reliefs of deities/animals, horned benches/bucrania, cult statues, wall ex-votos, skulls on platforms — features that “do not occur in normal houses” in combination (his claim). Continuity of cult is inferred from superimposed decorated buildings. Red-ochre and mirror burials are preferentially linked to sanctuaries; leopard-skin dress and belt-fasteners mark male priests. Goddess and bull imagery constitutes a Neolithic religion legible from the art.

## Position B — Contextual / practice continuum (strong form)

Hodder-project work (method charter *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* 1999; specialist microstratigraphy as reported in later syntheses such as Mithen 2003 citing Wendy Matthews) argues that buildings Mellaart labeled shrines show **routine toolmaking and domestic residues**; ritual and domestic life may not have been emically separate. House biography, burial, cattle symbolism, and clay/plaster maintenance form entangled practices ([[entanglement-theory|entanglement]]) rather than a temple-vs-house institutional split. “Shrine” becomes an etic convenience that over-partitions the sample.

### *Leopard’s Tale* synthesis of the continuum (Hodder 2006)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-leopards-tale-2006]].)* Hodder states the project found **no diagnostic basis** for Mellaart’s shrine/house split (e.g. Shrine E.VIB.10); elaborately decorated buildings are treated as **houses** carrying symbolic and ceremonial loads of varying intensity — “leopards and other wild animals appear on house walls, not separate temples.” All major functions modern towns separate (dwelling, production, cult, burial) concentrate in the house (with Özdoğan’s dense Central Anatolian agglomeration as regional frame). On Mellaart’s Level VIB plan, any paired “temple” geometry (10 with 8; 14 with 7) is flagged as possibly accidental adjacency; the modern project rejects the labels even while using the plan for layout thinking. Clean-north/dirty-south floor grammar, ovens, bins, and underfloor burial are the shared domestic syntax of decorated and undecorated buildings alike.

### 2009–17 building evidence densifying Position B (Hodder ed. 2022)

*(Attributed findings — Taylor and colleagues in [[hodder-catalhoyuk-excavations-2009-2017]], esp. Chs. 6–8; project framing Hodder Ch.1.)*

**Phase-level research question.** Phase 2 CRP (1996–99) explicitly asked whether mudbrick buildings were sometimes shrines (Mellaart) or **all houses** even when symbolically elaborate; early surface survey, geophysics, scraping and excavation found relative **site-wide uniformity** of houses with domestic activity and little basis for a priests/shrines sector (Hodder 2022 Ch.1 vs Mellaart). Differentiation is later re-theorised as **history houses** and other house types (Hodder & Pels 2010 via Hodder 2022) — elaborate multi-burial multi-generation rebuilds as neighbourhood ritual/burial foci — **not** a restoration of specialised temples.

**B.7 / Mellaart Shrine 8 southern annexes (Taylor 2022 Ch.6).** Building 7 = Mellaart’s Shrine 8; 2009–17 re-excavation of southern ‘annexe’ spaces (Sp.492 → Sp.470 → Sp.487) proved their association with the main northern structure. Findings undercut a fixed “great shrine + dedicated antechamber” model:

- Earliest Sp.492 has **oven and furniture atypical of a pure storeroom/annexe** — Taylor questions whether it should be classed as such.
- Double wall between Shrine 8 (Sp.176) and Sp.470 plastered on **both faces** → excavators (Klimowicz diary; Taylor) read an **internal partition** within a larger house-scale building, not a simple external annexe wall.
- Sp.470 last-use phytoliths = **wheat de-husking** (processing-area signature), not monumental ritual architecture.
- Sp.470 closure fills ~1.27 m deep match **house-closure** depths site-wide; crawl-hole → plastered niche → formal blocking tracks **domestic remodelling** over the building’s life.
- Upper deposits heavily truncated by Mellaart — shrine furnishings of the 1960s cannot be re-verified as a separate institutional class.

**B.43 / Mellaart E.VIII.27 ‘shrine’ with double leopard relief (Taylor 2022 Ch.8).** Building 43 (Level South L) is Mellaart’s House E.VIII.27, famed for ~6 ft **double leopard relief** on the west wall (removed 1965; Mellaart 1966). It sits at the top of a four-building **domestic stack** B.162 → B.161 → B.160 → B.43 (Levels I–L), with plan continuity (eastern-wall dogleg forced by the neighbouring Mellaart ‘Red Shrine’ stack Sp.581/553). Despite ‘shrine’ historiography:

- First building in the stack with **ovens only at the southern end** (classic later domestic grammar) and expanded side spaces (Sp.600 east; Sp.563 west).
- Northern Sp.235: thin, little-maintained plaster floors and scoured bins → **storage/side-room** function.
- Main Sp.236: multi-phase ovens, grey **dirty floors**, bins, hearth — classic southern domestic activity suite (heavily eroded; thrice abandoned: Neolithic, Mellaart 1965, 2004 project work that stripped remaining leopards).
- Early burials respect B.160 partition plaster → knowledge of previous house layout (history-house style continuity without requiring temple status).
- Adjacent Sp.581 remnants of Mellaart’s ‘red shrine’ S.VIII.31 preserve multi-layer **red plaster + bucranium** fragments — decorative package survives as truncated neighbour, not proof that B.43 was non-domestic.

**Cross-building patterns.** House size and elaboration vary through the South sequence (small/simple B.118, B.130 vs large multi-platform B.89/96/97) **without equating size with shrine status** (Taylor range synthesis). Crawl-hole↔niche conversions recur in B.7, B.160, and B.43 — access remodelled within building lives, cautioning against static plan-type readings (shrine antechamber vs house side room). Eastern-platform multi-burial concentration (e.g. B.96 “Swiss cheese” platforms; excavator diary language of burial/history house) supports continuum ranking by burial intensity, not temple dualism.

## Position C — Heritage multivocality

Goddess-spirituality and popular-matriarchy constituencies continue to read Mellaart’s plates as shrine religion; Turkish national and local interests, academic careers, and tourism negotiate the same material differently (Hodder 1999 heritage framing; *[[hodder-leopards-tale-2006|Leopard’s Tale]]* 2006 stakeholder chapters; UNESCO 2012 and display-constrained excavation in [[hodder-catalhoyuk-excavations-2009-2017|Hodder ed. 2022]]). Academic revision does not erase public shrine/goddess receptions — Position B’s domestic re-readings of B.7 and B.43 leopards sit beside continued popular shrine narratives of the same walls.
