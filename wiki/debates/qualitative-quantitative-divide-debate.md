# Qualitative–Quantitative Divide

A standing methodological dispute over whether social knowledge advances primarily through **variable-oriented** (large-N, statistical, net-effects) or **case-oriented** (small-N, configurational, interpretive) strategies — and whether the dichotomy itself is useful. Related but distinct from [[grand-theory-vs-abstracted-empiricism-debate]] (form of theory vs method-led survey craft in Mills's frame) and from [[generation-vs-verification-debate]].

Per the Voice and Attribution Protocol, positions are recorded, not adjudicated.

## Position 1 — Variable-oriented quantitative tradition

The postwar survey and multivariate tradition (partially overlapping Mills's target in [[abstracted-empiricism]]) holds that probability sampling, measurement, and statistical control deliver comparability, replicability, and cumulative inference that interpretive case work cannot match. Causal claims are typically framed as net effects of variables after [[control-variables|controls]], often as a simulation of experimental isolation. Lieberson's *[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]* describes this practice from inside as the dominant nonexperimental toolkit — Boyle's-law and Mill-style experimental analogues applied to observational data — even while he rejects much of its logic.

## Position 2 — Case-oriented qualitative tradition

Case-oriented work (ethnography, comparative-historical narrative, small-N comparison) prioritizes configurational depth, sequence, meaning, and limited scope conditions over net effects across many units. Critiques of variable-oriented work from this side emphasize context-destruction, thin process evidence, and the ecological and meaning gaps of aggregate measures. (Full primary representation in this vault is distributed across ethnography and comparative-historical sources; this position is stated in its strongest general form without equating all qualitative work.)

## Position 3 — Bridge and synthetic programs

[[ragin-charles|Ragin]] (*[[ragin-comparative-method-1987|The Comparative Method]]*, 1987) framed [[case-oriented-vs-variable-oriented]] strategies as a dialogue and proposed [[qualitative-comparative-analysis|QCA]] / [[multiple-conjunctural-causation]] as a formal middle path: set-theoretic comparison of configurations without abandoning systematic comparison. Mixed-methods programs likewise treat the divide as a false forced choice. QCA's own contested standing (robustness, remainders, measurement) is a sub-dispute; Ragin's method page notes Lieberson among later critics (2004 exchange — distinct from the 1985 monograph).

## Position 4 — Internal quantitative critique (Lieberson)

[[lieberson-stanley|Lieberson]] (*[[lieberson-making-it-count-1985|Making It Count]]*, 1985) argued (attributed) that much nonexperimental quantitative sociology fails **on its own scientific terms**. He remained sympathetic to rigor and quantification; his charge is that [[selectivity]], contamination of comparisons, assumed causal symmetry, variance-explained culture, level-of-analysis nonequivalence, and routine [[control-variables|control-variable]] practice produce mock experiments that reject good ideas and accept false ones. Constructive proposals include restricted legitimate controls, outcome analysis, [[superficial-vs-basic-causes]], [[transformational-causal-principle|transformational principles]], and a social theory of data. This is **not** Position 2: it is reform of variable-oriented work, not replacement by case methods.

## Position 5 — Narrative / sequential program (Abbott)

[[abbott-andrew|Abbott]] (*[[abbott-time-matters-2001|Time Matters]]*, 2001) argued (attributed) that the deep problem is not only identification technique but the **ontology** of [[general-linear-reality|general linear reality]] — fixed entities, univocal attributes, weak sequence, casewise independence, context-free main effects — back-formed from representational use of the GLM. His constructive answer is [[narrative-positivism]]: formal [[sequence-analysis]] ([[optimal-matching]], colligation of [[event-occurrence-distinction|events vs occurrences]], [[turning-point|turning points]]), plus demographic and network alternatives, without surrendering generalization to anti-method romanticism. He reports (attributed) a durable nonconversation with *both* quantitative colleagues (“we already know / no alternative”) and qualitative colleagues (“quantification is useless in principle”), and insists on disentangling narrative/analysis, interpretation/positivism, constructionism/realism, and situated/transcendent knowledge rather than conflating them into one qual–quant war. Related to Positions 3–4 as internal reform of formal social science, but distinct in prioritizing **order, plot, and process** over set-theoretic configuration (Ragin) or mock-experiment hygiene alone (Lieberson).

## Position 6 — Eventful temporality / critique of "experimental" time (Sewell)

[[sewell-william-jr|Sewell]] (*[[sewell-logics-of-history-2005|Logics of History]]*, 2005) argues (attributed) that the variable-oriented tradition rests on an **"experimental" conception of temporality** — the twin assumptions that causal laws are uniform across time and that sequences of occurrences are causally independent, interchangeable "trials." Both, he holds, are unhistorical: social causality is **temporally heterogeneous** (events can change "the very logic by which consequences follow"), and cases are rarely independent (the Bolsheviks modeled themselves on 1789). His critique of Skocpol's quasi-experimental comparative method (following Burawoy: it "freezes history") exemplifies the charge. Distinct from Positions 4–5: where Lieberson reforms identification logic and Abbott targets the linear ontology, Sewell targets the *temporal* assumptions and offers [[eventful-temporality|eventful temporality]] and [[eventful-sociology|eventful sociology]] as the alternative. Yet Sewell is not anti-quantitative: he argues (ch. 10) that quantification is indispensable because capitalist relations are semiotically constituted *as* quantitative via the commodity form, and that real quantitative practice has an unacknowledged "hermeneutical" character ("getting to know your data," the hermeneutic circle) — his "hermeneutical quantification." Sibling to Abbott's program; see [[semiotic-practice]].

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

- Most sociological research is nonexperimental; random assignment of the ideal laboratory type is usually unavailable (Lieberson's starting descriptive point; not disputed across positions).
- Large-N statistical tools and small-N case tools answer different questions and fail in different ways (evidence-class pluralism — vault framework).
- "Control for covariates" and "compare cases as configurations" are different operations with different assumptions (Ragin; Lieberson).
- Sequence and time-horizon assumptions of standard variable models are theoretically load-bearing whether or not one accepts Abbott's full narrative program (Abbott; related life-course and historical-sociology practice).
- Mills's 1959 polemic and Lieberson's 1985 monograph share dissatisfaction with method-led thinness but differ: Mills targets administrative survey craft and psychologism; Lieberson targets identification logic of mock experiments; Abbott targets the linear ontology those tools reify.
