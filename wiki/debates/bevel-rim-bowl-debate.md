# The Bevel-Rim Bowl Function Debate

What were the bevel-rim bowls of the [[uruk-culture|Uruk period]] for? These crude, mould-made conical bowls are the most abundant artifact of the Late Uruk world: H. J. Nissen documented that ~80% of the roughly 6,000 sherds from his soundings at [[uruk|Uruk]] (square KL XII) were bevel-rim bowls or conical cups, judged mass-produced on the evidence of paste, metrics, and surface treatment (as reported in [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]).

## Positions

**Ration containers (Nissen 1970).** Nissen proposed they served for disbursing cereal rations, connecting the bowl's profile to the Archaic sign for "cereal ration" (*ninda*) and the pictogram GU7 ("ration/distribution"). On this reading the bowls are material correlates of the redistributive bureaucracy that also produced the first writing (see [[origins-of-writing]]).

**Standard volume measures (Johnson 1973).** G. A. Johnson's geometric volume-estimation study found bowl volumes clustering around three modes (0.922, 0.647, 0.465 l), which he rounded to standard units of 1, 0.72, and 0.50 l — supporting a standardized-measure hypothesis compatible with Nissen's, though Johnson conceded the difficulty of matching these modes to known (and highly variable) barley ration rates, which ranged 40–96 sila/month for men across periods (Ellison 1981).

**Home-made votive containers (Beale 1978).** T. W. Beale countered that Johnson's modal distribution was "more apparent than real" and argued the bowls were home-made votive containers, not products of centralized mass production.

**Bread moulds (Millard 1988).** A. R. Millard proposed the bowls were moulds for baking bread — while still accepting that they were mass-produced.

## State of the question

Potts (1997) rejects Beale's home-made hypothesis as untenable given the uniformity of manufacture and the use of the wheel, but records the ration-container, standard-measure, and bread-mould readings as live alternatives. The dispute matters beyond ceramics: the ration reading underwrites influential models of Uruk-period administered redistribution ([[temple-economy]], [[craft-specialization]]).
