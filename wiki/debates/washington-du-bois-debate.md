# Washington–Du Bois Debate

Record of the dispute over Black leadership, education, and political strategy at the turn of the twentieth century, as staged in [[du-bois-w-e-b|W. E. B. Du Bois]]’s *[[du-bois-souls-of-black-folk-1903|The Souls of Black Folk]]* (1903), especially chapter III (“Of Mr. Booker T. Washington and Others”) and the education chapters (V–VI). **This page does not adjudicate.** Washington’s own full corpus is not yet ingested; his position is stated in its strongest form *as Du Bois presents it*, pending direct Washington ingest.

`resolution_status: open` — the historical leadership fight closed with changed conditions (NAACP, Great Migration, civil rights movement), but the underlying tensions (industrial vs. liberal education; conciliation vs. rights protest; elite vs. mass strategies) remain live in historiography and politics.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Booker T. Washington rose to national prominence after Reconstruction’s collapse (~post-1876) and especially after the 1895 Atlanta Exposition address.
- Tuskegee Institute and industrial education gained Northern philanthropic and Southern white support in the 1890s.
- Du Bois, Atlanta University sociologist and Harvard-trained scholar, published *Souls* in 1903 as both tribute to Washington’s service and systematic critique.
- Disfranchisement, Jim Crow, and lynching intensified in the same decades Washington’s program of conciliation was ascendant — each side narrates the causal link differently.

## Position A — Washington / Atlanta Compromise (as reconstructed in *Souls*)

Du Bois presents Washington’s program as linking three elements: industrial education, accumulation of wealth, and conciliation of the South. The Atlanta speech’s formula — “In all things purely social we can be as separate as the five fingers, and yet one as the hand in all things essential to mutual progress” — is labeled by Du Bois the **“Atlanta Compromise,”** “by all odds the most notable thing in Mr. Washington’s career.”

On Du Bois’s reconstruction, Washington asks Black people to give up **for the present**:

1. **Political power**  
2. **Insistence on civil rights**  
3. **Higher education of Negro youth**  

…and to concentrate on industrial training, property, and Southern white goodwill. Du Bois also records Washington’s “invaluable service” in counselling patience and thrift, his work building Tuskegee, and his opposition to some injustices — the critique is not a total rejection of the man.

*(Strongest form pending Washington’s own texts: economic self-help first; political rights will follow property and character; industrial education fits the mass need better than classical colleges.)*

## Position B — Du Bois’s dual stance

Du Bois formulates Washington’s **triple paradox**:

1. Artisans, businessmen, and property-owners cannot defend rights under modern competition **without suffrage**.  
2. Thrift and self-respect are counselled together with **silent submission to civic inferiority**, which saps manhood.  
3. Common-school and industrial training are advocated while higher learning is depreciated — yet **neither Negro common schools nor Tuskegee could remain open** without teachers trained in Negro colleges.

He distinguishes two classes of colored critics (bitter radicals vs. those who accept industrial training and a broad Atlanta outlook but refuse the half-truths). Final dual duty:

- **Cooperate** so far as Washington preaches thrift, patience, and industrial training for the masses.  
- **Oppose unceasingly** so far as Washington apologizes for injustice, undervalues the ballot, belittles caste’s emasculating effects, and opposes higher training of “brighter minds.”

Education chapters extend the position: true college aims at the end of life, not meat alone; the [[talented-tenth]] must have the key to knowledge; industrial schools are gates, not the temple; universities are the “Wings of Atalanta” that can lift the South past the Gospel of Pay.

### From “The Talented Tenth” essay (1903)

Additional load-bearing Du Bois moves (not only *Souls* ch. III):

- Object of education is not men carpenters but **carpenters men**; industrialism needs cultured teachers of teachers.
- Higher training of the best teachers **first**, or common/industrial systems throw money to the winds.
- **Tuskegee staffing paradox:** ~30 of Washington’s chief teachers are college graduates doing noble work, yet one effect of Washington’s propaganda, Du Bois argues, has been to throw doubt on the expediency of such training for Negroes.
- Rejects “plow before Senate” as a “foolish and mischievous lie” after 250 years of unfree plow labor.

### From *Economic Revolution in the South* (c. 1906)

Du Bois reconstructs a city-based Southern/Northern-capital settlement of **“Industry and disfranchisement”** — separate Negro masses from government and give technical training for skilled work — as a program that promised relief of race trouble and failed: white primary fraud, stripped school funds, mob violence, landlords wanting “docile” not educated farmers, white artisans rejecting educated Black artisans by votes and mobs. This frames the industrial-education settlement as political economy, not only school philosophy. (Washington’s own texts still not ingested.)
