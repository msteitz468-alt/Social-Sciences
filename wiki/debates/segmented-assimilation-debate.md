# Segmented Assimilation Debate

Dispute over whether post-1965 second-generation adaptation in the United States is best described as **divergent segmented paths** (including downward mobility into a marginalized underclass for some) or as **continued generational assimilation** into an expanding mainstream.

## Positions

### Segmented assimilation

Held by [[portes-alejandro|Portes]], [[rumbaut-ruben|Rumbaut]], Min Zhou, and collaborators. *[[portes-rumbaut-legacies-2001|Legacies]]* argues that race, hourglass labor markets, and inner-city countercultures create barriers early European second generations did not face in the same form; [[modes-of-incorporation]] and acculturation type ([[dissonant-acculturation]] vs [[selective-acculturation]]) sort youth into mainstream, ethnic-mobility, or downward tracks. CILS nationality gradients and psychosocial/school outcomes are offered as supporting evidence for divergence in adolescence.

### Classical straight-line assimilation

Older master narrative (e.g. Warner & Srole-type optimism cited in *Legacies* ch. 3): contact over generations yields merging with the mainstream; ethnicity becomes optional. Portes & Rumbaut treat this as factually incomplete for today's second generation, not merely normatively contested.

### Neo-assimilation / boundary-change variants

Later positions (often associated with Richard Alba, Victor Nee, and others — **not yet ingested as primary sources here**) argue that despite racialization and inequality, second-generation mobility and mainstream expansion remain the central tendency; they dispute the size, durability, or distinctiveness of "downward assimilation" as a segment. Recorded as a live rival cluster pending primary ingest.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Large post-1965 immigration from Latin America, the Caribbean, and Asia.
- Second-generation youth show rapid English acquisition in U.S. data including CILS (~98% English well/very well by mid-1990s follow-up in that panel).
- National-origin groups differ in average parental education, legal status, and local community resources.
- CILS adolescents often show lower multiyear dropout rates than native-parentage peers in the same districts — a fact both sides can claim (optimism about averages vs segmentation within averages).
- Whether nationality gaps constitute durable **segmented class destinies** or **transitory adolescent gaps** is the dispute.

## Evidence classes

Survey/longitudinal (CILS and successors); census/CPS; ethnography of enclaves and schools; later adult follow-ups (outside *Legacies*). Cross-class conflict (e.g. optimistic census mobility vs pessimistic ethnography) should update this page when ingested.

## *Legacies* policy frame (not a third "theory" position)

Portes & Rumbaut ch. 10 contrast **intransigent nativism** (e.g. California Prop 187) and **forceful assimilationism** (e.g. Prop 227) as mainstream ideologies that, they argue, undermine selective acculturation and can produce self-fulfilling ethnic polarization. Marked as **authors' position**, not field consensus.
