# Causation of the Difaqane / Mfecane

> A dispute over how to explain the destructive southern-African warfare of the 1810s–1830s, and over the colonial provenance of the standard account. Noted (without adjudication) in *[[comaroff-of-revelation-and-revolution-1991|Of Revelation and Revolution]]* (1991), which touches the upheaval as it reached the [[southern-tswana|Southern Tswana]].

The *difaqane* (Sotho-Tswana term; *mfecane* in Nguni) names the period of war, displacement, and depopulation across the highveld and beyond in the early nineteenth century.

## Position 1 — Shaka-centered chain reaction (conventional)

The received historiography (Thompson 1969; Lye 1969) attributes the upheaval to the rise of the **Zulu state under Shaka**, radiating outward as a "chain reaction" of displaced groups — the Tlokwa ("Mantatees" under the regent MmaNthatisi), Taung, Fokeng, and Ndebele (Mzilikazi) — reaching the Tswana world in 1822–23. It is buttressed by eyewitness-style missionary accounts (e.g., Broadbent 1865: "the land was strewn with human bones"), colored by "the evangelical sense that these were the battlefields of Satan."

## Position 2 — Cobbing's colonial-slaving revisionism

**Julian Cobbing** (1988, "The Mfecane as Alibi") argues the Shaka-centered narrative rests on "extremely flimsy" evidence and was propagated by settler history and the apartheid state — functioning as an *alibi* that blames African-on-African violence for depopulation actually driven by **European penetration**: Portuguese slave-raiding pressure from Delagoa Bay and Cape Colony demand for bonded labour, procured through Griqua/Bergenaar raiding on Sotho-Tswana communities. Many "difaqane battles," on this reading, were slave and cattle raids.

## Shared Factual Ground

Both sides accept that the period involved severe, bewildering sequences of attack and counterattack; heavy loss of life, cattle, and crops; and that missionaries were caught up in it (the Comaroffs note the Tlhaping largely escaped attack, partly through Griqua commandos summoned by Robert Moffat, prompting some to conclude "a morafe with a mission would never suffer military reverses"). The dispute is over *causation and the politics of the sources*, not over whether upheaval occurred.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-09)

The Comaroffs treat Cobbing's reanalysis as one that "certainly warrants careful evaluation" but do not fully endorse or reject it; subsequent scholarship has moderated both poles, accepting internal Zulu-state dynamics *and* external colonial-slaving pressures as jointly operative — hence `resolution_status: partially-resolved`.
