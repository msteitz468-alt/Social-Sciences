# Can Structural-Functionalism Explain Social Change?

A defining theoretical dispute of postwar sociology: whether [[structural-functionalism]], and Parsons's [[social-system|social-system]] theory in particular, is constitutionally unable to theorize social change — or whether that charge mistakes a division of theoretical labor for a blind spot. The dispute is narrower than the general [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate|consensus-vs-conflict]] argument: it concerns *change* specifically, and it turns on Parsons's own words in Chapters XI–XII of *[[parsons-social-system-1951|The Social System]]*.

## The Charge (conflict theorists)

Ralf Dahrendorf, David Lockwood, C. Wright Mills, and later Alvin Gouldner argued that a framework built on [[social-equilibrium|equilibrium]], [[functional-prerequisites|functional prerequisites]], and shared [[value-orientation|value]] integration has a **static bias**: change can appear only as *disturbance-and-restoration* (the domain of [[social-control]]) or as *adaptive differentiation*, never as endogenous, conflict-driven transformation. Because the model treats order as the explanandum, it must theorize conflict as [[deviance]] from central norms and cannot give material interests, power, and class their due — reducing sociology, in Lockwood's phrase, to an account of normative rather than "factual" substratum tensions.

## Merton's internal path (dysfunction → change)

Within functional analysis rather than against it, [[merton-robert-k|Merton]] (*[[merton-social-theory-social-structure-1968|STSS]]* Ch. III) argued (attributed) that exclusive focus on eufunctions of existing structures serves ultraconservative ideology, while exclusive focus on dysfunctions serves radical ideology. Rigorous analysis inventories **both**. He held that when a structure is clearly **[[dysfunction|dysfunctional]]** for designated units, “there develops a strong and insistent pressure for change,” and that strains accumulating as dysfunctions are a principal dynamic. Coupled with **functional alternatives** and rejection of the **indispensability** postulate, this is a change pathway *inside* the functional orientation — distinct from Parsons’s equilibrium/concession formulation and from conflict theory’s endogenous class-conflict model. See [[manifest-and-latent-functions]], [[middle-range-theory]].

## Parsons's Position

Parsons denied a static bias directly: "there is a certain falsity in the dilemma between 'static' and 'dynamic' emphases. If theory is good theory, whichever type of problem it tackles most directly, there is no reason whatever to believe that it will not be equally applicable to the problems of change and to those of process within a stabilized system." He distinguished *processes within* a system (equilibrating) from *processes of change of* the system's structure, insisted the equilibrium could be a **moving** one, rejected single-factor ("dominant factor") theories of change, and offered case analyses of change (institutionalized [[rationalization]]; the rise and "adaptive transformation" of revolutionary movements).

**But** — in the same chapter — Parsons conceded the decisive point his critics seized on:

> a general theory of the processes of change of social systems is not possible in the present state of knowledge. The reason is very simply that such a theory would imply complete knowledge of the laws of process of the system and this knowledge we do not possess.

He held this is a limit of *knowledge*, not of the *framework*, and that a theory of "particular sub-processes of change" is available; he distinguished a *paradigm* (what he had produced) from a *theory* ("a system of laws").

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides agree Parsons produced no general theory of change and that his equilibrium apparatus is analytically prior. Conflict theorists read this as confirming a disabling bias intrinsic to functionalism; Parsons (and later neofunctionalists) read it as an honest, provisional limit shared by any theory lacking complete process-laws, not a commitment to stasis. The two readings are not strictly contradictory — the paradigm/general-theory distinction lets both stand — but the tension between the "no static bias" rebuttal and the "not possible in the present state of knowledge" concession is the crux.

## Resolution Status

`partially-resolved`. The historical verdict of the critical turn went against grand functionalism, and "cannot handle change" became its textbook epitaph. Yet the neofunctionalist revival (Alexander) and evolutionary-differentiation theory (including Parsons's own later *Societies*, 1966) reopened the question, arguing functionalism *can* model change as differentiation and adaptive upgrading. What is settled: functionalism models change as adaptive/systemic rather than as endogenous conflict; whether that is a fatal limit or a legitimate specialization remains disputed.

*Curator's note (2026-07-08): the dispute is recorded here without adjudication; the change critique is also a major strand of the [[coming-crisis-of-western-sociology-debate|Gouldner]] reading and overlaps the [[consensus-vs-conflict-debate|consensus/conflict]] axis without being reducible to it.*

## British anthropology — Leach vs equilibrium (1954/1964)

A parallel **internal** critique of equilibrium analysis comes from social anthropology rather than conflict sociology. In *[[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-1954|Political Systems of Highland Burma]]* (1954; 1964 Introductory Note), [[leach-edmund|E. R. Leach]] argued that British SF had rested too long on organic-equilibrium assumptions; that historical facts are never in equilibrium; and that ethnographic "structure" is a model of ideal patterns treated *as if* equilibrated — corresponding also to how [[kachin|Kachins]] use verbal categories. His case is the [[gumsa-gumlao-oscillation|gumsa/gumlao oscillation]]: hierarchical and egalitarian poles as ideal types, empirical politics as compromise and flux under competition for power. [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]] (as Leach reports from *Order and Rebellion*, 1963) called the Kachin system "stable equilibrium" (true, Leach said, at the level of ideas, untrue at the level of facts) and had himself sustained organic readings of rebellion cycles. Fortes's Tallensi equilibrium passage in *African Political Systems* (1940) is Leach's explicit counter-type: if Kachins were equilibrated in that sense, Leach held, his book had been misunderstood. Positions only — see [[leach-political-systems-highland-burma-study]]; no adjudication.

**Gluckman's side in his own words (Order and Rebellion, 1963 Introduction; ingested [[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963]]).** Gluckman rejected Leach's "cyclical theory of social change" label for the gumsa–gumlao material: long-cycle repetitive change without transformation of pattern is, he argued, exactly what "equilibrium" has always meant — "a body in stable equilibrium (tending to recover equilibrium after disturbance)" (OED cited) — and "anyone reading Evans-Pritchard or Fortes… must conclude that Leach has misread them." At the same time he conceded the organic model itself: he renounced the organic analogy, describing social systems as open, only "largely" integrated fields of discrepant, non-cancelling processes, and withdrew his own earlier "civil war had a function in maintaining the system" phrasing (under Schapera's 1956 criticism) as "crude functional" language. Related satellite disputes recorded from the same Introduction: **Wilson vs Gluckman** — Monica Wilson (Frazer Lecture 1959) reread Nyakyusa/Ngonde cases of [[ritual-of-rebellion]] as ritualized "confession"; Gluckman called the substitution a logical fallacy (a narrow process cannot explain a complex whole) but held the readings compatible once function-in-the-political-field is distinguished from content; **Reay vs Gluckman** — Marie Reay (Oceania 1959) charged the rituals-of-rebellion analysis with covert psychologism; Gluckman blamed a misquotation ("act" read as "act *out*") and switched to "perform".

## Malinowski on culture change vs Gluckman's single social field (1940s)

An earlier round of the same question, within functionalism. **Malinowski's position** (*The Dynamics of Culture Change*, posthumous, ed. Kaberry, 1945): culture contact in colonial Africa comprises "three distinct orders of cultural reality: the African, the Western, and that of transition," each with "its own determinism"; institutions are integral wholes satisfying universal needs and cannot be decomposed without distortion; Europeans are not "integral" parts of tribal social life in the way chiefs and magicians are (attacking Fortes and Schapera for holding otherwise). **Gluckman's position** (review essays reprinted in [[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963]]): colonial Africa is a **single interdependent social field** — black and white, tribal and industrial sectors as parts of one field (illustrated with his Zulu data and a reanalysis of the Retief–Dingane–Mpande conflict of 1836–40); conflict is a normal mode of integration that Malinowski's scheme cannot accommodate, forcing him to file cooperative interracial relations under "contact and change" and conflictual ones under a separate "African reaction"; needs-functionalism is analytically sterile for change ("little point in sending an anthropologist… to find out that some Bantu tribe has a complex organization to satisfy the need of its members for food"); and Malinowski gives four mutually inconsistent definitions of "function" in one book. Gluckman charged Malinowski (and editor Kaberry) with distorting the "integral" concept into "well-integrated harmonious whole." Positions recorded, not adjudicated; see [[malinowski-bronislaw]].


## Gluckman vs Malinowski on social change (*Order and Rebellion*, essays VIII–X)

*(Attributed — [[gluckman-max|Gluckman]], *[[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963|Order and Rebellion]]* 1963; original essays on *Dynamics of Culture Change* and Malinowski appraisals.)*

**Position — Malinowski (as Gluckman reconstructs):** culture contact as organized impact between institutions; “three cultural realities” (tribal, white, intermediate) each a distinct integrated whole; history of little use; practical anthropology of culture change.

**Position — Gluckman:** the one-entry / three-phase scheme is **stultifying**; tribal and urban African life belong in **one social field** (detribalized *and* still tribalized); needs-based “common factor” is analytically sterile; structural analysis of power, cleavages, and dual authority is required. Grants: fieldwork code, institutions as units, kula ethnography. Feeds: [[malinowski-bronislaw]] · [[manchester-school]] · [[structural-functionalism]].

## The 1951 Murdock–Firth exchange (attributed — Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995, Epilogue)

*(Source: [[stocking-after-tylor-1995]]. Stocking organizes his historiographic epilogue around this American Anthropologist exchange as the moment British structural-functionalism's postwar self-image was directly challenged from outside.)*

**Position — George Murdock (1951, *American Anthropologist*):** critiqued British "structural-functionalist" social anthropology for narrowness of ethnographic interest, "theoretical parochialism," disinterest in general ethnography, neglect of history and cultural change, indifference to psychology, and — most pointedly — abandonment of "the culture concept": "Alone among the anthropologists of the world the British make no use of the culture concept." Murdock also argued [[radcliffe-brown-a-r|Radcliffe-Brown]] "seemed never 'to have modified his theoretical position in any significant way since its earliest formulation decades ago.'"

**Position — Raymond Firth's 1951 reply:** granted some validity to Murdock's criticisms but argued British social anthropology's narrowness was "more apparent than real," and defended the strategy of isolating "a particular sphere of inquiry" to build "a more precise framework of ideas" rather than attempting Murdock's more diffuse comparative-culture program.

Stocking notes that by the early 1950s three leading Association of Social Anthropologists members — Firth (*Elements of Social Organization*), [[nadel-s-f|Nadel]] (*Foundations of Social Anthropology*), and Evans-Pritchard (*Social Anthropology* and the 1950 Marett Lecture) — published overviews that, per later observers Stocking cites, themselves "challenged" the "old Oxford orthodoxy" associated with Radcliffe-Brown; Evans-Pritchard's challenge was the most radical, redefining social anthropology as "a kind of historiography," one of the humanities rather than a natural science — an internal echo of Murdock's change-and-history complaint, arrived at independently. Positions recorded per Stocking's presentation; not adjudicated. See [[applied-anthropology]] and [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]] for the same period's institutional context.
