# The Tupinambá Cannibalism Debate

What did the war-cannibalism complex of the 16th-century coastal [[tupinamba|Tupinambá]] — capture, prolonged captivity with a granted wife, ritual dialogue, execution, collective consumption, the killer's abstention and renaming — actually *do*? The chronicles (Staden 1557, Thevet 1575, Léry 1578, Cardim 1584, Anchieta, Soares de Souza 1587, Monteiro 1610) are rich but missionary-produced; every position works from the same corpus.

## Positions

**Incorporation of qualities (Métraux and the older literature).** Alfred Métraux held that eating the enemy transferred his qualities/energies to the eaters — supported by Monteiro's report that "they believe that the nature and condition of what they eat gets passed on to them." Métraux also suggested the old women's voracity served to "recharge their energies." Viveiros de Castro (1992) treats the substance-transfer version as too literal but retains an "intensional" variant (below).

**Maussian sacrifice (Florestan Fernandes, 1949/1952).** Fernandes, applying Hubert and Mauss, read Tupinambá anthropophagy as human sacrifice: the captive was the means of communion between the living and the group's dead; execution answered the compulsion of the deceased's spirit demanding vengeance; cannibalism was "a collective modality of direct and immediate communion with the sacred," the "restoration of the collective We." In its strongest form, the sacrifice of the victim was the *cause*, not the consequence, of the revenge imperative. Fernandes's remains the most systematic sociological account of the institution ever built from the chronicles.

**Ritual negation of affinity (Hélène Clastres, 1972/1975).** H. Clastres noted the semantic identity of enemy and brother-in-law (*tovaja*) and read Tupinambá warfare — which captured "brothers-in-law" rather than wives — as a displaced desire for a world without affines, connected to the prophetic quest for the Land without Evil: cannibal vengeance as a "ritual negation of alliance."

**Ontological predation / enemy-becoming (Viveiros de Castro, 1992; with Carneiro da Cunha 1985).** Viveiros de Castro argued against Fernandes that "no source confirms" the avenging-spirit compulsion — the sources on individual dead-spirits' intervention are "nonexistent," and Fernandes "treats the ethnographic materials like raw gems, cutting and filing to make them fit into the jewelry box of theory." On his reading, vengeance was general and vicarious, an a priori of the system rather than a debt to particular dead; the dead were "the instrument, not the subject" of Tupinambá religion — "the enemy was not the means, but the end." What was incorporated was not substance but the incorporeal *position* of Enmity ("cannibalism is inevitably a semiophagy"): an enemy-becoming, with society "constituted as a relation-to-the-enemy." He partially salvages Fernandes (the killer/captive pair as the two faces of the deceased) and partially endorses H. Clastres's *tovaja* insight while rejecting her "bias of negativity," arguing (with Carneiro da Cunha) that prophetism *affirmed* the warfare-cannibalism core rather than negating society. Combès (1987) is credited with showing the cannibal diet was itself held to be immortalizing.

## Shared factual ground

All parties accept the chronicled outline: warfare aimed at capture; captives lived for months or years with a granted woman (paradigmatically the killer's sister); execution followed a formal verbal duel in which the captive demanded future vengeance; everyone but the killer ate; the killer entered corpse-like seclusion, was scarified, and took a new name; dying by the enemy's club was honorable, dying at home and rotting was wretched. The dispute is over what mechanism and meaning organize these facts.

## Status

Open. Fernandes's model remains the reference account in Brazilian sociology of the institution; Viveiros de Castro's predation/becoming reading has been broadly influential in Americanist anthropology (feeding [[perspectivism]] and the [[ontological-turn]]), but it is an interpretation of the same 16th-century textual corpus, not new evidence — the evidence class is textual/archival only, with all its survivorship and missionary-production problems.
