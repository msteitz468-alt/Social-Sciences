# Prescriptive vs Preferential Marriage

The central theoretical dispute in the Anglophone reception of [[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss's]] [[alliance-theory]]: does the *[[levi-strauss-elementary-structures-1949|Elementary Structures of Kinship]]* concern **prescriptive** marriage systems (which positively enjoin a specific category of spouse) or, more broadly, **preferential** ones — and are these even two different kinds of system? Lévi-Strauss himself called the terminological split "the most unexpected" development to arise from the book's English reception.

## Position A — the systems are of two kinds; the book is about prescription (Needham)

[[needham-rodney|Rodney Needham]], in *Structure and Sentiment* (1962) and as editor of the 1969 English translation, argued that the proper object of the theory is **prescriptive** systems specifically — those where a rule actually enjoins marriage with a defined category — as against merely **preferential** ones expressing a statistical tendency. He defended his terminology as a literal rendering of Lévi-Strauss's own 1949 French (*les systèmes prescrivent le mariage avec un certain type de parents*) and cited Lévi-Strauss's 1958 remark that under a mere psychological preference matrilateral marriage "would indeed be more frequent 'but it would not need to be prescribed.'" Floyd Lounsbury's review of Needham's book identified a "fundamental misunderstanding" when the prescriptive/preferential contrast is conflated with the elementary/complex one.

## Position B — one reality, two levels of description (Lévi-Strauss)

In the 1967 preface Lévi-Strauss rejected the reduction, accusing Needham of a "fundamental misunderstanding": prescriptive and preferential are **not two classes of system but two levels of description of the same reality**. "A preferential system is prescriptive when envisaged at the model level; a prescriptive system must be preferential when envisaged on the level of reality." "Neither prescription nor preference is the test of an elementary structure." The true criterion, he maintained, is **structural**: the spouse is spouse solely because she belongs to an alliance category or stands in a defined kinship relation to Ego — not because of extrinsic qualities (wealth, attributes), which define complex structures. The distinction correlates with the elementary/complex distinction "only at the level of the model."

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides agree that real marriage systems rarely achieve statistical conformity to their stated rule (in Fiji only ~30% of marriages fit strict bilateral cross-cousin marriage), and that a "rule" and its realization diverge. They differ on whether that gap makes "prescriptive" and "preferential" *names for different systems* (Needham) or *names for the model and the reality of one system* (Lévi-Strauss).

## Related disputes

The reception also carried the **Leach–Lévi-Strauss** disputes over specific analyses (Kachin gumsa/gumlao stability; the [[murngin|Murngin]] "local lines" vs "descent lines"), where Leach called the book "a splendid failure," and the deeper practice-theoretic objection that marriage is *strategy*, not rule-execution ([[bourdieu-pierre|Bourdieu]]; [[strategy-vs-rule]], [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]).

## Resolution

`partially-resolved`: the field largely absorbed Needham's insistence on distinguishing rule from statistical practice while accepting Lévi-Strauss's point that the two "systems" are often one reality described at different levels. The dispute cooled without a decisive verdict, and both terms remain in use — a standing reminder that a kinship "rule" and its enactment are different objects.
