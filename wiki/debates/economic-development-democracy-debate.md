# Economic Development and Democracy Debate

## Positions

### Lipset modernization thesis

[[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] (*[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*, Ch II) argued that wealth, industrialization, urbanization, and education raise the probability of stable democracy. Concept: [[economic-development-and-democracy]]. Theory program: [[modernization-theory]]. Study: [[lipset-political-man-study]].

### Survival vs transition reanalyses

Later quantitative comparative politics (names such as Przeworski et al., not yet ingested as primary sources) often split Lipset's association: development strongly predicts *survival* of democracy conditional on already being democratic, with weaker or different effects on *transitions from* authoritarianism. Recorded here as a position cluster pending primary ingest.

### Critical and institutionalist lines

Dependency, historical-institutionalist, and case-based critics stress oil and resource wealth without democracy, successful authoritarian developers, colonial legacies, inequality, and elite bargains. They treat simple cross-sectional wealth–democracy tables as under-identified.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

A positive cross-national association between development indicators and democracy measures appears in many mid-century and later datasets; disagreement centers on causal direction, mechanisms, and scope conditions.

## Resolution status

**Open** — among the most reanalyzed propositions in comparative politics. Wiki records Lipset's claim as attributed and contested.
