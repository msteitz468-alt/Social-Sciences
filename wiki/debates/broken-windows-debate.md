# Broken Windows: Does Disorder Cause Crime?

A dispute over whether visible public "disorder" causes serious crime — and therefore whether order-maintenance policing that removes panhandlers, vendors, and loiterers is justified. It is simultaneously **empirical** (is there a disorder→crime causal chain? did broken-windows policing cause New York's 1990s crime drop?) and **interpretive** (what is "disorder," and who gets to define street life as disorderly?). Positions are recorded here in their strongest form; the wiki does not adjudicate.

## Position 1 — Broken windows / order maintenance

James Q. Wilson and George Kelling (1982) argue (attributed) that visible disorder signals "no one cares," inviting predation and serious crime; maintaining order is thus crime prevention. Kelling and Coles's *Fixing Broken Windows* (1996) extends the program to removing "disreputable or obstreperous or unpredictable people" and unlicensed vendors. Business Improvement District advocates in *Sidewalk* (e.g., counsel Andrew Manshel) explicitly ground their anti-vending policy in the theory and call unlicensed vending "an antisocial act." Evidence base: Zimbardo's abandoned-car demonstration; the correlation of order-maintenance policing with falling New York crime. See [[broken-windows]].

## Position 2 — Ethnographic critique (Duneier)

[[duneier-mitchell|Duneier]] (*[[duneier-sidewalk-study|Sidewalk]]*, 1999) argues (attributed) the theory illegitimately extends Zimbardo's demonstration — which concerned only **physical** disorder — to **social** disorder, wrongly assuming the two have equivalent effects. Social "broken windows" are people "capable of creating meanings"; the [[sixth-avenue-sidewalk-vendors|Sixth Avenue vendors]] constitute a working moral order and act as [[public-character|public characters]] signaling that "someone cares." He shows that quality-of-life regulation (**Local Law 45**, BID pressure) **destabilized** a peaceful informal order rather than restoring one — producing conflict, extortion, and police dependence. Duneier does not reject broken windows wholesale (he grants its community-policing side may have helped lower crime) but calls its application to people making "an honest living" its weakest part, and offers a mirror-image **"fixed windows"** thesis. Evidence base: multi-year participant observation, taped interaction, legal-history tracing.

## Position 3 — Structural / collective-efficacy critique (Sampson & Raudenbush; Sampson 2012)

[[sampson-robert-j|Sampson]] (with [[raudenbush-stephen|Raudenbush]]; *[[sampson-great-american-city-2012|Great American City]]* ch. 6 and prior SSO work) argues (attributed) that the strong broken-windows causal chain is mis-specified:

- **Tautology.** Many "disorder" items *are* crimes/ordinance violations — strong BW claims risk "crime causes crime."
- **Common-cause continuum.** Public disorder and predatory crime may sit on one seriousness continuum and share predictors ([[collective-efficacy]], concentrated disadvantage, density, land use); disorder need not be the essential causal link.
- **SSO findings.** [[systematic-social-observation|Systematically observed]] disorder is reliably measurable; poverty and mixed land use forecast it; CE predicts lower observed disorder and lower violence after structural and feedback controls. Observed disorder is only a moderate correlate of burglary/homicide and the link shrinks once structure is controlled. **Robbery** is a partial exception (direct association with visual cues).
- **Meaning and perception.** The same visual cues can be coded "edgy" vs "disordered" by context; survey "how much of a problem" items capture meaning, not just detection. Racial/immigrant composition predicts *perceived* disorder more than SSO-observed disorder; shared prior perceptions are sticky (community-area r≈0.89) and forecast later poverty and population decline when concurrent observed disorder does not.
- **Policy.** Eradicating disorder by law enforcement alone leaves common origins untouched and treats perceptions as unproblematic. If BW works, Sampson suggests it may work "perversely" — by amplifying stigma-driven outmigration and poverty traps rather than via the original offender-signal mechanism.

Evidence base: PHDCN community surveys, videotaped SSO (~23,816 face-blocks in 1995), census/police archives, leader surveys, longitudinal replications 1995→2002. See [[sampson-great-american-city-study]] · [[ecometrics]] · [[collective-efficacy]] · [[neighborhood-effects]].

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

All sides agree that New York crime fell sharply in the 1990s and that order-maintenance policing expanded in the same period; they disagree on the **causal reading** (broken-windows enforcement vs. broader crime-decline trends affecting cities that never adopted it) and on whether street vending/panhandling is "disorder" at all. All sides agree the vendors were poor, mostly Black men in an informal economy; they disagree on whether that economy is incipient crime or a moral order.

On the Chicago PHDCN evidence (Position 3's base), parties can grant that *some* association of visual cues with *robbery* appears, and that order-maintenance policing was widely exported; they disagree whether disorder is a general cause of predatory crime or a correlated symptom of low CE/disadvantage, and whether *perceptions* of disorder (racialized, sticky) matter more for neighborhood trajectories than observed cues.
