# Parsons's Convergence Thesis

The central interpretive dispute arising from *[[parsons-structure-social-action-1937|The Structure
of Social Action]]* (1937): did Alfred Marshall, [[pareto-vilfredo|Pareto]], [[durkheim-emile|Durkheim]],
and [[weber-max|Weber]] genuinely **converge**, independently, on a single
[[voluntaristic-theory-of-action|voluntaristic theory of action]] — or did [[parsons-talcott|Parsons]]
**impose** his own framework on four heterogeneous bodies of work? Per this wiki's protocol, no
adjudication in wiki voice; each position is stated in its strongest form.

## Position A — Genuine convergence (Parsons)

Parsons argued the appearance of the same conceptual scheme in writers of sharply different politics
and starting points is best explained by "the determination of the convergence by the adequacy of the
theories to the facts." He pre-argued against the alternatives: it is not an artifact of shared
personal sentiment (Durkheim's radical humanitarianism "was the commonest target for Pareto's biting
irony"); not the immanent development of each tradition (utilitarianism could and did instead develop
into Social Darwinism; anti-Marxian idea-critique into Sombartian emanationism); and not mere
accumulated error. He conceded it was not demonstrated with mathematical rigor, only that "no other
interpretation of these facts, taken all together, which could be seriously considered has ever been
put forward," and framed the sole way to reject it as holding that he had "radically misinterpreted"
all the writers — an empirically falsifiable claim.

## Position B — Imposed framework (critics)

Critics hold that the convergence is largely Parsons's construction: that his chapter-length
reconstructions (his own "'ideal type' of an 'ideal type'") select and reshape each writer toward a
predetermined synthesis, and that the readings of Durkheim and Weber in particular are selective —
Durkheim's late "sociological epistemology" is redescribed via Weber's *Wertbeziehung* to fit, and
Weber's methodological pluralism and pessimism are treated as an "unstable halfway point" toward
Parsons's own position. On this view the thesis is partly circular: the convergence is offered as
evidence for Parsons's theory of scientific development, while that theory frames what counts as
convergence.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

Both sides agree on the textual record: what each writer wrote, and that Parsons read them toward a
common action framework. They disagree on whether the commonality is **in the sources** or **in the
reading**. Both also agree the four writers did not cite one another on these points (Parsons treats
the mutual independence as strengthening convergence; critics treat it as making a single "discovered"
system less likely).
