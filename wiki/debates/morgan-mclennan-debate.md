# Morgan–McLennan Debate (Origin of Classificatory Kinship)

## Shared ground

Both Lewis Henry Morgan and John Ferguson McLennan were founding-era evolutionary anthropologists trying to reconstruct the history of the human family from its surviving traces. Both accepted that present-day "primitive" customs and terms could be read as evidence for earlier social forms. They disagreed sharply over **what classificatory kinship terminologies are and whether they carry historical information** — and over the priority of the concepts each had coined. Positions are recorded, not adjudicated in wiki voice. This page is anchored by the extended rebuttal Morgan added as a "Note" to chapter VI of *[[morgan-ancient-society-1877|Ancient Society]]* (1877); McLennan's own texts are not yet ingested, so his position is stated as Morgan reports it plus standard summary.

## Position 1 — Morgan (systems of consanguinity as historical fossils)

[[morgan-lewis-henry|Morgan]] (attributed, *Ancient Society* ch. VI Note; *[[morgan-systems-consanguinity-1871|Systems of Consanguinity]]*, 1871) holds that the [[classificatory-system-of-relationship|classificatory]] terminologies he tabulated for ~139 tribes and nations (by his count, four-fifths of humanity) are genuine, internally consistent **systems of consanguinity and affinity** — not casual courtesy terms. Because such systems are "neither adopted, modified, nor laid aside at pleasure," they persist as durable records of the marriage and family forms that produced them (see [[morgan-family-sequence]]). He defends the data against McLennan's charge that they are "utterly unscientific" and "mere modes of address," and argues McLennan **reversed the historical priority** of exogamy/endogamy and **conflated tribe with [[gens]]**. He cites Herbert Spencer as a third-party critic of McLennan on some points, and casts himself as defender of "the method of scientific inquiry" against speculation.

## Position 2 — McLennan (marriage-law variants; terms of no historical value)

J. F. McLennan (*Primitive Marriage*, 1865; *Studies in Ancient History*, 1876), as reported by Morgan and in standard summary, held that classificatory kinship terms were **"mere modes of address"** with no bearing on actual consanguinity, and therefore useless as evidence for the history of the family. He explained social evolution instead through **marriage-law variants** — female infanticide producing a scarcity of women, hence **polyandry** (Nair, Tibetan cases) and capture — and through his own influential coinages **exogamy** and **endogamy** as the primary organizing principles. On his account the history of kinship is a history of marriage rules, not of terminological systems recording group marriage.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

- Both agree classificatory terminologies exist and differ systematically from descriptive ones; they disagree on whether the terms are *evidence* (Morgan) or *noise* (McLennan).
- Both agree "primitive" societies illuminate the past; they disagree on which data class (terminology vs. marriage law) carries the signal.
- The dispute is partly a **priority dispute**: Morgan is defending his own 1871 work against a named rival's public attack, and each is promoting his own conceptual vocabulary.

## Stocking's account (Victorian Anthropology, 1987)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]] 1987.)* Stocking's reading fills out McLennan's side of the dispute directly from *[[mclennan-john-ferguson|McLennan]]*'s own *Primitive Marriage* (1865), which Stocking judges "one of the clearest, most elaborate, and least apologetic" expositions of the sociocultural-evolutionary [[comparative-method]] — methodologically opposed at root to [[maine-henry-sumner|Maine]]'s comparative jurisprudence. McLennan reconstructed marriage evolution as a sequence from primitive sexual [[primitive-promiscuity|promiscuity]] through **[[primitive-promiscuity|polyandry]]** (driven by female infanticide creating a male demographic surplus) to matrilineal kinship (paternity being undeterminable) and finally to patrilineal monogamy; he coined **exogamy** for tribal out-marriage and derived it from marriage by capture, itself a symbolic residue of that same infanticide-driven scarcity of women. Stocking reports McLennan's own language cast primitive marriage as effectively "rape," and records a posthumously published essay in which McLennan called female infanticide, as a Malthusian population check, "the most important step that was ever taken in the history of mankind" — a starkly amoral utilitarian account of civilization's origin in demographic violence.

McLennan's later career, per Stocking, became "bogged down" in a running **priority dispute with Maine** over the antiquity of patriarchy — pressed hardest as McLennan's matriarchal thesis was tested against new ethnographic cases such as William Robertson Smith's *Kinship and Marriage in Early Arabia*. [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]]'s 1885 review of the wider Maine–McLennan dispute judged that "converging research" had by then established the "early general prevalence of the system of kinship on the female side" — a verdict Stocking notes collapsed within a decade (Westermarck's 1891 counter-argument; Tylor's own 1896 acknowledgment of a "reaction" against matriarchal priority). Stocking also records that McLennan's own contemporaries were divided on his central claim: [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]] treated polyandry as merely an "exceptional phenomenon" rather than a necessary universal stage, and [[spencer-herbert|Spencer]] doubted the theory "taken as a whole," preferring endogamy and exogamy as independent, coexisting adaptations to tribal hostility rather than stages in a single sequence. Morgan's terminological approach eventually prevailed institutionally over McLennan's marriage-law approach as it was carried forward by Fison, Howitt, and Rivers — consistent with the Curator's note below on which half of the original dispute survived.

Stocking also cautions against a purely ideological reading of the Maine–McLennan matriarchy dispute: a revisionist feminist-historiographical position (which he cites but does not adopt outright) holds that the Victorian "matriarchal" orthodoxy was a conscious ideological construction, engineered to make patriarchy the evolutionary *goal* rather than the *origin*, once Maine's own attempt to prove patriarchy's timelessness had failed on the evidence. Stocking's counter-position stresses the complexity of actual intellectual influence: Johann [[bachofen-johann-jakob|Bachofen]]'s *Mutterrecht* (1861), independently credited with the "matriarchy" thesis, was neither a response to Maine nor a stimulus to McLennan (who did not read it until 1866), and Maine himself treated women's growing legal equality as a genuine "law of development" and permitted feminist organizations to circulate his work — complications that, on Stocking's account, do not fit a simple story of coordinated patriarchal defense.

## Curator's note

`resolution_status: partially-resolved`. Twentieth-century anthropology broadly vindicated Morgan's core methodological claim — that **kinship terminologies are systematic and carry historical/structural information** (retained and rebuilt by Rivers, Kroeber, Radcliffe-Brown, Lévi-Strauss, Trautmann) — against McLennan's dismissal of them as mere modes of address. But it rejected the substance both men were arguing toward: Morgan's group-marriage stages and McLennan's primitive-promiscuity-to-polyandry sequence are alike discredited as universal history (see [[unilinear-kinship-evolution-debate]]). So the terminological method survived; the evolutionary narratives on both sides did not. Recorded as the wiki's dated reading, not an adjudication of the original dispute.
