# Power and Agency in the New Institutionalism

An intra-program dispute, unusually well documented because the program's own charter anthology ([[powell-dimaggio-new-institutionalism-1991]], 1991) staged it. At issue: whether [[new-institutionalism]] can account for interests, power, and institutional change, or whether its signature move — explaining organizational similarity by legitimacy-seeking conformity — writes agents out of the story.

## Positions

**Isomorphism/homogenization ([[dimaggio-paul|DiMaggio]] & [[powell-walter-w|Powell]] 1983).** As [[organizational-field|fields]] become structured, organizations grow more similar through coercive, mimetic, and normative [[institutional-isomorphism|isomorphism]]. Early institutional work deliberately "defocalized" actors; DiMaggio (1988) noted this was purposeful, because "undue focus on actors has seemed to impair the production of sociology."

**Diversity under centralized authority ([[scott-w-richard|Scott]] & [[meyer-john-w|Meyer]]).** Scott (ch. 7) explicitly flags the divergence: "under some conditions, more highly structured organizational environments may create increased diversity of form" — centralized decision makers create specialized forms by design. Scott's seven-mechanism typology (imposition, authorization, inducement, acquisition, imprinting, incorporation, bypassing) is offered as a corrective to treating isomorphism as a master hypothesis.

**Power-based field theory ([[fligstein-neil|Fligstein]], ch. 13).** Institutional theories "have overestimated the role of norms in the construction of organizational fields and underestimated the relative power of actors in organizations." Fields are "a construction of powerful organizations… based on the interests of those organizations"; change requires shocks plus actors with both new interpretations and power.

**Interests and origins critique (Brint & Karabel, ch. 14).** "Our difficulties with the new institutionalism… have less to do with its tenets than with its silences": the program explains institutional form and functioning but not origins and transformations. Their community-college study grounds an account in organizational self-interest, "anticipatory subordination," and group struggle, in the lineage of Selznick, Michels, and Weber.

**Purposive institution-building (Galaskiewicz, ch. 12).** Against the portrait of organizations as "passive, reacting" entities: field leaders "can act purposively (albeit under conditions of bounded rationality) to construct and create institutions."

**Microfoundations first ([[zucker-lynne|Zucker]], ch. 4 postscript).** The macro strands confound institutionalization with resource-dependent strategic adaptation; only micro-level cognitive measures keep the construct analytically independent.

## Shared ground

All positions accept the core anti-utilitarian premise — that institutionalized rules shape actors and interests ([[rationalized-myth]], [[institutionalization]]) — and all were published together under the editors' own framing, which conceded the unresolved questions (intentionality, interest, micro-to-macro order) openly in the introduction. DiMaggio's own ch. 11 museums study argues institutionalization "bears… openings for substantial change," partially internalizing the critique.

## Status

Open as of the ingested source (1991). The volume's editors treated the power/agency gap as the program's research frontier rather than a refutation; later programs in this collection ([[strategic-action-fields-theory]]) built directly on the power-based position.
