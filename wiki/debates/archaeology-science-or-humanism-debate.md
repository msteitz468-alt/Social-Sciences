# Is Archaeology a Science, a Technique, or a Humanistic Discipline?

A standing dispute over what *kind* of knowledge archaeology produces and whether its
identity lies in its **techniques**, in **natural-science explanation**, or in **humane
interpretation**. The version recorded here is the mid-20th-century exchange visible in
[[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]]'s *[[wheeler-archaeology-from-the-earth-1954|Archaeology from
the Earth]]* (1954); it is a direct precursor of the later processual vs. post-processual
debate.

## Positions

**Archaeology as method and technique.** Walter W. Taylor (*A Study of Archeology*, 1948)
held, in the words Wheeler quotes, that "archaeology per se is no more than a method and a
set of specialized techniques for the gathering of cultural information… the archaeologist,
as archaeologist, is really nothing but a technician" — the substantive discipline being
anthropology/history, to which archaeology supplies data (position, Taylor per Wheeler
1954).

**Archaeology as a humanistic discipline employing scientific technique.** Wheeler
denounced that view "as nonsense," arguing the archaeologist "is primarily a fact-finder,
but his facts are the material records of human achievement; he is also, by that token, a
humanist," whose task is "revivifying or humanizing his materials with a controlled
imagination." He endorsed O. G. S. Crawford's formula — "archaeology is an art which employs
a scientific technique" — and held that archaeology "very properly adapts and adopts the
methods of natural science" without itself being a science "in the class-room meaning of the
term" ("at the best it is a very inexact science") (position, Wheeler 1954; Crawford 1953).

**Archaeology as (inexact) natural science.** A third position, which Wheeler resisted,
would model social and cultural change on organic/biological law — the tendency he
associated with Oswald Spengler's treatment of civilizations as "living beings of the
highest order," and against which he cited Henri Frankfort ("to take the biological metaphor
literally… is not morphology but mythology"), [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] (diffusion
distinguishes social from organic evolution), and Julian Huxley (position, various per
Wheeler 1954).

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

All sides agree that archaeology depends on rigorous technique and increasingly borrows from
the natural sciences, and that its evidence is material. They divide on what that entails:
the technique view infers that archaeology has no autonomous interpretive object; the
humanist view infers that technique serves a humane reconstruction that art and philosophy
inform; the scientific view infers that cultural change is law-governed and explanatorily
tractable. [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] occupies a middle register in Wheeler's text —
skeptical that "motives… are in fact hardly capable of genuine historical study," yet
insisting on the disanalogy between social and organic evolution.

## Current Standing

The dispute did not resolve; it re-erupted as **processual archaeology** (Binford and
others, from the 1960s) revived the science/explanation pole against culture-historical
description, and again as **post-processual archaeology** (Hodder and others, from the
1980s) revived interpretation and meaning against processual positivism. Wheeler's 1954
humanism and Taylor's 1948 technicism are early statements of the two poles. A dated
curator's note: this page rests on a single source (Wheeler 1954) reporting the exchange
from his own side; the positions of Taylor, Crawford, and the processualists should be
densified from their own works when ingested, and the page relinked to processual/
post-processual theory pages once those exist.

## The Later "Science Wars" Phase (Johnson 2011, attributed)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Johnson's *Archaeological Theory*]] (2011) reports a
later, more explicitly philosophical phase of the same underlying dispute. David Clarke
argued that use of scientific techniques "no more make archaeology into a science than a
wooden leg turns a man into a tree" (Clarke 1978: 465), against Jacquetta Hawkes's
traditional/humanist position that "however scientific the methods employed, the final
aims are historical" (Hawkes 1968: 257). Johnson lists two intellectually serious
objections to a positivist science-of-archaeology: (1) the past is dead and unobservable
directly, compromising testing (though geology and astronomy face analogous problems);
and (2) human behaviour is purposive and intentional, involving ideas with no direct
physical observability, making archaeological interpretation inherently hermeneutic
rather than strictly scientific.

**Positivism as political control.** Shanks and Tilley (late 1980s) argued the
theory/method separation central to **[[positivism]]** is fraudulent, since theory and
method cannot be separated in practice, and characterized positivism as "an archaeology
of NO" enforcing rules of allowable discourse: "as long as you conform to the rules of
positivist/empiricist discourse… the epistemology … police are waiting" (Shanks and
Tilley 1992: 23).

**Wylie's middle path.** Alison Wylie combined acknowledgment that science is inevitably
value-laden with an account of how archaeologists move empirically between data and
interpretation in practice — offered as a route beyond the "science wars" either/or.

**Johnson's own editorial assessment** (attributed; register note — this is the
textbook author's curatorial voice, not a documented convergence to be promoted to wiki
voice): Johnson states the "science wars" framing was, in his view and others', a false
either/or, and that processualists moved toward talk of "evaluation" rather than strict
"testing" while postprocessualists affirmed that data still constrain interpretation — a
convergence of tone rather than a resolution.

Johnson also notes parallel disputes elsewhere in the social sciences feeding this
debate's intellectual background: Comte and Durkheim's positivist sociological method
aiming at a predictive science of society, opposed by Anthony Giddens and others arguing
against the possibility of a neutral, value-free science of society; and some feminist
scholars' argument that science's claimed objectivity masks specifically male bias. See
[[sociology-of-knowledge]] for the broader philosophy-of-science background (Kuhn,
Feyerabend, social constructivism) that this debate draws on.
