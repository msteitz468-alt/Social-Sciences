# Land Nationalisation vs Municipalisation

Dispute within Russian Social-Democracy (peak articulation 1906–08) over the **form of land tenure** after confiscation of landlord estates in the bourgeois-democratic revolution: full [[land-nationalisation]] versus [[municipalisation]] of confiscated private lands with private allotments preserved — and secondary options of **division** for private property. Economic hinge: acceptance or repudiation of Marx’s [[absolute-rent]]. Political hinge: centralised peasant revolution and democratisation of the **whole** state versus local self-government as supposed bulwark.

**Source note (asymmetry):** this page is built from [[lenin-collected-works-vol-13-1962]] (*The Agrarian Programme of Social-Democracy in the First Russian Revolution, 1905–1907* and related 1908 pieces). Opponents’ positions are recorded in their **strongest form as reconstructed from Lenin’s quotations and paraphrases**, not from independent Maslov/Plekhanov/John corpuses. That asymmetry is a reliability fact, not a verdict.

## Positions

### Position A — Lenin: nationalisation of all land

[[lenin-vladimir]] argued that Russia’s bourgeois agrarian evolution has **two paths** — Prussian (landlord latifundia → Junker capitalism; Stolypin/Cadet line) and American (break-up of latifundia; free farmers) — and that only **nationalisation** fully expresses the peasant revolutionary path:

- **Economic content:** abolish **all** medieval landownership (landlord **and** allotment); transfer rent to the state; abolish absolute rent; free investment of capital; “unfencing” for free commercial agriculture; freest mobilisation of land.
- **Mass evidence (as Lenin reads 1905–07):** Peasant Union, First and Second Dumas, Bill of the 104 — peasant deputies demand nationalisation, including of their **own** lands; homestead gubernias also signed; not commune fetishism but free bourgeois farming.
- **Against “high capitalism only”:** nationalisation is condition **for** rapid capitalist development in agriculture, not merely its late effect; Marx allowed/advocated nationalisation in young bourgeois societies (Germany 1848, America 1846 contexts as Lenin cites); pure nationalisation rare but analogues in young democracies/Homestead-type measures.
- **Politics:** scopes of agrarian and political revolution the same; republic, elective officials, abolish standing army; revolutionary peasant committees on the spot; victory = revolutionary-democratic dictatorship of proletariat and peasantry — municipalisation incompatible with that real victory.
- **Restoration:** after nationalisation, restoration must smash millions of new farms; relative “guarantee” = deepest possible revolution, not paper municipal title. No absolute guarantee except socialist revolution in the West.
- **Socialism’s destiny** not tied to either bourgeois outcome; programme still nationalisation for best bourgeois conditions for the [[proletariat]].

### Position B — Municipalisation (Maslov / Menshevik Stockholm line)

Strongest form of the municipalisers’ case (as assembled from Lenin’s report of their arguments):

- **Fear of peasant Vendée:** nationalising allotments alienates the peasantry; exempt peasant land; municipalise only confiscated landlord estates.
- **Local democracy as bulwark:** large local self-governing bodies hold land against restoration and reaction; more flexible for regional diversity and “process of the revolution.”
- **Rent for the people:** municipal rent funds local needs (unemployed, model farms, crisis leases); nationalisation risks rent for army/navy under bourgeois central power (Maslov *Obrazovaniye* line as Lenin quotes).
- **Theoretical premise (Maslov):** repudiation of absolute rent — private landownership has no special economic significance under capitalism beyond who receives differential rent; therefore dual private/public tenure is coherent.
- **Plekhanov’s restoration key:** no absolute guarantee against restoration exists; programme must minimise harm; nationalisation does not eliminate economic basis of tsarism and is “anti-revolutionary”; municipalisation “will not surrender the land” to the old order.
- **Kostrov / John:** nationalisation isolates SD from peasantry; municipalisation more practical.
- **International analogy:** “municipal socialism” / Western local reforms as progressive content.

### Position C — Division for private property (Shanin, Finn, Borisov)

- Private ownership compatible with partial reform and technical progress (Shanin, Ireland example) — but Lenin: that is Stolypin path unless paired with confiscation without compensation in revolution.
- Free of municipalisers’ dualism and rent revision, yet (Lenin) wrong **moment**: over-estimate maturity of capitalist agriculture; division based on present allotments preserves half the medieval system; mass of peasants **now** demand nationalisation.
- Borisov: nationalisation when Russia is no longer a peasant country; programme must not bind SD to a particular form of economy during break-up — Lenin: that actually favours nationalisation flexibility.

### Position D — Peasant / Trudovik nationalisation (mass platform)

Not an SD faction, but the programme Lenin claims municipalisers failed to match:

- National land fund; local self-government by universal suffrage managing the fund within law (Bill of the 104, Clause 16 — Lenin: clearer anti-bureaucratic principles than Maslov’s “democratic principles” wording).
- Leave allotments and small holdings with owners pending gradual nationalisation; free farming on nationalised land; state loans for equipment.
- Spontaneous revolutionism against medieval landownership without always clear political conditions; idealisation of “promised land” of bourgeois freedom.
- Narodnik “labour principle”/equalisation = ideological cloak of progressive struggle against feudal latifundia (Lenin’s dual critique: do not absolute-ise as non-bourgeois order; do not miss progressive content).

## Current status

`resolution_status: open` — this is a **historical** dispute whose later political outcomes (1917 land decree, Soviet land law, etc.) are **not** adjudicated here from this source alone. Within the 1907–08 SD frame, Lenin treats the debate as decided **for nationalisation** by economic theory (absolute rent), Duma/peasant experience, and the two-path analysis; municipalisers and Plekhanov treated Stockholm dualism and restoration guarantees as decisive. The wiki records both (and divisionist/Trudovik) positions without converting Lenin’s polemic into wiki-voice victory.

**Curator note (2026-07-16):** Evidence base for opponents is Lenin-mediated; promote independent Maslov/Plekhanov texts when ingested. Do not collapse “municipalisation” into Western municipal socialism or into Kautsky’s large-estate municipal farming sense without attribution.
