# Agricultural Origins Causation Debate

What caused the shift from hunting-gathering to agriculture at the start of the [[holocene|Holocene]]? [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) frames this as a genuine, unresolved dispute between environmental and non-environmental explanations, and argues that no single model fits all regions.

## Shared factual ground

- The [[agricultural-revolution|transition to farming]] occurred independently in several regions (Near East, Mesoamerica, China, probably highland New Guinea and parts of Africa) within a few millennia of the Holocene's onset, with different domesticate packages (see [[domestication]]).
- It was gradual (a transitional proto-agricultural stage), not a sudden "revolution," despite Childe's term.
- Early-Holocene climate changed substantially in most of these regions.

## Positions

**Environmental-determinist / oasis model.** In its strongest early form, V. Gordon [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s (1928) oasis hypothesis: post-glacial desiccation concentrated humans, plants, and animals around shrinking water sources, forcing domestication. A refined climatic version holds that [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]] resource decline stimulated intensive management preceding domestication (Moore & Hillman 1992; see the [[natufian-younger-dryas-debate]]).

**Opportunist model.** Environmental *amelioration* — rising [[sea-level-change|sea levels]], expanding wild cereal stands — made new resources available that were opportunistically exploited (e.g. the marine shift at Franchthi Cave).

**Demographic / social model.** Binford (1968) and Cohen (1977): environmental change alone is insufficient, since comparable opportunities existed in earlier interstadials without being taken; population dynamics and social factors are the active variables.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Roberts' key empirical checks (attributed):
- The oasis model is **falsified for the Near East**: Lake Zeribar pollen (van Zeist) shows *increasing* early-Holocene moisture, the opposite of Childe's predicted desiccation.
- It **fits the African case better**: Sahelian-Sudanian cultivation followed Saharan [[desertification]] after ~5500 years ago.
- It **fails for Mesoamerica**: cultivation began in central Mexico, *not* in the region of most severe desiccation (the northwest) — an explicit anomaly against determinism.

## Curator's note (2026-07-10)

Roberts' own position is multi-causal and anti-determinist: environmental change is a real contributor whose weight varies by region and must be demonstrated, not assumed — consistent with the wiki's treatment of [[environmental-determinism]]. The debate remains open.

## Cauvin position — ideology / revolution of symbols (2000)

*(Attributed — [[cauvin-jacques]], *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000]]* 2000; do not adjudicate.)*

| Position holder | Causal claim (as of Cauvin 2000) |
|---|---|
| **Cauvin** | **[[revolution-of-symbols]]** among Khiamian foragers installs new religion (Goddess + Bull) ~9500 BC; farming ~9000 BC is second step. Stratigraphy inverts materialist cause→effect order. Ecological and purely demographic prime movers rejected for the Levantine origin. |
| **Cauvin vs Childe** | Grants oriental origins of European Neolithic; rejects simplified historical materialism / environmental determinism as explanation of domestication itself. |
| **Cauvin vs Braidwood** | Reinterprets “Culture was not ready” as mental/religious readiness and *desire* to intervene in nature, not only techno-economic lag in the nuclear zone. |
| **Cauvin vs population-pressure** | PPNA large villages without pan-Near-Eastern demographic expansion; labour organisation and social regrouping precede population-pressure explanations. |
| **Cauvin on climate (Postscript)** | Younger Dryas ~10,600 BC (not 11,000) at Abu Hureyra; moderate impact; not important causal trigger of Neolithic Revolution — drives abandonment of desertifying zones. |
| **Softened uptake** | Many later writers accept **ideology-before-economy** as hypothesis while rejecting mother-goddess cult label (e.g. Mithen on Göbekli/Jerf). |

## Scott position — *Against the Grain* (2017)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017. Political-scientist synthesis; not primary archaeobotany. Recorded as a position, not adjudicated.)*

| Claim cluster | Scott’s stance (as of 2017) |
|---|---|
| **Magic-moment Neolithic** | Rejected: no single fateful line from foraging to agriculture or savagery to civilization; planting is one event in deep fire-mediated niche construction from *Homo erectus* onward |
| **Tempo / lag** | ~4,000 years between key domestications and first agrarian economies based on them; ancestors did not rush into the Neolithic or earliest states |
| **Boserup “backs-to-the-wall”** | Recounted then **rejected for Mesopotamia / Fertile Crescent**: cultivation arose in abundance not scarcity; flood-retreat agriculture may invalidate pure “great toil” premise; theory “in tatters” without satisfactory replacement for why cultivation spread |
| **Why farm at all?** | Fixed-field farming’s huge drudgery vs forager labor (even in poor environments ~half time subsistence); Abu Hureyra researchers: no foragers in productive multi-seasonal locales would willingly cultivate staples; Younger Dryas + restricted mobility “pistol” **hotly contested** — Scott declines to adjudicate multi-millennial drivers |
| **Broad-spectrum then agriculture** | Accepts BSR archaeological reality; jury out on its causes/consequences; for agriculture proper 3–4 millennia later “the jury is in”: population pressure, restricted mobility of sedentists, large-game decline, deliberate **avoidance** of planting/stock as dominant mode because of work defending simplified landscapes |
| **Wetland / sequence** | Sedentism and early towns from wetland abundance, not irrigation-state package; classical “making the desert bloom by irrigation invents sedentism/state” narrative “totally wrong” for southern alluvium (Pournelle) |
| **Progress narrative** | Initial agriculture worsened well-being relative to foraging; multi-millennial process not civilizational Rubicon — densified under [[agricultural-revolution]], [[sedentism]], [[domestication]] |

**Relation to other positions on this page:** Scott is **anti-stadial** and **anti-ascent-of-man**, multi-causal by refusal of single prime movers, closer to demographic/avoidance-of-drudgery logics than to oasis desiccation or Cauvin ideology-before-economy (he does not engage Cauvin’s Goddess–Bull program as such). Wetland-abundance early sedentism is an empirical/position challenge to irrigation-state coupling more than a full rival “why plant?” theory. Adjacent (not identical) anti-linear themes: [[dawn-of-everything-debate]] — do not merge Scott with Graeber/Wengrow.

## Bogucki position — late-forager social transformation (1999)

*(Attributed — [[bogucki-peter|Bogucki]], *[[bogucki-origins-human-society-1999|The Origins of Human Society]]* 1999, Ch.5. World-prehistory synthesis; date-stamp regional sequences and genetics as of 1999. Do not adjudicate.)*

| Claim cluster | Bogucki’s stance (as of 1999) |
|---|---|
| **Rejects** | “Eureka” discovery of seed planting; Childe–Pumpelly oasis/desiccation as general cause; single-factor population push as stated; sedentism as *direct* cause of farming; irreversible one-way commitment everywhere |
| **Endorses / promising** | Multi-causal intentional consensus of 1990s; [[hayden-brian|Hayden]] competitive-feasting / social models (wide inferential gap but not dismissable); agriculture as part of late-forager social transformation, not a flash of insight |
| **Core mechanism** | Restructuring of [[pleistocene-band-society]] — especially **defection from sharing norms** — into sedentary **proto-households** that internalize risk/reward; sense of place and property as critical prerequisites |
| **Commitment ≠ initial use** | Initial cultivation/domestication distinct from long-run household commitment under risk and uncertainty; frontiers show mixed strategies |
| **Gender** | Labor reorganization under farming (women’s field/household load emphasized in synthesis) — not a “who invented agriculture” credit story |
| **Centers vs non-centers** | Pristine multi-regional centers (Near East, East Asia, Mesoamerica, eastern North America) + Harlan non-centers; Europe largely package dispersal / indigenous adoption debates, not independent founder-crop domestication |

**Relation to other positions:** Aligns with multi-causal and social/demographic clusters against oasis determinism; shares Hayden social-surplus demand with aggrandizer literature; overlaps Scott on long lag and non-magic-moment framing without Scott’s state-grain political thesis. Bridges to [[origins-of-hereditary-inequality-debate]] via the same household apparatus (Ch.6). See [[domestication]], [[sedentism]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[household-archaeology]].

See also [[original-affluent-society-debate]], [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]], [[neolithic-revolution]].

## Bellwood position — layered causation then dispersal (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; linked program [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]]. Do not adjudicate.)*

Bellwood’s contribution on this page is mainly about **why agriculture begins** (Ch. 1–3 synthesis) and secondarily about the **second revolutionary step** — spread beyond homelands — which he treats as historically as important as domestication itself. He does **not** offer a single prime-mover “why plant?” theory.

| Claim cluster | Bellwood’s stance (as of 2005) |
|---|---|
| **One-line explanation** | Rejected: causes must be **“layered in time”** — sequential reinforcing factors: prior sedentism, affluence/choice, co-evolution, environmental change/periodic stress, population pressure, suitable candidates for domestication |
| **Holocene climate** | Ultimate **enabler** (stabilization ~11,500 ya denser wild foods → sedentism/population → “competitive ratchet,” Richerson et al. 2001), **not** sole proximate cause of every regional transition — world did not all farm in 9500 BC |
| **Childe oasis / propinquity** | Classic desiccation story wrong as stated (domestication took off in **wetter early Holocene**); Younger Dryas drought stress means Childe “could have been partly right” / “not too far off the mark” |
| **Demographic packing** | Cohen / Smith / Boserup and Binford–Flannery marginal-zone packing recorded as serious positions; cultivation likeliest at **edges** of wild plant ranges |
| **Social competition** | Competitive feasting, status, valuables (Hayden, Cowgill) in rich environments allowing storage against sharing ethic — listed among layered factors |
| **Sedentism precondition** | Key but hard to prove; rare outside Levant/SE Turkey, Jomon Japan (which **resisted** farming), Sudan, perhaps central Mexico and N Andes |
| **Rindos co-evolution** | Better fit tuber/fruit New Guinea/Amazon than explosive cereal SW Asia/China/Mesoamerica; does not explain historical Rubicon |
| **Cauvin revolution of symbols** | Recorded among hard-to-falsify immediate precedents (female figurines, bulls); not adopted as Bellwood’s prime mover |
| **Younger Dryas as Levant trigger** | Widely favored engine; challenged by Tchernov 1997 and Cauvin 2000; if YD was the trigger, “gun took quite a while to go off” — widespread domesticates 500–1,000 years after YD and Natufian end |
| **Multi-regional origins** | Primary independent foci: Fertile Crescent SW Asia; Yangzi/Yellow China; New Guinea highlands; tropical Americas; Eastern Woodlands USA; *possible* Sahel/West Africa and southern India. Levant model unlikely to fit other regions unmodified |
| **Americas “why”** | Fit popular risk-minimization / packing / circumscription models (Carneiro 1970; Flannery 1986), but precise chronologies/environments obscure; if dependence postdates ~6–5 kya, direct Late Pleistocene–Early Holocene environmental causation hard to sustain |
| **Against continuum / chameleon HG–farming** | Hard to document free switching outside short Oceanic reverse adaptations; Old World ethnographic atlas shows sharp bimodal HG vs farming dependence |
| **HG unilateral adoption** | Few ethnographic cases of eager successful full agricultural adoption imparting expansionary success; encapsulation catch-22 — relevant more to **spread** than first origins |
| **Second revolutionary aspect** | After ~2,000 years SW Asian gestation, PPNB expansion and post-7000 BC Levant reconfiguration = “second, major stage of the Agricultural Revolution” — dispersal into NE Africa, Europe, central Asia, Indus |

**Relation to other positions on this page:** Closest to **multi-causal** (Roberts-style) and **demographic/social** layers without discarding environmental stress/affluence alternation; partially rehabilitates Childe via Younger Dryas rather than post-glacial desiccation; treats Cauvin ideology-before-economy as a Levantine position, not global prime mover; **orthogonal to Scott 2017** state/avoidance synthesis (Bellwood’s load-bearing stakes are comparative farming/language *dispersal* after origins); overlaps Bogucki on multi-causal late-forager social factors and Hayden feasting without adopting Bogucki’s household-defection apparatus as the core origin mechanism. Population dispersal is a **symptom of prior causes**, not an external prime mover — but once agriculture is established, demic expansion is his main explainer of continental language-family maps (see [[farming-spread-europe-debate]], [[demic-diffusion]]).

## Barker position — multi-causal contingency (2006)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006; Ch. 1 historiography + Ch. 10 synthesis. Do not adjudicate.)*

Barker’s global comparative synthesis treats the transition as **historically contingent decisions by foragers** facing perceived risks and opportunities **without hindsight**. He explicitly rejects progress ladders and “talent-spotting” narratives that cast foragers as half-knowing they were on the road to farming (against Bruce Smith’s casting-director metaphor as he reads it).

| Claim cluster | Barker’s stance (as of 2006) |
|---|---|
| **One-line explanation** | Rejected. Table 10.1 (via Gebauer & Price 1992) inventories dozens of proposed causes — climatic, demographic, social, ideological, “lunatic fringe”; general theorizing must take **multiple underpinning themes** together |
| **Agency without teleology** | Decisions often aimed to **preserve** a forager way of life under stress, not deliberately transform it (Rowley-Conwy on Mesolithic: “we know that agriculture was to appear… They didn’t”) |
| **Diamond “no invention”** | Endorses Diamond 1997: food production evolved as by-product of decisions made without awareness of consequences — not discovery/invention from supermarket hindsight |
| **Domestication ontology** | Domestication as **commensalism/mutualism** (O’Connor; plants “unconsciously domesticated” by many animals — Diamond); human uniqueness overstated; many failed domestication experiments (gazelle, Barbary sheep, N American floodplain weeds) |
| **Pleistocene pre-adaptation** | Widespread Pleistocene “interventionist” landscape relations presage husbandry; yet **no** Palaeolithic theistic cosmologies comparable to Neolithic — animism persists until farming cosmologies |
| **Holocene timing** | Many more societies than usually allowed began forms of plant/animal husbandry at or soon after the Pleistocene–Holocene boundary, **independently at regional scale**, in SW Asia, S Asia, E Asia, ISEA, Americas, N Africa |
| **Climate + intellect** | Aligns with Layton/Mithen-style coincidence: modern human creativity + rare early-Holocene climatic niche — not a single environmental prime mover |
| **Ideology** | Farming often co-develops with shift animism/totemism → theism: sky gods, land as controlled ancestor demanding favours, anxiety over cosmic order and sexuality (Martin; Hodder *domus* recorded; Cauvin ideology-first treated as Levantine thesis to engage, not global law) |
| **Against demic-primary** | Major problem with demic diffusion (Bellwood 2004; Diamond 1997): treats transition as unique sequence of farmer movements in an **otherwise static** world; mobility and exchange (including millets China→Europe group-to-group) are normal for modern humans |
| **Europe / IE words** | Farming lexicon in IE may mean speaking proto-IE was part of *being a farmer* across Ireland–Indus via exchange networks, not proof of SW Asian colonist wave (Haak et al. 2005 / Richards et al. 1996 genetics as he reads them: little Neolithic genetic input — **as of mid-2000s**) |

**Relation to other positions:** Closest to **multi-causal** (Roberts) and **Bogucki** late-forager social transformation without adopting a single household-defection apparatus; records **Hayden/Bender** prestige-feasting and **Cauvin** ideology as regional options; **challenges** [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]] / [[demic-diffusion]] as primary global mechanism while accepting mobility and exchange; orthogonal to Scott’s state-grain thesis. Pre-aDNA genetic numbers are **as of Barker 2006** and partly superseded — keep attributed.
