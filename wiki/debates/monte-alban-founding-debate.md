# The Founding of Monte Albán

Why was a new capital founded ca. 500 BC on a high, agriculturally marginal, waterless hill at the junction of the Valley of Oaxaca's three arms — and what kind of polity founded it? All parties share the settlement-survey facts: no prior occupation on the hill; sudden Early I population of ~5,000 (est.) growing to ~17,000 (Late I); simultaneous decline of [[san-jose-mogote]]; a catchment too poor to feed the city. The dispute is over the founding's political mechanism.

## Positions

**Disembedded capital / collective confederation** — [[blanton-richard|Blanton]] (1978) proposed Monte Albán as a *disembedded capital*: a new seat built on neutral ground by a confederation of formerly autonomous valley polities (compare Washington, D.C.), militarily motivated. The 2022 restatement ([[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|*Ancient Oaxaca* 2nd ed.]]) develops this through [[collective-action-theory]]: founding as a cooperative enterprise under external threat — voluntary in-migration from formerly separate polities, three nucleated barrios without internal walls or elite segregation, cooperative terrace/canal labor, a universalizing Cocijo ideology replacing older factional dual symbolism, and "faceless" early governance without ruler-glorifying monuments. Blanton et al. (2022) reject agricultural-locational counterarguments as "clearly inaccurate," reporting that of ~2,000 mapped terraces only a few were agricultural (Feinman & Nicholas 1990; Kowalewski 1982; Nicholas 1989).

**Chiefly relocation / synoikism led by San José Mogote** — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] and [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] (1996) read the founding as a defensive [[synoikism]] led by the San José Mogote chiefly elite (an Etla-headed confederacy), continuous with the Rosario-phase chiefdom's leadership — elite agency and inter-chiefdom warfare, not a neutral-ground compact among equals.

**Dominant-polity / agrarian-locational critique** — Sanders, Santley, Nichols, and Willey (1978–1988) rejected the disembedded-capital thesis, arguing a single dominant agrarian polity unified the valley and that the hill's piedmont setting offered agricultural (terrace/irrigation) advantages. Blanton's rebuttals: no Period I palace known; the site sits >4 km from good alluvium; the terraces were residential.

**Conquest-driven expansionist state** — Spencer and Redmond, from excavations at [[san-martin-tilcajete]] and the [[cuicatlan-canada|Cuicatlán Cañada]], emphasize coercion: Tilcajete's El Mogote burned in Early I, its successor complex at El Palenque destroyed, and extra-valley territories subjugated — the Monte Albán polity as expansionist and militarist from early on. Blanton et al. (2022) accept the conquest evidence but treat holdout subjugation and external tribute as developments compatible with (and, in Period II, corrosive of) an initially more collective formation.

## Shared ground

All positions accept defensive/military motivation, the survey demography, and Zapotec-region cultural continuity; none claims the founding was purely peaceful or purely coerced. The disagreement concerns whose agency (confederate communities vs. chiefly elite vs. a dominant polity) and which mechanism (cooperation vs. conquest) predominated.
