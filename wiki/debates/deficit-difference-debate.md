# Deficit vs. Difference: Language, Class, and Educability

A dispute over how to interpret class-patterned differences in speech and their
consequences for schooling, arising from [[bernstein-basil|Basil Bernstein]]'s code
theory and its reception. Positions recorded here in their strongest form, without
adjudication; see the Shared Factual Ground section for what all sides accept.

## Positions

### 1. Deficit / code-restriction reading (a popular reading of Bernstein, which Bernstein himself explicitly disputed)

Some readers and popularizers of Bernstein's work took the restricted/elaborated
code distinction to mean that working-class children socialized into a restricted
code suffer a linguistic or cognitive *deficiency* relative to middle-class children
socialized into an elaborated code, and that this deficiency explains their lower
school "educability" (verbal IQ scores, abstraction, academic performance). This
reading fed "verbal deprivation" framings in education policy.

**Bernstein's own position is distinct from this popular reading and explicitly
denies it.** In "A Sociolinguistic Approach to Socialization; with Some Reference to
Educability" (1972), Bernstein states his "functional notions are entirely
independent of 'ungrammatical' or substandard features... it is orientation and use
of communicative modes that is in question, not fundamental capacity for logical
analysis and conceptual thought." He explicitly cites Labov (1969a) approvingly as
support for this denial. Bernstein's actual claim: children limited to a restricted
code, primarily because of the subculture and role systems of family, community, and
work, face "a major problem of educability whose source lies not in the genetic code
but in the culturally determined communication code" — i.e., a cultural/institutional
mismatch between home code and the elaborated code the school demands, not an
innate or linguistic deficiency. Bernstein insists the restricted code "should not be
disvalued," attributes each code its own "vast potential... considerable metaphoric
range and unique esthetic capacity," and calls for the school (not the child or
family) to adapt: "we must ensure that the material conditions of the schools we
offer... are refracted through an understanding of the culture these children bring
to the school." He links restricted-code prevalence to class only as a hedged
tendency ("broadly speaking... we could expect"), not an identity claim, and
explicitly declines to extend his family-role-system account to the very different
US case of Black Americans' speech, citing his own lack of expertise on that
context.

Even editors Gumperz and Hymes flag the interpretive risk within the same volume:
"A dichotomy such as elaborated and restricted codes lends itself to dichotomous
thinking. Bernstein warns against it, but the point needs to be emphasized."

### 2. Difference position (Labov)

William Labov's "The Logic of Nonstandard English" (1969) argues that nonstandard
and African-American English speakers command a full, logically complete linguistic
system; what looks like "restricted" or deficient speech in formal interview
settings is an artifact of the interview situation itself (a asymmetrical,
intimidating context that suppresses fluency), not evidence of impoverished
linguistic or cognitive capacity. Per Gumperz's Introduction to the volume, Labov's
1969 finding is that children using a "restricted code" in school interviews prove
highly creative and effective communicators in culturally realistic, informal
settings — directly challenging any reading that ties restricted-code use to
class-based verbal-skill deficits. On this position, the deficit reading confuses
*code* (an artifact of situation and register) with *competence*, and confuses
class-linked test performance with underlying linguistic ability.

### 3. Interactional / indexicality reading (Gumperz, Garfinkel)

Per Gumperz's editorial framing (Introduction, and echoed via Garfinkel's concept of
indexicality), an alternative account holds that "elaborated" and "restricted" are
not fixed properties of speakers or classes at all, but different modes of signaling
and calling on shared background knowledge between interlocutors: interpretation of
any communicative act always depends on such background knowledge, and what looks
like code "restriction" may simply reflect a higher degree of shared context between
interactants rather than a lesser communicative repertoire. This reading relocates
the phenomenon from speaker-attribute to interactional-context, sidestepping the
deficit/difference frame's premise that code is primarily a property of the
individual or class.

## Shared Factual Ground

All positions agree that: (a) working-class and middle-class speech samples show
statistically patterned differences correlated with family type and social class
(Bernstein's own data); (b) formal, asymmetric elicitation settings (school
interviews, IQ-style tests) can suppress fluency and depress apparent verbal
performance regardless of underlying competence (a point Bernstein himself accepts
via his approving citation of Labov); (c) no position defended in this literature
holds that any group lacks the fundamental capacity for logical analysis or abstract
thought — the live dispute is over how much causal/institutional weight to give to
code differences in explaining school "educability" gaps, and over whether the
popular "deficit" label ever fairly represented Bernstein's own theory.

## Afterlife (summary level)

The "verbal deprivation" framing that some US educational programs (e.g.,
compensatory-education initiatives of the era) drew from a deficit reading of code
theory became a target of Labov's 1969 paper, which was aimed partly at correcting
readings of Bernstein's work as much as at the specific programs. This wiki does not
extend the afterlife account beyond what the ingested primary sources (Bernstein
1972, Gumperz's 1972 framing of Labov 1969) support; later reassessments of
Bernstein's reception are not yet documented from primary sources in this
collection.
