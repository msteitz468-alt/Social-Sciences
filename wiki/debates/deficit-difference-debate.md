# Deficit vs. Difference: Language, Class, and Educability

A dispute over how to interpret class- and race-patterned differences in speech and their consequences for schooling. Positions recorded in their strongest form, without adjudication. Primary wiki bases: Bernstein (1972) via Gumperz & Hymes; Labov *[[labov-language-inner-city-1972|Language in the Inner City]]* (1972), especially chs. 5–6 and the related study page [[labov-language-inner-city-study]].

## Positions

### 1. Verbal deprivation / cultural deficit (educational psychology programs)

**Bereiter & Engelmann (1966)** and associates (Urbana academically oriented preschool) held that many black four-year-olds arrive without a language adequate for school learning. Labov records their claims at full strength: communication by gestures and "badly connected" phrases (*They mine*, *Me got juice*); inability to ask questions; treatment "as if the children had no language at all"; characterization of the speech as "a basically nonlogical mode of expressive behavior" rather than an underdeveloped SE. Their program taught a limited engineered language; answers like *In the tree* (to *Where is the squirrel?*) were reprehended until the child produced the full sentence.

**Martin Deutsch** and associates advanced **cultural deprivation**: missing home factors (adult verbal interaction, abstract reasoning, long-range goals) explain school failure. Evidence often came from questionnaires about dinner-table talk and museum trips plus lab/school tests — Labov notes little direct observation of black home verbal interaction in that literature.

**Popular deficit reading of Bernstein** took restricted code as linguistic/cognitive deficiency explaining low educability. This fed Operation Head Start–era "repair the child" logic.

### 2. Bernstein's own code-difference-with-consequences position

**Bernstein's position is distinct from the popular deficit reading and explicitly denies innate deficiency.** In "A Sociolinguistic Approach to Socialization" (1972), he states functional notions are independent of "ungrammatical" or substandard features; orientation and use of communicative modes are at issue, not fundamental capacity for logical analysis. He cites Labov (1969) approvingly against deficiency readings. His claim: children limited to a restricted code (via family/community/work role systems) face a major educability problem whose source is the culturally determined communication code, not the genetic code — a **mismatch** with the elaborated code the school demands. He refuses to disvalue restricted code and declines to extend his family-role account to Black American speech for lack of expertise. Gumperz & Hymes warn that the elaborated/restricted dichotomy "lends itself to dichotomous thinking."

### 3. Labov's difference / anti-deficit / cultural-conflict position

[[labov-william|William Labov]] ("The Logic of Nonstandard English," 1969; *Language in the Inner City* ch. 5) argues:

1. **Verbal deprivation has no basis in social reality.** Black children in urban ghettos, he asserts, receive extensive verbal stimulation, hear more well-formed sentences than middle-class children, and participate fully in a highly verbal culture with the same basic vocabulary, conceptual capacity, and logic as other English speakers.
2. **Test monosyllables measure defense, not capacity.** Asymmetric school interviews (including, in one case, a skilled black Harlem-raised interviewer with eight-year-old Leon) produce the Bereiter pattern; the child has devices for saying nothing when "anything he says can literally be held against him."
3. **Vernacular logic and style.** Contrasting Jets member **Larry H.** with middle-class **Charles M.**, Labov presents nonstandard speech as concise and effective; middle-class elaborated style can be empty verbosity. Negative concord is not "illogical double negation" once BEV grammar is understood (see also ch. 4, Speedy's *It ain't no cat can't get in no coop*).
4. **Six-step fallacy chain** from deficit reasoning (formal silence → school failure cause → middle-class speech as learning prerequisite → dialect = logic capacity → mimicry as logic training → predicted academic gains). He rejects steps that equate dialect form with conceptual inability.
5. **School, not child.** Massive failure requires examining social and cultural obstacles and the school's inability to adjust; Head Start "designed to repair the child, rather than the school" inverts the problem.
6. **Reading and peer status (ch. 6).** Structural SE–BEV differences are largely rule extensions; unique BEV structures are few and "unlikely" to explain disastrous reading failure. Major cause: **cultural and political conflict**. Metropolitan Achievement scores for 46 street-group members/marginals: none above grade, almost none above fifth-grade reading regardless of grade; 32 nonmembers show more learning (11 on/above grade). Prestige in street culture (Miller 1958 focal concerns) does not reward school success.
7. **Teaching implication:** approach SE through knowledge of the child's system; EFL *methods* (not treating BEV as a foreign language) underline studying the native dialect as coherent communication.

### 4. Genetic escalation (Jensen 1969) as Labov frames it

**Arthur Jensen** (*Harvard Educational Review* 1969) argued compensatory programs failed as a group and that evidence is "less consistent with a strictly environmental hypothesis than with the genetic hypothesis," differentiating Level I (associative) vs Level II (conceptual) learning by class and race. Labov treats this as the endpoint deficit theory was "originally designed to avoid" yet leads to. He challenges Jensen's use of Heber SES–IQ tables (lowest-SES blacks estimated 42.9% IQ < 75): among 75 Harlem boys in low status categories, only three scored below 75, with retest explanations. He argues even basic grammatical rules require conceptual operations more complex than Jensen's object-sorting experiments, so Level II superiority claims would absurdly imply "primitive language" for large populations.

### 5. Interactional / indexicality reading (Gumperz, Garfinkel)

Per Gumperz's editorial framing, "elaborated" and "restricted" need not be fixed speaker or class attributes but modes of signaling and calling on shared background knowledge. What looks like restriction may reflect higher shared context. This relocates the phenomenon to interaction, sidestepping speaker-attribute deficit/difference premises.

## Shared Factual Ground

Recorded as shared across serious participants in these sources: (a) patterned class-linked speech differences exist (Bernstein's data; Labov's variables); (b) formal, asymmetric elicitation can suppress fluency (Labov's central methodological claim; Bernstein accepts via citing Labov); (c) none of Bernstein, Labov, or Gumperz holds that any group lacks fundamental capacity for logical thought; (d) urban black school reading averages lagged national norms by years in the late 1960s (Labov cites NYT school scores) — the dispute is **explanation and remedy**, not the existence of the achievement gap.

## Afterlife (summary level)

Labov's 1969/1972 chapter became the standard linguistic citation against verbal-deprivation programs and remains a reference point in AAVE education debates. Bernstein's reception continued to oscillate between deficit misreadings and difference-with-consequences readings. Later secondary reassessments of Bernstein, Head Start meta-analyses, and post-1970s AAVE pedagogy are **not yet** documented from primary sources in this collection beyond the Labov and Gumperz–Hymes bases.
