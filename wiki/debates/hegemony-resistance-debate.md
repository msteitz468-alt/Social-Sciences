# Hegemony vs. Resistance — Do Subordinate Groups Consent to Domination?

The core question: does ideological domination secure the *consent* (or at least the resigned acquiescence) of subordinate groups, or is apparent consent a performance masking dissent? The positions below are recorded in their strongest form; the fullest single statement of the anti-hegemony case is [[scott-domination-arts-resistance-1990|Scott 1990]], whose chapter 4 frames the whole dispute.

## Positions

### 1. Thick hegemony / false consciousness (Gramsci and successors)
[[gramsci-antonio|Gramsci]] argued (Prison Notebooks) that hegemony operates at the level of thought: the "active man-in-the-mass" has a practical consciousness uniting him with fellow workers but an inherited, contradictory "theoretical consciousness" producing "moral and political passivity." Dominant classes rule through consent manufactured in civil society, coercion held in reserve. Gaventa's Appalachian study (*Power and Powerlessness*, 1980) added a third dimension of power: elite control of media and socialization cultivates legitimating beliefs and a "culture of defeat." On this view, [[false-consciousness]] — subordinates believing in the values that subordinate them — is a real and central mechanism.

### 2. Thin hegemony — naturalization
A weaker claim, which Scott attributes to formulations in Bourdieu ("the naturalization of its own arbitrariness"), Giddens ("naturalization of the present"), and Willis ("a pervasive naturalism"): ideology need not persuade subordinates that the order is *just*, only that it is *inevitable*, producing resignation rather than consent. Scott noted a recurring slide in this literature (Bourdieu, Barrington Moore) from "inevitable" back to "just," re-fattening the thin theory.

### 3. Scott — performance without consent
Scott argued both versions fail. The thick theory cannot survive the record of slave, serf, and untouchable dissent ("orthopraxy without orthodoxy," per studies of caste cited against Moffatt's incorporation thesis); the thin theory cannot explain why subordinate groups so often *rebelled* on exaggerated estimates of their own power — "it is not the miasma of power and thralldom that requires explanation." Ritual compliance signals only that there is no realistic alternative to compliance. Genuine hegemony, Scott argued, requires stringent conditions almost never met: either a realistic prospect that subordinates will themselves rise to power, or total atomization and surveillance (his limit cases: Korean War POW camps, Milgram's laboratory). He inverted Gramsci: subordinates are *freer at the level of thought* (safe offstage) and *more constrained at the level of action* — hegemony misreads the constraint's location. Willis's own finding that the school's believing "conformists," not the cynical "lads," posed the deeper threat supports Scott's corollary: radicalism comes from those who took the promise seriously and were betrayed. Any hegemonic ideology's promises are a "symbolic Achilles heel" — critique within the hegemony (naive monarchism deployed strategically by Russian peasants, per Daniel Field; Mathiesen's Norwegian prisoners wielding institutional norms) is indeterminate between belief and manipulation.

### 4. Safety-valve theory of ritual reversal
A distinct sub-dispute: Gluckman, Turner, and others read rituals of reversal, carnival, and licensed aggression as tension-discharge mechanisms that stabilize order ("removed the stopper to stop the bottle being smashed" — Sales, quoted by Scott). Scott's rebuttal: experimental evidence (Berkowitz) shows displaced aggression does not reduce hostility; elites historically tried to *suppress* carnival and world-upside-down prints (Kunzle's evidence; Franco's carnival ban); and revolts repeatedly erupted from carnival itself (Le Roy Ladurie's *Carnival in Romans*, 1580). He proposed carnival is better seen as "an ambiguous political victory wrested from elites."

## Shared factual ground

All sides accept that public deference under domination is pervasive; that open revolt is rare; and that the archive over-represents official discourse. The dispute is over what the quiescence *means* — consent, resignation, or performance — and each side reads the same behavioral record through its answer.

## Status

Open. Scott's position became the reference point for 1990s–2000s resistance studies, itself criticized for romanticizing resistance and stretching the concept (a critique voiced within the resistance literature as much as against it). Neo-Gramscian and Bourdieusian traditions continue to defend versions of thin hegemony; the empirical indeterminacy Scott identified (public behavior underdetermines belief) remains the crux neither side has dissolved.
