# Processual vs. Post-Processual Archaeology Debate

Standing dispute over what archaeology can know, how it should reason, and whether fieldwork is neutral data recovery or interpretation-laden practice. Positions in strongest form; no wiki-voice adjudication. Related earlier framing: [[archaeology-science-or-humanism-debate]]. Programs: [[processual-archaeology]], [[post-processual-archaeology]].

## Position: Processual / New Archaeology

Linked: [[processual-archaeology]], [[binford-lewis]], [[middle-range-theory-archaeology]], [[ethnoarchaeology]].

**Strongest form.** Archaeology should be anthropology of the past: explain **cultural process** and systemic variability, not only construct trait chronologies. Material remains are products of past cultural systems; social organization and adaptation are in principle recoverable via explicit problem formulation, testable propositions, quantification, and **middle-range theory** linking statics to dynamics ([[formation-processes]], [[ethnoarchaeology]], [[actualistic-studies]] — as densified in *[[binford-in-pursuit-of-the-past-1983|In Pursuit of the Past]]* 1983). Universalizing scientific method and regional research designs (survey, sampling, screening) improve reliability over culture-historical impressionism. Theory-ladenness is real but managed by independence of testing instruments and severe attempts at disconfirmation — not by dissolving the data/theory distinction in practice.

**Field implication (as critics and historians characterize the package):** well-defined research designs; ecological-regional frames; intensive recovery; standardized recording compatible with comparison and CRM accountability.

## Position: Post-Processual / Interpretive Archaeology

Linked: [[post-processual-archaeology]], [[hodder-ian]], *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* (1999).

**Strongest form (Hodder 1999).** Material culture is **meaningfully and historically constituted**; meaning is relational, both general and local. Data are dynamic relations among objects, contexts, and interpretations — not a stock of objective facts later clothed with theory. **Interpretation occurs at the trowel’s edge**: method and recovery already depend on interpretation; excavators are not technicians. Linear “description then interpretation” practice contradicts what sophisticated theory already admits about theory-ladenness. Method should become **recursive, reflexive, interactive, multivocal**. Natural sciences and digital tools are not enemies: they enter a generalized hermeneutic **fitting** of multiple strands. In a globalized heritage world, authority cannot rest on closed professional discourse alone; multiple communities have stakes in the past. The live issue is **use of data as evidence for interpretation**, not a sterile subject/object dualism.

**Field implication:** specialist–excavator interaction during digging; diaries/video situating coded records; fluid databases; hypertext multivocal presentation; local–global negotiation of heritage claims ([[catalhoyuk]] as laboratory).

### Strongest form densified from Shanks & Tilley 1987 (critical charter)

Linked: [[shanks-michael]], [[tilley-christopher]], *[[shanks-tilley-social-theory-and-archaeology-1987|Social Theory and Archaeology]]*, [[logic-of-contingency]].

**Strongest form (S&T 1987, attributed).** After 150 years of empiricism, archaeology still has only rudimentary understanding of the past; no amount of excavation, survey, ethnoarchaeology, or **middle-range empiricism** closes the past–present gap — only conceptual tools that reinscribe past into present. Theory has been wrongly reduced to methodology (detective/therapist images; method as pharmacopoeia). Processual **social archaeology** operates a **logic of necessity**: the social as intelligible totality; history with a rational substratum enabling evolutionary and systems explanations via reified variables (subsistence, technology, ranking), functional adaptation, and occlusion of the individual. Against this, a **logic of contingency**: no hierarchy of determination; social order as open field of relations; primacy of political negotiation, strategy, and power; particularity not dissolved into abstract categories. Material culture is active **signifying practice**, not passive reflection of ethnicity or adaptive function. Abstract chronological time and stage evolution impose Western capitalist valuations. Archaeology is always a politics and morality; no neutral algorithm evaluates theories; validity hinges on intention and interest; "Choosing a past… is choosing a future"; archaeology is nothing if it is not **critique**.

**Field implication (as S&T frame it):** not primarily a new recovery package but a transformation of how the past is **written** and for whom — self-reflexive texts, engagement with museums/media/heritage, value-committed rather than disinterested research. (Hodder 1999 later supplies an explicit recursive field methodology for a related program.)

### Strongest form densified from *Re-Constructing Archaeology* (1987/1992)

Linked: *[[shanks-tilley-reconstructing-archaeology-1987|Re-Constructing Archaeology]]*, [[fourfold-hermeneutic]], [[redemptive-aesthetic]], [[social-archaeology]].

**Strongest form (RCA, attributed).** Objectivity/value-freedom is a **disabling ideology** that imports unrecognized capitalist values (economic optimization, systems-as-pathology, evolution's politicized time, mathematization-as-prestige). Archaeology is a **[[fourfold-hermeneutic|fourfold hermeneutic]]** (discipline + present social life + alien culture + past–present reconstruction); it cannot be fixed by methodological recipes. Museums and heritage industries reify the past as commodity, aesthetic high culture, narrative information, simulacrum, shop-front, mythical heritage, or discovery-hero spectacle — a [[redemptive-aesthetic]] is proposed instead. Style is social/signifying practice that can insert **ideology**; modern material culture (beer cans) shows design under social constraint and disciplinary power. **Radical pluralism** assesses archaeologies by commitment, not neutral method; feminist archaeology and Indigenous/American Indian conflicts over remains expose power effects of archaeological discourse.

**Critics' four charges (as S&T summarize in 1992 Appendix)** — positions, not adjudication: (1) rejection of objectivity / unfettered subjectivity; (2) hopeless relativism of incommensurable pasts; (3) inability to distinguish fictional from real pasts; (4) disciplinary impotency. **Authors' reply (attributed):** materialist account of knowledge (subjectivity, material culture, texts as embodied practices); contingent objectivity and networks of resistance; reject both pure fiction and new-archaeology "scientific" pasts as idealist; "not just anything goes." Named reception they record: *Norwegian Archaeological Review* 22 (sympathetic: Bender, Herschend, Hodder, Nordbladh, Olsen; opposed: Renfrew, Trigger, Wenke); Kristiansen 1988; Watson 1990.

## Shared Factual Ground

- Both reject pure antiquarian collecting without research questions.
- Both use stratigraphy, typology, dating, survey, and (increasingly) laboratory science.
- Both produced major projects, textbooks, and institutional power.
- Both agree at a high level that data are theory-laden; they **diverge on what practice must do** about that fact.
- Hybrid “processual-plus” and interpretive projects are common by the 1990s–2000s (diversity Hodder himself notes as “post-postprocessual” multivocality and cognitive-processual / neo-Darwinian splits inside the older camp).

## Points of Sharpest Conflict

| Issue | Processual stress | Post-processual stress |
|---|---|---|
| Goal | Explanation of process / systems | Interpretation of meaning / practice / history |
| Testing | Independent middle-range tests | Part–whole coherence + correspondence in fitting |
| Field role | Standardized recovery for later analysis | Interpretation constitutive of recovery |
| Universals | Law-like / general method package | Context-appropriate methodology; diversity |
| Politics of past | Often backgrounded as bias to control | Constitutive; multivocality obligatory |
| Science | Core identity | Tools inside interpretive accommodation |

## Resolution Status

`open`. No consensus “winner.” Method vocabulary mixed in training programs; CRM standardization often pulls processual-codification ways; research and heritage politics often pull interpretive-multivocal ways. Hodder (1999) accepted that postprocessualism had **not** yet produced a unified field methodology and offered a project rather than a settled package.

## Position densified — landscape phenomenology (Tilley 1994)

[[tilley-christopher|Tilley]] (*[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]*) attacks environmental-functional site location and abstract spatial science as modernist myth-making that erases perception and social memory; he offers phenomenological walking and monument–topography analysis as the alternative "middle-range" between site plan and distribution map. Processual responses (and later GIS formalists) treat this as under-specified and presentist. Recorded as positions on both sides; see [[phenomenological-archaeology]] and [[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-study]].

## Later development — Johnson (2011): term obsolescence and convergence themes

*(Attributed to Matthew Johnson's own stated position and to the scholars he reports; see [[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011]].)*

- **Postprocessual-term obsolescence.** Johnson states, in his own explicit view, that the "processual/postprocessual wars" characterizing English-language theoretical debate have ended; in his and others' judgment the term "postprocessual archaeology" ceased to have much meaning after about 2000, though it still appears in dated critical comments.
- **Hegmon's "processual plus" (2003).** Michelle Hegmon's "processual plus" describes North American archaeology as having absorbed gender, agency, symbolism, and materiality themes and political-cultural context while shifting its language (evolution of culture → diverse trajectories; social organization → social strategies; humans in environment → humans as part of environment). Hegmon explicitly denied this meant "theoretical rapprochement" ("I am not suggesting that there is a theoretical rapprochement"); critics noted it left out Indigenous, Marxist, feminist, postcolonial, and Darwinian/behavioural archaeologies.
- **Three convergence themes.** Johnson identifies three themes that emerged across the processual/postprocessual lines in the 2000s: **agency** (see [[agency]], [[structure-vs-agency-debate]]), **materiality** (see [[materiality]]), and **reflexive field practice** — theorizing fieldwork itself (see [[hodder-ian]]'s Çatalhöyük reflexive method).
- **Symmetrical archaeology.** [[symmetrical-archaeology|Symmetrical archaeology]] (Witmore 2006, drawing on Latour's actor-network theory) is presented by Johnson as a further 2000s-era attempt to move past the processual/postprocessual opposition, arguing humans and nonhumans should not be treated as ontologically distinct; Johnson states he is "profoundly sceptical" it can reorder the discipline but calls it "a coherent manifesto."
- **Hodder's "moving on" and Johnson's response.** Hodder (2006, as quoted by Johnson) argues "what is needed is not a merging or a fusion of processual or post-processual archaeology, but a moving on from a debate caught in a tired opposition between subject and object." Johnson agrees with this in principle but is skeptical it is fully achievable given the enduring pull of naive empiricism in the discipline (see [[archaeological-pragmatism]]).
- **Johnson's own definition of theory.** Johnson's preferred working definition of "theory" — "the order you put facts in" — is chosen for accessibility and for allowing diverse theoretical viewpoints to coexist; positivists and middle-range theorists define theory narrowly (testable propositions, separate from technique), while postprocessualists and critical theorists define it broadly (all archaeological practice, including "factual" statements and techniques, is theory-laden). See [[archaeological-pragmatism]], [[pragmatism]].

## Position densified — Renfrew & Bahn textbook (2012)

*(Attributed — Renfrew & Bahn, [[renfrew-bahn-archaeology-2012|Archaeology]], 6th ed. 2012. Textbook **position recording** of how a major cognitive-processual synthesis frames the debate; not adjudication.)*

**On the postprocessual challenge.** Renfrew & Bahn report that 1980s–1990s postmodernist currents encouraged diverse approaches termed postprocessual; Hodderian arguments that there is no single correct inference path and that data are “theory laden” draw charges of relativism / “anything goes.” Earlier Shanks & Tilley writings provoked anti-scientific reactions; later tones soften; “interpretive archaeologies” (plural) is preferred by some over “postprocessual.” Critical Theory’s claim that objective social knowledge is illusory is reported as opening the way (in processualist response) to fringe archaeologies if total relativism follows.

**Empathy critique.** Interpretive approaches share with cognitive archaeology a focus on individuals’ thoughts but urge empathetic entry into past minds; Renfrew & Bahn hold there is no easy way into past minds and that the methodology of empathy is unclear — answering both early New Archaeology skepticism and unstructured empathy with **explicit procedures** for analyzing concepts and ways of thought ([[cognitive-archaeology]]).

**Functional-processual / Marxist shared ground vs postprocessual.** Functional-processual and Marxist archaeologies share willingness to consider long-term change and social relations within whole societies and cannot always be clearly distinguished; both contrast with structuralist and postprocessual approaches. Some major complex-society work (Flannery, Wright, Earle) continues outside interpretive tradition; Paleolithic human-development questions remain broadly scientific/processual.

**“Debate largely over” + dual succession.** Ch. 12 states the postprocessual debate is “largely over,” leaving early-21st-century **interpretive archaeologies** alongside **cognitive-processual** archaeology. Cognitive-processual remains mainstream processual (explain, generalize, test hypotheses; reject total relativism and privileged insight into “meaning”) while incorporating symbols, ideology as active force, material culture as constitutive of social reality (Hodder’s point accepted), internal conflict, long-term trends, methodological individualism, and rejection of extreme positivism / universal “laws of culture process.” On European megaliths, functional-processual, neo-Marxist, and postprocessual accounts are said to need not contradict. Material engagement (cognitive-processual) and material entanglement (interpretive; Hodder) are noted as possible convergence points. Anglophone dominance of the debate itself is flagged as a form of scientific colonialism (Ch. 15).

## Position densified — the interpretive charter's terms (Hodder & Hutson 2003)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-reading-past-2004|Reading the Past]], 3rd ed., chs. 8–10, ingested 2026-07-10. The interpretive side's own statement of what "testing" means and why it is not relativism.)*

Hodder & Hutson recast the dispute as one about **different approaches to testing**, not testing vs. no-testing. The interpretive position (attributed): there are no universal, context-independent instruments of measurement for *cultural* processes; [[middle-range-theory-archaeology|middle-range theory]] succeeds for non-cultural processes (how clay/bone respond to force) but contradicts itself when applied to mind and meaning, because its authors (Binford & Sabloff, Renfrew, Flannery & Marcus) simultaneously claim universal relationships *and* culture-specific "cognitive phylogenies" — positions that "simply do not compute." Validation is instead by **coherence and correspondence** ("fitting"), with data treated as "real" and constraining though neither objective nor subjective. Crucially, the interpretive side here **rejects** "anything-goes" relativism and empathy: "not everything can be said with equal integrity." Their **guarded/dialectical objectivity** goes beyond Shanks & Tilley's "networks of resistances" (which they judge too "presentist") to a mutual constitution of past and present. This is the position the processual side (Binford; Renfrew's persistent worry about the "spectre of relativism," 2001) contests. See also [[hermeneutics]], [[data-constraint-on-interpretation]].

### Sherratt — integrating the cycle (1997 Introduction)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]*, Introduction. )* Sherratt refuses a unilinear “advance” from processual to post-processual archaeology. He models Enlightenment/Romantic (and Modernist/Postmodernist) **cycles**: 1960s New Archaeology as system-building mini-Enlightenment (demography, carrying capacity; his own Section II papers); 1980s turns toward individualism, symbols, and contextual relativism (Section V). Progress, he argues, requires **critical combination** of constrained economic possibilities and meaningful action — not soggy compromise or nihilist particularism. Positions both schools; does not adjudicate the wider debate.
