# The Formalist–Substantivist Debate (economic anthropology)

The central methodological controversy of mid-twentieth-century **economic anthropology**: does the toolkit of formal (marginalist) economics apply to all human societies, or only to market societies? The debate was launched by [[polanyi-karl|Karl Polanyi]]'s work — the substantivist claims of *[[polanyi-great-transformation-1944|The Great Transformation]]* (1944), sharpened in *Trade and Market in the Early Empires* (1957) — and ran through the 1960s. It bears directly on the wider question of whether "the economic" is a universal or a historically instituted category.

## Position 1 — Substantivism ([[polanyi-karl|Polanyi]], George Dalton, Conrad Arensberg)

The **substantive** meaning of "economic" — the provisioning of wants through an instituted process of interaction with nature and other people — is universal; the **formal** meaning — economizing, allocating scarce means among alternative ends — presupposes market institutions and price-making markets. Applying marginalist theory (scarcity, maximizing, marginal utility) to non-market societies is therefore an anachronism, the "economistic fallacy." Economies are integrated by [[forms-of-integration|reciprocity, redistribution, householding, and exchange]]; only the last runs through markets. Economic life is [[embeddedness|embedded]] in social relations, so it must be studied through its concrete institutions, not through an assumed universal rationality. (See [[substantivism]].) [[sahlins-marshall|Marshall Sahlins]]'s *[[sahlins-stone-age-economics-1972|Stone Age Economics]]* (1972) — opening "This book is substantivist" and written partly to rebut Nash's (1967) declaration that substantivism was dead — is a late, influential entry on this side, arguing that primitive exchange rates respond to supply and demand without any market price-mechanism and that "in its bourgeois form the process is not general, while in its general form it is not bourgeois." Sahlins also reframed the debate as partly ideological ("formal economics flourishes as ideology at home and ethnocentrism abroad").

## Position 2 — Formalism (Melville Herskovits, Raymond Firth, Scott Cook, Harold Schneider)

**Economizing is universal.** Wherever means are scarce relative to ends — which is everywhere — people make allocative choices, and the concepts of marginal analysis (opportunity cost, maximizing, marginal utility) illuminate those choices in "primitive" and peasant economies as much as in market ones. Firth's Tikopia and Malay fisherfolk studies applied economic reasoning to non-market production; Scott Cook's "The Obsolete Anti-Market Mentality" (1966) charged substantivism with a romantic, dualist caricature of non-market societies and a covert anti-market ideology. Formalists held that the substantivists confused the *institutional form* of an economy with the *analytic tools* used to study it.

## Pre-formulation: Firth 1939 (attributed)

The debate's vocabulary and its central question are both already present in [[firth-raymond|Firth]]'s *[[firth-primitive-polynesian-economy-study|Primitive Polynesian Economy]]* (1939, primary-ingested). Firth adopted J. R. Hicks's distinction between **"substantial" and "formal" economic categories** ("he requires... a set of 'substantial' as well as 'formal' economic categories, to adopt the useful distinction made by J. R. Hicks"), argued against Robbins that Tikopia has no "final organizer" analogous to a price system, and concluded that "strictly speaking... in a primitive community with no money, no prices... there are no economic values" — value must be rebuilt as equivalence, "a substantial rather than a formal use of the term." Yet he simultaneously held that scarcity, choice, capital, and rational-choice postulates apply to Tikopia in modified form, provided "subsidiary postulates about human behaviour in defined cultural situations." His 1939 position is thus genuinely **intermediate**: later debate-era filing of Firth as a plain formalist flattens a text both camps drew on. His three characteristics of a primitive economy — personalized economic relations, the profit motive conditioned by social ends, reciprocity embedded in a wider social code — read as a substantivist program written fifteen years before Polanyi's "The Economy as Instituted Process."

## Shared ground, framed per side

Both sides accept that non-market societies exist and differ institutionally from market economies; both use ethnographic evidence (much of it the same — Malinowski's [[kula|Kula]] and gift typology in *[[malinowski-argonauts-1922|Argonauts]]* (now primary-ingested), Firth). Malinowski's demolition of "Primitive Economic Man" and his distinction of ceremonial exchange from [[gimwali]] barter, and Mauss's *[[mauss-the-gift-1925|The Gift]]* (now primary-ingested) on [[total-prestation]], [[potlatch]], and total social phenomena, are **precursor evidence** later mobilized especially by substantivists; formalists read the same record as evidence that economizing and calculation appear even in non-market settings. Note Mauss also insists markets are a universal human phenomenon — not a simple "no markets" ethnography. They divide on whether marginalist theory is a **universal analytic** (formalists) or a **culture-bound description of one institutional type** (substantivists), and correspondingly on whether "scarcity-driven maximizing" is a human universal or a market-society artifact.

## Resolution

**Dissolved rather than settled.** Neither side "won"; by the 1970s the debate was largely abandoned as economic anthropology moved on to **Marxist** (French structural Marxism — Meillassoux, Godelier) and later **culturalist** and practice-theoretic approaches, which reframed the questions rather than answering the formalist/substantivist one. Substantivism lost ground within anthropology but its core concept, [[embeddedness]], migrated into economic sociology (Granovetter, 1985), where Polanyi's influence is now strongest. The underlying question — whether "the economy" is a universal or an instituted category — remains live.
