# Defining Historical Archaeology

What makes archaeology "historical" is itself the field's founding dispute. The wiki's [[historical-archaeology]] page carries the working synthesis; this page records the positions. Primary carriers in the vault: [[orser-historical-archaeology-modern-world-1996]], [[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996]], and [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006]] (whose introduction frames the dispute directly).

## Positions

**Document-defined.** John Moreland (2001) defines the field by the presence of written documents in the society investigated, in a long tradition treating literate societies as a special subject (Goody on writing's transformation of temporality). Barbara Little's phrase "people with history" (1994) marks a related framing. Critics reply that documents are simply another form of material culture (Wilkie), that oral tradition carries deep historicity (African historical archaeology has relied on it for the precolonial and colonial past — DeCorse, Schmidt), and, with Eric Wolf (1982), that there is a political imperative to reject models of non-literate societies as "without history."

**European-expansion-defined.** James Deetz (1977: 5): "the archaeology of the spread of European cultures throughout the world since the fifteenth century, and their impact on and interaction with the cultures of indigenous peoples." Dominant in North America for the later 20th century; challenged as Eurocentric and as excluding Europe's own recent past (Courtney 1999; Funari et al. 1999; Tarlow & West 1999) — see [[deetz-james]].

**Modern-world / archaeology-of-capitalism.** [[orser-charles-e|Orser]] (1996) argued only defining the field by the modern world "unlocks its power" ([[global-historical-archaeology]]); Leone & Potter (1999) defined it as the archaeology of capitalism. Wilkie & Bartoy (2000: 748) observed the Annapolis school's influence became "so pervasive that many... have come to consider historical archaeology synonymous with the archaeology of capitalism" — itself now a position under critique for totalisation and Eurocentric periodization (Funari et al. 1999; Lucas in Hicks & Beaudry 2006).

**Period-defined, post-1500.** Hicks & Beaudry (2006) use "historical" to mean "broadly the post-1500 period, strongly resisting any attempt to separate the field from the archaeology of earlier periods," and frame the field as "a contemporary and creative practice" extending into the 20th century and the [[archaeology-of-the-contemporary-past]] — against cut-off points such as Newman's (2001) end-of-Victorian terminus and against Tarlow & West's "de-familiarising" programme, which they read as still Britain-bound.

**British terminological tradition.** UK practice long split the period as "post-medieval archaeology" (c. 1450–1750) and "industrial archaeology" thereafter, an object/production-focused, museum- and rescue-rooted tradition (Crossley 1990) — a division Hicks & Beaudry treat as institutional artifact rather than historical boundary; see [[industrial-archaeology]].

**Wider-discipline gloss.** Barry Cunliffe (afterword, 2006) argues from a prehistorian's seat that compartmentalizing "prehistoric/Roman/medieval/historical" archaeologists is "arbitrary and alien" to long-occupied European sites, urging holistic study of the record entire — while also editorializing that the field "deserves to be more than just a eurocentric archaeology of capitalism" (attributed; his framing).

## Status

Open. In practice the international literature (and this wiki) uses the post-1500 period frame while recording the modern-world and document-based framings as live research programs rather than boundaries.
