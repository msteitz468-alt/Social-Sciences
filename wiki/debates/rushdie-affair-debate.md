# The Rushdie Affair and British Multiculturalism

The controversy over Salman Rushdie's *The Satanic Verses* (1988) — protests, the 1989 Bradford book-burning, Khomeini's fatwa, and the British government's response — became a battleground over multiculturalism, secularism, and British identity. This page records the positions as analyzed in [[asad-talal|Asad]]'s *Genealogies of Religion* (chs. 7–8), currently this wiki's sole ingested source on the affair; coverage is correspondingly Asad-centric.

## Positions

**Liberal-literary.** The novel is imaginative literature; threats and book-burning attacked free expression and, per Asad's diagnosis of this position, the "sacred role performed by literature in modern culture." Some critics (e.g., Penelope Lively, as cited by Asad) held the text self-sufficient for its meaning — a stance Asad ironically labels the "'fundamentalist' position."

**Assimilationist / core-values.** Home Office minister John Patten's open letter and "On Being British" (1989) framed the affair as requiring immigrants to affirm British values, freedom of speech, and undivided loyalty. Fay Weldon's *Sacred Cows* praised American "uni-culturalist policy" and declared the Qur'an "food for no-thought." (Asad notes the Carlyle lineage of such Qur'an-dismissal.)

**Postmodern-hybridity.** Homi Bhabha read the novel as showing "there is no such whole as the nation, the culture, or even the self," modeling hybrid emergent identities; Paul Gilroy's account of syncretic black British culture points the same way against fixed Britishness.

**Asadian.** Asad argues the affair was "yet another symptom of postimperial British identity in crisis," not an immigrant-adjustment problem: Patten's rhetoric is "the atavistic voice of an English colonial governor"; the elite's fear concerned a threat to *authority* (the exclusive right to define homogeneity and difference), not to power, because Muslims "were able to use the liberal language of equal rights in rational argument against the secular British elite"; the demand for unconditional concession to "core values" is "a famous bourgeois ruse," since those values are themselves products of past contestation. Against Bhabha/Gilroy he argues that demonstrating a culture's syncretic origins "tells us nothing about whether the unity is coherent," and that power typically governs by *denying* subject populations a distinct unity. His reading of the novel itself: its treatment of the Prophet draws on the Christian anti-Muslim polemical tradition (via Norman Daniel); it misattributes hadith-derived and Shi'i-specific rules to Qur'anic revelation; and its sneer targets a politically vulnerable immigrant population rather than functioning as satire, which "requires the satirist's grounded understanding of the target's moral structure."

**British-Muslim positions (diverse).** Asad documents the range: Zaheera's liberal equal-treatment protest ("one law for Christians and one for Muslims"); Shabbir Akhtar's argument that the controversy was resolvable through recognition of prejudice rather than new law; Modood's critique of "black" as a category forcing Asians into an Afro-diasporic frame; Gautam Sen's solidarity shift ("we blacks are all Muslims now"). Asad's analytic point about legal recourse: modern libel law "cannot cope with the idea of malicious statements leading to moral or spiritual injury because it cannot locate and quantify the damage in monetary terms."

## Shared factual ground

The novel was published in 1988; it was publicly burned in Bradford in January 1989; the British government made unprecedented "core values" statements to Muslim citizens (Patten, July 1989); the 1983 Mandla v. Dowell Lee test defines legal "ethnic group" status in Britain (long shared history + distinct cultural tradition as essential criteria); the 1981 census counted just over two million nonwhite Britons, fewer than half Muslim.

## Status

Open; the positions structure ongoing disputes over multiculturalism, hate-speech law, and secularism. Coverage note: all positions above are recorded as presented in Asad 1993 — a partisan participant as well as analyst; future ingests (e.g., Parekh, Modood, liberal replies to Asad) should rebalance this page.
