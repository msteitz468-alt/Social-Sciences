# Can Religion Be Defined? (Geertz–Asad and after)

Whether "[[religion]]" names a universal, cross-culturally definable domain, or a historically specific Western construct, is one of anthropology's structuring theoretical disputes. Its modern focus is Asad's critique of Geertz.

## Position 1 — Universalist-definitional (Tylor; Geertz)

At its strongest: religion is a recurrent, functionally distinctive feature of human societies that comparison requires us to define. [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] (1871) offered the minimal definition "belief in spiritual beings." [[geertz-clifford|Geertz]] ("Religion as a Cultural System," 1966) offered the most influential 20th-century definition — a system of symbols establishing powerful moods and motivations by formulating conceptions of a general order of existence clothed with an aura of factuality — grounding a comparative anthropology in which religion is "a distinctive space of human practice and belief which cannot be reduced to any other." The definitional project underwrites comparative religion, the sociology of religion's measurement tradition, and the cognitive science of religion; abandoning it, defenders argue, dissolves the object of comparison altogether.

## Position 2 — Genealogical (Asad)

At its strongest: "there cannot be a universal definition of religion, not only because its constituent elements and relationships are historically specific, but because that definition is itself the historical product of discursive processes" ([[asad-talal|Asad]] 1993). Asad's supporting arguments: the lineage of universal definitions runs from Herbert of Cherbury's Natural Religion through Locke to Kant's "religion itself" vs. "historical confessions" — a specifically post-Reformation Christian trajectory; the separation of religion from power that such definitions presuppose "is a modern Western norm, the product of a unique post-Reformation history"; and Geertz's definition fails internally — inconsistent uses of "symbol," a Parsonian dualism of culture vs. social reality, belief treated as a prior interior state (a privatized modern Christian conception, against the medieval evidence that power and *disciplina* implanted dispositions), and an unexplained claim that ritual generates conviction ("Mustn't the ability and the will to adopt a religious standpoint be present prior to the ritual performance?").

## Subsidiary dispute — is "belief" universal?

Asad reports the three-way scholarly dispute he draws on: Needham (1972) — belief is nowhere a distinct mode of consciousness; Southwold (1979) — belief relations exist in every society; Harré (1981) — belief is a mental state but "confined to people who have certain social institutions and practices" (the position Asad finds most persuasive).

## State of play

The debate remains open. Asad's critique became a charter for the anthropology of secularism and of Islam (see [[discursive-tradition]]); Geertzian and cognitive-universalist programs continue. Both sides agree on the descriptive history (the modern concept's post-Reformation privatization); they disagree on whether that history disqualifies the category. No adjudication is recorded here.
