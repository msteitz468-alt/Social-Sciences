## The Dispute

How early bronze metallurgy began in mainland Southeast Asia — and whether it was
independently invented there rather than introduced along a steppe–China pathway. The
question turns on the radiocarbon and thermoluminescence dating of Northeast Thai sites,
especially [[non-nok-tha]] and [[ban-chiang]], whose stratigraphy proved difficult to date
securely, and on later AMS dating of organic ceramic temper. As recorded by Higham
(1989, 2002); the dispute is a matter of dating and reanalysis, not of a single
interpretive reading.

## Positions

**Very early bronze (the 1960s–70s claims).** When [[non-nok-tha]] was the sole known
bronze-bearing site, Solheim (1968) and Bayard (1972a) advanced dates implying Southeast
Asian bronze-working "far earlier than had hitherto been imagined" — potentially in the
later 4th or 3rd millennium BCE, which would make the region a candidate for independent
(and precociously early) invention of bronze metallurgy. Parallel claims followed from
[[ban-chiang]] (Gorman and Charoenwongsa 1976). These claims received wide popular
circulation and, Higham notes, risked becoming "engrained in the literature."

**Later bronze — Higham 1989 reconciliation (c. 2000–1500 BCE).** Joyce White's (1986)
restudy of [[ban-chiang]] placed initial bronze (Early Period III) later in the sequence,
and Higham and Kijngam's (1984) work at [[ban-na-di]] gave a well-provenanced chronology
from sealed contexts. Reconciling the sites, Higham (1989) concluded initial bronze
casting fell **c. 2000–1500 BCE** — later than the 1960s–70s proposals, though still
locally cast (crucibles, moulds, and casting spillage attest on-site production).

**Still later bronze — Higham 2002 AMS revision (c. 1500–1000 BCE) and diffusion path.**
In *Early Cultures of Mainland Southeast Asia* (2002), Higham states that the early
claims "have not been confirmed, and must be set aside." He reports that **AMS dating of
rice chaff used as ceramic temper** in specimens from [[non-nok-tha]] and [[ban-chiang]]
shows "the earliest bronzes were being cast within the period 1500–1000 BC." In the
closing synthesis he places mainland Southeast Asia in a **diffusion pattern**: knowledge
of bronze-working crossed the steppes, spread across China, and entered first mainland
then (much later) island Southeast Asia. Similar casting technology from the Hong Kong
coast to central Thailand and from Yunnan to the Mekong Delta is offered as supporting
pattern evidence. Local on-site casting is not denied; independent *invention* of the
technology in Southeast Asia is what he rejects. (As of Higham 2002; later Ban Non Wat
and Ban Chiang dating programmes may refine or contest the interval further.)

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both early-claim and revisionist sides agree bronze was **locally cast** at the key
Northeast Thai sites (not merely imported finished goods) and that early dating was
compromised by real taphonomic problems — [[non-nok-tha]] lacked excavation shelter so
sun and rain degraded visible stratigraphy, with shallow intercutting graves;
[[ban-chiang]] suffered beetle bioturbation and grave-digging that relocated older
charcoal. They divide on how much of the early dating survives scrutiny and on whether
local casting implies independent invention. Higham (2002) regards the "extremely early
metallurgy" claims as overturned by AMS temper dates and situates the technology within
a steppe–China diffusion pathway.
