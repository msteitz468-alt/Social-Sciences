## The Dispute

How early bronze metallurgy began in mainland Southeast Asia — and whether it was
independently invented there rather than introduced from China. The question turns on the
radiocarbon and thermoluminescence dating of two Northeast Thai sites, [[non-nok-tha]] and
[[ban-chiang]], whose stratigraphy proved difficult to date securely. As recorded by
Higham (1989); the dispute is a matter of dating and reanalysis, not of a single
interpretive reading.

## Positions

**Very early bronze (the 1960s–70s claims).** When [[non-nok-tha]] was the sole known
bronze-bearing site, Solheim (1968) and Bayard (1972a) advanced dates implying Southeast
Asian bronze-working "far earlier than had hitherto been imagined" — potentially in the
later 4th or 3rd millennium BCE, which would make the region a candidate for independent
(and precociously early) invention of bronze metallurgy. These claims received wide
popular circulation.

**Later bronze (the downward revision).** Joyce White's (1986) restudy of [[ban-chiang]]
placed initial bronze (Early Period III) later in the sequence, and Higham and Kijngam's
(1984) work at [[ban-na-di]] gave a well-provenanced chronology from sealed contexts.
Reconciling the sites, Higham (1989) concluded initial bronze casting fell **c. 2000–1500
BCE** — later than the earlier proposals, though still locally cast (crucibles, moulds,
and casting spillage attest on-site production).

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides agree bronze was locally cast at these sites (not merely imported) and that the
dating was compromised by real taphonomic problems — [[non-nok-tha]] had no excavation
shelter, so sun and rain degraded the visible stratigraphy, and its graves were shallow
and intercut; [[ban-chiang]] suffered beetle bioturbation that homogenised the upper soil,
and grave-digging relocated older charcoal, so charcoal near a skeleton need not date the
burial. They divide on how much of the early dating survives scrutiny. Higham (1989)
regards the "extremely early metallurgy" claims as substantially revised downward.
