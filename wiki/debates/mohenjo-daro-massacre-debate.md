## Positions

### Position A — Massacre by invading Aryans (Wheeler 1959; the older, once-popular view)

Mortimer [[wheeler-mortimer|Wheeler]] described the "HR Area Tragedy" at Mohenjo-daro — 14
skeletons (13 adults and 1 child), some allegedly bearing "axe or sword-cuts" — as the
victims of raiders, writing in his own words: "That final blow has often enough been
described… In that moment Mohenjo-daro was dead." Wheeler's account linked these
skeletons, together with three other clusters found at the site (the "VS Area Tragedy," 6
skeletons; the "Long Lane Group," 9 skeletons; and the "Well Room Tragedy," 2 skeletons),
to the collapse of the Indus Civilization via an Indo-Aryan invasion, reading Rgvedic
references to *pur* (forts or strongholds) destroyed by the god Indra as corroborating
textual evidence ("Indra stands accused"). The reading was popular among some historians
of ancient India, with A. L. Basham (1967) cited by Possehl as an exponent.

### Position B — Debunked; no evidence of a massacre or an invasion (Dales 1964; K. A. R. Kennedy 1984/1995/2000; endorsed by Possehl 2002)

This position rests on a multi-pronged, largely independent critique:

1. **Taphonomic and contextual critique.** All four "tragedy" groups occur near the
   surface, in the site's Late Period strata, without formal grave cuts, and at different
   levels within each group — in the HR group, one skeleton lay 15 cm higher than another's
   legs — a pattern inconsistent with a single violent event and single simultaneous
   deposition.
2. **Direct skeletal re-examination.** K. A. R. Kennedy re-examined the alleged "sword or
   axe cut" trauma originally reported by Sewell and Guha (1931) and found the "cut" on
   skeleton 10 was not a fresh wound at time of death: bone resorption indicates it was
   received 30–70 days before death. An "old healed lesion" reported on skeleton 1 is
   irrelevant to any massacre and that skeleton likely postdates the others. Of roughly
   thirty "tragedy" skeletons examined, only the skull from the "Long Lane Group" shows a
   wound Kennedy judged was "in all probability… the cause of immediate death" — i.e., only
   one skull among the entire assemblage shows unambiguous lethal trauma.
3. **Chronological argument.** Mohenjo-daro's abandonment (c. 1900 BCE) precedes the
   Rgveda's codification (c. 1000 BCE) by roughly nine centuries. The Rgveda itself does
   not describe conquest of the Indus cities, is centered geographically on the Punjab
   rather than Sindh (where Mohenjo-daro sits), and records feuding among Aryan groups and
   with indigenous peoples rather than a campaign of urban massacre. Possehl further notes
   no comparable "massacre" evidence exists at Harappa or other sites in the Punjab region
   the Vedic texts actually describe.
4. **Alternative causal hypothesis (Mackay; Possehl, tentative).** If any of the deaths
   were in fact violent, they may reflect the breakdown of civic order in a late,
   slum-like end-stage occupation — victims of internal disorder or of "hill peoples" at
   the frontier — rather than an external Aryan conquest.

Possehl's own synthesis of the source's position: "no one knows how or why these people
died," but the combined weight of the taphonomic, biological, and textual evidence
"thoroughly debunk[s]" (his word, citing Dales and Kennedy) the invasion-massacre
narrative as an explanation for the Indus Civilization's decline.

## Superseded framework, noted in passing

Sewell and Guha's original 1931 racial typology of the Mohenjo-daro skeletons (Proto-
Australoid / Mediterranean / Alpine / "Mongolian-Alpine" branches) is an obsolete
biological-race framework; K. A. R. Kennedy (1977) judged its use "discredited for many
years" despite continuing to be widely referenced. This typology should not be repeated
in wiki voice on any page discussing the history of Indus physical-anthropological
classification.

## Four-way non-identity (mandatory, load-bearing)

This debate is entirely about the **cause of death** of a specific skeletal sample — a
biological/skeletal-evidence question. It does not resolve, and must never be conflated
with, three separate questions: (a) whether Indo-European-speaking populations (a
**linguistic** category) arrived in South Asia during this era; (b) whether a self-
identified "Aryan" **ethnic or cultural** group existed and interacted with Harappan
populations; or (c) any **genetic** population-history claim about migration into South
Asia. Kennedy's skeletal reanalysis speaks only to cause of death and the dating of
trauma for a specific set of skeletons; it is not itself evidence against Indo-Aryan
migration as a linguistic or genetic phenomenon — that is a separate, much later, and
still-active scholarly question outside this debate's scope. It is evidence only against
the specific claim that these particular skeletons document a violent conquest event.
Skeletons are people, not languages, not ethnic labels.

## Resolution status

Filed `resolution_status: resolved-by-consensus` — Possehl (2002) reports the massacre-
by-invasion reading of the Mohenjo-daro skeletons as discredited within the discipline on
independent taphonomic, biological, and textual grounds, with no articulate current
defender in this source. This resolves the narrow question of what killed these
specific skeletons; it does not resolve, and should not be read as resolving, the larger
and separately live question of the Indus Civilization's transformation and any Indo-
Aryan population presence (see [[indus-transformation-debate]]).
