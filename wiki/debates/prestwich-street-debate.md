# The Prestwich Street Dispute

Dispute over the exhumation of an undocumented burial ground in Green Point, Cape Town, discovered during construction in May 2003 — the sharpest documented confrontation between rescue archaeology and post-apartheid community memory politics. Recorded here from Lawrence & Shepherd's account in ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 5). `resolution_status` note: the *procedural* outcome was settled (appeals exhausted, exhumation completed); the underlying ethical dispute was not resolved by consensus — see Outcome.

## Background

Green Point lay outside colonial Cape Town's formal boundaries in the 17th–18th centuries: the site of the gallows and of documented and undocumented burial grounds holding the town's underclasses — enslaved people, free blacks, artisans, sailors, washerwomen, paupers, executed criminals, shipwreck victims (Hart 2003). The area was subdivided as real estate in the 1820s. In the late 1960s–early 1970s its black and coloured residents were forcibly removed under apartheid to the Cape Flats, an episode paralleling the better-known District Six removals. By 2003 the area was rapidly gentrifying.

## Timeline

- **Mid-May 2003** — human bones found during construction on the Prestwich Street block; work halted under the National Heritage Resources Act; the developer commissioned the Archaeological Contracts Office (UCT-affiliated), permitted by the South African Heritage Resources Agency (SAHRA) for rescue exhumation.
- **11 June 2003** — exhumation began; public consultation run by historical archaeologist Antonia Malan (Cultural Sites and Resources Forum) during the statutory 60-day period.
- **29 July 2003** — ~500 individuals exhumed (about one body per square metre); total estimates rose from 200 to 1,200.
- **Aug–Sept 2003** — interim cessation, contested public meetings; SAHRA CEO Pumla Madiba resumed work on 1 September, stating objectors "were highly emotional and did not give real reasons" (Kassiem 2003); Hands Off Prestwich Street Ad Hoc Committee (HOC) formed 4 September, led by Anglican ministers Michael Wheeder and Terry Lester, both from families affected by forced removals.
- **19 Nov 2003 / 12 Jan 2004** — SAHRA appeals committee upheld the permit; further appeal to the Minister of Arts and Culture failed. All remains had by then been moved to temporary storage in Napier House; the development proceeded.

## Positions

**Scientific value / rescue position** (Special Focus Reference Group — mainly UCT archaeologists and human biologists; SAHRA; some public participants). Judith Sealy proposed reinterment "in individual caskets, in a crypt or mausoleum" permitting both honouring the dead and "careful, respectful, scientific study by bona fide researchers"; "These skeletons are also – literally – our history, the ordinary people of Cape Town, whose lives are not written in the official documents of the time... one of the most powerful ways to [recover their history] is through the study of their skeletons" (Sealy 2003: 1). Supporting voices at consultation framed exhumation as "giving history back to the people" and warned that stopping meant "half a story is being told" (Malan 2003).

**Memory and dignity / non-disturbance position** (HOC; community leaders, many veterans of the anti-apartheid struggle; Christian and Muslim clergy; University of the Western Cape academics; Khoisan representatives). The HOC argued the needs of "archaeology as a science" had been given precedence over "community socio-cultural history, of collective remembering and of acknowledging the pain and trauma related to the site," and that exhumation "echoes, albeit unintentionally, the apartheid regime's forced removals from the same area" (Hands Off Committee 2003b: 2). Consultation minutes record Yvette Abrahams (UWC): "Is this a public participation process or a rubber stamping exercise... How can [a] permit be given for the bodies to be dug up before I am consulted?"; a Haven Shelter representative: "Why are white people, and white women, scratching in our bones? This is sacrilege"; Zenzile Khoisan: "Stop robbing graves – stop robbing graves!" (Malan 2003). The HOC held candle-lit vigils and erected a billboard at St George's Cathedral: "Stop the exhumations! Stop the desecration!"

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides accepted that the burials were those of Cape Town's colonial-era underclasses, largely absent from the documentary record, and that the site sat within a landscape already marked by apartheid-era erasure. The scientific side read that absence as the strongest argument *for* osteological study (the only recoverable history); the opposition read the same facts as grounds for non-disturbance and commemorative control by descendant communities. A leaked internal SAHRA memo noted that conserving the site would have "disastrous consequences for the developer," adding "the matter is urgent, as the apartments in the development have been pre-sold" (Hands Off Committee 2003a) — documented institutional-financial pressure on the heritage decision, acknowledged by neither position as decisive.

## Outcome and significance

Procedurally the pro-exhumation position prevailed at every stage; Lawrence & Shepherd narrate the episode as "a story of failure" for the opposition, and argue it shows historical archaeology in the post-colony finding its place "precisely through engaging with the complex play of contemporary forces and interests" — confronting "the necessary entanglements of race, culture and identity in the post-colony" ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], ch. 5). The case is a standing reference point for debates over descendant-community consent, rescue-archaeology ethics, and the politics of [[heritage-management]]; compare the African Burial Ground in New York, where descendant-community involvement was institutionalized (see [[urban-archaeology]]).
