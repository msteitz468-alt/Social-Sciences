# Farming Spread into Europe Debate

How did agro-pastoral economies move from western Asia into Europe after ~7500 BC — primarily by **migrating farmers**, by **indigenous Mesolithic adoption**, or by **regionally mixed** processes? [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] stages the dispute across SE Europe, the LBK loess corridor, Mediterranean coasts, and early classical genetics. **No adjudication here** — positions in strongest form. Genetic claims are **as of Mithen 2003** (pre-aDNA revolution).

## Shared Factual Ground

- Farming economies using western Asian domesticates (cereals, sheep, goats, and related stock) are present in **Greece by ~7500 BC** in Mithen’s synthesis, with boat transport of seed-corn and livestock from western Asia; substantial Mesolithic presence near Franchthi Cave coexists with early farmers for up to a millennium with limited initial impact on tools and foraging.
- **[[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]]** emerges ~5500 BC on Hungarian plain fringes and spreads rapidly across central Europe with long houses and band-decorated pottery.
- Late Mesolithic **complex coastal foragers** (e.g. [[ertebolle-culture|Ertebølle]], Portuguese estuary middens, Téviec/Hoëdic) persist into the 5th–4th millennia BC and then transition to farming on regionally specific schedules (~3900 BC for Danish cases in Mithen).
- Mediterranean sequences show **site-level mixes** of Mesolithic and Neolithic traits that different scholars explain as indigenous selection or as stratigraphic/dating error plus coastal colonist farmsteads now flooded.
- Modern (classical) genetic geography of Europe was already used by the 1990s to estimate immigrant vs pre-Neolithic ancestry fractions — methods and numbers disputed even before genome-wide aDNA.

## Position A — Demic Diffusion / Wave of Advance (Immigrant Farmers)

**Holders (as reported by Mithen):** Gordon Childe’s immigrant-package tradition; Luca Cavalli-Sforza’s *History and Geography of Human Genes* (1994); João Zilhão’s Mediterranean colonisation model; Colin Renfrew’s linkage of Neolithic migrants to proto-Indo-European spread (language package).

**Strongest form:** Neolithic economy spreads primarily because **people move**, carrying a complete package (ceramics where present, domesticates, new settlement forms). Cavalli-Sforza reads a southeast→northwest European gene-frequency gradient as the legacy of a Neolithic **wave of advance**, giving Mesolithic populations little formative role and implying LBK farmers descend from West Asian immigrants. Zilhão argues Mediterranean “mixed” cave assemblages are stratigraphic mess (burrowing, misidentified sheep/ibex, bad dates); first farmsteads were coastal and are now flooded; caves are seasonal farmer camps — return toward Childe. Portuguese case: boatloads of colonists ~5700 BC near Gruta do Caldeirão while Mesolithic estuary middens continue until abandonment ~5000 BC. Renfrew: proto-Indo-European spoken by Neolithic Anatolia/western Asia ~7500 BC and spread with migrant farmers; Basque and Finnish mark Mesolithic survival zones.

## Position B — Indigenous Mesolithic Adoption / Continuity

**Holders (as reported by Mithen):** Marek Zvelebil (LBK created by indigenous adopters on Hungarian plain periphery); Lewthwaite and Peter Rowley-Conwy (Mediterranean selective adoption); Bryan Sykes et al. mtDNA results as Mithen reads them; Mithen’s own closing synthesis that Mesolithic roles were at least as great as immigrants.

**Strongest form:** Local hunter-gatherers **choose** farming traits without being demographically replaced. Zvelebil: Mesolithic people on the Hungarian plain periphery adopt and adapt farming, **creating LBK themselves**, so central European LBK farmers are descendants of indigenous Mesolithic groups rather than of the immigrants who first reached Greece. Mediterranean: at single sites, foragers selectively take sheep/goats, Cardial pottery, or limited cereals without becoming full farmers (Corsica/Sardinia sheep where deer scarce; Arene Candide wild pigs + milked sheep). Sykes et al. (821 Europeans, six mtDNA lineages): only ~15% young enough and West-Asian-linked for Neolithic immigration; **85% of lineages dated 23–50 kya** (Mesolithic/ice-age ancestry) — wave of advance reduced to a “tiny ripple.” Mithen notes later convergence: Cavalli-Sforza immigrant contribution reduced to ~28% and Sykes raised above 20% — figures too close to sustain a pure either/or, implying indigenous Mesolithic people played a role **at least as great** as immigrant farmers.

## Position C — Hybrid / Regionally Partitioned Processes

**Holders:** Implicit in Mithen’s multi-chapter structure and in many fieldworkers who accept colonisation in some corridors and adoption in others.

**Strongest form:** SE Europe and some Mediterranean coasts see real pioneer farmers by sea; loess LBK expansion may mix leap-frog colonisation (~25 km/generation) with indigenous recruitment; northern complex foragers hold out until social and environmental pressures tip them into farming (Ertebølle ~3900 BC) with material culture that still differs from classic LBK. Franchthi shows long side-by-side coexistence before farming culture “overwhelms” foragers by unclear mix of abandonment, conversion, or intermarriage. Frontier exchange (furs for LBK axes) and intermarriage are mechanisms of gene and culture flow without requiring a single continent-wide mode.

## Stakes

- **Demography vs culture:** Is the European Neolithic mainly a population replacement event or a lifestyle conversion?
- **Language history:** If Renfrew’s farming–IE link holds, linguistic maps track demic spread; if not, later migrations (e.g. steppe models outside Mithen’s frame) must carry IE.
- **Genetics methods:** Classical gradients and mtDNA fractions (2003 horizon) vs later genome-wide aDNA — the debate’s empirical baseline has shifted; this page records the Mithen-era shape without declaring post-2010 winners.
- **Ethics of narrative:** Immigrant vs indigenous framings have modern political resonances; scholarly positions should stay tied to archaeological and genetic evidence classes.

## Renfrew 1987 primary layer (demic + language)

*(Attributed — [[renfrew-colin]], *[[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987|Archaeology and Language]]*).* Renfrew is a clear **Position A** holder: farming spread in Europe mainly by **wave of advance** demic process (not pure acculturation), with first farmers in Greece/Crete before 6000 BC (calibrated Greece before ~6500 BC; Orkney ~3500 BC), imported plant package, density jump ~0.1 → 5–10/km², small local moves. Variable pace (rapid [[linear-pottery-culture|LBK]]; slow Scandinavian neolithic). He **couples** this demography to primary European [[indo-european-languages|Indo-European]] speech ([[anatolian-hypothesis]]) — a language claim not required by demic diffusion alone. Admits incompatibility with strong west-Mediterranean indigenous Neolithic models (Barker, Lewthwaite). Basque/Etruscan-type pockets as denser-mesolithic survival under partial acculturation. Full IE linkage on [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]]; demic mechanism densified on [[demic-diffusion]].

## Position layer — Sherratt (1972–97 essays)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]. )* Not a single demic-vs-indigenous verdict. Early European farming is modeled as **horticulture first** (fixed-plot groundwater / moist soils; Ch.2–3), with later **plough** expansion opening interfluves and restructuring settlement (Ch.6, 10–11). **Insertion vs adoption** and megaliths as longhouse surrogates for native recruitment appear in the Introduction and Ch.13–14 (“Instruments of Conversion”). Corded Ware / Bell Beaker / Pit-Grave horizons are later transformative packages associated with secondary products and mobility — **associated_with**, not identity equations. Dates and genetics as of essay horizons; subordinate to aDNA where conflicting.

## Position layer — Bellwood *First Farmers* (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; program [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]]. Genetic numbers **as of Bellwood 2005** — classical markers, early mtDNA/Y, limited aDNA; pre-genome-wide aDNA revolution. Do not adjudicate.)*

Bellwood is a **strong demic / Position A lean** with explicit **admixture and regional partitioning** — closer to hybrid process language than pure replacement, but he assigns primary *drive* to expanding farmers, not Mesolithic unilateral adoption.

### Strongest form (Bellwood 2005)

- **Mechanism:** Farming, languages, and genes often spread together from agricultural homelands via [[demic-diffusion]] (Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza 1984 frame: wave of advance ~1 km/year SE→NW; continuous admixture; original farmer genetic configuration watered down toward the Atlantic — not forager extinction). Early Neolithic/Formative dispersals are coherent when communities already **depend mainly on agriculture**; low-energy HG resource management cannot account for massive cultural-linguistic punctuations.
- **Against pure HG adoption as main European driver:** Ethnography rarely shows foragers successfully taking over the full economy, technology, and language of neighboring farmers at expansionary scale. “Just observing that some Mesolithic populations probably became incorporated into a Neolithic cultural landscape tells us nothing very useful at all” about *what drove* Neolithic expansion (his Britain/N Europe line). Impetus rests with land-hungry farmers from S/E; Mesolithic incorporation is secondary.
- **Regional mixture (his own synthesis, archaeological chapters):** Rapid farmer dispersals in Greece, Mediterranean coastline (Zilhão Cardial maritime colonization; sharp break), Danube, Germany/Low Countries (LBK cultural-replacement episode; loess selectivity; Sr mobility); slower spreads with substantial Mesolithic involvement/resistance on W Europe and Atlantic/Baltic fringes (Ertebølle availability phase; Keeley hostile frontier Belgium; Gregg exchange mosaic S Germany; Kooijmans “differences in mentality”). Cyprus PPNB (~8500 BC) and Crete Knossos Stratum X (~7000 BC empty-island colonization) as demic starts. Caves likelier to harbor surviving HGs with gifted/borrowed farmer goods than successful farmers — caution on Mediterranean cave “continuity.”
- **Genetics as of 2005 (positions he stages):** Classic PC1 ~30% modern European variation SE→NW (Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza); Piazza et al. ~26%. Cultural-diffusion school (Richards, Sykes, Semino et al.): most modern Europeans essentially local Paleolithic; Neolithic SW Asian input minor (Richards <25% mtDNA Neolithic; Sykes 20–30% SW Asian mtDNA; Semino ~78% Y Paleolithic refugia, ~22% Neolithic Levant). Demic school (Sokal, Barbujani, Chikhi, Rosser, Underhill, Cavalli-Sforza 2003): significant farmer contribution; **Chikhi et al. 2002** Y-admixture ~**50%** average modern European Y Neolithic (85–100% SE Europe, 15–30% France) — higher than classic PC or Richards mtDNA. Barbujani et al.: cannot equate age of haplotype group with age of population. Fix 1999: SE–NW cline (esp. HLA) could reflect disease selection under farming, not only demic diffusion — Cavalli-Sforza/Barbujani reply cline too complex for selection alone.
- **Bellwood’s lean:** 1996–2000 cultural-diffusion comeback despite overlapping 20–30% Neolithic contribution figures; disagreements are about **strength** of the wave of advance, not its existence. Europe is not one place — SW Asian component largest SE/south, smallest W/N. King & Underhill 2002: Y Eu9 correlation with painted pottery/figurines Levant–Anatolia–SE Europe–S France supports male demic diffusion; ancient LBK mtDNA of SW Asian origin (Jones 2001) = some female movement; Bentley et al. 2003 Sr isotopes = non-local (Mesolithic?) females marrying into riverine LBK. Bocquet-Appel 2002: rapid Neolithic birth-rate increase (~500-year rupture with forager stationary regime) — fertility of earliest farmers; does **not** alone prove demic diffusion. Pinhasi & Pluciennik: SE European Neolithic drawn from central Anatolian Neolithic (Çatalhöyük); Mediterranean more Mesolithic–Neolithic admixture than SE Europe. Vencl 1986: tenfold CE skeletal increase and complete replacement thesis (strong demic extreme).
- **With Chikhi and Barbujani:** strength of Neolithic advance was **considerable**; Mesolithic populations contributed genes clinally but “did not contribute very much to the resulting patterns of archaeology and language in Europe,” except temporary isolation. Agrees with Richards et al. 2003 that pure farming+language+genes grand synthesis should not be over-simple, yet still holds demic strength considerable. Pure demic alone never sole source — demic **with mixing**, sex-specific migration, bottlenecks (see [[demic-diffusion]]).
- **Language stake (Renfrew/Bellwood package):** IE spread south of the Baltic not associated with massive contact-induced change of the Austronesian-in-Melanesia type → suggests coherent population expansion; non-IE substrata and Basque/Paleoeuropean pockets as residual Mesolithic tips — language package densified on [[anatolian-hypothesis]] and [[steppe-hypothesis-debate]], not required by demic farming alone.

**Relation to Positions A–C on this page:** Bellwood densifies Position A demography and Renfrew’s language coupling while conceding Position C regional partitioning and continuous admixture; he rejects Position B as the *main* European mechanism. Chronological setup: after ~2,000 years Levant gestation, agricultural dispersal into Europe takes off after 7th-millennium reconfigurations; farming introduced from outside the agricultural homeland (analogous to Ali Kosh outside wild cereal ranges).

## Position layer — Barker *Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory* (2006)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006]]* Ch. 9–10. Genetics/isotopes **as of mid-2000s** (Haak et al. 2005; Bentley et al. 2003; Richards et al.). Do not adjudicate.)*

Barker’s Europe chapter is a **strong Position B lean** (piecemeal indigenous adoption and long forager–farmer interaction) with explicit rejection of simple *ex oriente lux* colonist waves as the primary story — while accepting that SW Asian domesticates ultimately supply the package.

### Strongest form (Barker 2006)

- **Package origin vs process:** Cereals and caprines are SW Asian domesticates, but **how** they enter Europe is regionally variable: southern Europe (Cardial/Impressed, Greek tells, Cyprus), Balkans (Starčevo–Körös–Vinča), LBK loess corridor, Baltic/Atlantic availability–substitution–consolidation, Britain/Ireland — each with different tempos and Mesolithic roles.
- **Against steady wave of advance:** Chronology of first domesticates does not fit steady SE→NW spread; variable intensity of domesticate use and long interaction with indigenous foragers sit uneasily with SW Asian colonist-farmer theory.
- **LBK:** Cultural package and long houses recorded; Barker stages leapfrog/enclave models against pure wave; Sr isotopes and early aDNA (Haak 2005) used to argue **complexity**, not a single demic verdict (**as of 2006**).
- **Complex foragers:** Ertebølle and other late Mesolithic seascapes as sophisticated systems that adopt farming on their own schedules (availability phase language).
- **Ideology and diet:** Push/pull/ideological factors at transition; isotope diet debates for Britain noted as contested.
- **Language:** If IE farming vocabulary is real, Barker prefers exchange/identity mechanisms over Renfrew-style Neolithic colonist IE (wheels/vehicles post-date EN; animal bias in lexicon).
- **Global frame (Ch. 10):** Demic diffusion models fail because they treat a highly mobile species as static until the Neolithic “event”; millet and other crop exchange shows long-distance transfer **without** mass migration.

**Relation:** Closest to Zvelebil / Rowley-Conwy indigenous-adoption and Mithen hybrid weightings; **opposes** strong demic readings of Ammerman–Cavalli-Sforza and Bellwood’s “considerable demic” European lean. Later genome-wide aDNA (post-2010) reopened demic strength — record as supersession pressure on Barker’s mid-2000s genetic citations, not silent overwrite.
