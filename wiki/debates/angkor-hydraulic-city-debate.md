## The Dispute

Whether the vast reservoirs (*baray*) and moats of [[angkor]] were the productive core of
an irrigation-based "hydraulic city" that fed a large population, or monumental works with
little direct agricultural function. The barays are enormous — Suryavarman I's Western
Baray held up to ~70 million m³ — so the question bears directly on how the Angkorian
[[mandala]]'s economic base, and its eventual decline, are understood. Recorded here from
Higham (1989, 2002); not adjudicated in wiki voice.

## Positions

**Hydraulic city (Bernard-Philippe Groslier).** Groslier (1979) held that the barays fed
a large distributary irrigation network sustaining intensive rice agriculture; he estimated
the system could water up to ~86,000 ha and yield ~126,000 tonnes of rice, sustaining on
the order of 600,000 people, and periodised the water works into successive royal phases.
On this reading the silting-up of the system contributed materially to Angkor's rise and
decline. Higham (2002) summarises this school as arguing that "the rise of the Angkor
state and, indeed, its decline and abandonment were determined by the success and failure
of the irrigation system."

**Sceptic (Willem van Liere; Higham 2002 leaning).** Van Liere (1980) argued there is **no
physical evidence** of distributary channels leading from the barays to the rice-fields;
the stored water, he held, never reached the fields and had no impact on food production.
The barays were thus primarily symbolic, ceremonial, or urban-supply features. Higham
(2002) strengthens this side: he reports Acker’s calculations that baray irrigation would
have supplied only ~2.5% of the population Groslier envisaged; notes that inscriptions are
silent on irrigation (in contrast to Sri Lankan epigraphy); rereads Zhou Daguan as not
describing irrigation agriculture; and concludes that the barays represent the oceans of
Mount Meru — **symbolic waterscapes** — rather than the basis of royal hydraulic power.
On this reading, irrigation should be **removed** from explanations of both Angkor’s rise
and its collapse. Higham’s preferred agricultural model is **decentralized** rain-fed
bunded rice (plus floating/flood-retreat and swidden) under grandee-family labour control:
"not a royally-inspired hydraulic civilization" (Higham 2002).

**Intermediate (Bennet Bronson; Higham 1989 lean).** Bronson (1978) accepted the water was
ultimately used for fields but judged the impact modest: at most ~6,700 ha for a single
four-month crop (~10,000 tonnes of rice, ~50,000 people/year). Higham (1989) had leaned
toward an intermediate reading — barays extending **continuity of supply** beyond the wet
season rather than raising raw yield — closer to Bronson than to Groslier. His 2002
synthesis moves clearly toward the sceptic pole on large-scale baray irrigation.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

All parties accept the scale and dating of the barays and moats and that Angkor sat in a
monsoon rain-shadow mitigated by the Tonle Sap fishery. They divide on whether stored
water was distributed to fields, on the magnitude of any agricultural effect, and on
whether hydraulic failure explains decline. Higham (2002) cautions that high estimates
ignore sedimentation, laterisation, evaporation losses, and non-irrigated food sources.

## Curator's Note

*(2026-07-10)* First recorded from Higham 1989 (intermediate lean).

*(2026-07-15)* Higham 2002 updates the same author's position toward the **sceptic** pole
(symbolic barays; decentralized rain-fed agriculture; remove irrigation from rise/collapse
explanations). Subsequent airborne-lidar survey of greater Angkor (post-dating both
volumes) has substantially reshaped the question by mapping extensive engineered water
networks; that evidence is **not** in either Higham source and must be added when later
