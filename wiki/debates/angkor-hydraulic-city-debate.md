## The Dispute

Whether the vast reservoirs (*baray*) and moats of [[angkor]] were the productive core of
an irrigation-based "hydraulic city" that fed a large population, or monumental works with
little direct agricultural function. The barays are enormous — Suryavarman I's Western
Baray held up to ~70 million m³ — so the question bears directly on how the
[[angkor|Angkorian]] [[mandala]]'s economic base, and its eventual decline, are
understood. As recorded by Higham (1989); this is a live interpretive dispute, not
adjudicated here.

## Positions

**Hydraulic city (Bernard-Philippe Groslier).** Groslier (1979) held that the barays fed
a large distributary irrigation network sustaining intensive rice agriculture; he estimated
the system could water up to ~86,000 ha and yield ~126,000 tonnes of rice, sustaining on
the order of 600,000 people, and periodised the water works into successive royal phases.
On this reading the silting-up of the system contributed materially to Angkor's decline.

**Sceptic (Willem van Liere).** Van Liere (1980) argued there is **no physical evidence**
of distributary channels leading from the barays to the rice-fields; the stored water,
he held, never reached the fields and had no impact on food production. The barays were
thus primarily symbolic, ceremonial, or urban-supply features.

**Intermediate (Bennet Bronson).** Bronson (1978) accepted the water was ultimately used
for fields but judged the impact modest: at most ~6,700 ha for a single four-month crop
(~10,000 tonnes of rice, ~50,000 people/year), considering the "hydraulic" claim
exaggerated.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

All parties accept the scale and dating of the barays and moats and that Angkor sat in a
monsoon rain-shadow mitigated by the Tonle Sap fishery. They divide on whether stored
water was distributed to fields, and on the magnitude of any agricultural effect. Higham
(1989) cautions that **both** the high and low estimates ignore sedimentation,
laterisation, evaporation losses, and non-irrigated or floating-rice food sources; he
suggests the barays' real function may have been to **extend continuity of supply beyond
the wet season** rather than to raise raw yield — an intermediate reading closer to
Bronson than to Groslier.

## Curator's Note

*(2026-07-10)* Subsequent airborne-lidar survey of greater Angkor (post-dating Higham
1989 and not part of this source) has substantially reshaped the question by mapping an
extensive engineered water network; the debate as recorded here reflects the state of
argument c. 1989 and should be updated when later sources are ingested.
