## Positions

**Position A — Long chronology / "long pause" model (dominant 1950s–1990s).** Based on radiocarbon dates obtained from the 1950s through the 1980s (Emory, Suggs, Sinoto), Eastern Polynesia was held to have been settled as early as circa 200 BCE–AD 1 (Marquesas), implying a "long pause" of six to nine centuries between the Western Polynesian ([[hane|Tongan/Samoan]]) settlement (circa 900–800 BCE) and the Eastern Polynesian dispersal. This position was supported at the time by historical linguists (Pawley 1996; Marck 2000), who argued that roughly 1,300 cataloged Proto Polynesian lexical innovations required a lengthy in-situ homeland period before the eastward dispersal.

**Position B — Short chronology / chronometric hygiene (Spriggs and Anderson 1993; consensus per Kirch as of 2017).** Spriggs and Anderson (1993) first proposed drastically compressed dates by applying "chronometric hygiene" — discarding radiocarbon dates that failed to meet strict sample-reliability criteria — placing the Marquesas at AD 300–600 and the central/northern/eastern archipelagoes at AD 600–950, with New Zealand at AD 1000–1200. Subsequent AMS (accelerator mass spectrometry) redating of short-lived, taxonomically identified samples (avoiding the "old wood"/driftwood in-built-age problem that had inflated the earlier dates) pushed the dates later still: Wilmshurst, Anderson, Higham, and colleagues, together with Kirch's own synthesis, converge on central/southeastern Eastern Polynesia settled circa AD 900–1100, [[hane|the Marquesas]] circa AD 900–1000 (Conte and Molle 2012 at Hane; Rolett 1998 at Hanamiai, Tahuata: AD 1025–1300), Hawai'i circa AD 940–1130, and New Zealand ([[maori|Māori]]) settled last, circa AD 1200–1300 (Wilmshurst et al. 2008 rat-bone dating: rat established by AD 1280; Wilmshurst and Higham 2004: rat-gnawed seeds AD 1140–1340).

## Methodological Cause of the Original Error

The long-chronology dates were compromised by (a) crude solid-carbon-counting radiocarbon methods with large standard errors, and (b) bulk-charcoal samples from hearths and ovens that incorporated old-growth or driftwood timber carrying "in-built age" older than the actual occupation event. AMS dating, introduced from the 1990s, permits ±15-year errors and direct dating of single small (about 10 mg) short-lived samples (seeds, twigs), resolving the problem. This methodological account is treated as an eligible wiki-voice statement once attributed to the chronometric-hygiene literature, since it concerns dating technique rather than a contested interpretation.

## Resolution

Resolved-by-consensus, per Kirch's own account (Kirch 2017), which frames the short-chronology revision as "closing the debate" with "a new consensus emerging."

## Residual Sub-Dispute: Rapa Nui

A narrower residual dispute survives within the broader short-chronology consensus. [[rapanui|Rapa Nui]]'s settlement date: Hunt and Lipo (2006, 2011) argued for colonization circa AD 1200, based on their own AMS redating program. Kirch, citing the full Anakena Bay AMS date set (including Steadman, Vargas, and Cristino 1994), places Rapa Nui's colonization at AD 1000–1100, treating Anakena as the resolving evidence against Hunt and Lipo's later date. This sub-dispute is presented by Kirch as effectively settled in favor of the earlier date, but is recorded here as the debate's one remaining live disagreement rather than folded silently into the general consensus.

## Cross-Class Convergence

The short chronology rests on convergent evidence from AMS [[radiocarbon-dating]] of short-lived plant and faunal remains (notably Pacific rat bone) and, on larger islands where primary habitation sites are difficult to locate, paleoenvironmental proxies (charcoal influx, pollen/forest-clearance signatures). All dates in this debate are treated as calibrated, AMS-era radiocarbon unless flagged otherwise; Suggs's original circa 150 BCE Ha'atuatua date is explicitly superseded, an "old wood" artifact of the earlier bulk-charcoal method, not current chronology.
