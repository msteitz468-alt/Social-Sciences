# The Said–Lewis Debate over Orientalism

The central scholarly controversy generated by *[[said-orientalism-1978|Orientalism]]*
pitted Edward [[said-edward|Said]] against the Princeton Orientalist historian **Bernard
Lewis**, himself one of the scholars Said criticized. The exchange (chiefly 1982, revived
in Said's 1994 Afterword) became emblematic of a broader dispute over whether Western
scholarship on the Islamic world is a vehicle of domination or a legitimate field of
inquiry.

## Position 1 — Said

Orientalism, in its strongest form, is a Western **[[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]]**
that produced "the Orient" as an object of knowledge inseparable from projects of imperial
control. The scholarship cannot be evaluated as if it floated free of power; its very
categories, tropes, and institutional forms are complicit. Lewis, on this view, is not a
neutral judge but a participant defending the guild — and his post-1967, post-1973
writings on Islam and the Arabs exemplify the political inflection of "expert" knowledge.

## Position 2 — Lewis

Orientalism as a scholarly tradition is a genuine, cumulative, self-correcting field of
philology and history, no more inherently imperialist than the study of classical
antiquity. Lewis charged Said (in "The Question of Orientalism," 1982) with factual and
chronological errors, a tendentiously selective canon (ignoring the great German
tradition, which had no significant imperial project in the East), guilt by association,
and an anti-scholarly relativism that would delegitimize the study of other cultures
altogether.

## Said's Specific Charges Against Lewis (in the book)

In "Orientalism Now," Said made Lewis his exhibit of politicized modern expertise
(attributed to Said): that Lewis traced the Arabic *thawra* ("revolution") etymologically to
a camel "rising up," in order to trivialize modern Arab revolutionary politics as mere
excitement; that Lewis retitled his 1964 essay "The Revolt of Islam" as "The Return of
Islam" in a 1976 *Commentary* version, shifting the agency of a described event "from
political leaders to instinctive communal loyalty" so as to make Islam appear an irrational,
anti-Semitic mass phenomenon; and that Lewis's writing systematically omits Zionist
colonization, the Israeli Emergency Defense Regulations, and the West Bank/Gaza settlements
while professing a historian's ethic of impartiality. In the 1994 Afterword Said also
rejected as false Lewis's analogy between criticizing Orientalism and attacking classical
philology, arguing "Orientalism and Hellenism are radically incomparable" because
Orientalism accompanied the colonial conquest of the very region it studied. Said further
denied ever having called Orientalism "a conspiracy" or the West "evil" — "I never did that
anyway."

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides agree that European scholarship on the East expanded alongside European empire,
and that some Orientalists served colonial administration. They disagree on what follows:
for Said the connection is constitutive of the knowledge; for Lewis it is contingent and
does not impugn the scholarship's validity. Both also agree the German philological
tradition was central to Orientalist scholarship — Said treats its omission as a
manageable qualification, Lewis as a fatal one.

## Current Standing

Unresolved and still live. Postcolonial studies broadly sided with Said and the book
became canonical; many historians and area specialists continue to press Lewis-style
objections (Robert Irwin's *For Lust of Knowing* (2006) is a book-length restatement).
Said replied to Lewis and other critics in the **1994 Afterword**, and reframed the whole
argument against the post-9/11 climate in the **2003 Preface**.

## Enacted in archaeology: Bahrani vs. Lewis (2010)

*(Attributed — Zainab Bahrani, "Conjuring Mesopotamia," in [[preucel-mrozowski-contemporary-archaeology-2010|Contemporary Archaeology in Theory]], 2010.)* The Said–Lewis dispute is
enacted within archaeology when Bahrani rebuts Bernard Lewis's claim (*The Shaping of the Modern
Middle East*, 1994) that only Greeks and Jews "survived with a continuing identity and memory" from
the ancient Middle East — a framing that, she argues, casts Assyrians, Babylonians, and Persians as
perpetual civilizational Others and severs ancient "Mesopotamia" from modern Iraq. See
[[orientalism]], [[said-orientalism-1978]].
