# Populism and the Origins of American Antisemitism

Were the Populists of the 1890s the source of twentieth-century American antisemitism, ordinary carriers of a general prejudice, or a scapegoat for a society-wide phenomenon?

## Shared factual ground

Documented in Dinnerstein (1994): agrarian antisemitic rhetoric emerged in the late 1870s amid post-1873 silver-demonetization distress, casting Jews as "detested middlemen" tied to eastern and international finance; Populist rhetoric used "Rothschild" and "Shylock" tropes (Ignatius Donnelly: Jews "alone thrive" under "bad laws" because they deal only in money); an AP reporter at the 1896 St. Louis convention recorded "extraordinary hatred of the Jewish race"; and Jews were nonetheless "not the major concern of Populists." Bryan's "cross of gold" imagery carried, in Dinnerstein's reading (attributed), a religious resonance rather than deliberate antisemitic intent.

## Positions

**Hofstadter/Handlin.** The 1950s accounts located the origins of modern American antisemitism in Populist ideology and rhetoric — part of Hofstadter's broader status-anxiety reading of agrarian protest.

**Revisionists.** 1960s scholarship defended the Populists: their antisemitism was rhetorical, marginal, and typical of the age; singling them out mistakes idiom for ideology.

**The society-wide reading.** Unger's *The Greenback Era* (1964) documented antisemitic tendencies among silverites and agrarians while embedding them in a wider monetary-politics culture; Dobkowski (1978) argued the depth and breadth of American antisemitism from the 1870s through the 1920s far exceeded what any origin-in-Populism story allows; Victor Ferkiss described antisemitism as "endemic throughout the rural West." Dinnerstein extends this line: the tropes predate Populism (Christian heritage, Rothschild myths from the 1830s press — see [[jewish-world-conspiracy-myth]]), pervaded urban elites, churches, and the South, and ran forward through Henry Ford's rural-Populist-era formation and 1980s farm-crisis antisemitism.

## Status

Partially resolved: few scholars now defend the strong Hofstadter thesis; the live disagreement concerns how much explanatory weight agrarian conspiracy culture carries within the broader account. Related pages: [[antisemitism]], [[dinnerstein-antisemitism-in-america-study]].
