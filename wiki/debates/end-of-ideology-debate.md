# End of Ideology Debate

## Positions

### End-of-ideology proponents (mid-century)

[[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] (*[[lipset-political-man-1960|Political Man]]*, Ch XIII), with Bell, Shils, Aron and others, described a decline in doctrinal intensity between democratic left and right in affluent industrial societies — illustrated by the 1955 Milan "Future of Freedom" conference. Concept: [[end-of-ideology]]. Related: [[ideology]].

### Critics

Critics argue the claim naturalized 1950s Atlantic consensus, ignored race, empire, and gender, and was overtaken by New Left, civil rights, religious politics, neoliberalism vs social democracy, and populism. Some read it as ideology *of* the center rather than end of ideology.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

European socialist parties did moderate nationalization programs in the 1950s; welfare-state baselines were widely accepted among major democratic parties in several countries. Whether that equals "end of ideology" is the interpretive dispute.
