# The Ur Royal Cemetery (Retainer-Burial Interpretation)

The Royal Cemetery of [[ur|Ur]], excavated by Sir Leonard Woolley (1922–34) and
dated to just before the First Dynasty of Ur (c. 2600 BCE), contained a small number
of exceptionally rich tombs (gold vessels and daggers, lyres, Puabi's headdress,
Meskalamdug's helmet) accompanied by **collective burials of attendants** — from 3 to
as many as 74 people, mostly women, apparently interred with the principal. Within
Mesopotamia the practice is attested essentially only here (and possibly at
[[kish|Kish]]). What it means is disputed.

## Positions

As presented by [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]]:

**Woolley's "living/substitute gods" reading (endorsed by Roux as plausible but
explicitly contestable).** The tomb-owners may have been regarded as living gods or
"substitute gods" — human stand-ins for the city's divine couple — and so entitled to
carry a whole household into the afterlife. Supporting threads: the epic "The Death of
Gilgamesh," which describes a king going to the grave with his retinue; the tradition
that Gilgamesh became a netherworld god; and his nearness in the king-list to
Meskalamdug's generation. The Sacred Marriage rite, in which the ruler played a divine
role, is invoked as the ideological backdrop.

**Skeptical reading.** The interpretation leans on a single literary text and a chain
of analogy. That most tombs were plundered in antiquity leaves the pattern of titles
(only two `lugal`/king and two `nin`/queen names among 17 inscribed tombs) hard to
read — a pattern Roux himself calls "disconcerting." Whether the attendant deaths were
willing ritual participation or coercion, and whether the occupants were kings, priests,
or something else, are left open.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides accept the material facts: the wealth of the principal tombs, the reality of
the accompanying multiple burials, and the near-absence of the practice elsewhere in
Mesopotamia. They differ on the *inference* from grave to belief system: the first
treats the burials as evidence of a specific royal-divinity ideology; the second holds
that the evidence underdetermines any single account. Roux's own verdict: "this theory,
as all others, is open to criticism, and the drama of the Cemetery of Ur remains a
mystery."

## Bioarchaeological Reexamination (Baadsgaard et al. 2011; Vidale 2011)

Van De Mieroop 2016 reports modern forensic work on the attendant skeletons that directly addresses the voluntary-vs-coerced question the curator's note below had flagged as outstanding. CT-scan and forensic analysis of the Ur skulls (Baadsgaard, Monge, and Zettler 2011) found evidence of **blunt-force cranial trauma**, and a related reanalysis (Vidale 2011) points to death by **a pointed instrument** followed by deliberate post-mortem treatment — bodies were heated and embalmed (including with mercury) and re-dressed before final burial. This overturns the specific mechanism in Woolley's original account, which held the attendants died willingly by ingesting poison in a calm, ceremonial final act (Woolley 1982). The new evidence establishes that the attendants died by violence, not by a peaceful self-administered death — a factual update to the "how did they die" question, though it does not by itself resolve whether the killing was ritually sanctioned (per Woolley's or Cohen's 2005 legitimation reading), coercive (Dickson 2006), or reflects war-captive status. (Attributed: Baadsgaard et al. 2011; Vidale 2011, as reported in Van De Mieroop 2016.)

## Comparative Textual Evidence and the "Human Grave Goods" Reading (Potts 1997)

[[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts (1997)]] adds comparative textual evidence bearing directly on this debate and stakes out a distinct position on what the Royal Graves' additional burials should be called (attributed).

**Grave goods for the underworld: Ur-Nammu's death text and the Lugalanda/Ninenishe burial.** A lament recounting Ur-Nammu's death (Kramer 1967:114) describes him offering weapons, warrior's equipment, oxen, kids, and fattened sheep as gifts to Nergal, "Enlil of the Netherworld." P. Steinkeller (1990:21 n.29; 1980) analyzed a pre-Sargonic text (DP 75) recording extensive goods given by Lugalanda of Lagash to his son Ur-tarsirsira on the burial of the son's wife Ninenishe — a boxwood threshing sledge (compared by Steinkeller to Pu-abi's sledge in the Royal Cemetery), a team of she-mules, furniture, combs, spindles, cosmetics equipment, jewellery (including six carnelian necklaces), and a slave girl. Steinkeller connected this text to human-sacrifice practice as extensively discussed earlier by Gadd (1960) and Green (1975) with reference to the Ur Royal Cemetery, where up to eighty individuals were interred in a single Royal Grave; per H. Schutzinger, these individuals were treated as grave goods themselves rather than as persons receiving proper funerary rites (position, attributed to Schutzinger).

**A methodological warning against reading grave goods as status markers.** A second pre-Sargonic text from Adab (OIP 104: App. to no. 32), roughly contemporary with DP 75, shows similar goods deposited as part of a field-purchase payment intended for a wife's grave upon her death ("she dies and lives buried together with him"). Potts argues such texts warn against assuming grave goods directly reflect an individual's status or social role in life, and that the artificial distinction sometimes drawn (e.g. by Strommenger 1957–1971:605–8) between "personal belongings" and "grave goods" finds no confirmation in the written record — a methodological caution bearing directly on any inference from the Royal Cemetery's material wealth to the tomb-owners' living status or divinity.

**Burial-form typology.** Potts catalogues the wide range of Mesopotamian grave forms attested across periods — simple pit inhumation, mat burials, shaft graves, shaft-to-brick-chamber/sarcophagus burials, sherd burials, jar burials (household vessels for infants in early periods; purpose-made vessels from Ur III onward), vat burials, wooden/reed sarcophagi (at Ur, from late Jamdat Nasr), ceramic "bath-tub" sarcophagi (from ED II), later flexed-burial sarcophagi, large stone sarcophagi for Neo-Assyrian queens, anthropoid sarcophagi (Achaemenid, possible Egyptian influence), and Seleucid/Parthian "slipper coffins." Clay pipes inserted into sarcophagus lids in Neo-Babylonian intra-mural burials and Old Babylonian Mari burials are read by Groneberg (1990:257) as a means of tending the dead — connecting burial architecture to the *kispum* ancestor-offering practice (see [[mesopotamian-religion]], [[household]]).

**Potts's position: retainer burial, not "collective burial."** Potts argues Mesopotamian burial was, from the Ubaid period onward, overwhelmingly primary and individual, with little evidence of ossuaries or true collective burial — a marked contrast with the Oman peninsula, where hundreds were buried in communal tombs during certain periods. On this comparative basis, Potts explicitly declines to classify the Ur Royal Graves as "collective burial": "the additional persons buried alongside that person must be considered 'human grave goods'" — his view being that each Royal Grave's primary purpose was interment of a single individual, with the retainers functioning analytically as grave goods rather than as co-equal burial subjects. This is a **third position** in the debate, orthogonal to the Woolley "living/substitute gods" reading and the skeptical reading above: it does not adjudicate *why* the principal was accorded such treatment, but does take a definite stand on how the retainer deaths should be classified relative to comparative Mesopotamian burial practice — recorded here as Potts's own position, not merged with the existing two.
