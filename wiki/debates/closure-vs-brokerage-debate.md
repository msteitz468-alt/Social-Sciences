# Closure vs. Brokerage

A foundational dispute within contemporary network sociology over **what network
structure produces social capital**. Two accounts prescribe *opposite* shapes for
the same ego-network. The disagreement is genuine and theoretical — both sides use
network analysis, both call the resource "social capital," and they diverge on the
structural condition that creates it.

> No adjudication in wiki voice. Each position is stated in its strongest form
> below, followed by the shared factual ground and a dated curator's note.

## Position 1 — Closure ([[coleman-james|Coleman]])

Social capital arises from **network closure**: dense, redundant, interconnected
relationships. *(Attributed — [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman]] 1988/1990.)*
Closure is what makes **norms and sanctions** enforceable (a wrongdoer can be
collectively punished only if the injured parties are connected), what sustains
**trust** and reliable obligation, and what supports **intergenerational**
transmission (parents who know each other's children can monitor and reinforce).
Where the network is open — full of [[structural-holes|structural holes]] — the
third party escapes sanction and norms decay. On this account brokerage across
holes is precisely the *problem* closure solves.

## Position 2 — Brokerage ([[burt-ronald-s|Burt]])

Social capital arises from **brokerage across structural holes**: sparse networks
that reach *non-redundant* contacts. *(Attributed — [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]] 1992.)*
Redundant, closed networks waste resources on overlapping information and offer no
room to negotiate; the player who spans holes as the [[tertius-gaudens|tertius
gaudens]] gets diverse, early, non-redundant information *and* control advantages,
and Burt's two studies find such players earn higher market profits and earlier
promotions ([[burt-structural-holes-study]]). On this account closure forgoes the
information and control benefits that constitute social capital's competitive
payoff.

## Position 3 — Complementarity (Burt 2005 synthesis)

*(Attributed — [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]], *[[burt-brokerage-and-closure-2005|Brokerage and Closure]]* 2005.)* Closure and brokerage do **different jobs** and are **twice complementary**:

1. **Joint production of social capital.** Brokerage increases *variation* (access, timing, control; [[vision-advantage|vision]] / good ideas). Closure decreases variation and supplies the trust, reputation, and collaborative alignment needed to *deliver* brokerage value. Advantage is highest for **[[network-closure|closure]] within a group combined with brokerage beyond the group** — Burt’s [[structural-autonomy|structural autonomy]] model and stylized fact #3 (Fig. 3.5).
2. **Mutual cure for failure modes.** Unrestrained brokerage risks organization *chaos* (conflicting goals, units competing); closure’s reputation mechanism realigns. Unrestrained closure risks organization *arthritis* ([[bandwidth-echo|echo]]: amplified trust *and* distrust, groupthink, “ignorant certainty”); brokerage cracks inertia with selection and synthesis.

Burt restates four **stylized facts**: (1) brokers do better; (2) vision is the mechanism of brokerage returns; (3) performance peaks at closure-within + brokerage-beyond; (4) closure reinforces existing structure (sticky ties, stable reputations). He prefers integrating the Coleman and holes traditions via structural autonomy over choosing one structure exclusively.

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

- Both sides agree network **structure** — not just who you know but how your
  contacts are connected to each other — has real consequences; they disagree on
  which structure pays. (Coleman: connected contacts; Burt 1992: disconnected
  contacts; Burt 2005: *both*, sequenced by task.)
- Both agree the payoff is **contingent on task and position**. Burt's manager
  study documents that brokerage returns *reverse* for women and entry-rank men;
  2005 adds that brokerage pays more for non-routine work with few peers.
- Burt 2005 defines **trust** as committing to a relationship before knowing how
  the other will behave, and treats closure as amplifying both trust and
  **distrust** under the [[bandwidth-echo|echo]] mechanism — not only Coleman-style
  productive trust.
- Both descend from the same milieu ([[harrison-white|Harrison White]]'s network
  program; [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]'s weak ties are a brokerage-side
  antecedent), and both are strands of the broader
  [[social-capital-theory|social-capital]] literature alongside
  [[social-capital|Bourdieu's]] classed capital and Putnam's civic tradition.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-10)

Primaries now include Coleman *[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|Foundations]]*, Burt *[[burt-structural-holes-1992|Structural Holes]]*, and Burt *[[burt-brokerage-and-closure-2005|Brokerage and Closure]]*. Position 3 is no longer a gesture: it is Burt’s own 2005 program statement with multi-sample corroboration (supply-chain managers, bankers, electronics, secondary cases). The debate remains **open** as a field-level choice of emphasis — many studies still treat closure and brokerage as rivals rather than as Burt’s dual surface — and Lin’s social-resources theory is still not ingested. No wiki-voice adjudication of which structure “wins.”
