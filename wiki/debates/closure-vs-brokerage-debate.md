# Closure vs. Brokerage

A foundational dispute within contemporary network sociology over **what network
structure produces social capital**. Two accounts prescribe *opposite* shapes for
the same ego-network. The disagreement is genuine and theoretical — both sides use
network analysis, both call the resource "social capital," and they diverge on the
structural condition that creates it.

> No adjudication in wiki voice. Each position is stated in its strongest form
> below, followed by the shared factual ground and a dated curator's note.

## Position 1 — Closure ([[coleman-james|Coleman]])

Social capital arises from **network closure**: dense, redundant, interconnected
relationships. *(Attributed — [[social-capital-coleman|Coleman]] 1988/1990.)*
Closure is what makes **norms and sanctions** enforceable (a wrongdoer can be
collectively punished only if the injured parties are connected), what sustains
**trust** and reliable obligation, and what supports **intergenerational**
transmission (parents who know each other's children can monitor and reinforce).
Where the network is open — full of [[structural-holes|structural holes]] — the
third party escapes sanction and norms decay. On this account brokerage across
holes is precisely the *problem* closure solves.

## Position 2 — Brokerage ([[burt-ronald-s|Burt]])

Social capital arises from **brokerage across structural holes**: sparse networks
that reach *non-redundant* contacts. *(Attributed — [[burt-ronald-s|Burt]] 1992.)*
Redundant, closed networks waste resources on overlapping information and offer no
room to negotiate; the player who spans holes as the [[tertius-gaudens|tertius
gaudens]] gets diverse, early, non-redundant information *and* control advantages,
and Burt's two studies find such players earn higher market profits and earlier
promotions ([[burt-structural-holes-study]]). On this account closure forgoes the
information and control benefits that constitute social capital's competitive
payoff.

## Position 3 — Complementarity (later synthesis)

*(Attributed.)* Closure and brokerage do **different jobs**: brokerage generates
new value (vision, information, opportunity) across groups, while closure helps
*realize* value within a group (trust, coordination, execution). The two are
jointly optimal — a broker also needs cohesion behind them. Burt himself moves
toward this position in *Brokerage and Closure* (2005 — not yet ingested).

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

- Both sides agree network **structure** — not just who you know but how your
  contacts are connected to each other — has real consequences; they disagree on
  which structure pays. (Coleman: connected contacts; Burt: disconnected contacts.)
- Both agree the payoff is **contingent on task and position**. Burt's own manager
  study documents that brokerage returns *reverse* for women and entry-rank men,
  who benefit from a sponsored, more hierarchical (closure-like) network — a
  finding closure theorists read as evidence for their side and Burt reads as
  "borrowed social capital."
- Both descend from the same milieu ([[harrison-white|Harrison White]]'s network
  program; [[granovetter-mark-s|Granovetter]]'s weak ties are a brokerage-side
  antecedent), and both are strands of the broader
  [[social-capital-theory|social-capital]] literature alongside
  [[social-capital|Bourdieu's]] classed capital and Putnam's civic tradition.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-09)

The wiki currently holds both primaries — Coleman via
*[[coleman-foundations-social-theory-1990|Foundations]]* and Burt via
*[[burt-structural-holes-1992|Structural Holes]]* — but not Burt's own 2005
synthesis or Lin's social-resources theory, so the "complementarity" position is
recorded from the primaries' own gestures rather than from an ingested statement.
The debate is **open**: the field largely treats closure and brokerage as
context-dependent rather than one refuting the other.
