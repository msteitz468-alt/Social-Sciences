# Modernization vs Dependency / World-Systems Debate

Standing theoretical dispute over the unit of analysis, the causes of cross-national inequality, and whether "development" is a national catch-up path or a relational position in a polarizing world-economy.

## Positions (strongest form)

### Modernization / developmentalism

National societies are the units. Economic development (industrialization, education, urbanization, wealth) supports democracy and "modern" institutions ([[modernization-theory]]; [[lipset-seymour-martin|Lipset]] and others). Backwardness is primarily internal (traditional values, institutions); integration into world markets can aid catch-up.

### Dependency

Underdevelopment is produced by historical integration into capitalism on unequal terms. Core–periphery structures and dependency relations explain persistent inequality. National liberation and structural change (not mere growth) are required. (Primary dependency monographs not yet ingested — position recorded from secondary citation in this vault's world-systems source.)

### World-systems analysis

The unit is the [[capitalist-world-economy|capitalist world-economy]] as a historical system. [[core-periphery|Core–periphery]] (and [[semiperiphery]]) positions are structural; national "development" is mobility within a polarizing system. [[wallerstein-immanuel|Wallerstein]], [[amin-samir|Amin]], [[arrighi-giovanni|Arrighi]], and others. Globalization rhetoric is treated as phase-ideology, not a new theory of convergence.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

All sides observe large cross-national income and power gaps in the nineteenth–twentieth centuries and late-twentieth globalization of trade and finance. They disagree on **mechanism** (internal modernization vs relational exploitation/polarization) and **unit** (nation vs world-economy).
