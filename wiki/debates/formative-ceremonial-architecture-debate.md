# Formative Andean Monumental Architecture: State Power or Nonhierarchical Devotion?

Does crossing a threshold of labor investment in nonresidential architecture indicate at least incipient centralized political power? The Initial Period / [[formative-stage]] monumental centers of coastal and highland Peru (ca. 1800–1000 BCE) are the battleground.

## Position A — Political centralization (Sheila and Thomas Pozorski)

Excavating in the Casma Valley since 1979 (Huaynuná, Las Aldas, Pampa de las Llamas-Moxeke, Sechín Alto), the Pozorskis conclude: "Clearly, the Initial Period polities were very advanced and had attained a state level of political development" (S. & T. Pozorski 1987:126). At Pampa de las Llamas-Moxeke they read the Moxeke mound as the seat of religious power and Huaca A as a secular storage/distribution center — paired evidence of "centralized authority at the site" (1986:383–84). Yet at [[huaca-de-los-reyes]], Thomas Pozorski's own labor analysis (350,000 person-days achievable by ~240 workers in under 25 years, working agricultural off-seasons) led him to concede that "organizational efforts need not have been as substantial or as rigid as might be supposed" (Pozorski 1980:104), and the Pozorskis describe that site's function as "fundamentally religious" (1994:64) — an internal nuance worth preserving.

## Position B — Cumulative devotion without centralization (Richard Burger and Lucy Salazar-Burger)

From Huaricoto, Cardal, and Mina Perdida: monumental complexes were "the net result of myriad small construction episodes throughout centuries of occupation." Huaricoto's small kiva-like chambers were buildable through "rotating ritual authority" mobilizing labor via kinship and friendship (1986:69); Cardal's mound accumulated over ~400–500 years, achievable by ~100 people working two months a year — "a rather modest lifestyle" (1991:292–93), explicitly contrasted with Giza and Teotihuacan. Ritual paraphernalia (the fanged Mina Perdida effigy) is interpreted through ethnographic analogy with Amazonian shamanism — an analogy the authors themselves flag as controversial, resting on a posited shared Andean-Amazonian substratum of belief (1998:45).

## Position C — Reframing: the basis of religious authority (Jerry Moore)

Moore (1996b, 2005) argues both sides mistake the variable. Applying Sullivan's (1988) typology (medium / ecstatic shaman / canonist) to eight ethnographic South American cases, he finds ecstatic shamans never produce permanent, regional, public-scale ceremonial architecture (they work in dwellings, plazas, men's houses), while canonists (Warao, Kogi) do build temples and durable sacred centers. Formative sites sort accordingly on permanence/ubiquity/visibility: the permanent, regional, axial-processional centers ([[huaca-de-los-reyes]], Purulén, Montegrande, Sechín Alto) imply canon-based religious authority — neither state administration nor ecstatic shamanism. Against Burger's incrementalism, Moore points to ritual entombment (Cardal's buried and replicated staircase; [[kotosh]]'s interred temples) as intentional continuity-marking practice characteristic of canonists. He concedes the Burger position "may be correct" on the absence of a state.

## Shared factual ground

Monument scale alone is a poor index of political organization — all parties' labor estimates converge on modest workforces; the disagreement is over what social form organized them. Related sub-disputes: Conklin vs. Pozorski on Huaca de los Reyes's construction sequence; Bonnier's "Mito tradition" vs. Burger/Salazar-Burger's "Kotosh Religious Tradition" periodization (see [[kotosh]]); Williams's temple-field vs. Lathrap's cayman reading of U-shaped complexes.
