## Shared Factual Ground

Both positions agree that Indo-European is a real, comparatively reconstructible language family (see [[indo-european-languages]]) that must have had a single geographic point of origin and a subsequent dispersal. Both agree the dispersal is prehistoric and therefore not directly attested in any surviving text. Following the 2015–2018 ancient-DNA record summarized by [[reich-david|Reich]] ([[reich-who-we-are-2018]]), both positions must now account for two independently documented, demographically large population movements: an Anatolian-farmer-descended expansion into Europe beginning roughly 9,000 years ago, and a steppe-descended ([[yamnaya-culture]]) expansion into Europe beginning roughly 5,000 years ago (as of Reich 2018). The dispute is over which of these movements — or what combination — carried Indo-European speech, and where the language family's ultimate point of origin lay.

Anthony (2007) further records that proposed homelands have ranged from India and the Altai to Germany, Anatolia, and “Atlantis,” and that the debate has run for almost two hundred years in academic, political, and absurd registers alike — context for why attributed voice and non-adjudication remain mandatory here.

## Position A: Steppe/Kurgan Hypothesis

**Held by:** Marija Gimbutas (originator of the Kurgan hypothesis); J. P. Mallory; David Anthony (*[[anthony-horse-wheel-language-2007|The Horse, the Wheel, and Language]]*, 2007); and, per Reich (2018), supported on genetic grounds by [[reich-david|Reich]]'s own lab findings.

### Gimbutas 1991 — primary Kurgan package (now ingested)

*(Attributed — [[gimbutas-marija|Marija Gimbutas]], *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]* 1991, esp. Ch.10 “The End of Old Europe.” This is Position A’s foundational primary voice; Anthony 2007 below is a **refinement**, not a replacement. All claims here are hers — not wiki voice.)*

**Framing.** Gimbutas labels Proto- or Early Indo-Europeans as **“Kurgan” people** who arrived from southern Russia on horseback; first contact with Old Europe’s borderlands (Lower Dnieper, west of the Black Sea) began mid-5th millennium B.C., with continuous flow lasting two millennia. She coined the blanket term **“Kurgan tradition”** (1956) for seminomadic pastoralists who built round funeral mounds (Russian *kurgan*, from Turkish = barrow/tumulus). The end of her **Old Europe** (Neolithic Europe before the Indo-Europeans: settled agriculture, Goddess-centered symbolic system) is, in her account, a transformative clash of two ideologies, social structures, and economies — comparable in trauma to the conquest of the American continent — producing a later European **“marble cake”** of non-IE and IE elements with a strong non-IE linguistic/mythological substratum (Gimbutas 1991).

**OE vs Kurgan contrast (her binary).** She contrasts Old Europe as peaceful, sedentary, matrifocal, matrilineal, and sex-egalitarian with Kurgans as warlike, patriarchal, hierarchical pastoralists with pit graves under cairn/mound, rudimentary agriculture, and seasonal semi-subterranean settlements. Burial ideologies are “entirely distinct”: OE emphasize cyclic regeneration from the body of the Great Mother (tomb-as-womb; few personal grave goods); IE emphasize lineal continuity of powerful males, mortuary houses, personal goods including tools, weapons, horses, wives/servants/slaves, and lavish royal tombs (Gimbutas 1991). Load-bearing dual-ideology statement (verbatim): “The Old European and Indo-European belief systems are diametrically opposed… There is no possibility that this pattern of social organization could have developed out of the Old European matrilineal, matricentric, and endogamic balanced society. Therefore, the appearance of the Indo-Europeans in Europe represent a collision of two ideologies, not an evolution.”

**Three infiltration waves (her chronology).** “The Kurgan tradition became manifest in Old European territories during three waves of infiltration: I at c. 4400–4300 B.C., II at c. 3500 B.C., and III soon after 3000 B.C. This chronology does not represent the evolution of a single group but of a number of various steppe peoples who shared a common tradition…” (Gimbutas 1991). Her Russian-archaeology glossary: **early Yamna = Kurgan I**; **Mikhailovka I / Maikop = Kurgan II**; **late Yamna = Kurgan III** (*yama* = pit under barrow). These are **her** correlations of named archaeological packages with wave labels — `associated_with`, not identity of culture with people or language. See [[yamnaya-culture]], [[sredni-stog-culture]], [[corded-ware-culture]].

| Wave | Date (cal B.C., her figure) | Source area (her claim) | Diagnostic package (her inventory) | Principal impact zones (her claim) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-wave / Kurgan formation | ~5000–4500 | Middle Volga forest-steppe → Lower Volga; Sredniy Stog II into Dnieper ~4500 | Horse cult, shell-tempered pottery, weapons, mobile pastoralism | Steppe north of Black Sea; first Dnieper contact mid-5th mill. |
| Wave 1 (Kurgan I) | c. 4400–4300 | Lower Volga / Early Yamna (= Kurgan I); via Lower Dnieper | Round barrows, pit graves, male elite, horse-head scepters, suttee, weapons usable from horseback | Moldavia, S Romania, E Hungary; catastrophic for Varna, Karanovo-Gumelniţa, Vinča, Lengyel; Cucuteni survives; LBK/Rössen hill forts; single graves → Ireland/England by mid-4th mill. |
| Wave 2 | c. 3500 (2nd half 4th mill.) | North Pontic Maikop / Mikhailovka I (not Volga) | Elite mortuary houses, stone cupolas, orthostats/stelae, arsenical bronze, apsidal hill-fort houses, wagons/ox teams | Cucuteni → Usatovo amalgamation; Baden–Vučedol–Ezero; Globular Amphora; hill-fort rule of central Europe; Alpine stelae |
| Wave 3 | soon after 3000 (~3000–2900) | Late Yamna (Lower Dnieper–Don–Volga) into E-central Europe | Deep pit graves, timber huts in pits, ochre, low barrows + stone rings/ditches, poor inventory + elite pins/daggers | Romania, Bulgaria, Yugoslavia (Banat), E Hungary; pushes Vučedol W/S; Corded Pottery expansion; later Bell Beaker as Vučedol+Yamna amalgam |

**Wave 1 impact (her narrative).** Earliest Kurgan graves in Moldavia dated Cucuteni A2–A3, c. 4400–4300 B.C.; almost exclusively male elite barrows vs even male–female ratios in contemporary OE cemeteries. Suvorovo (Moldavia) and Casimcea (Dobruja): chieftains with horse-headed scepters; she reads suttee (female consort sacrificed) and horse cult as first appearance of IE ideology in east-central Europe. For Karanovo-Gumelniţa, Vinča, Varna, and Lengyel she claims catastrophic disruption and a dislocation cascade into Yugoslavia, Hungary, and the Upper Danube; Cucuteni survives Wave 1 intact (~800 years coexistence) until Wave 2 amalgamation. Central European transformation around 4000 B.C. — mixed agricultural-pastoral economy, classed patriarchal society, hill-fort rule — she interprets as successful **Indo-Europeanization** via collision and gradual **hybridization**, not indigenous gradual development (Gimbutas 1991).

**Wave 2–3 and Corded/Beaker aftershocks (her narrative).** Wave 2 turns North Pontic/Maikop packages on still-flourishing Cucuteni; Usatovo = coexisting unequal Kurgan elite + indigenous Cucuteni; Baden–Vučedol–Ezero and Globular Amphora as amalgams under Kurgan dominance. Wave 3 late Yamna (~3000 B.C.) = massive infiltration and final Indo-Europeanization; Corded Pottery (= Battle Axe) expansion into former TRB territories as Wave 3 repercussion — she **prefers** Corded as later phase of Globular Amphora pushed N/NE by Yamna influx rather than new Yamna people from the east; Bell Beaker as Vučedol + Late Yamna amalgam (Vinkovci-Samogyvár parent) (Gimbutas 1991).

**Horse-riding mechanism (her model).** She places horse domestication between eastern Ukraine and northern Kazakhstan around 5000 B.C. or earlier (tarpan, not Przewalski); cites Dereivka horse-bone dominance and antler cheek pieces, Repin ~80% horse, etc. Riding, she argues, cut travel time by a factor of five or more, enabled cattle-looting/horse-stealing raids, wealth accumulation, trade, violence, and outward migration once the steppe was “conquered.” Pastoral economy + large herds + riding + male strength to control animals contributed, in her view, to transition from “matrism” to “armored patriarchy” in southern Russia by ~5000 B.C. at latest. *(Later hygiene: Anthony 2007 records that the famous Dereivka cult-stallion bit-wear claim was retracted as Scythian intrusion — method lesson for her early riding package, not a dismissal of the steppe homeland core.)*

**IE equation (her method).** Reconstruct PIE culture from comparative IE linguistics and mythology, supported by early historic records, and **equate** with Kurgan archaeology of the 5th-millennium Volga forest-steppe/steppe and North Pontic; linguistic homeland must lie between Finno-Ugric, Semitic, and Caucasian families; consolidation possible ~5000–4500 before “proto-Indo-European outburst into Europe.” Linguistic inventory she aligns with Kurgan: sheep/cattle/pig/horse/goat/dog; wool and weaving; cattle as wealth; horse as sacred riding animal; weapons lexicon matching archaeological kit; vehicle *wegh-* and wheel *rotha*; metallurgy lacks common IE word — fits Kurgan I without metallurgy, copper via Cucuteni barter, metallurgy from Transcaucasia in Wave 2. PIE social structure (her reconstruction): patrilineal warrior-chief leadership; exogamous marriage into patrilocal families; *pot/*dompoti warrior-patriarch; nested *domos/*weik/*gen/*teuta* (Gimbutas 1991).

**Hybridization / “marble cake” outcome.** Collision of OE and IE symbolic structures yields hybridization in which IE prevails while OE survives as undercurrent: “The functions and images of Old European and Indo-European deities, beliefs in an afterlife, and the entirely different sets of symbols prove the existence of two contrasting religions and mythologies. Their collision in Europe resulted in the hybridization of two symbolic structures in which the Indo-European prevailed while the Old European survived as an undercurrent.” Four-stage conclusion 4500–2500 B.C.: (1) Wave 1 shock and Danube shifts; (2) Wave 2 hill-fort conversion and matristic→patriarchal process; (3) Wave 3 late Yamna + Corded/Vučedol extensions NE/NW/S; (4) Bell Beaker as last large warlike horse-riding outmigration to W Europe (Gimbutas 1991). Transitions she lists: matrilineal→patrilineal; learned theacracy→militant patriarchy; sexually balanced→male-dominated hierarchy; chthonic goddess religion→IE sky-oriented pantheon.

**What this layer is not:** Adjudication of ethnic-replacement scale, “peaceful OE,” suttee-as-IE-ideology, 5th-millennium riding as settled science, or pots=language equations — all remain **Gimbutas positions**. Renfrew’s Anatolian alternative, Mallory’s 1989 survey synthesis, and Anthony’s wagon/wool termini, franchising mechanism, and narrowed Yamnaya core are successive attributed layers below/above.

### Mallory 1989 — English survey synthesis of Position A (now ingested)

*(Attributed — [[mallory-j-p|J. P. Mallory]], *[[mallory-in-search-of-the-indo-europeans-1989|In Search of the Indo-Europeans]]* 1989 / 1991 pb. Pre-aDNA. Principal late-1980s English-language survey of the steppe case; Anthony 2007 repeatedly credits Mallory with Gimbutas as thirty-year Pontic-Caspian advocates. Not identical to Gimbutas’s dual-structure Old Europe package.)*

**Frame.** Mallory places himself in the “conventional wisdom” of IE studies against [[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]’s 1987 challenge and against Gamkrelidze–Ivanov’s Armenian/southern Caucasus solution. The book is dual-aim: general survey of earliest IE peoples *and* homeland argument — “not simply… yet another solution.” Indo-European is “fundamentally a linguistic concept”; ethnonyms are short-hand for speakers with “no necessary reference to a specific physical type or material culture.” Prehistoric BCE dates in the book are tree-ring-calibrated radiocarbon (BC = cal BC).

**Homeland definition.** The homeland is not a singularity or “Big Bang” but “the spatial expression of a vaguely defined temporal division of that linguistic continuum.” Temporal frame from cultural vocabulary ~**4500–2500 BC**; postulated area ~250,000–1,000,000 km². Consensus band he reports: **Dnieper to Ural**, fifth–third millennia BC — “embraced by most scholars” engaged in earliest IE study, while precise borders remain disputed (Mallory 1989).

**Constraint stack (Ch.6).** Neighbours: **Finno-Ugric/Uralic** strongest (early loans when FU still undifferentiated; forest-steppe Volga–Ural contact most economical); Semitic and Kartvelian comparisons weaker/more distant; Altaic insufficient for Altai homeland. Internal linguistics: centre-of-gravity toward Central–Eastern Europe; Anatolian early separation; substrates denser on periphery. [[linguistic-palaeontology]]: cultural vocabulary time-depth roughly 4th millennium BC (hard bounds ~5000–2500 BC); beech argument limited; salmon argument (Diebold) can support Pontic-Caspian; horse “on balance” better supports steppe/forest-steppe; wheeled vehicles 4th millennium. Archaeology–language: “pots do not equal people” but “equally perverse to assume that there can be no correlation.”

**Vs Renfrew / Anatolian hypothesis (Position B).** Mallory’s cumulative case: Anatolia historically non-IE (Hattic, Hurrian); farming colonists may have brought *some* language but unlikely PIE; Renfrew’s ~6500 BC expansion incongruent with PIE vocabulary (wheels, horse, secondary products, silver, beaver, social terms); cannot explain PIE–Uralic similarities; fails Indo-Iranian/Tocharian via NW Black Sea farming wave; dialect time-depths incongruent; Linear A unreadable if IE in Crete since 6000 BC. Load-bearing verdict: **“Anatolia is the wrong place at the wrong time and migrations from it give the wrong results. A brave run, perhaps, but Renfrew’s solution is not a convincing one”** (Mallory 1989). Balkan homeland (Diakonov et al.) treated as “Anatolian-Aegean once removed”; Gamkrelidze–Ivanov Armenia/S. Caucasus without archaeological support he finds convincing.

**Kurgan package (as Mallory receives it).** He presents [[gimbutas-marija|Gimbutas]]’s Kurgan tradition as the attractive steppe solution many accept: congruent with reconstructed culture, multi-trait package, expansions into Europe/Corded Ware, Caucasus–Anatolia, southern Siberia. Opposition he records is often *not* that Pontic-Caspian was non-IE, but that the homeland must have been *larger* — “no alternative homeland from which archaeologists would derive *all* of the cultures of our late Indo-European territory.” He also records critics who say invasion arguments are better explained without Kurgan expansions, and commits Ch.7–8 to testing a more confined homeland.

**Archaeological sequence (Ch.7–8, as of 1989).** Pontic-Caspian Neolithic as **native European** populations adopting ceramics/livestock (not West Asian farmer colonists east of Criş/LBK/Tripolye). Eneolithic ~4500–2500 BC: Sredny Stog, Khvalynsk, etc., culminating in **[[yamnaya-culture|Yamnaya]] cultural-historical area** (~3600–2200 BC, >70 ¹⁴C dates; Merpert nine variants; Bug/Dniester to Ural/Emba) as last entity putatively assignable a PIE date. Expansions: strong eastward case ([[afanasievo-culture]] ↔ Yamnaya package; [[andronovo-culture]] as Indo-Iranian staging); SE Europe/W Anatolia best archaeological case (sceptres → Usatovo hybrids → 3rd-millennium Yamnaya kurgans; NW Anatolia/Troy preferred over Caucasus for Anatolian IE); Corded Ware as NW vector for Germanic/Baltic/Slavic (± Celtic/Italic) with evidentiary caution — “enough evidence to charge, but not enough to convict” on steppe–Corded genetic relationship. Mechanism: migration/infiltration + bilingualism; pastoral mobility; social permeability/clientship over pure military destiny — “Indo-Europeans do not always win.” Recapitulation: PIE evolved from Pontic-Caspian hunter-fishing languages into a **4500–2500 interaction sphere**, not a single isolated tribe.

**Reflexivity (Ch.9).** Mallory historicizes the Aryan myth: racial supremacy was misappropriation of a linguistic concept grafted onto existing prejudices, not a necessary consequence of comparative philology; Max Müller’s blast against “Aryan race, Aryan blood”; Nazi “Aryan” inconsistent with linguistic Aryan (Yiddish IE; Romany better claim than North Europeans). Most secure legacy = the languages themselves (~2 billion speakers). See also Political Misuse section below.

**Relation to later layers.** Anthony 2007 **narrows** Gimbutas’s broad Kurgan label, adds wool/wheel termini and franchising, but treats Mallory as co-founder of the modern steppe case. Reich 2018 adds aDNA demography Mallory lacked. Mallory’s pre-aDNA expansion narratives stay dated archaeological-linguistic arguments.

### Anthony 2007 — primary steppe case (now ingested); refinement of the Kurgan package

Indo-European languages, on Anthony’s account, spread from a **Pontic-Caspian steppe homeland** (grasslands north of the Black and Caspian Seas — modern Ukraine and southern Russia, west of the Urals) where **Proto-Indo-European** was spoken broadly **4500–2500 BCE**. He credits Gimbutas and [[mallory-j-p|Mallory]] with arguing persuasively for this location for about thirty years and holds that linguistic and archaeological discoveries “converge on the probability” of that homeland; alternatives are “increasingly difficult to square with new evidence” (Anthony 2007). **Relation to Gimbutas 1991:** Anthony retains the Pontic-Caspian homeland and Old Europe as non-IE agriculturalists, but **narrows** “Kurgan culture” to the EBA Pit-grave / [[yamnaya-culture|Yamnaya]] steppe core (rejecting her later expansion of the label to Maikop and many European Late Neolithic assemblages), replaces multi-wave “invasion” rhetoric with **franchise / prestige-driven language shift**, and supplies wool and wagon chronological termini Gimbutas did not center. He is refinement of Position A’s archaeological-linguistic package, not replacement of her homeland claim.

**Chronological termini (wool and wheel).** Two reconstructed vocabulary sets date when late PIE must have been spoken:

1. **Woven wool** and a developed sheep lexicon (including *HwlHn- ‘wool’, with cognates almost all branches **including Hittite**, so archaic PIE depth);
2. A **five-term wagon semantic field**: *kwékwlos ‘wheel’; *rot-eh₂- second ‘wheel’; *aks-/*h₂eks- ‘axle’; *h₂ih₃s- ‘thill’; *wégheti ‘to convey/go in a vehicle’ (plus wagon nouns).

Neither woven-wool textiles nor wheeled vehicles existed before ~**4000 BCE** (possibly not broadly before ~**3500 BCE**), yet late PIE speakers regularly spoke of them — so the reconstructable late stage falls **after 4000–3500 BCE**. Anthony argues wagon/wheel terms cannot be post-PIE inter-branch loans (they lack loan sound patterns and show inheritance from PIE); if these classic reconstructions were false, comparative method itself would be undermined. Terminal date ~**2500 BCE** from the birth of late daughters (Greek, Indo-Iranian). Maximum archaic-to-late window ~**4500–2500 BCE** (Anthony 2007).

**Phased PIE and branch order (Anthony).** Archaic PIE (partly preserved only in Anatolian) before ~4000 BCE; early PIE (partly in Tocharian) ~4000–3500 BCE; late PIE (Italic/Celtic source with full wagon vocabulary) ~3500–3000 BCE, expanding with the [[yamnaya-culture]] horizon from ~3300 BCE. Pre-Anatolian may have split **before wagons** (~4200–3900 BCE westward into Old Europe via Suvorovo-type movements). Then east ~3700–3300 BCE (Repin→[[afanasievo-culture]] ≈ Pre-Tocharian); western Yamnaya Danube/Carpathian ~3100–3000 BCE (Pre-Italic/Pre-Celtic incubation); Corded Ware contact ~2800–2600 BCE (northern IE / Pre-Germanic path via Usatovo–TRB); Pre-Greek ~2500 BCE; Pre-Indo-Iranian ~2500–2200 BCE via late Yamnaya/Poltavka→[[sintashta-culture]] (Anthony 2007).

**Archaeological match.** Pontic-Caspian cultures between Caucasus and Urals fit the reconstructed vocabulary: domesticated horse/cattle/sheep sacrifice, at least occasional grain cultivation, wagons, institutionalized status in funeral ritual; temperate flora/fauna and honeybee geography; deeper relations with Proto-Uralic than with Caucasian languages; economy of farmers and stockbreeders (Anthony 2007). Classic mobile pastoralists of the Yamnaya horizon (~3300–2500 BCE) are Anthony’s principal archaeological correlate of late PIE.

**Why not Anatolian/Caucasian homeland (Anthony’s counter-arguments).** Evidence for a Caucasian or Anatolian homeland is weak: many proposed Semitic→PIE loans rejected; accepted loans (silver, bull) could travel far; Nichols shows PIE/Kartvelian/Semitic contacts were **indirect** via unknown intermediaries; Proto-Kartvelian postdates PIE/Proto-Semitic; Uralic relations closer. First Anatolian Neolithic farmers likely spoke **Afro-Asiatic** (northern Syrian origin), so farming dispersal should have carried that language, not PIE. Early written Anatolian IE (Hittite, Palaic, Luwian) shows little internal diversity, limited speaker base, and heavy borrowing from prestigious non-IE Hattic/Hurrian — not the pattern expected after 3,000+ years of in situ Neolithic evolution. Renfrew’s slow-evolution pan-European PIE dialect chain 6500–3000 BCE requires unrealistically uniform change, contradicts Neolithic material-culture diversity, and exceeds plausible tribal language territories under foot transport. **“The wagon vocabulary is incompatible with the first-farming/language-dispersal hypothesis”** (Anthony 2007). Gray–Atkinson phylogenetic dates favoring early Anatolian split are reported but raise the same wagon-vocabulary synchronization problem. See also [[anatolian-hypothesis]] Critiques.

**Mechanism: franchise, not invasion.** Anthony rejects equating language simply with artifact types. Pre-IE languages of Europe were abandoned, he argues, because membership in associated social groups became **stigmatized** under post-3300 BCE conditions of increased mobility, pastoral economies, status-ranked politics, and inter-regional connectivity. Five factors enhanced steppe/PIE prestige: superior horse breeding and trade; riding for herding and raiding; oath-bound patron-client contracts (*h₁óitos); guest-host hospitality (*ghos-ti-); potlatch-like gift/feast political theater including praise poetry. Initial out-of-steppe spread resembled a **franchising operation** more than organized military conquest: some migration and confrontation at first penetration, then reproduction of patron-client agreements so genetic link to original immigrants became remote while myths, rituals, and institutions continued. “There was no Indo-European invasion of Europe” — Usatovo dialect spread up the Dniester differed from the Yamnaya Danube stream; even that was fissioning tribal segments seeking pastures and clients, not a coordinated invasion (Anthony 2007). Multi-staged replacements; no single root cause (not race, pure demography, or spiritual qualities); historical analogies include lingua franca and élite dominance (also Renfrew’s Model II for later cases).

**Three constrained parameters for western IE archaeology (Anthony):** (1) late PIE dialects did expand; (2) they expanded into eastern and central Europe from a Pontic-Caspian homeland; (3) Pre-Italic, Pre-Celtic, and Pre-Germanic separations from late PIE probably ~3300–2500 BCE (Anthony 2007).

### Reich 2018 genetic densification of Position A

Indo-European languages spread from a steppe homeland via the [[yamnaya-culture]] migration into Europe after roughly 5,000 years ago, and via a parallel steppe-descended migration into South Asia contributing to [[ancestral-north-indians]] ancestry. David Anthony's linguistic argument, as Reich reports it, rests on the shared wagon vocabulary present across most branches but absent from the earliest-diverging Anatolian branch — implying most IE languages descend from a population using wagon technology no earlier than about 6,000 years ago, which would rule out the much earlier (9,000–8,000-years-ago) Anatolian farming expansion as their source.

Reich (2018) states that ancient-DNA evidence of a mass, demographically real Corded-Ware-associated migration after roughly 5,000 years ago undermines the Anatolian hypothesis's core objection to steppe-driven dispersal — namely, that such a rapid, wide-ranging population replacement was implausible. Reich concludes that the genetic evidence "tips the scales" toward some version of the steppe hypothesis, while stating that the exact original homeland location remains open. Reich's own further inference — that the original Indo-European-speaking population may have lived **south of the Caucasus** (in the Iran/Armenia region) before splitting into a steppe-bound branch (becoming the Yamnaya, via mixture with steppe hunter-gatherers) and an Anatolia-bound branch (ancestral to Hittite speakers) — is offered explicitly as his own speculation ("suggests to me"), not an established archaeogenetic finding, and is recorded here as attributed, not as part of the settled steppe-hypothesis case. Note tension with Anthony’s north-of-Caucasus Pontic-Caspian placement: both are Position A family members on European dispersal via Yamnaya-related groups, but they diverge on **ultimate** homeland geography.

## Position B: Anatolian/Farming-Dispersal Hypothesis

**Held by:** Colin Renfrew ([[renfrew-colin]]), *[[renfrew-archaeology-and-language-1987|Archaeology and Language]]* (1987); see [[anatolian-hypothesis]].

### Renfrew's own case (as of 1987)

Indo-European languages of Europe trace primarily to **first farmers** who entered Greece from **Anatolia** and whose speech rode a **demic wave of advance** of farming across Europe (density ~0.1 → ~5–10/km²; local moves of tens of kilometres, not organized migrations). Renfrew rejects as pan-European solutions: Kurgan dispersal ~3500–3000 BC; Corded Ware and Beaker unities ~2900–2000 BC; later Urnfields — "none of them is large enough to apply to the whole of Europe" (Renfrew 1987). Only an "embracing" Kurgan+Corded+Beaker synthesis (as in [[gimbutas-marija|Gimbutas]]'s wider hypothesis) could claim that scope, and he rejects that package.

**Processual commitments (Ch.4–6):** language ≠ people ≠ race ≠ archaeological culture (Max Müller); linguistic palaeontology does not uniquely fix a south Russian homeland; Beaker/Corded packages better read as social/status phenomena (Shennan, peer-polity interaction) than as ethno-linguistic invasions. **Three replacement models:** (I) Demography/Subsistence (farming wave of advance — Ammerman & Cavalli-Sforza); (II) Élite Dominance; (III) System Collapse. Primary European IE spread is Model I; historically attested later replacements (Rome, Magyars) are Model II.

**Chronology he uses:** first European farmers Greece/Crete before 6000 BC; calibrated farming in Greece before ~6500 BC, Orkney/Europe by ~3500 BC; seed-corn genealogy back to Greek EN and western Anatolia. Non-IE historic pockets (Basque, Etruscan) modeled as denser-mesolithic or partial-acculturation survivors. Celtic speech as *in situ* crystallization of early western IE after farming, not Hallstatt/La Tène folk migration (ch.9). Indo-Iranian left more open (Hypothesis A early farming vs Hypothesis B later mounted élite dominance).

**What he says is sheer hypothesis:** that the first Greek farmers already spoke early Indo-European — the archaeological demography does not prove the language label by itself (Renfrew 1987).

### Later assessments of Position B

Reich (2018) reports that the Anatolian hypothesis "has lost its best evidence": its implicit premise — that no later population movement was demographically large enough to have carried a *second*, later wave of Indo-European speech into Europe — is contradicted by the scale of the ancient-DNA-documented Corded Ware/steppe migration. This is Reich's assessment, recorded here as an attributed position statement made by a proponent of the rival hypothesis, not adjudicated by the wiki.

Anthony (2007) attacks Position B on **linguistic chronology** independent of aDNA: wagon and wool vocabularies force late PIE after ~4000–3500 BCE; first-farming dispersal ~6500 BCE cannot carry a language that still shares those terms across branches; Gray–Atkinson early dates do not dissolve the wagon problem; slow-evolution pan-European PIE is rejected on dialect-geography and Neolithic diversity grounds (see Position A and [[anatolian-hypothesis]]).

### Bellwood 2005 — Position B ally (pre-aDNA; pre-Anthony 2007 book)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; comparative frame [[early-farming-dispersal-hypothesis]]. Recorded as a Position B densification and as a critique of steppe assumptions **as of 2005** — before Anthony’s 2007 monograph as ingested here and before nuclear aDNA. Do not adjudicate.)*

**Held with:** Renfrew farming–language package; Gray & Atkinson 2003 computational dates (as Bellwood reads them); worldwide radial farming/language outflows from agricultural heartlands.

**Strongest form of Bellwood’s Position B case:**

1. **Homeland lean.** Anatolia is “coming into focus as the most likely homeland region for PIE” on a sound but not absolute modern linguistic consensus *(as of 2005)*. Increasing treatment of Anatolian as basal coordinate branch makes Anatolia relevant to homeland geography. Reconstructed vocabulary does not rule out Anatolia and supports it to a degree (e.g. “mountain” against purely flat Pontic steppes per Gamkrelidze & Ivanov as he cites them); SW Asian domesticates, grain, plough, weaving/wool → possibility that PIE was an **early farming society**. Caveat: if Anatolian is basal, only Anatolian-witnessed items are secure PIE vocabulary. Gray & Atkinson 2003 → PIE ~7000 BC with Anatolian first divergence — strong support for Anatolian homeland + Neolithic initial dispersal *(as of 2005)*; Forster & Toth 2003 → 8100±1900 BC. Most linguists he surveys place PIE between ~3500 and 7000 BC; computational dates strengthen the Neolithic package. Full densification: [[anatolian-hypothesis]].

2. **Four assumptions of the Pontic “world view” (his list) — all “under attack” *(as of 2005)*:** (1) early wheeled-vehicle terms + carts archaeologically ~3000 BC on the steppes (Anthony 1995; Bakker et al. 1999) as decisive; (2) reconstructed PIE society implies patrilineality/pastoralism; (3) glottochronology intuition that IE is ≤5–6 kya (late Neo/EBA not early Neo); (4) Anatolian languages non-native to Anatolia → irrelevant to homeland. He notes no proposed IE family tree actually **demands** a Pontic homeland.

3. **Elite dominance critique.** Kurgan/Childe/Gimbutas and horse-riding steppe reconstructions (Anthony 1991/1995 as he reports them) that require PIE elite dominance over larger Neolithic populations are “more in guesswork than sociolinguistic reality.” Reconstructed PIE is not exclusively pastoralist; steppe archaeology of the relevant period is not entirely pastoralist either. Levine et al. 1999 (as cited): horse riding only late 2nd millennium BC → irrelevant for PIE dispersal. Mallory 1997 (staunch Pontic supporter, as Bellwood reports): no longer convinced domesticated horses and wheeled vehicles **must** be reconstructed to PIE.

4. **Archaeological “nail in the coffin” (via Renfrew 1987).** Kurgan-based late Neolithic/Bronze Age conquering pastoralists across most of Europe left **no** corresponding continent-wide archaeological horizon.

5. **Phylogeny.** IE largely rake-like (Nichols, Watkins) — wide early dispersal + contact before subgroup separation; Anatolian exception undermines pure rake; Gray/Atkinson/Ringe/Warnow favor more stratified phylogeny compatible with Renfrew.

6. **Indo-Iranian.** Majority linguistic opinion *(as of Bellwood 2005)* places relatively late Indo-Iranian separation and north-of-Black-Sea→Caspian homeland with Andronovo ~2nd millennium BC and IA arrival at/after Mature Harappan decline (Anthony, Parpola, Mallory, Masica, Kuzmina). **Bellwood caveat:** depends partly on early Indo-Aryans as pastoralists — no clear archaeological justification in South Asia, Rigveda, or the IE family tree as he reads them. Renfrew’s tentative Mehrgarh Neolithic IA from Anatolian homeland: Bellwood holds the linguistic position of IA within IE does **not** support antiquity as far as 6000 BC. His own South Asia scenario favors earlier agricultural continuity for IA-related Chalcolithic maps over pure late Andronovo invasion — densified under [[indo-european-languages]] / source page, not as a separate homeland verdict here.

7. **Comparative program.** IE is one radial farming-dispersal case among others (Afroasiatic, Elamo-Dravidian, Sino-Tibetan, Austronesian, Niger-Congo/Bantu, etc.); expected archaeology–linguistics correlations (homelands near ag homelands; progressive youth with distance) are “much greater than we could expect from chance alone.”

**What this layer is not:** A post-2015 genetic rebuttal of Corded Ware/steppe demography (Reich 2018), nor an engagement with Anthony 2007’s full wagon/wool *terminus post quem* as that book is ingested on Position A. Bellwood 2005 and Anthony 2007/Reich 2018 are successive attributed layers; later layers do not erase earlier positions from the debate record.

## Current Standing (attributed layers)

**As of Gimbutas 1991:** three-wave Kurgan infiltration from the Pontic-Caspian / Volga steppe (Kurgan I early Yamna ~4400–4300; Kurgan II Maikop/Mikhailovka I ~3500; Kurgan III late Yamna soon after 3000) is the mechanism of Indo-Europeanization; Old Europe vs Kurgan is a collision of ideologies (matristic/peaceful/temple-centered vs androcratic/warlike/sky-god), not evolution; horse riding enables steppe expansion; hybridization yields a “marble cake” with OE as undercurrent. Primary package for Position A’s classical Kurgan form — see subsection above.

**As of Renfrew 1987:** the problem is solvable without Kurgan/Corded/Beaker primary dispersals; farming + wave of advance is the only process early and large enough for pan-European IE; shared "IE society" (warrior aristocracies, Dumézilian tripartition as original package) is largely a modern myth built on too-late social forms.

**As of Anthony 2007:** linguistic termini (wool, wheel/wagon) plus Pontic-Caspian archaeological match and a sequenced migration cascade make a steppe homeland 4500–2500 BCE the best-supported synthesis; Anatolian farming-dispersal is incompatible with the wagon vocabulary; expansion mechanism is institutional franchising and prestige-driven language shift, not a single invasion. Refines Gimbutas’s homeland core while rejecting her broad Kurgan label and invasion framing. Pre-aDNA: Anthony could not yet cite nuclear genetic replacement magnitudes.

**As of Bellwood 2005:** worldwide farming/language dispersal program treats Anatolian Neolithic as preferred PIE homeland; Pontic assumptions “under attack”; elite-dominance steppe reconstructions sociolinguistically weak; computational dates favor Neolithic depth; pre-aDNA and pre-Anthony-2007-book as ingested here.

**As of Reich 2018:** the field's balance of evidence has shifted toward the steppe hypothesis following the post-2015 ancient-DNA record, while the precise original homeland remains unresolved — DNA can establish that population movements occurred but "cannot of course reveal what languages people spoke" (Reich 2018). `resolution_status: partially-resolved` reflects this: the dispersal *mechanism* for much of Europe (steppe-related migration, demographically real and large-scale) is now well evidenced genetically, and Anthony’s archaeological-linguistic package supplies the chronological and institutional frame for linking that demography to language — but the ultimate linguistic *homeland* (north vs south of the Caucasus), Anatolian branch history, and whether an earlier farming-linked IE layer also existed, are not settled by genetics alone. This is a layered summary of attributed positions, not a wiki-voice verdict. Primary Gimbutas (1991), Renfrew (1987), Bellwood (2005), Anthony (2007), and Reich (2018) are now all on disk. Genetics rehabilitates a *demographic* steppe vector without endorsing Gimbutas’s goddess/collision narrative.

## Political Misuse — Recorded as Positions, Not Wiki Voice

The homeland question has repeatedly been weaponized for nationalist and racial ideology, independent of its scholarly merits. Reich (2018) records two specific cases attributed to those ideologies' own claims, not asserted by the wiki:

- **Nazi "Aryan" ideology:** built in part on Gustaf Kossinna's settlement-archaeology identification of Corded Ware distribution with ancient German territory (see the Historiography section of [[corded-ware-culture]]) and on 19th–20th-century "Aryan purity" narratives asserting a pure ancestral Indo-European ("Aryan") people. Reich argues this ideology is undermined by the genetic findings, since the Yamnaya themselves were formed by mixture, as were the Corded Ware and Bell Beaker populations that followed (as of Reich 2018).
- **Hindutva no-outside-contribution claims:** a political position, per Reich, asserting that Indian civilization and its populations owe nothing to outside migration. Reich reports this is in tension with the finding that roughly half of [[ancestral-north-indians]] ancestry derives from steppe/Iranian-farmer-related migrations less than 5,000 years old (as of Reich 2018).

Anthony (2007) acknowledges the bad history of combining prehistoric archaeology and historical linguistics (citing Hobsbawm on historians supplying raw material for bigotry) and argues that a convincing PIE narrative must now be pegged to a vast array of archaeological facts and remain uncontradicted by facts outside the chosen path — 19th-c. Aryan fantasies (e.g. Madison Grant) were sparse-linguistic + racist + social Darwinist, not dense archaeological argument.

These are recorded here as documented political uses of the homeland question, attributed to the parties who advanced them — the wiki does not adjudicate the ideological claims and states no position on them beyond recording how scholars characterize the evidence relative to each.

## Position layer — Sherratt & E.S. Sherratt (1988)

*(Attributed — A. Sherratt & E.S. Sherratt, “The Archaeology of Indo-European: An Alternative View,” in *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]* Ch.19. )* Rejects simple **[[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]] farming = Indo-European** equations and simple single-wave Gimbutas kurgan identity equations. Reconstructs **PPIE/*PIE stages with Circumpontic contact axes; Corded Ware, Bell Beaker, and Pit-Grave/Yamnaya appear as **contact and transmission networks**, not as “the Indo-Europeans.” **Languages ≠ archaeological cultures ≠ genetic populations ≠ self-conscious peoples.** Traction, horse, and secondary products (Ch.6–7) supply the economic conditions Sherratt links to steppe expansion — still a **position**, date-stamped 1988, subordinate to later aDNA (Reich 2018 layer on this page) and to Anthony’s 2007 primary synthesis. Culture-horizon scaffolding for this position: [[baden-culture|Baden]] package, [[globular-amphora-culture|GAC]] Hamburg–Kiev band, late [[funnel-beaker-culture|TRB]] → Corded Ware transformation (see those pages; all `associated_with`, not identity).
