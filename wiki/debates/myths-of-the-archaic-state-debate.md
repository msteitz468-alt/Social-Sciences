# Myths of the Archaic State (Neo-Evolutionist Models vs. Yoffee’s Critique)

Positions recorded in strongest form; the wiki does not adjudicate. Related: [[cultural-evolutionism-debate]], [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]], [[processual-postprocessual-debate]].

## Position 1 — Neo-evolutionist / stage-model archaeology

Mid-twentieth-century social anthropology ([[white-leslie-a|White]], [[steward-julian-h|Steward]], [[service-elman-r|Service]], [[fried-morton-h|Fried]], [[sahlins-marshall|Sahlins]]) and its archaeological operationalization held that societies can be typed into successive levels (band–tribe–chiefdom–state; or egalitarian–rank–stratified–state). Prehistoric sequences were modeled by “contemporary ancestors” — ethnographically known societies treated as living representatives of stages. Archaic/primary states were often characterized as large territorial polities with strong central control over goods, services, and information, identifiable via trait lists or correlate packages (monumentality, settlement hierarchy, craft specialization, etc.). See [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], [[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model]], [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]], [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]].

## Position 2 — Yoffee’s “myths” critique (2005)

In *[[yoffee-myths-of-the-archaic-state-2005|Myths of the Archaic State]]*, [[yoffee-norman|Norman Yoffee]] argues that much of this image is **myth** in the sense of “a thing spoken of as though existing”: factually wrong for many earliest states and implausible within social evolutionary theory. On his account:

- Earliest states were often **territorially small** (city-states), not totalitarian mega-polities.
- **Power was limited and contested** among semi-autonomous corporate groups; rulers were repressive but not omnipotent managers of all flows.
- **Cities**, not abstract “stages,” are the central arenas of differentiation, struggle, and new ideologies of order.
- Stage labels and ethnographic analogies **flatten developmental sequences** and convert living societies into fossils of others’ pasts.
- Neo-evolutionism **cannot explain** collapse, non-state trajectories (e.g. Chaco, Cahokia), everyday social roles, or the “evolution of simplicity” (state projects of legibility and legal/administrative simplification).

Yoffee retains **social evolution** as world-historical explanation of change while rejecting neo-evolutionist typology as research program. Empirically he leans on Mesopotamia plus comparative city data (Egypt, China, Indus, Andes, Mesoamerica).

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

Both sides address the archaeological appearance of cities, hierarchy, and specialized leadership after the Pleistocene/Neolithic. Both use comparative cases. They disagree on (a) whether ethnographic stage-analogues are valid models, (b) the typical scale and despotism of earliest states, and (c) whether social types or historically specific power/identity processes should organize research.

## Six myths Yoffee lists (attributed, condensed)

1. Earliest states were basically the same kind of thing.
2. Totalitarian despots monopolized goods, services, information, and true law.
3. Evolution adequately modeled by ethnographic stage analogues (“contemporary ancestors”).
4. States as totalities that “do” things as corporate actors without internal struggle.
5. Trait-list / correlate checklists identify stages reliably.
6. Cities, states, and civilizations are rare exceptions requiring special explanation (vs expected post-Pleistocene outcomes once surplus is possible; non-state histories need equal explanation).

## Yoffee’s Rule (attributed)

If one can still productively argue whether a society is a state or isn’t, **then it isn’t** — a methodological jab at endless chiefdom-vs-state identification.

## Position 3 — Scott 2017: fragility, “collapse,” and the ascent-of-man myth (affinity with Yoffee, not identity)

*(Attributed — [[scott-james-c|James C. Scott]], *[[scott-against-the-grain-2017|Against the Grain]]* 2017. Scott names [[yoffee-norman|Yoffee]] among top Mesopotamian specialist guides; affinity is claimed here as **thematic**, not as Scott restating Yoffee’s six myths or rejecting social evolution in Yoffee’s terms.)*

### Ascent-of-man as civilizational narrative (Introduction)

Scott characterizes the standard narrative codified by early agrarian kingdoms as an **“ascent of man”** story: agriculture replaces savage hunter-gatherers/nomads; fixed-field crops guarantee settled life, formal religion, society, and law; refusal of agriculture is ignorance. Lineage of progress theorists listed: Hobbes, Locke, Vico, Morgan, Engels, Spencer, Spengler, social Darwinist schemes, band→village→town→city sequences. Wetland origins of sedentism overlooked partly from indelible association of civilization with major grains; marshes coded as untamed waste whose “civilizing” is drainage into taxable grain fields.

### Fragility and short life expectancy (Ch.6; Introduction)

- Early states were rarely formidable Leviathans for long; interregna, fragmentation, and “dark ages” more common than consolidated rule; “stateness” as continuum more/less rather than either/or.
- Scholarly consensus (as Scott reports): first archaic states fragile and short-lived; Adams on Ur III’s century of five kings as unusual stability; **Yoffee et al.** on concentrations of power typically fragile and short-lived; Broodbank on bewildering foundation/abandonment cycles.
- Mesopotamian state development non-linear: statelets short life expectancy; interregna more common than “regna”; Neolithic proto-urban complex already fragile (rainfall, floods, pests, disease); state superimposition added taxes and warfare fragility.
- Intrinsic fault lines: diseases of crowding; deforestation/siltation/floods; salinization and yield decline (Introduction Ch.6 thumbnail). Epidemic disease “grossly underestimated” in demographic fragility (Preface).

### Normalizing “collapse” (Ch.6; affinity with Yoffee/Cowgill/Simon)

- Archaeological “collapse” = at least abandonment/destruction of the monumental court center — typically read as catastrophic loss of complexity (elite disappearance, end of monumental building, evaporation of administrative literacy, reduced trade/craft).
- Scott insists collapse does **not** necessarily mean regional population decline, decline in health/well-being/nutrition (**may represent improvement**), or dissolution of culture rather than reformulation and decentralization.
- Following Yoffee and Cowgill’s use of Herbert Simon’s modularity / “nearly decomposable” units: much “collapse” as **disassembly** of larger fragile polities into smaller, more stable, self-sufficient components; disappearance of apical center need not imply disorder for those units.
- Melancholy of “collapse” as artifact of heroic-era archaeology’s focus on monumental centers as icons of civilization; what was lost were concentrated ruins, written record, and luxuries — the apex, not necessarily the social base. Civilizations should not be confused with states they typically outlast.
- Aim: **“normalize” collapse** as often periodic, possibly salutary reformulation — especially cycles of centralization/decentralization in command economies. “Why deplore ‘collapse,’ when the situation it depicts is most often the disaggregation of a complex, fragile, and typically oppressive state into smaller, decentralized fragments?”
- Speculative trigger: resistance and flight of core subjects when rulers pressed the grain core under emergency (Akkadian late stages; Ur under Amorite threat).

### Where Scott diverges from / exceeds Yoffee on this page

Yoffee’s program is a research-design critique of neo-evolutionist stage ladders, trait lists, and ethnographic analogues. Scott **shares** the small-scale, fragile, non-totalitarian early-state image and the modular-collapse reading, but **adds** a strong **coercion/bondage** thesis, a **grain-legibility** fiscal ecology, and a **welfare-improving dark ages** normative reframe aimed at civilizational historiography. He does not, in this book, systematically dismantle band–tribe–chiefdom–state as Yoffee does. File coercion under [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]; grain under [[grain-hypothesis]].

**Shared factual ground (framed):** Both Yoffee and Scott treat earliest Mesopotamian (and comparative) states as fragile and limited rather than totalitarian mega-polities; both challenge progress teleologies. They are not the same theory.
