# Mead–Cooley Debate on the Origin of the Self

## Shared Factual Ground

Both [[cooley-charles-horton|Cooley]] and [[mead-george-herbert|Mead]] reject a pre-social, atomistic individual as the starting point of psychology and sociology. Both hold that the [[the-social-self|self]] is social in character and that communicative life is essential to selfhood. Both belong to the American [[pragmatism|pragmatist]] and early social-psychology milieu. The dispute is not "is the self social?" but **by what mechanism does the self arise, and is self-feeling an origin or a consequence?**

## Position A — Cooley (and James): self-feeling and the looking-glass

As developed in *[[cooley-human-nature-social-order-1902|Human Nature and the Social Order]]* (1902), Cooley treats the **[[looking-glass-self|looking-glass or reflected self]]** as a primary form of social selfhood: we imagine our appearance in another's mind, imagine their judgment, and experience pride or mortification. The social self is "any idea, or system of ideas, drawn from the communicative life, that the mind cherishes as its own." Society is studied in the imagination through **personal ideas**. William James's empirical self and self-feeling supply related vocabulary. On this line, affective and imaginative experience of others' attitudes *is* how the social self is lived and known; intimate association ([[primary-group|primary groups]]) stocks the sentiments that make human nature "social."

**Strongest form of the position:** without the felt, imagined judgment of others there is no account of pride, shame, honor, or vanity — the everyday texture of selfhood — and any purely cognitive genesis underdescribes the self as lived.

## Position B — Mead: cognitive role-taking origin

In *[[mead-mind-self-society-1934|Mind, Self, and Society]]* (as reconstructed), Mead argues that self-feeling experiences "do not account for the origin of the self," because the self must already exist for such feelings to be referred to it. The self is "essentially a cognitive rather than an emotional phenomenon." Its defining property is reflexivity via the [[significant-symbol|significant symbol]] and [[taking-the-role-of-the-other|taking the role of the other]]; unity of self is given by the [[generalized-other|generalized other]]. Mead names Cooley and James among those who ground the self in prior self-feeling and thus, he claims, cannot explain how a self arises outside of or before the social process of communication.

**Strongest form of the position:** a theory of self must explain *genesis*, not only the adult phenomenology of reputation; only a mechanism that makes the individual an object to himself (role-taking through significant symbols) does that without circularity.

## Status

**Open** as a theoretical dispute about priority and mechanism; **partly dissolved** in textbook synthesis, which often presents Cooley's looking-glass and Mead's role-taking / generalized other as complementary stages or levels (particular others → organized community). Serious scholarship keeps the difference: Cooley’s program is organic, imaginative, and affective; Mead’s (as received) is behaviorist-pragmatist and cognitive. Neither side is adjudicated in wiki voice.
