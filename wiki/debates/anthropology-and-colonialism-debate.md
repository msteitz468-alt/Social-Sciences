# Anthropology and Colonialism Debate

Standing dispute over how anthropology — especially British social anthropology — was formed by, served, resisted, or relatively escaped European colonial power. Listed among early standing debates in this vault's design ("anthropology's colonial origins and the decolonization debate"). Primary vault charter text: [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]] (ed.), *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]* (1973; Hull seminar 1972).

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

Parties generally accept that:

- Much classical ethnography was done in territories under colonial administration; *Pax Britannica* conditions made intensive fieldwork practicable.
- Colonial governments sometimes commissioned, funded, or used ethnographic knowledge ([[indirect-rule]], native courts, labour/land policy, Closed Districts).
- Postwar decolonisation and 1960s–70s radical critique forced the discipline to confront production context (Gough 1968; Hymes; Asad 1973; Leclerc 1972).
- Organisational expansion of anthropology after 1945 (ASA 1946–) can coexist with doctrinal crisis (Asad's framing).
- Individuals varied: some resisted particular policies; some held applied posts; institutes (e.g. Rhodes-Livingstone) had mixed funders and conflicts.

They disagree on **degree of determination**, **intent vs structural complicity**, **variation by person/institute/empire**, and **what decolonizing practice requires**.

## Positions

### 1. Structural handmaiden / constitutive coloniality

Anthropology's object ("the primitive"), methods, and functionalist holism were shaped to be useful to — or congruent with — colonial rule. Strong New Left (Goddard, Banaji, Anderson), Gough, elements of Faris (Nadel as clear applied colonial agent; functionalism as control ideology), Lackner (functionalism as answer to Indirect Rule ideologues), and Feuchtwang (sponsorship effects; inverted colonial divide) press versions of this line. Asad's **determination thesis** is a sophisticated structural version that need not imply conspiracy.

### 2. Reluctant imperialist / third-pole ambivalence ([[james-wendy|Wendy James]])

Fieldworkers often saw themselves as critics or buffers of administration; research form **entailed liberalism**; double ambivalence toward officials and nationalists; anthropology as constrained **third pole**, vehicle of criticism as well as product of colonial authority. Opposes cosy-conspiracy *and* pure-handmaiden stories.

### 3. Relative autonomy / establishment continuity

Professional leaders treat talk of crisis as alarmist; "specialisation" as vitality; classic functionalist assumptions still viable (Asad's characterisation of the establishment). Firth's 1972 Radcliffe-Brown Lecture (Marfleet) answers "gut-Marxist" critics as travesty. Asad treats smooth-continuity talk as myth — recorded as the position he opposes, not wiki verdict.

### 4. Sponsorship and institutional determination

Feuchtwang: discipline vs profession; commissioned use, priority lists, diplomatic limits; IAI/Lugard turning point; CSSRC/CDWA; ASA boom. Brown on [[wilson-godfrey|Godfrey Wilson]] and the RLI: funders (colonial governments, BSA Co., copper mines) as "hidden taxation," not simple hire; board without scientists; Wilson's conflicts with mines/admin; institute servant of neither yet not in a vacuum. Concrete cases for knowledge/power without reducing to payroll.

### 5. Two European images of non-European rule (Asad)

Functionalist **consent** image of African political authority vs orientalist **despotism** image of Islamic rule — different historical formations, shared order-problem, both potentially reassuring to colonial ruling classes. Distinct from pure handmaiden claims about individual applied posts.

### 6. Third-World / colonized standpoint

Ahmed (Sudan): Nkrumah epigraph; African intellectual hostility; administrators' racialised attitudes (Slatin, Churchill, MacMichael); EP questionnaire/grant conditions; archives after independence; anthropology *was* an aid among other things. Willis: [[fipa|Fipa]] Kaswa myth as indigenous critique — colonialism interrupted dialogue. Marfleet elevates Mafeje ("Ideology of Tribalism") and Magubane as inside critiques tying theory to colonial system parts.

### 7. Bibliographical map (Marfleet 1973)

Gregg & Williams (1948) → Hooker, Maquet, Worsley, Lévi-Strauss → American social-responsibility critique (Berreman, Gough, Moore) → British New Left → African anthropologists → Firth & Leclerc. Useful as historiography of the debate itself.

### 8. Post-1973 continuations

*Writing Culture* (textual authority; Asad on cultural translation), Said/Orientalism (adjacent), world-system political economy ([[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] cites Asad 1973), historical anthropology of colonial consciousness (Comaroffs), contemporary decolonize-the-curriculum programs. Continuations, not settlements of 1973 positions.

### 9. Loose symbiosis / elective affinity (Kuper, *Anthropology and Anthropologists*)

[[kuper-adam|Adam Kuper]]’s Chapter 4 (2015 text of the 1973 project) is a **historiographical position** inside this debate, not a primary colonial archive. He records the Nkrumah-painting / *African Political Systems* emblem (via Galtung); Nadel’s 1955 applied-anthropology lecture attacked by West African students; 1960s African intellectual charges; 1970s Said-led Orientalism framing; and Foucault-inspired knowledge–power / symbiotic readings (de L’Estoile).

**Kuper’s own thesis (attributed):** critics seized on confidential reports and rash funding promises but could not turn up particularly shocking instances or a **consistent pattern of collaboration**; older-generation responses included defensive memoirs. He reconstructs instead a **loose elective affinity** between functionalist holism and Lugardian [[indirect-rule|Indirect Rule]] (Malinowski’s 1927 note that Lugard’s doctrine was a “Complete Surrender to the Functional Point of View”); IAI/Rockefeller funding that gave Malinowski a near-monopoly of applied African research fellowships; limited applied yield from many fellows; Richards/Mair applied realism vs theoretical ethnographic present; Hailey *African Survey* and Colonial Development and Welfare research budgets (social research a small share); RLI (Wilson → Gluckman); and purist Oxford (Evans-Pritchard) vs LSE applied tracks. Overlaps **Position 3** (relative autonomy) and **Position 4** (sponsorship) without adopting full determination (Position 1) or James’s “reluctant imperialist” phenomenology (Position 2).

### 10. Institutional-market formation vs. unresolved policy efficacy (Stocking, *After Tylor*, 1995)

*(Attributed — [[stocking-george-w|Stocking]], *[[stocking-after-tylor-1995|After Tylor]]*, Ch. 8, "Anthropological Institutions, Colonial Interests, and the First Cohorts of Social Anthropologists.")* Stocking frames this same dispute explicitly and declines to adjudicate it; both positions are recorded here in their strongest form, per Stocking's own presentation.

**Position A — functionalism as colonial-administrative tool.** Stocking reports (citing, without endorsing) a late-1960s/1970s critical current holding that "functionalism" was "the anthropology appropriate to a new phase of British colonialism," sustaining an "established and routinized colonial order by clarifying the principles of traditional native systems through which 'indirect rule' could be carried on." A stronger inversion of this position held that functionalism's "ethnographic holism and cultural relativism" were themselves "products of colonial practice" rather than autonomous "theoretical innovations of academic anthropology." During this period anthropology was condemned metaphorically as the "child," "step-child," "problem-child," or "tool" of imperialism. Specific formulations Stocking cites: Perry Anderson (1969) described social anthropology as an export by "British imperial society" of a "totalizing" social analysis "proscribed at home"; Henrika Kuklick (1991) read British anthropology via the "rise of the meritocratic ideal" and "projection of British folk-models of the state"; Anna Grimshaw and Keith Hart (1993) argued Malinowski's and Radcliffe-Brown's anthropology "was compatible in many important ways with a bureaucratic class on the rise."

**Position B — Stocking's own cautionary/skeptical stance.** Stocking states such interpretations "have sometimes been argued by means of selective documentation based on theoretically motivated assumptions of 'correspondence' between anthropological theory and colonial practice—sometimes in a polemically charged manner," and calls for eschewing "aprioristic correspondences, casual metaphorical characterization, and anachronistic moral judgment" while still affirming that "colonialism was a critically important context for the development of anthropology." He treats the colonial market for anthropology (funding, government posts, training demand for colonial probationers) as demonstrably central to social anthropology's *institutionalization*, while leaving its influence on actual colonial *policy* an open, unresolved question — he states explicitly that "the actual utilization of functionalist anthropology by colonial administrators, as opposed to its putative utility for the colonial system... lies outside the scope of the present narrative."

**Illustrative case cutting against simple determination:** Jomo Kenyatta's 1936 admission to Malinowski's African Institute seminar (over Lugard's lone dissenting vote, on Malinowski's claim that "scientific anthropology" had a "depoliticising influence" on Kenyatta) is a documented instance of the colonial encounter cutting against, rather than for, colonial control — Kenyatta went on to lead Kenya's independence movement, deploying functionalist argument in *Facing Mount Kenya* (1938) to defend traditional practices (including clitoridectomy) while calling for "complete emancipation." Also documented: the Colonial Office barred German scholar Paul Kirchoff from entering any British colony in 1931–32 on suspicion of being a "communist agitator," illustrating direct colonial-administrative vetting of anthropological personnel.

Overlaps Position 1 (Anderson/Kuklick/Grimshaw & Hart citations) and Position 3/9 (relative-autonomy and elective-affinity readings) without adopting either as adjudicated.

## Pre-1968 evidence: Gluckman's positions (Order and Rebellion, 1963)

From [[gluckman-order-and-rebellion-1963]] — contemporaneous, pre-critical-turn material bearing on both sides:

- **Anti-Malinowski applied anthropology (1940s):** Gluckman quoted and rejected Malinowski's "practical anthropology" counsel that "wise colonial statesmanship" should manage African expectations and make clear "that no full identity can ever be reached" — "I for one reject him as advocate of the anthropologists," calling the proposed compromise an anthropologist "crawling on his knees to beg some white groups for a few more crumbs." Evidence that sharp anti-colonial positions existed *inside* the functionalist generation.
- **Bureaucracy literature and Indirect Rule (1963 Introduction):** Gluckman linked the late-1950s anthropological turn to "bureaucracy" (Fallers, Southall, M. G. Smith, Colson, Richards, *East African Chiefs*) to the Indirect Rule policy climate and postwar Colonial Office service agendas, criticizing it for neglecting actual power struggles, nationalist parties, and trade unions (citing C. Leys, *New Statesman*, 19 March 1960); he cited his own *Analysis of a Social Situation in Modern Zululand* (1940) as an earlier attempt at exactly that.
- **Evidentiary bias of colonial sources:** Gluckman argued pre-professional accounts (travelers, missionaries, administrators) were systematically biased toward the exotic and toward "darkness and barbarity" justifying improvement missions (Junod's 26 pages on Tsonga circumcision ritual vs 5 on childhood as his example).
- **RLI entanglement, self-recorded:** the Village Headman essay (1949) noted district officers valued the headman as an "invaluable non-commissioned officer of native administration" — the [[intercalary-role]] research object was simultaneously an administrative instrument. Cross-ref Brown's RLI chapter in Asad 1973 (above).

## Resolution Status

**Open.** Wide acceptance that colonial conditions matter for disciplinary history; no consensus model of complicity, autonomy, or repair. This page records positions; it does not adjudicate.

## Cross-Discipline Note: Archaeology (attributed, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]] extends this debate's terms into
archaeology specifically, quoting Smith and Wobst (2005a): "At heart, archaeology is a
colonialist endeavour ... Privileging the material over the spiritual and the scientific
over the religious, archaeological practice is solidly grounded in Western ways of
knowing the world" (attributed). He frames large-scale 19th–20th century Western
collection of archaeological/ethnographic material and human remains as, in the eyes of
many Indigenous peoples, plunder — "the cultural counterpart to the processes of
colonialism." See [[indigenous-archaeology]] for the fuller archaeological-theory
treatment.
