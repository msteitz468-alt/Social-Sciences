# Anthropology and Colonialism Debate

Standing dispute over how anthropology — especially British social anthropology — was formed by, served, resisted, or relatively escaped European colonial power. Listed among early standing debates in this vault's design ("anthropology's colonial origins and the decolonization debate"). Primary vault charter text: [[asad-talal|Talal Asad]] (ed.), *[[asad-anthropology-colonial-encounter-1973|Anthropology and the Colonial Encounter]]* (1973; Hull seminar 1972).

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

Parties generally accept that:

- Much classical ethnography was done in territories under colonial administration; *Pax Britannica* conditions made intensive fieldwork practicable.
- Colonial governments sometimes commissioned, funded, or used ethnographic knowledge ([[indirect-rule]], native courts, labour/land policy, Closed Districts).
- Postwar decolonisation and 1960s–70s radical critique forced the discipline to confront production context (Gough 1968; Hymes; Asad 1973; Leclerc 1972).
- Organisational expansion of anthropology after 1945 (ASA 1946–) can coexist with doctrinal crisis (Asad's framing).
- Individuals varied: some resisted particular policies; some held applied posts; institutes (e.g. Rhodes-Livingstone) had mixed funders and conflicts.

They disagree on **degree of determination**, **intent vs structural complicity**, **variation by person/institute/empire**, and **what decolonizing practice requires**.

## Positions

### 1. Structural handmaiden / constitutive coloniality

Anthropology's object ("the primitive"), methods, and functionalist holism were shaped to be useful to — or congruent with — colonial rule. Strong New Left (Goddard, Banaji, Anderson), Gough, elements of Faris (Nadel as clear applied colonial agent; functionalism as control ideology), Lackner (functionalism as answer to Indirect Rule ideologues), and Feuchtwang (sponsorship effects; inverted colonial divide) press versions of this line. Asad's **determination thesis** is a sophisticated structural version that need not imply conspiracy.

### 2. Reluctant imperialist / third-pole ambivalence ([[james-wendy|Wendy James]])

Fieldworkers often saw themselves as critics or buffers of administration; research form **entailed liberalism**; double ambivalence toward officials and nationalists; anthropology as constrained **third pole**, vehicle of criticism as well as product of colonial authority. Opposes cosy-conspiracy *and* pure-handmaiden stories.

### 3. Relative autonomy / establishment continuity

Professional leaders treat talk of crisis as alarmist; "specialisation" as vitality; classic functionalist assumptions still viable (Asad's characterisation of the establishment). Firth's 1972 Radcliffe-Brown Lecture (Marfleet) answers "gut-Marxist" critics as travesty. Asad treats smooth-continuity talk as myth — recorded as the position he opposes, not wiki verdict.

### 4. Sponsorship and institutional determination

Feuchtwang: discipline vs profession; commissioned use, priority lists, diplomatic limits; IAI/Lugard turning point; CSSRC/CDWA; ASA boom. Brown on [[wilson-godfrey|Godfrey Wilson]] and the RLI: funders (colonial governments, BSA Co., copper mines) as "hidden taxation," not simple hire; board without scientists; Wilson's conflicts with mines/admin; institute servant of neither yet not in a vacuum. Concrete cases for knowledge/power without reducing to payroll.

### 5. Two European images of non-European rule (Asad)

Functionalist **consent** image of African political authority vs orientalist **despotism** image of Islamic rule — different historical formations, shared order-problem, both potentially reassuring to colonial ruling classes. Distinct from pure handmaiden claims about individual applied posts.

### 6. Third-World / colonized standpoint

Ahmed (Sudan): Nkrumah epigraph; African intellectual hostility; administrators' racialised attitudes (Slatin, Churchill, MacMichael); EP questionnaire/grant conditions; archives after independence; anthropology *was* an aid among other things. Willis: [[fipa|Fipa]] Kaswa myth as indigenous critique — colonialism interrupted dialogue. Marfleet elevates Mafeje ("Ideology of Tribalism") and Magubane as inside critiques tying theory to colonial system parts.

### 7. Bibliographical map (Marfleet 1973)

Gregg & Williams (1948) → Hooker, Maquet, Worsley, Lévi-Strauss → American social-responsibility critique (Berreman, Gough, Moore) → British New Left → African anthropologists → Firth & Leclerc. Useful as historiography of the debate itself.

### 8. Post-1973 continuations

*Writing Culture* (textual authority; Asad on cultural translation), Said/Orientalism (adjacent), world-system political economy ([[wolf-eric-r|Wolf]] cites Asad 1973), historical anthropology of colonial consciousness (Comaroffs), contemporary decolonize-the-curriculum programs. Continuations, not settlements of 1973 positions.

## Resolution Status

**Open.** Wide acceptance that colonial conditions matter for disciplinary history; no consensus model of complicity, autonomy, or repair. This page records positions; it does not adjudicate.

## Cross-Discipline Note: Archaeology (attributed, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]] extends this debate's terms into
archaeology specifically, quoting Smith and Wobst (2005a): "At heart, archaeology is a
colonialist endeavour ... Privileging the material over the spiritual and the scientific
over the religious, archaeological practice is solidly grounded in Western ways of
knowing the world" (attributed). He frames large-scale 19th–20th century Western
collection of archaeological/ethnographic material and human remains as, in the eyes of
many Indigenous peoples, plunder — "the cultural counterpart to the processes of
colonialism." See [[indigenous-archaeology]] for the fuller archaeological-theory
treatment.
