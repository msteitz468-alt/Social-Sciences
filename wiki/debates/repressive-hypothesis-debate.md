# Sexual Repression vs. the Productive Deployment of Sexuality

A theoretical dispute over whether modern Western society **repressed** sexuality (which liberation would free) or **produced** it (so that "liberation" tracks the very power it means to escape). The dispute is largely staged by [[foucault-michel|Foucault]] in *[[foucault-history-of-sexuality-1978|The History of Sexuality]]* Vol. I against the Freudo-Marxian tradition of Wilhelm Reich and — in this vault — [[marcuse-herbert|Herbert Marcuse]]. Positions are recorded in their strongest form; no adjudication in wiki voice.

## Position A — The Freudo-Marxian liberation thesis (Reich, Marcuse)

Advanced capitalist society represses instinctual, especially sexual, life; this repression is both a psychic cost and a support of domination. For **Reich**, sexual repression underwrites authoritarian character and fascism, and sexual liberation is inseparable from political revolution. For **[[marcuse-herbert|Marcuse]]** the picture is more dialectical: *Eros and Civilization* theorizes "surplus repression" beyond what civilization requires, while *One-Dimensional Man* argues that advanced society now practices [[repressive-desublimation]] — a *controlled release* of sexuality that integrates rather than liberates. (Marcuse thus already complicates any simple "more sex = more freedom" reading.) Common ground of the position: sexuality is a real force with a nature of its own, on which power acts largely negatively.

## Position B — The Foucauldian productive-power thesis

Foucault rejects [[the-repressive-hypothesis|the repressive hypothesis]] as the frame. Modern power is productive: it did not silence sex but incited a "discursive explosion," multiplied "perversions," and built a [[scientia-sexualis]] via [[confession]]. "Sexuality" is a "historical construct" of the [[deployment-of-sexuality]], not a pre-given drive power holds in check. Crucially, Foucault historicizes the *repression thesis itself*: it arose as a **class-differentiating discourse** (the bourgeoisie "first tried [sexuality] on themselves," then claimed to be "subjected to a regime of repression so intense"). Hence the interwar critique "around Reich," and the twentieth-century "sexual revolution," were "nothing more, but nothing less... than a tactical shift and reversal in the great deployment of sexuality" — not a break with power. His counter-slogan: "The rallying point for the counterattack against the deployment of sexuality ought not to be sex-desire, but bodies and pleasures."

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both sides agree that the modern West placed sex at the center of political and personal life, tied it to the family, medicine, and the state, and generated an enormous discourse about it. They divide on what that means: for Position A the discourse is the symptom and instrument of a repression to be overcome; for Position B the discourse is *productive* machinery, and treating it as repression is to remain inside it. They also divide on **capitalism**: both link the sexual order to capitalism, but Position A via repression of the labor force, Position B via the productive "autosexualization" of the bourgeoisie and the later importation of the deployment into the working class.

## Curator's note (2026-07-09)

The two positions are not fully symmetrical: Marcuse's [[repressive-desublimation]] already concedes that liberalized sexuality can serve domination, which narrows the distance from Foucault more than Foucault's polemic against "the repressive hypothesis" allows. Foucault's own target is closer to a flattened Reichian version. Whether Foucault's genealogy of repression-as-discourse *refutes* the Freudo-Marxian account or merely *reframes* it remains open. Recorded as an open theoretical dispute.
