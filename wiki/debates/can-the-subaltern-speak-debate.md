# Can the Subaltern Speak? Debate

Standing dispute over what Spivak’s question commits scholarship to — about archives, gender, representation, intellectual responsibility, and the politics of “giving voice.” Positions in strongest form; no wiki-voice adjudication.

## Positions

### 1. Spivak 1988 (original CSS)

Spivak argued that radical Western theory (her reading of Foucault and Deleuze’s “Intellectuals and Power”) reinstates a sovereign subject by treating the oppressed as able to speak and know for themselves while the intellectual becomes transparent. Representation has two senses — German *darstellen* (re-presentation / portrait) and *vertreten* (speaking-for / proxy) — whose conflation underwrites essentialist utopian politics. The [[subaltern]] as female is “doubly in shadow.” The British abolition of *sati* is read as the scene crystallized in “White men are saving brown women from brown men,” interlocking with the nativist “The women wanted to die.” Bhuvaneswari Bhaduri’s 1926 suicide is an attempted speech rewritten by others. Closing thesis (attributed): **the subaltern cannot speak**; representation has not withered away; the female intellectual has a circumscribed task she must not disown.

### 2. Spivak 1999 (CPR revision) and 2010 “In Response”

In the revised text (Morris 2010 Part One), Spivak calls the passionate 1988 lament “the subaltern cannot speak!” an **inadvisable remark**. She insists Bhubaneswari was not a “true” subaltern by strict class definition; the despair came from failure of communication *within her own family* and a “new mainstream,” not only colonial law. She tracks the great-grandniece working for the “New Empire” as further historical silencing. In “In Response” (2010) she refuses *sati* as a generalizable example of subaltern silence; links Rup Kanwar’s mother’s smile (1986) to scriptural desire; and compares death-as-text (Bhubaneswari; suicide bombers as rearranged desire) while stressing gendering of postponed death. Trajectory unfinished: schools, secularism, uncoercive rearrangement of desires.

### 3. Recovery / voice

Subaltern silence is primarily missing documentation. Historians and ethnographers can and should recover subaltern consciousness through archives, oral history, and testimony. Spivak’s thesis, on this view, risks paralyzing political and historiographic work. (Position reconstructed from Spivak’s and Morris’s characterizations of misreadings and from the early Subaltern Studies recovery mandate as Birla describes it.)

### 4. Subaltern Studies after Spivak (Chatterjee 2010)

Partha Chatterjee argues Spivak’s essay was the “poststructuralist moment” for the collective, facilitating a laborious method shift — not mere French fashion, but entangled with Indian political change: subaltern classes inserting themselves into electoral and welfare governmentality as elites retreated. Dual reception: project’s life in India vs. “postcolonial studies” in the Anglo-American academy. Guha’s “Chandra’s Death” marks an early signal of change. Spivak remains “incorrigibly heterodox”; Subaltern Studies was “deeply enriched by her intellectual comradeship.”

### 5. Slogan misreading (Morris; Spivak; Birla)

The classroom reduction — “subalterns cannot speak, period” — is rejected. Morris: silence is not simple archival absence to be filled by information retrieval (though Spivak endorses retrieval); exit from subalternity is to be desired. Birla: the essay is misread when reduced to identity politics or nativism; authenticity discourses can themselves silence.

## Shared Factual Ground

- Essay first presented c. 1983 (University of Illinois / related titles); published 1988 in Nelson & Grossberg (eds.), *Marxism and the Interpretation of Culture*; revised in Spivak, *A Critique of Postcolonial Reason* (1999); both versions in Morris (ed.) 2010.
- Archive cases center on colonial India: *sati*/widow sacrifice discourse; Bhubaneswari (Bhuvaneswari) Bhaduri, d. 1926.
- Gramsci and Subaltern Studies are explicit interlocutors; Foucault, Deleuze, Marx, and Derrida are structural foils or resources.
- The phrase “strategic essentialism” is part of reception history more than of the 1988 text’s named apparatus (see [[strategic-essentialism]]).

## Trajectory

Open. The debate structures postcolonial pedagogy, feminist historiography, and critiques of development/human-rights “voice” projects. Spivak’s own revisions are part of the dispute, not external to it. Deep study: [[spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study]]; hub [[hubs/studies/spivak-can-the-subaltern-speak-study]].
