# Bohannan–Gluckman Debate (Comparison and Translation in Legal Anthropology)

The charter methodological dispute of [[legal-anthropology|legal anthropology]]: how the
legal or jural institutions of a non-Western society should be described, and whether they
can be compared across cultures. It pits [[bohannan-paul|Paul Bohannan]]'s
*[[bohannan-justice-judgment-tiv-1957|Justice and Judgment Among the Tiv]]* (1957) against
[[gluckman-max|Max Gluckman]]'s *The Judicial Process among the Barotse* (1955), and is a
local, sharply drawn instance of the wider emic/etic and translation problems.

## The positions

**Bohannan (folk-system priority).** The anthropologist must render the people's own
**[[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk system]]** — for the [[tiv|Tiv]], the concept
**[[jir]]** ("court, case, moot") and the category of **debt** (*injô*) rather than "tort"
and "contract" — before applying any analytical or comparative frame. His central warning:
"The anthropologist's chief danger is that he will change one of the folk systems of his own
society into an analytical system, and try to give it wider application than its merit and
usefulness allow." Western "law," in this view, is the English-speaking folk system for
social control, not a science; using it to organize Tiv material does the material violence.

**Gluckman (comparative jurisprudence).** Working on the [[lozi|Lozi]] (Barotse), Gluckman
held that refined concepts from jurisprudence — "law," the "reasonable man," categories of
wrong — are analytical instruments that travel, and that a legal ethnography must be written
so its material can enter comparative problems. He charged that Bohannan's Tiv book was not
merely non-comparative but rendered its material unusable by others, coining the phrase
**"cultural solipsism."**

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

Both agree the Tiv and Lozi settle disputes through recognizable tribunals and that faithful
ethnography matters; both are heirs of British [[structural-functionalism|structural
functionalism]]. They divide on whether comparison should **begin** from indigenous
categories and build outward (Bohannan) or from a refined common vocabulary applied to each
case (Gluckman) — and on whether "law" names a comparable universal or a Western folk
category.

## Gluckman’s 1965 restatement (*Politics, Law and Ritual*, Ch. V)

*(Attributed — *[[gluckman-politics-law-ritual-1965|Politics, Law and Ritual in Tribal Society]]*, 1965.)* The textbook chapter “Dispute and Settlement” embeds the debate in a broader **pluralist vocabulary of social control**:

- **No single “strict sense” of law.** Controversies that assume “law” has one meaning are sterile; important social-phenomena words have multiple referents. Gluckman prefers saying societies with binding rules of right conduct, mostly observed and penalized if not, **have law** (even without courts) rather than “no law” — while still specializing a hierarchy of words (litigation, adjudication, mediation, custom, sanctions) for different facts.
- **Against cultural solipsism.** Bohannan (and others) treat Western jurisprudential vocabulary as a **[[folk-system-and-analytical-system|folk-system]]** illegitimately raised to analytical status; Gluckman replies that if that were correct, comparison would require inventing a whole non-national technical language — a stultifying program. He rejects reading Bohannan’s Tiv *jir* ideal of concurrence as proof that Tiv process is incomparable to adjudication: judges *do* decide against recalcitrants in Bohannan’s own cases.
- **Adjudication vs mediation.** Gluckman treats **adjudication** (evidence → facts → decision for one party in courted societies) and **mediation**/adjuster processes (including Lovedu *khoro*, Tiv *jir*, Eskimo song-contests as a distinct genus) as separable problems that need not all be collapsed under one definition of “law.”
- **Malinowski vs Radcliffe-Brown “theories of law”** were largely terminological: each applied the word to a different set of facts (reciprocity/right–duty vs sanctioned force of political society).

This restatement does not close the debate; it is Gluckman’s mid-career public position for teaching and comparison. Barotse judicial monographs remain the primary empirical base for his side.

## Bohannan's later reply

In the 1968 second-impression preface, Bohannan declined to add the comparative chapter
critics wanted, judging the polemic more perishable than the ethnography ("I know no book in
which polemic does not age more quickly than factual and interpretative substance"). He
denied ever claiming Tiv culture was unique; insisted he opposed only the use of
jurisprudence "as a *deus ex machina*"; and located the dispute in the old split between
Tylorian comparison (define, then classify and correlate — "turned to computers") and
Boasian comparison (inquire, then subdivide and find pattern — "turned to semantics"),
holding that the two "need each other desperately." He also volunteered a self-criticism:
having argued that only institutions of judgment, not substantive law, are comparable, he had
nonetheless slid into describing Tiv substantive marriage law.

## Curator's note (2026-07-09)

Recorded here as an open methodological debate, not adjudicated. The exchange is a standard
teaching set-piece and feeds directly into the broader [[qualitative-quantitative-divide-debate|
qualitative–quantitative]] and emic/etic discussions; it also prefigures the processual/
disputing-process turn (Nader) that partly dissolved the original terms by shifting attention
from "law" to "the dispute" as the unit of study.
