# The Rational Choice Controversy (political science)

## Dispute

Does **rational choice theory** deliver empirical explanatory success in political
science commensurate with its theoretical prominence, or is its reputation for success
an artifact of [[method-driven-research|method-driven practices]] that shield it from
empirical test? The dispute was crystallized by [[green-donald|Green]] and
[[shapiro-ian|Shapiro]]'s *[[green-shapiro-pathologies-1994|Pathologies of Rational
Choice Theory]]* (1994) and the rejoinders it provoked, gathered in the 1995 *Critical
Review* symposium.

## Positions

### Green & Shapiro (1994)

Applied rational choice theory in political science has produced remarkably little
tested empirical knowledge relative to its theoretical elaboration and disciplinary
prestige. Its apparent successes — in the [[paradox-of-voter-turnout|turnout]],
[[free-rider-problem|collective-action]], legislative [[social-choice|cycling]], and
[[spatial-model-of-voting|spatial]] literatures — dissolve on inspection into a
recurring set of [[method-driven-research|methodological pathologies]] (post hoc
theory development, slippery and vaguely operationalized predictions, searching for
confirming evidence, projecting evidence from theory, arbitrary domain restriction).
These are symptoms of **method-driven rather than problem-driven** research and of the
"characteristic aspiration" to a universal theory of politics. Green and Shapiro
predict rational choice "in its present form" will lose influence as evidence
accumulates, and argue it "fares best in environments that are evidence poor." Their
remedy is problem-driven inquiry (asking "what explains X?" rather than "how might a
rational choice model explain X?"), relinquishing pure universalism, and stating
falsification conditions. They stress they do not reject the rationality postulate, do
not object to formalism, and count some modest rational choice studies (Fiorina 1994;
Aldrich 1980; and, with qualifications, Kiewiet & McCubbins 1991, Cox & McCubbins 1993,
Krehbiel 1991) as models of achievable work.

### Rational choice defenders (1995 symposium; not yet ingested)

The rejoinders (Ferejohn, Fiorina, Ordeshook, Shepsle, Cox, and others in the 1995
*Critical Review* symposium, collected in Friedman 1996) — **recorded here as positions,
not ingested** — argued that Green and Shapiro caricature the literature and select its
weakest applications; that "post hoc" theory development and idealization are normal,
productive features of any maturing science (invoking Lakatos and Kuhn); that Green and
Shapiro apply an impossibly demanding and selectively applied standard of empirical
success that no social-science theory of comparable scope would meet; that they
understate genuine achievements; and that rational choice is not the monolithic
universalist theory the book attacks. Green and Shapiro anticipated and replied to ten
such counterarguments in their chapter 8.

### Intermediate / reformist position

A middle position accepts that the critique correctly targets universalist over-reach
and empirical laxity but holds that the productive response is *better empirical
testing within* rational choice rather than its retrenchment — a stance associated with
the subsequent "empirical implications of theoretical models" (EITM) movement. Green
and Shapiro themselves gesture toward this in praising domain-specific, well-tested
rational choice work.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed Per Side

Both sides agree that formal rational choice theory grew from a small 1950s subfield to
disciplinary prominence, that theoretical elaboration has far outpaced empirical
application, and that classic results (Arrow's theorem, Downs's median voter, Olson's
free-rider logic) are foundational. They part on what that imbalance *means*: for Green
and Shapiro it is a symptom of method-driven pathology and a reason to expect decline;
for defenders it is the normal maturation lag of an ambitious research program whose
best empirical work was only then arriving.

## Resolution Status

**Open.** The exchange reshaped methodological self-consciousness in the discipline
(contributing to later "empirical implications of theoretical models" programs) without
producing consensus on the empirical standing of applied rational choice. Curator has
not adjudicated.
