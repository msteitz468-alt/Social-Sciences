# Does the Antiquities Market Drive Looting? (Collecting Ethics Debate)

A live ethical and policy dispute over the trade in [[unprovenanced-antiquities|unprovenanced
antiquities]]: whether museum and private collecting finances the looting of archaeological
sites; whether **nationalist retentionist cultural property laws** protect the past or
nationalize it; and what collectors, curators, legislators, and scholars ought to do. The
vault now holds primary advocacy statements from **both** major poles:
[[renfrew-colin|Colin Renfrew]]'s *[[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000|Loot, Legitimacy and
Ownership]]* (2000) and [[cuno-james|James Cuno]]'s *[[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Who Owns
Antiquity?]]* (2008; afterword 2011). Each position is stated in its strongest form from
its own source; no wiki-voice verdict is asserted.

## Position A — Demand-side ("collectors are the real looters")

Held by Renfrew, and (attributed within his text) Ricardo Elia, Neil Brodie, Kathryn
Walker Tubb, and the McDonald Institute's Illicit Antiquities Research Centre.
Primary statement: [[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000]].

- The market is a **demand-pull** system: collector and museum appetite for antiquities is
  what pays looters to dig, so the moral and legal leverage lies with buyers, not only
  diggers. Elia's formulation, endorsed by Renfrew: "**collectors are the real looters**."
- **"Unprovenanced antiquities are likely to be looted antiquities"** — absence of
  documented provenance is presumptive evidence of illicit origin (post-1970 dividing
  line).
- The real harm is **loss of [[archaeological-context|context]]**, hence irreversible loss
  of knowledge — not merely a property wrong. A beautiful object without context "can tell
  us little that is new."
- Buying unprovenanced material is likened to **ransoming a kidnap victim** or **collecting
  the eggs of endangered birds** — the "good home" / "Good Collector" rationale is a
  legitimizing fiction. Museums that exhibit or publish such objects perform "antiquity
  laundering."
- **Prescription:** neither institutions nor collectors should buy, accept, or even
  loan-display unprovenanced antiquities; no tax relief for such donations; states should
  ratify the 1970 UNESCO and 1995 Unidroit Conventions, restrict imports, and license
  dealers; scholars should refuse to authenticate or publish unprovenanced pieces.

## Position B — Cultural internationalism and the encyclopedic museum

Held programmatically by [[cuno-james|James Cuno]] (*[[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008|Who Owns
Antiquity?]]*, 2008/2011), drawing on legal scholar **John Henry Merryman** ("cultural
internationalism"; the triad **preservation, truth, access**) and British Museum director
**Neil MacGregor**. Private collectors and dealing-trade voices often converge on outcomes
with this camp; Cuno is the vault's primary statement of the museum-side program.

### Surrogate-debate thesis (central, attributed — Cuno 2008)

Cuno argues that the public fight over museum acquisition of unprovenanced antiquities is
**not** primarily museums vs. archaeologists about looting. It is **museums vs. modern
nation-states** and their nationalist claims on heritage; archaeologists, on his account,
are allies of those states. Settling the acquisition fight in favor of archaeologists and
source governments would, he claims, **not** protect the archaeological record but would
preserve nation-states' ownership claims — a diversion while the world divides along
nationalist and sectarian lines.

### Core claims (attributed — Cuno 2008/2011)

- **"Antiquity cannot be owned."** Antiquities are the common heritage of humankind
  ("people, not peoples"). Findspot within modern borders is "a matter of chance"; there
  is "no natural and indelible connection" between antiquities and modern nation-states.
- **[[cultural-property|Cultural property]] is a political construct** — whatever a sovereign authority
  claims it to be. Modern populations often do not share language, religion, law, or art
  with the ancient makers whose objects happen to lie under their territory (his Egypt /
  Pharaonic example).
- **Nationalist retentionist cultural property laws** nationalize heritage, fail as
  site-protection tools (they relocate markets rather than stop looting), promote
  sectarian identity politics, and — channeling Merryman on UNESCO 1970 — condone
  "over-retention" or "hoarding."
- **[[encyclopedic-museum|Encyclopedic museums]]** (British Museum model) present many
  world cultures under one roof, foster cosmopolitan curiosity and hybridity, and differ
  from **national museums** that legitimize a local identity narrative. Their Western
  concentration is not, for Cuno, an argument against the ideal; the ideal should be
  expanded worldwide (loans, exchanges, new institutions).
- **Meaning without findspot:** antiquities retain aesthetic, technological, iconographic,
  and epigraphic meaning outside archaeological context; much Near Eastern and Maya
  history, he claims, is known from unprovenanced seals, tablets, and ceramics.
- **"Clean hands" ethics** that refuse museum acquisition of already-looted objects leave
  them unknown in private hands — which he presents as ethically contestable.
- **Prescription:** restore scientific excavation and **[[partage]]**; allow
  *reasonable* acquisition of unprovenanced antiquities; strengthen and spread
  encyclopedic museums; share collections and expertise; shift regulation from pure
  ownership to Merryman's **preservation / knowledge / access** stewardship triad;
  disperse risk rather than concentrate objects in presumed countries of origin
  (Afghanistan, Iraq cited).

### Afterword updates (attributed — Cuno 2011)

- The **14 January 2009** U.S. acceptance of a modified **China** import-restriction
  request (CPIA/CPAC process; Paleolithic through Tang categories, plus older monumental
  sculpture/wall art) is read as **foreign-policy politics**, not archaeological
  protection.
- **AAMD** 2008 guidelines ("normally should not acquire" without pre-1970 export or
  legal post-1970 export) are treated as a symbolic bright line that freezes Western
  museum acquisition without stopping looting or private/non-Western buying.
- Patriotic "recovery" purchases (Poly Museum / Summer Palace zodiac heads; Cai Mingchao
  Christie's bid) illustrate nationalist identity politics that, on his reading, exclude
  non-Han subjects of the Chinese state.

### Empirical densification of Position A (attributed — Brodie et al. 2006)

[[brodie-kersel-luke-tubb-archaeology-cultural-heritage-antiquities-trade-2006|Archaeology, Cultural Heritage, and the Antiquities Trade]] (eds. [[brodie-neil|Neil Brodie]], [[kersel-morag-m|Morag M. Kersel]], [[luke-christina|Christina Luke]], [[tubb-kathryn-walker|Kathryn Walker Tubb]], 2006) is not a second manifesto but a **case-and-law densification** of demand-side claims. Key attributed contributions to Position A:

- **Market structure.** Brodie and Kersel model the trade as **archaeologically rich (source) → transit → destination** networks; laundering occurs where “no questions asked” free-port or weak-documentation regimes rebrand objects as legitimate commodities. Finders typically receive **less than 1%** of eventual retail value (Kersel citing Borodkin); a Hebron→Jerusalem→NYC coin cascade example multiplies village prices ~**50×**.
- **Landscape metrics, not only object counts.** Roosevelt & Luke’s 2001 Lydian tumulus survey: of **397** inspected mounds, **90%** showed looting and **18%** were completely destroyed — arguing that restitution of a famous “hoard” (Lydian Hoard / Met → Turkey 1993) does not restore destroyed landscapes.
- **Conflict cases as demand + enforcement failure.** Brodie’s Iraq 1991–2005 chapter (Bogdanos inventory: **&gt;13,864** objects stolen from the [[iraq-national-museum|Iraq National Museum]] by Jan 2005 assessments; mass southern site damage) and van Krieken-Pieters on Afghanistan (looting **plus** iconoclasm) supply wartime evidence Position A reads as proving international demand and weak import-side controls.
- **Dealer prosecutions as structure, not anecdote.** Watson on Schultz / Tokeley-Parry / Medici / Symes: Switzerland as entrepôt; inventory scale far larger than open-auction surface; banks and freeports as laundering infrastructure.
- **Museum “reputation laundering.”** Renfrew’s ch. 13 restates: exhibition/publication of unprovenanced pieces is “the up-market version of money laundering”; “no documentation, no acquisition.”
- **Shared-patrimony education (Lazrus)** and **emotion (Tubb)** address demand-side culture change without adopting Cuno’s encyclopedic-collecting prescription — record as allied stewardship rhetoric, not identical to Position B.

Legal densification (Papa Sokal; [[gerstenblith-patty|Gerstenblith]]): U.S. **CPIA 1983**, **NSPA/McClain/Schultz**, ARPA, sentencing guideline §2B1.5 — the domestic teeth behind UNESCO norms. These are **brute legal facts** as reported; their *sufficiency* remains position-dependent.

### Complication — legal markets and indigenous subsistence digging (attributed — Hollowell 2006)

Julie Hollowell’s St. Lawrence Island (Sivuqaq) chapter documents a **legal** antiquities market under U.S. private-property and ANCSA subsurface title, in which Yupik “subsistence diggers” recover “old things” (including pre-MMPA walrus ivory) for sale. Islanders do not frame themselves as “looters.” Hollowell argues free-market theory **fails** to protect the archaeological record even where digging is lawful; the case **complicates** simple illicit/licit binaries without adjudicating Position A vs B. Record as a distinct empirical type on [[illicit-antiquities-trade]], not as a counter-proof that all collecting is harmless.

## Position C — Cultural nationalism (related axis)

Source states (Turkey, Greece, Italy, China, Egypt, and others) assert that antiquities
are **national patrimony** belonging to the country of origin as a matter of sovereignty
and identity, independent of the knowledge-loss argument. This grounds [[restitution]]
suits and import-restriction requests (e.g. Italy 2001, China 2005→2009 U.S. MOUs as
reported by Cuno) and overlaps with Position A on many *outcomes* while differing in
*rationale* (identity and sovereignty vs. context destruction). Cuno's Chapters 3–4 treat
Turkish and Chinese cases as nation-building uses of archaeology and law; those readings
are **his positions**, not wiki-voice findings about those states. The broader
nationalism axis is also at [[archaeology-and-nationalism-debate]] and
[[nationalism-and-archaeology]].

## Shared Factual Ground (framed per side)

All sides accept that: large-scale looting and market flows of antiquities occur; named
instruments exist (1954 Hague; **1970 UNESCO**; **1995 Unidroit**; U.S. **CPIA 1983** and
CPAC process; national patrimony laws); the **1970** date functions as a professional
watershed even where it lacks automatic juridical force; major museums have returned
objects and tightened acquisition codes (AAMD 2006–2008 trajectory as Cuno reports);
encyclopedic museums and national museums are different institutional types with
different histories. What divides the sides is the **causal and moral reading**:

| Question | Position A (Renfrew) | Position B (Cuno) | Position C (source states) |
|---|---|---|---|
| What drives looting? | Collector/museum demand | Source conditions + failed retentionism; acquisition ban is diversion | Often: foreign demand and historical theft |
| What is the primary wrong? | Context/knowledge loss | Nationalist falsification of ownership; loss of access/hybridity | Violation of national patrimony / sovereignty |
| Unprovenanced object | Likely looted; do not acquire | May still carry meaning; reasonable acquisition possible | State property if from our soil |
| Best institutions | Strict acquisition codes; ratify conventions | Encyclopedic museums + partage | National museums; export control; restitution |

## Resolution Status

**Open.** Practical museum policy since ~2000 has moved toward Position A's acquisition
restraints (AAMD 1970 line; high-profile returns; dealer prosecutions such as Schultz
2001 as reported by Cuno), while source-state retention and MOU regimes have expanded
(Italy, China). The underlying dispute — demand-side complicity vs. cultural
internationalism vs. national patrimony — remains unresolved. No adjudication in wiki
voice.

## Curator's note (2026-07-15)

As of Renfrew + Cuno ingests, Positions A and B each rest on a primary advocacy source
([[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000]], [[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008]]). **Brodie et al. 2006**
adds multi-author **empirical and legal densification** of Position A (and allied
stewardship voices) without replacing Cuno as Position B’s primary statement; Position C
gains practitioner testimony (Pachauri on India) but still lacks a full source-state
theoretical treatise. Hollowell’s legal-market case is a category complication, not a
third pole. Merryman’s essays remain desirable as Position B’s legal-theory backbone.
