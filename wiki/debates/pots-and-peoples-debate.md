# Pots and Peoples (archaeological culture ≠ people)

A standing dispute in prehistoric archaeology over whether a named **[[archaeological-culture|archaeological culture]]** (a recurrent package of pots, tools, houses, burial rites) may be treated as the material signature of a **people** — a self-conscious or ethnic community that migrates, diffuses, and has a history. The culture-historical program of [[childe-v-gordon|V. Gordon Childe]] and continental *Kultur* archaeology answered yes (or at least worked as if yes); later theory and genetics treat that equation as the load-bearing error of the paradigm. This wiki's standing rule is the **four-way non-identity** of archaeological culture, language, genetic population, and self-conscious people — any equation across those categories is a *claim* requiring argument and attribution (`associated_with`, not identity).

## Positions

### Position A — Trait complex as people (culture-historical working assumption)

[[childe-v-gordon|Childe]] defined an archaeological culture as a complex of regularly associated traits and treated cultures as the units that *act* in prehistory: they spread, borrow, colonize, and leave "survivals." In *[[childe-dawn-european-civilization-1958|The Dawn of European Civilization]]* (6th ed., 1957/58) and *[[childe-danube-in-prehistory-1929|The Danube in Prehistory]]* (1929) he writes of "Danubian folk," "Beaker-folk," "megalith builders," and "Battle-axe peoples" as historical agents. The 1929 Preface is the vault's clearest method statement: culture = material expression of a people; race only when exclusive skeletal association is present. Continental antecedents (notably Kossinna's ethnic prehistory) are harsher in equating pottery provinces with *Völker*; Childe's English synthesis softens racial language but retains the people-unit for narrative.

### Position B — Four-way non-identity (processual, post-processual, wiki rule)

Critics of culture-history argue that pottery styles, house plans, and burial rites can spread by trade, fashion, craft specialization, or elite emulation without population replacement; that "a culture" is an **etic construct** imposed by the analyst; and that equating pots with peoples licensed nationalist and racist prehistories. Processual archaeology dissolved the culture-as-people unit into systems and adaptive strategies; post-processual work further stressed meaning, agency, and the politics of ethnic labels. This wiki encodes the critique as standing method: archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genes ≠ people; use `associated_with` when sources assert identity.

### Position C — Case-specific association (aDNA era)

Ancient DNA (as of Reich 2018 and collaborators) has shown that *some* archaeological packages — notably [[corded-ware-culture|Corded Ware]] and parts of the [[bell-beaker-culture|Bell Beaker]] phenomenon north of the Alps — track substantial population turnover, while others (e.g. Iberian Beaker material without steppe ancestry) do not. That does not reinstate Position A as a general rule; it licenses **attributed, dated, case-by-case** association claims. Pots still are not people by default.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Named archaeological cultures (Danubian I / LBK, Corded Ware, Bell Beaker, Únětice, etc.) are standard analytic units in European prehistory.
- Childe's definitions and *Dawn*/*Danube* narratives are the Anglophone charter for treating those units as historical actors.
- Radiocarbon calibration overturned the short *[[ex-oriente-lux|ex oriente lux]]* chronology that often traveled with culture-history; the pots–peoples equation is a separable issue that survived that chronological collapse in modified form.
- aDNA has vindicated migration for some packages and rejected it for others (as of post-2010 work — date-stamp all genetic claims).

## Cases from *Dawn* (6th ed., 1957/58) — attributed

Childe’s Europe-wide narrative multiplies named “folk” agents. Representative load-bearing passages (all **as of Childe 1958**):

| Childe’s agent language | Material package | How *Dawn* uses it |
|---|---|---|
| Immigrant shepherds and cultivators | cereals + domestic stock | Ch. I close: mesolithic gatherers cannot invent farming; demic carriers required |
| Danubian folk / Danubian I | LBK longhouses, *Bandkeramik*, shoe-last adzes | Paradigm farming culture spreading across the loess |
| Beaker-folk | Bell Beakers, archery kit, sometimes metallurgy | “Merchant adventurers” / hybridizing folk (Ch. XII) — not always a single people even in Childe |
| Battle-axe / Corded peoples | corded pottery, battle-axes, single graves | Invasion/fusion narratives in the North and Forest zones |
| Megalith builders | passage/gallery graves | Often *idea/religion* diffusion rather than one people — Childe hedges |
| East Mediterranean prospectors | Los Millares tholoi, Iberian copper | Colonist-craftsmen model for metallurgy at the SW edge |
| Minyans / first Greek-speakers | Minyan ware, MH sequence | Culture → language equation (Ch. V) — especially strong identity claim |

Internal hedges in the same book matter for Position A’s strength: Childe groups disparate microlith industries under a “rather misleading” Tardenoisian label; treats Battle-axe as not one culture in places; and rejects Elliot Smith/Perry hyper-diffusion (“Children of the Sun”) when northern megaliths lack Egyptian imports. The people-unit remains the *default narrative grammar* even when particular equations are softened.

## Additional Statement of Position B: Johnson's Glossary (attributed/wiki voice, Johnson 2011)

[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Matthew Johnson]]'s textbook glossary offers its own
compact version of the four-way non-identity rule this page encodes (wiki voice, as
Johnson's own explicitly presented working definition): **"An archaeological culture is a
repeatedly recurring assemblage of traits – pottery and house forms, burial practices –
seen over a discrete time and space. It may or may not relate to a human culture."**
Johnson distinguishes four senses of "culture" in circulation (normative, systemic,
structural-linguistic, civilizational), underscoring that no single sense straightforwardly
licenses a culture-to-people equation.

Johnson also cites **Furholt (2008)** (attributed, bibliographic) as "an effective case
study critiquing the equation of archaeological 'culture' with cultural group" — a
further, independent scholarly voice for Position B beyond the processual and
post-processual critiques already recorded above. Notably, this caution is not confined
to Childe's critics: even [[childe-v-gordon|Childe]]'s own 1929 *Danube* Preface
(Position A's canonical statement) hedges that culture-to-people equations require
independent skeletal/racial evidence to be pressed with confidence — Johnson's glossary
entry can be read as a plainer restatement of a caution already present, if
underemphasized, in the culture-historical charter text itself.

## Resolution status

**Partially resolved** at the level of method: the field no longer treats culture = people as a default. **Open** as case-level historical questions (which packages track demic movement, which track only style or practice). This page does not adjudicate individual migration claims.
