# Service vs Fried Political Typology Debate

## Shared ground

Both [[service-elman-r|Service]] and [[fried-morton-h|Fried]] worked in mid-century US **neo-evolutionary** political anthropology. Both sought general levels of political organization beyond pure historical particularism and pure synchronic functionalism; both used comparative ethnography as imperfect evolutionary evidence; both influenced archaeological complexity language; both drew on Sahlins’s general/specific evolution frame (Service collaboratively; Fried applicatively). Redistribution, reciprocity, and the problem of the state are common vocabulary even when definitions diverge.

## Positions

### Service — band / tribe / chiefdom (integrative levels)

[[band-tribe-chiefdom-model|Service]] (*[[service-primitive-social-organization-1971|Primitive Social Organization]]*, 1962/1971) classifies by **modes of social integration**:

- **Band:** residential associations of nuclear families; familistic economy/polity/religion; [[patrilocal-band]] as expectable aboriginal type (softened 2nd ed.).
- **Tribe:** pan-tribal [[sodality|sodalities]] integrating multiple communities; personal-charismatic leadership; still broadly egalitarian relative to chiefdom.
- **Chiefdom:** permanent redistributional center; office of chief; hereditary rank — short of state force monopoly.
- **State:** largely beyond PSO (force monopoly / specialized apparatus in later work).

### Fried — egalitarian / rank / stratified / state (status and resources)

[[egalitarian-rank-stratified-state-model|Fried]] (*[[fried-evolution-of-political-society-1967|Evolution of Political Society]]*, 1967) classifies by **status scarcity** and **access to basic resources**:

- **[[egalitarian-society]]:** as many prestige positions as capable persons; reciprocal economy; no law; no ranking/stratification.
- **[[rank-society]]:** limited prestige positions without necessary resource monopoly; redistribution; relatively stable.
- **[[stratified-society]]:** differential access to basic resources; unstable; nearly unobservable ethnographically as nonstate form.
- **[[the-state]]:** power organized superior to kinship; maintains stratification; [[pristine-state]] vs [[secondary-state]].

### Fried’s explicit critique of “tribe” (as position)

Fried (EPS Ch. 4, attributed) praises Service–Sahlins treatment of tribe as sodality-linked level but **rejects tribe as evolutionary stage**: most tribes are secondary/colonial creations; tribal borders are no clearer than band borders; the **chiefdom** carries the evolutionary advance in Service–Sahlins schemes; real questions are ranking and stratification. He also disputes treating external polity as **military-only** — feasts and potlatch organize intercommunity systems.

### Service-side contrast (as position)

Service retains **tribe** as a general level defined by pan-tribal associations and treats **chiefdom** as the ranked redistributional stage without Fried’s intermediate “stratified nonstate” construct. Band→tribe→chiefdom is an integration sequence; resource stratification is not the primary cut.

### Later critiques of stage models

Reject unilinear or stage ladders; treat “chiefdom” and “stratified society” as reified; prefer historical pathways, dual-processual models, heterarchy, network complexity (positions to be expanded as sources arrive).

## Points of tension (not verdicts)

| Issue | Service emphasis | Fried emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| Primary axis | Mode of integration | Status scarcity + basic-resource access |
| Middle form | Tribe (sodalities); chiefdom (office + redistribution) | Rank (prestige); stratified (resources) as unstable bridge |
| “Tribe” | General evolutionary level | Mostly secondary; cul-de-sac |
| State origin | Beyond PSO; later force-monopoly work | From stratified instability; pristine/secondary |
| External polity | Strong military competition theme | Feasts/redistribution as co-equal organizers; war tracks resource pressure |
| Observability | Levels illustrated ethnographically | Stratified nonstate almost unfindable — reconstruct |

## Resolution status

**Partially-resolved / absorbed:** neither pure program dominates research design; both vocabularies survive in textbooks and archaeology. Primary/secondary (pristine/secondary) state language is especially durable from Fried; chiefdom from Service. The **disagreement on cut-points** remains analytically productive rather than settled by consensus.

## Johnson (2011) on the pair as one method (attributed)

Matthew Johnson’s textbook synthesis (*[[johnson-archaeological-theory-2011|Archaeological
Theory]]*, Ch.9) treats Service’s and Fried’s typologies as **methodologically
identical**: both were compiled from ethnographic comparison, both share the same
start point (egalitarian/band) and rough end point (state), and both proceeded by
seeking archaeological correlates of typological stages in the record. On this
reading, the substantive disagreement over cut-points (recorded above) sits inside a
shared research strategy rather than reflecting two fundamentally different research
programs.

## Service (*Origins of the State and Civilization*, 1975) — sharpening the origin-of-the-state contrast

Service's 1975 book gives his fullest statement of the **integrative/benefit** account of state origins (see [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]]), built on the same [[chiefdom]]/[[redistribution]] apparatus as *Primitive Social Organization* but focused squarely on *why* states form rather than on the levels typology alone. As a position, this sharpens rather than resolves the cut-point disagreement recorded above: Service records that he and Fried were friends and Columbia colleagues since 1946, and states explicitly that his 1975 book "diverges from Fried's book in concentrating more on the actual origins of the state, whereas he has only one chapter on this subject." The dedicated origin-of-the-state contrast — integrative/theocratic (Service) vs. stratification/conflict (Fried, who explicitly denied warfare or slavery caused pristine-state origins) — is tracked in full on [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]; this page retains the typology-level (band/tribe/chiefdom vs. egalitarian/rank/stratified/state) cut, recorded as position only.
