# Cultural Homogenization vs Heterogenization (Globalization)

A standing dispute in the cultural analysis of [[globalization]], framed sharply by
[[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]: "the central problem of today's global interactions
is the tension between cultural homogenization and cultural heterogenization"
*(Appadurai 1996)*. Positions recorded, not adjudicated.

## Position A — Homogenization

Global cultural flows tend toward **convergence**. Appadurai identifies a
"homogenization argument" literature (he names Schiller 1976, Mattelart 1983,
Hamelink 1983) that "subspeciates into either an argument about **Americanization**
or an argument about **commoditization**" — a world drawn toward a media- and
market-driven monoculture. Political-economy and world-systems readings (cf.
[[world-systems-analysis]]) stress capital's homogenizing and polarizing power.

**Strongest form:** metropolitan media, commodities, and language hegemonies
overwhelm local forms, producing a standardized global culture on unequal terms.

## Position B — Heterogenization / Indigenization

[[appadurai-arjun|Appadurai]]'s counter: imported forms are **indigenized** "at
least as rapidly as forces from various metropolises are brought into new
societies" — applying "to music and housing styles as much as… to science and
terrorism, spectacles and constitutions." Homogenizing instruments get "repatriated
as heterogeneous dialogues of national sovereignty, free enterprise, and
fundamentalism," with the state acting as "arbitrageur" of this repatriation of
difference. Fear of absorption is scalar and relative, not simply "Americanization"
("Indonesianization," "Japanization," "Indianization"): **"One man's imagined
community is another man's political prison."** See [[vernacular-globalization]] and
the [[global-cultural-flows|scapes/disjuncture]] framework.

**Strongest form:** "the globalization of culture is not the same as its
homogenization"; the system runs on the mutual cannibalization of sameness and
difference, not convergence.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

Both sides agree that metropolitan (esp. U.S.) media and commodities circulate
globally at unprecedented scale. They disagree on the **outcome**: whether reception
converges toward a monoculture (homogenization) or generates new, locally inflected
differences (heterogenization), and on how much weight to give capital's structuring
power versus local agency.
