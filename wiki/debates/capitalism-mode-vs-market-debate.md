# Capitalism as Mode of Production vs as World Market

Live theoretical dispute over **what makes a system capitalist**: a distinctive [[mode-of-production|mode of production]] centered on wage labor and capital's command of production, or a **world-market / world-economy** structured by core–periphery accumulation in which diverse labor regimes (slavery, sharecropping, wage labor) are equally "capitalist" once integrated.

## Positions

### Mode-of-production specification (Wolf)

[[wolf-eric-r|Eric R. Wolf]] in *[[wolf-europe-people-without-history-1982|Europe and the People Without History]]* (esp. Ch. 3, Ch. 10) distinguishes the **capitalist mode** from **mercantile wealth** and from the **capitalist world market**. Load-bearing claims (attributed):

- Capitalism begins when monetary wealth can **buy [[labor-power]]** and control means of production; wealth that only skims products is not capital — "There is no such thing as mercantile or merchant capitalism… only mercantile wealth. Capitalism… must be capitalism-in-production."
- The capitalist mode dates to the **late eighteenth century**, not to 15th–18th-c. mercantile expansion (contra Frank/Wallerstein).
- Capitalist market relations are **predicated on** the mode, not the reverse (with Polanyi).
- Putting-out and commodity peonage **border** capitalism but do not yet buy labor power in a competitive job market or control the labor process; surplus is unequal exchange, not surplus value.
- Endorses Mandel's "articulated system of capitalist, semi-capitalist, and pre-capitalist relations… dominated by the capitalist world market" — mode may dominate without turning all peoples into industrial surplus-value producers; preserves heterogeneity vs pure core–periphery erasure.

### World-economy specification (Frank / Wallerstein program)

As Wolf characterizes them: [[world-systems-analysis|Wallerstein]] and A. G. Frank define capitalism as production for the market and surplus transfer by nonproducing entrepreneurs. For Wallerstein especially, how [[social-labor|social labor]] is deployed is secondary — all surplus producers under capitalist exchange are "proletarians," all surplus takers "capitalists." These models, Wolf argues, collapse mode into world market and identify European expansion since the fifteenth century with capitalism entire. The [[world-systems-analysis]] program itself treats the modern [[capitalist-world-economy]] as a single historical system of endless accumulation with varying [[modes-of-labor-control|modes of labor control]] by zone — a related but not identical vocabulary.

### Articulation / middle positions

Mandel (via Wolf) and many historical anthropologists stress **articulation**: capitalist circuits reorganize non-capitalist modes without immediate homogenization. Mercantile alliances with political power, slavery as subsidiary under all modes, and reserve-army mobilization of kin-ordered/tributary populations are cases.

## Shared Factual Ground

European expansion intensified long-distance commodity flows; industrial capital transformed production in cores; peripheral labor regimes were heterogeneous (slave, tribute, kin, wage, indenture). Dispute is classification and causal priority, not the bare occurrence of trade or factories.

## Resolution Status

**Open.** Pedagogically both vocabularies remain in use; research often combines connectionist global frames with mode-sensitive local analysis. Related: [[modernization-vs-dependency-world-systems-debate]].
