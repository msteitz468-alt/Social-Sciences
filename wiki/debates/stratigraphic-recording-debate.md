# Section vs. Plan: What Is the Primary Stratigraphic Document?

A methodological dispute in field archaeology over **what carries the authoritative record
of a site's stratigraphy** — the physically preserved vertical *section*, or the *plan*-based
recording system whose sequence is reconstructed analytically. It runs through the second
half of the twentieth century and is the methodological through-line of
[[lucas-gavin|Gavin Lucas]]'s *[[lucas-critical-approaches-fieldwork-2002|Critical Approaches
to Fieldwork]]* (2002). Do not read the positions below as settled; each is stated in its
strongest form.

## Position 1 — The section is primary (grid-and-baulk)

For [[wheeler-mortimer|Mortimer Wheeler]] and his follower Kathleen Kenyon, "stratigraphy
*was* the section": stratification "must, by its nature, always be controlled from the side"
(Wheeler 1954), and the [[box-grid-excavation|box-grid]]'s standing baulks preserve
continuous vertical sections as the site's primary control and record. A 1974 statement
(Grinsell et al.) held that the excavator's interpretation "depended entirely on sections,
so they stand as the only record of that interpretation." Lucas argues (attributed) this
conception was **biased toward "layers"** — an "overly geological conception" — and that
Wheeler and Kenyon did *not* treat the interface/cut as a numbered stratigraphic unit in its
own right.

## Position 2 — The plan is primary (single-context / Harris matrix)

From Martin Biddle's Winchester Research Unit (criticizing the grid as "imposed on the
site") to Edward C. Harris's [[harris-matrix|matrix]] (1973) and
[[single-context-recording|single-context recording]] (1975), the sequence is built
**analytically** from independently-recorded contexts, and the [[open-area-excavation|open
area]] is excavated baulk-free in plan. Harris's topological definition of the unit (by its
interface, independent of content) allows **negative units** (cuts) to be recorded as units
— which section-and-content approaches miss. On this view "stratigraphy became truly
independent of the section," and the 1974 "sections are the only record" claim "swiftly
became untrue."

## Position 3 — The deposit's content is primary (geological / geoarchaeological)

The geoarchaeological school (Gasche & Tunca 1983; Farrand; Stein 1987; developed from soil
science and sedimentology) defines a stratigraphic unit by its **lithologic content** and
describes the record as fundamentally a natural datum incorporating cultural material —
Stein: "culture is only one of the many… agents of transport and deposition." Lucas frames
its opposition to the Harris "form" school as a **form-vs-content** contrast: the content
school captures depositional constitution but tends to miss negative (cut) events, while the
Harris school captures topological events but ignores a unit's material constitution and the
visibility/scale problem of ambiguous boundaries.

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

All sides agree excavation is destructive and non-repeatable, so the *record* — however
constituted — bears the epistemic weight. They disagree on which record is primary and on
whether the model of stratigraphy should be geological or archaeological. In practice the
plan-based single-context system won the deeply-stratified urban context and much of British
and North American field practice from the 1970s–80s, while section drawing remains a
standard control; the geological/archaeological modelling question remains open.

## Curator's note (2026-07-10)

Lucas's own move (attributed) is to reject the terms of the object-focused debate: he argues
both the Harris matrix and the geological school treat the stratigraphic unit as an
**object** with no intrinsic temporality, and proposes foregrounding the unit's **duration
and event-character** instead (see [[archaeological-record]], [[eventful-temporality]]). This
is his position, not an adjudication of the section-vs-plan question.
