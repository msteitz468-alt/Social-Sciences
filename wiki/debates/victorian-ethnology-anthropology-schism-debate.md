# The Victorian Ethnological–Anthropological Schism (1863–1871)

The institutional and intellectual split in 1860s British anthropology between the **Ethnological Society of London** and the breakaway **Anthropological Society of London** — a dispute simultaneously about the name, method, politics, and racial doctrine of the science of man. [[stocking-victorian-anthropology-1987|Stocking]] treats it (attributed) as the organizational expression of three distinct mid-century orientations, resolved in 1871 by a compromise — the Anthropological Institute — that he reads as a Hegelian thesis–antithesis–synthesis. Positions are recorded here in their strongest form; the wiki does not adjudicate, and the racial-species doctrines below are historical positions, not findings.

## Background

The oldest lineal ancestor of British anthropological institutions was, Stocking notes, not disinterested science but the **Aborigines Protection Society** (founded 1837 by Thomas Hodgkin, out of the group supporting Thomas Fowell Buxton's 1835–36 Parliamentary Select Committee on Aborigines). Its combination of humanitarian purpose and scientific curiosity produced, in 1843, the **Ethnological Society of London** (from Richard King's 1842 prospectus, on the model of the 1839 Société Ethnologique de Paris). The Ethnological Society carried the Prichardian commitment to monogenism and an environmentalist, religiously-humanitarian explanation of racial variation (see [[ethnology]], [[prichard-james-cowles]]).

## Position 1 — The "ethnologicals"

Monogenist (the unity of the human species "never in doubt"), environmentalist, humanitarian, and — after 1864 — the institutional base of the Darwinian evolutionists ([[lubbock-john|Lubbock]] elected president 1864; [[huxley-thomas-henry|Huxley]] 1868). Predominantly dissenting middle-class Liberals (roughly 3:1 Liberal), an "intellectual aristocracy" of respectable gentlemanly conduct. They defended the older word "ethnology" — Lubbock called it "an older and prettier word" than "anthropology."

## Position 2 — The "anthropologicals"

[[hunt-james|James Hunt]], following [[unknown|Paul Broca]]'s Société d'Anthropologie de Paris, founded the **Anthropological Society of London** in 1863, defining "anthropology" as "the science of the whole nature of man" against a merely "ethnological" science of races hamstrung by biblical dogma. The group was **polygenist**, emphasized racial classification, and rejected Darwinian evolution (Hunt: little difference between "a disciple of Darwin and a disciple of Moses"). Hunt's presidential address "On the Negro's Place in Nature" argued the extreme hereditarian racism he had, by his own account, "imbibed from the late Dr. Knox." Socially the anthropologicals were more marginal and more Tory; their inner "Cannibal Club" affected a deliberately provocative style.

## Position 3 — The Darwinian evolutionists

Huxley, Lubbock, Wallace, and (early) [[tylor-edward-burnett|Tylor]] were hostile to Hunt's society despite the later association of "anthropology" with Darwinism — Huxley called it "that nest of imposters." They resisted the *name* "anthropology" while pursuing the Darwinian science of "Man's Place in Nature" from within the Ethnological Society. Stocking argues (attributed) that Darwinism let them come to terms *with* polygenist race science rather than simply rejecting it: Huxley described Darwin's theory as "reconciling and combining all that is good in the Monogenistic and Polygenistic schools" (see [[polygenism]], [[psychic-unity-of-mankind]]).

## Resolution — the Victorian anthropological compromise

After repeated clashes (1864–1870) at the British Association over section naming, and several failed amalgamation attempts, the two bodies merged in 1871 as the **Anthropological Institute of Great Britain and Ireland** — the name proposed by Huxley (recognizing "the science but not the society" of the anthropologicals), with council seats evenly divided and [[lubbock-john|Lubbock]] as president. A residual faction seceded (the short-lived London Anthropological Society), later readmitted only as individuals. Stocking reads the outcome (attributed) as a synthesis absorbing both ethnology and polygenist physical anthropology while rejecting *both* active humanitarianism *and* overt political racism — a "science" held apart from party strife and from the present life of its "dark-skinned savage subject matter." The founding coincided fittingly with Darwin's *Descent of Man* (1871).

The debate is filed `resolved-by-consensus` in the narrow institutional sense (the 1871 merger ended the organizational war). Its intellectual residues — the entanglement of the discipline with scientific racism and the marginalization of its humanitarian origins — remain live matter for the [[race-and-culture-debate]] and the [[anthropology-and-colonialism-debate]].
