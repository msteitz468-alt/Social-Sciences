# Universalism vs. Relativism in Psychiatric Diagnosis

Whether psychiatric disorders are cross-culturally universal natural kinds or culturally constituted
categories — a standing dispute in [[cross-cultural-psychiatry|cross-cultural psychiatry]] that
[[kleinman-arthur|Kleinman]]'s *[[kleinman-rethinking-psychiatry-1988|Rethinking Psychiatry]]* stakes out
a middle position within.

## Positions

**Position A — Universalism.** The major mental disorders are real, biologically grounded, and
identifiable everywhere. Jane Murphy (1976), arguing against labeling theory, found that insanity is
similarly recognized across cultures: "there seems to be little that is distinctively cultural in the
attitudes and actions directed toward the mentally ill"; responses track the person's behavior more than a
"preexisting cultural set." Westermeyer & Wintrob and others find every society has *some* concept of
madness. Biological psychiatry adds twin/adoption evidence for disorders like schizophrenia.

**Position B — Relativism / particularism.** Concepts of self, emotion, body, and illness differ enough
across cultures that each culture's normal/abnormal is distinctive (Rosaldo 1980; Lutz 1985; Good 1977;
Schieffelin 1985). Lutz argues professional psychological categories are themselves "one kind of
ethnopsychology." Some cultures have no label for depression, or conceive sadness in ways that "make no
sense in Western society." Radical versions (a Foucauldian line) treat categories like schizophrenia as
inseparable from a culturally specific "bounded self."

**Position C — Cultural interactionism (Kleinman's).** Both poles capture part of the truth. "The
epidemiological data do not sustain the radical cultural relativist argument… The chief mental disorders
are diagnosable worldwide" — yet imposing an unvalidated category is a [[category-fallacy|category
fallacy]]. Florid psychosis is constrained by shared psychobiology in *form*, while imputed cause,
salient symptoms, course, stigma, and even label-existence are culturally particular. Kleinman names this
a "cultural interactionist or modified relativist" stance and rejects both "the extreme relativism of some
antipsychiatry anthropologists" and "the universalistic fundamentalism of some card-carrying biological
psychiatrists."

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

All sides accept that (a) severe psychosis is recognized in some form across societies, and (b) symptom
expression, help-seeking, and stigma vary cross-culturally. They divide on whether the *categories* carve
nature at its joints (A), are cultural constructions (B), or are dialectical products of biology and
culture jointly (C). The evidentiary crux is whether cross-culturally invariant "core symptoms" (e.g., the
WHO IPSS schizophrenia findings) reflect a universal disease or an artifact of instruments and restrictive
sampling designed to find universals — the reliability-vs-validity problem.

## Resolution Status

**Open.** The debate is not settled; the DSM's later cultural-formulation apparatus and the growth of
global mental health both reflect Position C gaining ground without displacing biological psychiatry.
