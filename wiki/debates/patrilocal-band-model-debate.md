# The Patrilocal Band-Model Debate

Whether the hunter-gatherer band is essentially a **territorial, exogamous,
patrilocal/patrilineal unit** (the classical model) or an **open, fluid, bilateral
grouping** (the flux model). Part III of *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man the Hunter]]*
(1968) is the debate's decisive moment: a run of new fieldwork collectively broke the
classical model's claim to be the modal form.

*(Positions recorded in strongest form; the field's convergence is noted below.)*

## Position A — the classical band model

*(Radcliffe-Brown 1931; [[steward-julian-h|Steward]] 1936/1955; [[service-elman-r|Service]]
1962.)* Radcliffe-Brown held that Australian local organization consisted of the **horde** —
"the male members of a patrician, their wives, and unmarried female members" — an autonomous
unit drawing food from its own exclusive territory, trespass "justifiably... punished by
death." Steward formalized the [[patrilineal-band]] (vs. [[composite-hunting-band|composite]]
and family bands), and Service recast it as the [[patrilocal-band|patrilocal band]], the
"basic form for all hunter-gatherers in the past," with composite/family bands as
acculturative products. At the symposium B. J. Williams defended a version as "an optimal
solution... over a very broad range of environmental diversity."

## Position B — the flux/flexibility model

*(Hiatt, Woodburn, Damas, Helm, Turnbull, Lee, Marshall.)* New fieldwork found the classical
unit did not exist as described. Hiatt's review "failed to find a single indisputable
example" of the patrilocal horde, concluding investigators were "looking for something that
never existed." [[hadza|Hadza]] camps (Woodburn) were "open, flexible," non-territorial, and
best not called "bands" at all; [[mbuti|Mbuti]] (Turnbull) organized by "flux" along "lines
of dissent rather than of descent"; [[netsilik|Central Eskimo]] (Damas), Dogrib (Helm),
Nambikwara, and [[juhoansi|!Kung]] all showed **bilateral primary-linkage** composition (a
couple joins a group where at least one spouse has a close consanguine). Lee reframed the
issue as Leach's distinction between "conscious models of ideal group structure" and
"statistical models derived from the real choices of real people" — Radcliffe-Brown had
mistaken the first for the second.

## Points of contention

- **Acculturation vs. baseline.** Service explained composite/flexible bands as post-contact
  degradation; Eggan (defending Radcliffe-Brown as a "worthy pioneer") nonetheless argued the
  reverse — flux and bilaterality are the baseline, and the patrilineal band "a specialization
  on [a] more flexible, amorphous base."
- **Reconstruction reliability.** Birdsell defended recovering the classical model by careful
  ecological reconstruction (Strehlow's Aranda, Tindale's Kaiadilt); Hiatt replied that all
  Australian fieldwork, Radcliffe-Brown's included, was post-contact, so no one held an
  epistemic advantage.
- **Stated rule vs. practice.** Gardner (Paliyans), Woodburn (Hadza), and others documented
  gaps between informants' stated patrilocality/cross-cousin rules and observed
  bilateral/mixed practice.

## Resolution

The symposium is generally read as **settling the empirical question against the strong
classical model**: band composition among hunter-gatherers is typically fluid and bilateral,
the "band" itself is a shaky cross-cultural unit (Damas), and no single band type is modal
(Eggan: "Bands show a much greater variety than either Steward or Service allow"). Steward's
and Service's typologies survive as historical reference points and as descriptions of
*particular* cases, not as a universal law — see [[patrilocal-band]], [[patrilineal-band]],
[[composite-hunting-band]], [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]], and the
[[hunter-gatherer-band-organization|band-organization comparison]].
