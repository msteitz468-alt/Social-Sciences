# Post-development debate

Live dispute over whether **development** should be reformed, replaced, or abandoned as a framework for global inequality and well-being — and over whether treating development as [[discourse-foucauldian|discourse]] is adequate social science.

## Positions

### 1. Post-development / discourse critique

Holders: [[escobar-arturo]], Wolfgang Sachs and the *Development Dictionary* circle, related writers (Rahnema, Nandy, parts of political ecology). Claim (strongest form): development is a historically produced [[regime-of-representation|regime of representation]] that invented the [[third-world|Third World]] as underdeveloped, failed on its own promises, and cannot be fixed by adding "participation," "women," or "sustainability" without exiting the discourse. Task: [[post-development|postdevelopment]] alternatives rooted in cultural difference and social movements.

**Adjacent anthropological effects analysis (not identical):** [[ferguson-james|Ferguson]] (1990) shows how development discourse and projects in Lesotho depoliticize poverty while expanding bureaucratic state power ([[anti-politics-machine]]; [[ferguson-anti-politics-machine-study]]). Shared Foucauldian tools and object; punchline is vivisection of effects rather than a program to unmake development as language.

### 2. Reformist alternative development

Holders: practitioners and theorists of participatory development, basic human needs, sustainable development, some feminist development approaches. Claim (strongest form): the failures are of *bad* development; inclusion, empowerment, and ecological limits can redeem the project without abandoning poverty reduction as a universal goal.

### 3. Materialist / political-economy critique of post-development

Holders: various Marxist, dependency, and development-studies critics (to be densified as secondary sources are ingested). Claim (strongest form): discourse analysis romanticizes locality, underestimates capital and imperialism as material structures, and offers thin substitutes for redistribution and public goods; people need resources, not only new narratives.

### 4. Mainstream development practice

Holders: World Bank / UN / national planning common sense (as characterized by critics). Claim (strongest form): development is the application of capital, science, and policy to raise living standards; failures are technical or political-implementation problems.

## Shared factual ground (framed per side)

- Large-scale postwar development institutions and vocabularies did emerge after c. 1945 (Truman, UN, Bretton Woods).
- Many measured outcomes in parts of the global South disappointed mid-century promises (debt crises, famines, persistent poverty — magnitudes and causes disputed).
- "Development" remains a dominant policy language even after Cold War three-worlds language weakened.

## Resolution status

**Open.** Post-development remains cited and contested; reformist and growth agendas remain institutionally dominant. No field-wide consensus.
