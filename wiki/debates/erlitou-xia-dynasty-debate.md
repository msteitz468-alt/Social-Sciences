# Is the Erlitou Culture the Xia Dynasty?

Chinese texts compiled roughly a millennium after the second millennium BCE record a **Xia
dynasty** preceding the Shang. When [[erlitou|Erlitou]] was discovered in 1959 — during an explicit
search for the Xia capital — its temporal and spatial overlap with the textually described Xia made
identification tempting. The dispute is recorded here as positions in their strongest form, not
adjudicated in wiki voice. It is classified as `source-reliability` because it turns on whether
much-later transmitted texts can be used to attach a dynastic name to an excavated assemblage.

## Position 1 — Traditionalist / historiographical identification

Most Chinese archaeologists hold that the [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou culture]] (or its late phase)
directly corresponds to the historical Xia (traditionally c. 2070–1600 BCE), given the spatial and
temporal overlap with textually described Xia territory in the Yiluo Basin. The discoverer Xu
Xusheng sought the Xia capital at the site, and many subsequent scholars (e.g. Du & Xu 2006) accept
the equation.

## Position 2 — Xia Nai's evidentiary-criterion approach

Xia Nai (1985), influenced by V. G. Childe's urban-revolution concept, argued that
civilization/statehood in China should be identified by four **archaeologically detectable
criteria** — state-level political organization, an urban center, writing, and metallurgy — rather
than by matching textual place-names. On this basis he identified (late-phase) Erlitou as the site
of Chinese civilization's emergence, a claim compatible with but logically independent of the Xia
identification, and one he himself called "conservative."

## Position 3 — Agnosticism (Liu & Chen)

[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen (2012)]] judge the historiographical approach
methodologically weak: the Xia texts were composed roughly a millennium after the events, give
brief and ambiguous place references, and **no writing has been recovered from any pre-late-Shang
site** that could name a polity (Erlitou's forty-odd pottery glyphs are not accepted as writing).
They explicitly **decline to correlate Erlitou with the Xia**, while not ruling out that a people or
dynasty so named existed and might one day be archaeologically recognized: *"We do not correlate
Erlitou with the Xia dynasty… We are not ruling out the possibility, however, that Xia, either as a
people or as a dynasty so named, may have existed in antiquity."*

## Position 4 — The Yanshi / Shang-conquest reading

A widely held view treats the rise of **Yanshi Shang City** (Erligang-style) in the late-Erlitou-
Phase-IV heartland as the archaeological trace of Shang's historical conquest of Xia — i.e. Erlitou
= Xia and Yanshi = the succeeding Shang (Du & Wang 2004; CASS 2003).

## Shared factual ground, framed per side

All parties accept that the Erlitou culture (c. 1900–1500 BCE, per Liu & Chen's adopted chronology)
overlaps in time and space with the region and dates ascribed to the Xia; that Erlitou was a
state-level urban center with monopolized bronze ritual production; and that no name-bearing text
has been found at the site. They differ on whether later transmitted texts license attaching the
dynastic name "Xia" to this assemblage — a general instance of the **four-way non-identity** problem
(an archaeological culture is not automatically a named dynasty or people).
