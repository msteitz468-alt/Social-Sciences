# The Sex/Gender Distinction and the Subject of Feminism

Whether feminism needs a stable category "women," and whether **sex** is a pre-cultural biological fact on which **gender** is built, or is itself a product of the gender apparatus. The distinction was second-wave feminism's key analytic weapon against "biology is destiny"; [[butler-judith|Judith Butler]]'s *[[butler-gender-trouble-1990|Gender Trouble]]* (1990) argued it collapses under its own logic. Each position is recorded below in its strongest form; the wiki does not adjudicate.

## Shared Ground

All parties reject biological determinism and hold that the social position of women demands explanation rather than being read off nature. They disagree on whether a bounded subject "women," and a pre-discursive "sex," can or should anchor feminist theory and politics.

## Position 1 — The sex/gender distinction as feminist foundation

On this view, sex is the immutable biological substrate and gender its variable cultural construction; separating them lets feminism attribute women's subordination to culture, not anatomy. Beauvoir's "one is not born, but rather becomes, a woman" is the seed; Gayle Rubin's "sex/gender system" (1975) is its systematic form — kinship and Oedipal mechanisms transform biological males and females into hierarchized genders, each child beginning with "all of the sexual possibilities available to human expression." The distinction underwrites the empirical program of [[doing-gender]] and much of [[feminist-anthropology]].

## Position 2 — Sex is already gender (Butler)

Butler argued the distinction undoes itself: if gender is the cultural meaning of a sexed body and can vary independently, it becomes "a free-floating artifice"; and if the ostensibly natural "sex" is shown to be culturally produced, then "perhaps it was always already gender, with the consequence that the distinction between sex and gender turns out to be no distinction at all." Sex, on this reading, is "the discursive/cultural means by which 'sexed nature'… is produced and established as 'prediscursive'." Butler further argued, following [[foucault-michel|Foucault]], that juridical systems "produce the subjects they subsequently come to represent," so the feminist subject "women" is "discursively constituted by the very political system that is supposed to facilitate its emancipation."

## Position 3 — Materialist collapse into a political category (Wittig)

Monique Wittig also rejected the sex/gender distinction, but from a materialist direction: "the category of sex is neither invariant nor natural, but is a specifically political use of the category of nature that serves the purposes of reproductive sexuality." For Wittig "sex" is "always already female" (men occupy the unmarked universal), and "a lesbian is not a woman," because "woman" exists only in a binary opposition to "man" sustained by heterosexuality. The remedy is to overthrow the category of sex itself. Butler admired but criticized this as retaining "a doer behind the deed" — a humanist sovereign subject and a foundationalist ontology of pre-social unity.

## Position 4 — "Women" as a necessary strategic subject

Against the dissolution of the category, this position holds that feminist politics requires a subject to represent; abandoning "women" risks disarming the movement. Butler's own later activism (chairing the International Gay and Lesbian Human Rights Commission, 1994–97) moved her toward conceding "universality" a strategic, if never innocent, use. The tension between anti-foundationalist critique and political necessity is the debate's live core.

## Curator's Note (2026-07-10)

The debate is genuinely **open** and cross-cuts the disciplines: the empirical sociology of gender ([[doing-gender]]) largely retains a working sex/gender distinction, while post-structuralist theory follows Butler in collapsing it. The wiki's evidence-class concern applies — the distinction's fate looks different from an ethnomethodological, a genealogical, and a materialist-feminist vantage, and no single evidence class settles it.


## Gilchrist (1999) — archaeology of unstable sex/gender

*(Attributed — Gilchrist, *[[gilchrist-gender-and-archaeology-1999|Gender and Archaeology]]* ch.1, 4, 7.)* Gilchrist treats the sex/gender distinction as historically and scientifically unstable: Laqueur’s one-sex→Enlightenment two-sex model; sex as spectrum rather than duality (Nordbladh & Yates and related sources as she cites); multiple genders (two-spirit, hijra, Byzantine eunuch) and mortuary mismatches between osteological sex and gendered grave packages. She holds that pure social constructionism and pure sociobiology are both inadequate; feminist archaeology should **engage** cognitive and medical science while using queer theory to disrupt binary protocols — not to project modern gay/lesbian identities onto antiquity.
