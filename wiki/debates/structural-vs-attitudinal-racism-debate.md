# Structural vs. Attitudinal Accounts of Racism

A standing dispute in the sociology and social psychology of race over what *racism* fundamentally is — a property of social structures or a property of individual minds — and, consequently, how it should be measured and combated. The dispute is central to [[bonilla-silva-eduardo|Eduardo Bonilla-Silva]]'s *[[bonilla-silva-racism-without-racists-2018|Racism without Racists]]*, which defines itself against the attitudinal tradition.

## Position 1 — Racism as structure (Bonilla-Silva)

Bonilla-Silva argues (attributed) that racism is best conceptualized as a [[racialized-social-system|racialized social system]] — "a network of social relations at social, political, economic, and ideological levels that shapes the life chances of the various races." What survey researchers call racist *attitudes*, he reframes as *racial ideology* ([[colorblind-racism|color-blind racism]]) produced by the structure. On this view, hunting for prejudiced individuals ("racists") misdirects analysis, since a racial order can be reproduced by ordinary, self-consciously non-prejudiced people. He quotes David Wellman: "prejudiced people are not the only racists in America." He also reframes the "can Blacks be racist?" question (against [[omi-michael|Omi]] & [[winant-howard|Winant]], who hold they can) as really a question about *prejudice*, arguing Black Americans lack the structural power to build a rival racial order.

## Position 2 — Racism as individual prejudice/attitude

The dominant U.S. tradition treats racism as measurable individual prejudice — from *The Authoritarian Personality* (Adorno et al., 1950) through survey-based attitude research. On this view racial progress is legible in declining prejudice scores, and racism is unevenly distributed across persons (a premise Bonilla-Silva attacks as the "good whites/bad whites," "poor whites are the real racists" framing). Bonilla-Silva stages his structural model explicitly against **racial optimists** (who read attitude change as genuine progress), **symbolic-racism** theorists (Sears, Kinder), and Schuman's "pesoptimism."

## Position 3 — Group position / laissez-faire racism (intermediate)

Bobo, Kluegel, Smith, Jackman, and Sidanius treat white prejudice as an ideology defending white *group* interests and privilege — the position Bonilla-Silva regards as closest to his own, while still faulting it for retaining a psychological "prejudice" frame rather than a fully materialist, structural one (attributed).

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

All sides accept that white racial-attitude survey scores fell sharply after the 1960s. The attitudinal tradition reads this as real progress; Bonilla-Silva reads it as the *rearticulation* of racism into a color-blind idiom that surveys built for the Jim Crow era cannot detect — hence his methodological preference for in-depth interviews and mixed designs.
