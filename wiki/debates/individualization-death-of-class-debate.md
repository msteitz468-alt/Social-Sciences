# Individualization and the "Death of Class"

Whether [[reflexive-modernization|reflexive modernity]] has decomposed **class** as a structuring social category, or whether class persists as a determinant of life-chances beneath a thinning layer of class *identity*. [[beck-ulrich|Ulrich Beck]]'s [[individualization]] thesis is one of the sharpest statements of the first position; class-analysis sociology mounts the second.

## Position A — The Individualization Thesis

[[beck-ulrich|Beck]] argued (in *[[beck-risk-society-1986|Risk Society]]*, 1986, Part II) that in the postwar welfare states, rising living standards, expanded education, intensified mobility and competition, and the juridification of labor relations dissolved the "unstable unity of shared life experiences mediated by market and shaped by status" that constituted [[weber-max|Weberian]] social class. His summary formula: "**we increasingly confront the phenomenon of a capitalism without classes, but with individualized social inequality**" (Beck 1986: 96). Crucially, Beck did *not* claim inequality had lessened — income and wage-labor inequalities "remain stable" — only that they had been *individualized*: stripped of collective class-cultural identity, milieu, and consciousness, and re-experienced as personal (psychological) problems. He presented this as "the exact mirror image of the Marxian position": individualization can only entrench itself once welfare-state prosperity has removed the material immiseration that [[marx-karl|Marx]] made the precondition for class formation. Later developed with Elisabeth Beck-Gernsheim (*Individualization*, 2002). A stronger, adjacent version is Pakulski and Waters's *The Death of Class* (1996).

## Position B — The Class-Analysis Defense

Class-analysis sociologists (paradigmatically John Goldthorpe and Gordon Marshall, "The Promising Future of Class Analysis," 1992) reply that the thesis conflates the *decline of class identity and class politics* with the *decline of class structure*, which is not in evidence. Structured inequalities of employment relations, income, health, and mortality remain strongly patterned by class position and are robustly transmitted across generations; the demonstrable weakening of class *voting* and class *consciousness* does not license the inference that class has ceased to shape life-chances. On this view individualization is a claim about culture and subjectivity mis-stated as a claim about structure, and much of the evidence Beck cites (labor-market flexibilization, the "do-it-yourself biography") itself falls hardest along class lines.

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

Both sides accept that class *identity*, class *milieus*, and class-based *voting* weakened across the postwar West, and that inequality of condition persisted. They divide on the inference: Position A reads weakened identity as evidence that class has decomposed *as a social force*; Position B reads persistent structured inequality as evidence that class remains causally central whatever has happened to identity. The dispute is partly about the *definition* of class (a lived cultural formation vs. a structural location in employment relations) and partly about levels of analysis (subjective identity vs. objective life-chances).

## Status

Open. The debate remains a live axis in the sociology of stratification and of modernity, feeding into arguments over precarity, the "precariat," and whether contemporary inequality is best theorized through class, through individualized risk, or through both.
