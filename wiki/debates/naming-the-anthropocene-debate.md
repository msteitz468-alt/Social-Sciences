# Naming the Present Epoch

A live dispute over what to call the current interval of planetary environmental transformation — and, through the name, over what is taken to be its agent and cause. The naming is not merely terminological: each candidate encodes a different causal story and a different politics. The wiki records the positions; it does not adjudicate.

## What Is at Stake

The [[anthropocene|Anthropocene]] proposal (Eugene Stoermer, early 1980s; popularized by Paul Crutzen with Stoermer in 2000) names a geological epoch in which humanity has become a dominant Earth-system force. Critics argue that "*anthropos*" — Species Man — misassigns the agent: it is not humanity as such but particular economic and colonial formations that drove the transformation. The alternative names each relocate the cause.

## Positions

**Anthropocene (species-level human agency).** Human activity, aggregated, is the dominant influence on the Earth system, warranting a new geological epoch after the Holocene. Proposed base dates range from an early-Holocene "early Anthropocene" through the 1610 Columbian Exchange low to the mid-20th-century "Great Acceleration" / nuclear bomb spike. Held by much of the Earth-system science community; the term is entrenched and, as Haraway notes, "less controversial to many important players" than its rivals. (See [[anthropocene]].)

**Capitalocene (capital, not humanity-as-such).** The transformation is driven by the historical accumulation regime of capitalism, not by an undifferentiated human species. The term was proposed by Andreas Malm in a 2009 seminar in Lund and developed by the Marxist sociologist Jason W. Moore (*Capitalism and the Web of Life*, 2015), who argues that capital and power develop *through* the web of life rather than acting upon an external "nature," and that "cheap nature" is now exhausted. Haraway adopts the term (dating it to the "long sixteenth and seventeenth centuries" of sugar, plantations, genocide, and slavery, not the steam engine) while warning against telling it in a "fundamentalist Marxist," totalizing register. (See [[capitalocene]].)

**Plantationocene (plantation logic as motor).** The present is defined by the plantation — the enclosed, extractive, forced-labor monocrop system — as the model and motor for both industrial capitalism and today's factory farming and agribusiness. The term was collectively generated in a recorded 2014 conversation at Aarhus University (participants included [[tsing-anna-lowenhaupt|Anna Tsing]], Haraway, the biologist Scott Gilbert, Nils Bubandt, Noboru Ishikawa, and Kenneth Olwig). It foregrounds slavery and colonial land transformation as constitutive. (See [[plantationocene]].)

**Chthulucene (multispecies making-with).** Haraway's own coinage (Greek *khthôn*, earth + *kainos*, now), naming a "timeplace" for multispecies "staying with the trouble" and refusing the human- or capital-centered frame entirely. Where the other names diagnose a cause, the Chthulucene proposes an ethic and ontology — [[sympoiesis]], [[making-kin]], [[response-ability]] — for ongoing, damaged, more-than-human worlds. Haraway treats the Anthropocene as "more a boundary event than an epoch." (See [[chthulucene]].)

## Shared Factual Ground, Framed per Side

All parties accept that human-driven Earth-system change is severe and, in key respects, irreversible; that its onset and acceleration are historically and geographically uneven; and that the industrial fossil-fuel economy is deeply entangled with colonial and plantation histories. They disagree on which of these facts is *the* cause worth naming, and therefore on the name — and priority disputes attach to several terms (Malm vs. Moore on Capitalocene; the collective vs. individual authorship of Plantationocene), which is itself part of the argument about whether such thinking is individual or sympoietic.

## The "Make Kin Not Babies" Sub-Dispute

Haraway's Chthulucene program carries a contested normative rider: her slogan "Make Kin Not Babies," which pairs reproductive justice with a call for a deliberate long-run reduction of human numbers (she imagines a fall from ~11 billion toward "2 or 3 billion" over "a couple hundred years," achieved through kin-making rather than biological reproduction). Reproductive-justice, feminist, Indigenous, and postcolonial critics argue this echoes population-control and eugenic discourses that historically targeted the Global South, regardless of Haraway's stated anti-racist, non-coercive intent. Haraway insists the argument is decolonial and feminist and rejects coercive population control. The wiki records both positions in their strongest form and does not adjudicate.

## Current Status

**Open.** No consensus term exists; the alternatives circulate together in the environmental humanities, while Earth-system science largely retains "Anthropocene" (which the International Union of Geological Sciences declined to formally ratify as an epoch in 2024). The dispute is productive precisely because the name is a compressed theory of the cause.
