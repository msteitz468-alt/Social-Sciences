## The Dispute

How old is strict endogamy within India's [[institutions/caste|jati]] system, and where did it come from? Three positions are recorded here, each stated in its strongest form.

**Position A — Tribal continuity ([[karve-irawati|Irawati Karve]]).** Jati originated from formerly independent endogamous tribal groups that were incorporated into a stratified social system by political elites, who then placed themselves at the top; tribal distinctiveness was preserved over the long run through caste endogamy. On this view, jati-level endogamy is old and rooted in pre-state tribal social organization.

**Position B — Colonial construction ([[dirks-nicholas|Nicholas Dirks]] and revisionist historians).** Strong caste endogamy as practiced and documented in the modern era was not a feature of ancient India; it was substantially strengthened, systematized, or in significant part invented as a British colonial governance strategy beginning in the eighteenth century, including through colonial census-taking and administrative classification.

**Position C — Genetic antiquity (David Reich, from archaeogenetic evidence).** Population-genetic data — deep, jati-specific [[founder-event|population bottlenecks]] dated to thousands of years ago (e.g., the Vysya, roughly 3,000–2,000 years ago), and genetic differentiation among jatis living in the same village running two to three times higher than that between northern and southern Europeans, unexplained by region, social status, or [[ancestral-north-indians|ANI]]-ancestry proportion alone — is presented as evidence that long-term, strict endogamy substantially predates British colonialism by millennia (as of Reich 2018).

## Shared Factual Ground

All sides agree that the British colonial administration formally documented, classified, and in some respects codified caste categories through census operations, and that this administrative activity shaped the modern legal and bureaucratic category of "caste." Where the positions diverge is on whether *strict reproductive endogamy* — as opposed to administrative classification — is itself a colonial-era phenomenon or a much older social pattern that colonial administration merely recorded and hardened. Reich's genetic evidence speaks specifically to the antiquity of reproductive isolation between named groups, which he argues is difficult to reconcile with a purely eighteenth-century-and-later origin for strict endogamy, while leaving open how much colonial administration intensified, formalized, or reshaped an already-old pattern (as of Reich 2018).

## Resolution Status

Open. The genetic evidence bears directly on the antiquity of reproductive endogamy but does not by itself adjudicate the historians' and anthropologists' broader claims about the political and administrative construction of caste categories, hierarchy, and varna assignment, which are separate (though related) questions from bottleneck dating.
