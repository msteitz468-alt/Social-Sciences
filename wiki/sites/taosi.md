# Taosi

## Discovery and Excavation History

Taosi (2600–2000 BC) is located north of the Chong (Ta'er) Mountains in the Linfen Basin, southern Shanxi, and covers roughly 300 ha, making it the largest Late Longshan regional center in the middle Yellow River valley (Liu, L. 1996b, cited in Liu & Chen 2012). The site has been the subject of multiple excavation campaigns by the Institute of Archaeology, Chinese Academy of Social Sciences, spanning burial excavation, settlement survey, and specialist architectural/astronomical analysis (He, N. 2004, 2007a, 2008, 2009). Excavation quality is high relative to many Chinese Neolithic sites, with over 1,000 burials systematically classified and specialist studies (archaeoastronomy, metallurgy, lithic sourcing) conducted on recovered material — but see Key Debates below for interpretive disputes that persist despite this excavation depth.

## Occupation Sequence

Taosi formed the apex of a three-tiered settlement hierarchy with surrounding smaller sites in the Linfen Basin. More than 1,000 excavated burials classify into three ranks: the majority are small tombs with few or no grave goods; large tombs (under 1% of the total) contained hundreds of items, including elaborate pottery, jade (cong tubes and bi discs with designs paralleling the [[liangzhu-culture]] far to the southeast), alligator-skin drums, and other exotic ritual goods. Elite families lived in walled-off palatial structures separate from commoners, who occupied semisubterranean houses (Liu, L. 1996a, 2004; Yan & He 2005).

A rammed-earth semicircular terrace and column structure (IIFJT1), found within a small enclosure and dated to the mid-Taosi phase, has an alignment permitting observation of sunrise through gaps between columns from the Chong Mountains, corresponding to positions in a calendrical cycle; it has been interpreted as an astronomical observatory covering roughly 1 ha (He, N. 2007a). A lacquer pole (180 cm) found in large tomb IM22, dated to the middle Taosi phase (2100–2000 BC), has been identified as a gnomon shadow template for measuring solar shadows at equinox and solstice (He, N. 2009). Liu and Chen (2012) note that if these conclusions are reliable, "this structure is the earliest observatory in China," and suggest the Taosi elite "may have held great ritual power by possessing astronomical knowledge needed to determine the calendar" — both framed as attributed inferences, not settled fact.

Metal finds include a piece-mold-technique copper bell (small tomb), a gear-shaped arsenic-bronze object attached to a jade *yuan* disc (medium burial), and a possible basin-rim fragment from a palatial foundation, all dated to the late Taosi phase (Zhang, D. 1984; Guojia 2002; He, N. et al. 2008). Metal was not part of the regular elite-burial prestige-good inventory at Taosi; that practice developed only later at [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]] (Allan 2007). Taosi was also a stone-tool manufacturing site, with a matching-tool-type quarry identified at Mount Dagudui, 7 km south (Tao 1991; Wang, X. et al. 1987); Liu and Chen suggest the Taosi elite "may have controlled access to the raw material at Dagudui," with lithic manufacture carried out across many households and products used in exchange (Zhai 2011).

In the late phase, Taosi shows evidence of political turmoil: the large rammed-earth enclosure was destroyed, the early-phase palatial area was converted to craft production (stone/bone artifacts, bone arrowheads), human skeletal remains near the palatial area show signs of violence, and some elite burials were broken into and disturbed (Liu, L. 2004; Yan & He 2005). Taosi was abandoned by the start of the second millennium BC; the cause of collapse is unclear, though some scholars propose destruction by floods around 2000 BC (Xia & Yang 2003).

## Significance

Liu and Chen (2012) conclude Taosi "was the most important economic, political, and religious center of the region," showing "characteristics of early urbanism," though they judge its political dominion likely limited to the mountain-circumscribed Linfen Basin. Its scale, burial hierarchy, and (contested) observatory make it a central reference site in debates over Late Neolithic Chinese social complexity and state formation (see [[chiefdom]], [[state-formation]]).

## Key Debates

- **Astronomical observatory (position).** The IIFJT1 structure's identification as an observatory, and the IM22 lacquer pole as a gnomon, rest on He's (2007a, 2009) interpretation; Liu and Chen present the conclusion as conditional ("if these conclusions are reliable") rather than established.
- **Pottery glyphs (position).** Pottery glyphs recovered at Taosi have been read under two competing scholarly interpretations, which Liu and Chen describe as unresolved.
- **Cause and tempo of collapse (position).** Whether Taosi experienced gradual late-phase decline or a sudden catastrophic end remains unresolved; flood destruction (Xia & Yang 2003) is one proposed but unconfirmed mechanism.
- **Yao/Shun legendary-kings attribution (position, rejected by the authors).** Some Chinese archaeologists link Taosi to the legendary kings Yao and Shun (of the Five Emperors/Wudi tradition), including linking the observatory to Yao's astronomical achievements per ancient texts (Xie 2007; He 2004). Liu and Chen explicitly reject using these accounts to determine Taosi's historical affiliation, noting the relevant texts "were recorded more than a thousand years later than the Taosi culture" — an authorial rejection of the identification, recorded here as the authors' own attributed position rather than a wiki-voice verdict.
- **Contrast with [[wangchenggang]].** No evidence of elaborate elite burials has been found at Wangchenggang, unlike Taosi; Liu and Chen suggest this mortuary-pattern contrast "may be related to different leadership strategies used by the Longshan elite" rather than to gaps in excavation coverage.
