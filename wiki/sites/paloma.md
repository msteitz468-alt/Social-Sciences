## Discovery and Excavation History

Paloma is located on the central Peruvian coast, on the northern margin of the Chilca Valley (Benfer 1984, 1999; Engel 1980; Quilter 1989). Dwellings occupy approximately 6,500 m² (designated Unit I) across three chronological phases: 7,000–5,500 B.P. (Levels 400–500), 5,500–5,200 B.P. (Level 300), and 5,200–4,600 B.P. (Level 200). As with other B.P.-dated Archaic sites in Moore (2005), the calibration status is unstated in Moore 2005 and should be treated as likely uncalibrated pending verification.

## Occupation Sequence

Paloma houses varied in plan from circular to quadrilateral; some were domed and some flat-roofed. Wall supports of willow or cane were sometimes paired or reinforced on the windward side, with a framework of lashed canes probably covered by reed mats and insulated with grass or vegetation (Quilter 1989:15). Houses averaged 10.9 m² and were densely clustered with no obvious internal organization and no clear plaza space (Quilter 1989:18–19).

## Significance

Moore (2005) includes Paloma in his soundscape reconstruction (his Figure 2.20, based on Benfer 1999), grouping it with Acha-2 and [[asana]] as part of an egalitarian-pattern soundscape set: dwelling spacing close enough for house-to-house conversation but not for overheard whispers, which Moore argues is consistent with nonhierarchical social organization lacking storage-based status accumulation.

## Key Debates

No dedicated debate page treats Paloma. Its role in Moore's argument is as supporting evidence for the broader Archaic egalitarian-soundscape thesis; the interpretive leap from dwelling spacing to social structure is Moore's attributed theoretical claim, not an independently established finding.
