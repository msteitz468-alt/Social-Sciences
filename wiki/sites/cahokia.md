# Cahokia

Largest [[mississippian-culture|Mississippian]] center in the American Bottom (East St. Louis region). Primary North American complexity case in [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 15 — ranked below Mesoamerican [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacán]] on specialization, product diversity, class stratification, and productivity (as of their 2007 judgment).

## Discovery and Excavation History

W&O (2007) treat Cahokia as a comparative type-site in world-prehistory teaching, not as an excavation-history monograph. They report Monks Mound dimensions, mound counts, population estimates, and a famous elite burial assemblage as evidence of ranked society. Detailed expedition histories, Mound 72 specialist debates, and post-2007 demographic revisions require regional literature.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 15; mid-2000s narrative.)*

- **Regional backdrop:** [[mississippian-culture|Mississippian]] (~A.D. 800–1650; florescence after ~A.D. 600) defined (Stoltman via W&O) by intensive maize-beans-squash agriculture, chiefs/elites, theocracy, fortified towns, mounds/tombs, occupational specialization, mortuary cults, shell-tempered pottery, bow-and-arrow triangular points, and wall-trench houses. Newer maize strains tolerating ~120 frost-free days enabled northern expansion; ¹³C isotope studies show sharp rise in maize consumption ~A.D. 900–1000 in Missouri/Arkansas (as of W&O 2007).
- **Cahokia peak:** By ~A.D. 1250, >100 mounds in ~13 km²; **Monks Mound** >30 m high, base ~241 × 316 m (>6.5 ha) — textbook caption compares base area favorably with Giza’s Great Pyramid. Environs population ~30–40,000 around A.D. 1200 across towns and 40+ villages (as of W&O 2007).
- **Mortuary ranking:** Elite burial assemblage includes ~20,000 shell beads, ~800 arrowheads, mica/copper sheets, plus sacrificed individuals (4 mutilated men; 118 women, many apparently ritually strangled) — read as class-based society (as of W&O 2007).
- **Decline context:** Immediate cause of broader Mississippian decline framed as lack of immunity to Old World diseases (measles, smallpox, cholera, etc.); Ramenofsky’s settlement-count analyses imply inland epidemic spread ahead of face-to-face contact (as of W&O 2007). Southeastern Ceremonial Complex motifs resemble Mesoamerican themes — opinions differ on direct contact vs. local usefulness after complexity rose.

## Significance

- Largest prehistoric settlement north of Mexico in the textbook comparison (as of W&O 2007).
- Anchor for whether temperate eastern North America could produce states: W&O summarize eastern evolution as the “familiar script” of complexity (foraging → plant manipulation → maize → monuments, cults, hierarchies) stabilizing **lower** than Mesoamerica due to domesticates, technology, and agricultural potential. Most archaeologists do not class Mississippian mound societies as states because nearly all people remained full-time maize farmers with few specialists (W&O ch. 7 application).
- Comparative ceiling case vs [[teotihuacan]]: not functionally equivalent in specialization, product diversity, class stratification, or productivity (W&O position).
- Feeds [[complex-society]], [[chiefdom]], and [[band-tribe-chiefdom-model]] teaching alongside Moundville and Hopewell.

## Key Debates

- **Centralized chiefdom / embryonic state vs. Milner’s decentralized, segmented, less populous dynamic system** — position recording as of W&O 2007; no wiki-voice verdict.
- **Ecological ceiling:** temperate forest ecology, extensive maize + hunting/foraging, and lack of irrigation/nomad-interaction stimuli (Moundville parallel in textbook) as limits on “potential” for urban statehood.
- **Mesoamerican contact** via Southeastern Ceremonial Complex — direct vs. minor borrowing.
- **Disease chronology** of decline (Ramenofsky and others) vs. pre-contact reorganization.
- Do not equate the archaeological center with a single ethnonym, language, or genetic population without attributed argument.
