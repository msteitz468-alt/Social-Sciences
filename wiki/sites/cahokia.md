# Cahokia

Largest [[mississippian-culture|Mississippian]] multi-mound center north of Mexico, in the American Bottom floodplain of southwestern Illinois. Densified primarily from [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner, *The Moundbuilders* (2021)]] (fieldwork region of the author); earlier textbook layer: [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Long known; heavily damaged by plow, urban sprawl, and a commercial strip (Highway 40) through the site. Milner notes that much of the site, including Monks Mound, is now preserved as an Illinois state park gradually expanded over years. Nineteenth-century St. Louis “Mound City” mounds (related regional landscape) were largely destroyed by mid-century (Milner Ch. 1 preservation narrative). Modern research includes large-scale settlement and mortuary projects; excavation quality varies by project era.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Milner 2021 unless noted.)*

- **Setting:** Mississippi floodplain with fertile ridges among lakes, swamps, and abandoned channels — **wetlands** essential to supporting dense population (fish and aquatic resources “absolutely essential” in Milner’s reading).
- **Scale:** Mounds usually attributed to the site spread across ~**3.9 sq mi (10 km²)** (boundaries partly arbitrary); **more than 100 mounds** — more than any other Mississippian site.
- **Monks Mound:** ~**100 ft (30.5 m)** high; base ~**13.8 acres (5.6 ha)**; ~**814,000 cu yd (622,000 m³)** of soil; four terraces; wooden buildings on flats; **about half** of all Cahokia mound earth went into this single monument. Work thought begun by the **10th century CE**, with a boom in the **11th**. Immense plaza leveled to the south; Monks Mound at the **northern** (prestigious) edge nearest the abandoned river channel.
- **Palisade:** stout palisade with bastions enclosed the core for roughly a century, rebuilt multiple times.
- **Woodhenges:** large post circles interpreted as tracking the sun — more ritual than practical astronomy (Milner).
- **Population (Milner position):** peak early Mississippian on the order of **~3,000**, possibly up to **two or three times** that — **not** the 10,000–40,000 figures favored in some textbook accounts (W&O 2007 cited ~30–40,000 environs; treat as contested estimates, not wiki-voice fact). Labor to build mounds was within local capacity if spread over centuries: a few thousand people with one laborer per household working **&lt;2 weeks/year** (Milner).
- **Mound 72:** multi-stage low linear mound; high-status burials with thousands of marine-shell beads and fine objects; **≥150 sacrificial victims** in rectangular pits (sex-skewed groups; one pit with arrow trauma; four headless, handless men) — major evidence for ritual killing and ranked ideology.
- **Decline:** noticeable by early **14th century** (population drop; public architecture reused for residences; old mounds used for ordinary burials); largely deserted by **~1400 CE** as part of broader midwestern population redistribution/reduction (Medieval Warm Period expansion then climate deterioration toward Little Ice Age — Milner synthesis).

## Significance

- Type case for Eastern Woodlands **platform-mound [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]]** and for the distinction **Mississippian ≠ chiefdom** (not all Mississippian sites show strong ranking).
- Anchors comparative complexity debates short of Mesoamerican/Old World states.
- Labor and population arguments (Milner) push back against coerced mega-population readings of Monks Mound.
- Links: [[moundville]], [[spiro]], [[southeastern-ceremonial-complex]], [[chiefly-cycling]], [[eastern-woodlands-chronology]].

## Key Debates

- Peak population (low thousands vs tens of thousands).
- Degree of political centralization and urbanism.
- Causes of fourteenth-century decline (climate, warfare, political failure, multi-causal).
- Interpretation of Mound 72 sacrifice and SECC-related ideology.
- Textbook (W&O 2007) vs specialist (Milner 2021) demographic figures — **do not silently overwrite**; both attributed.

### Fagan (1991) demographic figures — a third data point (attributed)

[[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]] illustrates the demographic dispute directly:
in the same chapter he cites a regional/American-Bottom figure of **~30,000** (citing Milner
1990) *and*, when he walks back the "city" framing, an estimate of **as few as ~500** for the
**walled central precinct** (citing Muller 1987). These are best read as **different units of
analysis** — regional population vs. central-precinct residents — rather than a flat
contradiction, and they bracket the wiki's Milner-2021 central estimate of **~3,000**. Recorded
here attributed; do not silently reconcile. Source: [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991]].

## Historiography

Colonial and nineteenth-century mound myth ([[moundbuilder-myth]]); professional excavation and CRM; late twentieth-century Cahokia as “city” debates; twenty-first-century revisions of demography, wetlands economy, and collapse timing (as of Milner 2021).
