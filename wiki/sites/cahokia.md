# Cahokia

Largest [[mississippian-culture|Mississippian]] multi-mound center north of Mexico, in the American Bottom floodplain of southwestern Illinois. Densified primarily from [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner, *The Moundbuilders* (2021)]] (fieldwork region of the author); earlier textbook layer: [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Long known; heavily damaged by plow, urban sprawl, and a commercial strip (Highway 40) through the site. Milner notes that much of the site, including Monks Mound, is now preserved as an Illinois state park gradually expanded over years. Nineteenth-century St. Louis “Mound City” mounds (related regional landscape) were largely destroyed by mid-century (Milner Ch. 1 preservation narrative). Modern research includes large-scale settlement and mortuary projects; excavation quality varies by project era.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Milner 2021 unless noted.)*

- **Setting:** Mississippi floodplain with fertile ridges among lakes, swamps, and abandoned channels — **wetlands** essential to supporting dense population (fish and aquatic resources “absolutely essential” in Milner’s reading).
- **Scale:** Mounds usually attributed to the site spread across ~**3.9 sq mi (10 km²)** (boundaries partly arbitrary); **more than 100 mounds** — more than any other Mississippian site.
- **Monks Mound:** ~**100 ft (30.5 m)** high; base ~**13.8 acres (5.6 ha)**; ~**814,000 cu yd (622,000 m³)** of soil; four terraces; wooden buildings on flats; **about half** of all Cahokia mound earth went into this single monument. Work thought begun by the **10th century CE**, with a boom in the **11th**. Immense plaza leveled to the south; Monks Mound at the **northern** (prestigious) edge nearest the abandoned river channel.
- **Palisade:** stout palisade with bastions enclosed the core for roughly a century, rebuilt multiple times.
- **Woodhenges:** large post circles interpreted as tracking the sun — more ritual than practical astronomy (Milner).
- **Population (Milner position):** peak early Mississippian on the order of **~3,000**, possibly up to **two or three times** that — **not** the 10,000–40,000 figures favored in some textbook accounts (W&O 2007 cited ~30–40,000 environs; treat as contested estimates, not wiki-voice fact). Labor to build mounds was within local capacity if spread over centuries: a few thousand people with one laborer per household working **&lt;2 weeks/year** (Milner).
- **Mound 72:** multi-stage low linear mound; high-status burials with thousands of marine-shell beads and fine objects; **≥150 sacrificial victims** in rectangular pits (sex-skewed groups; one pit with arrow trauma; four headless, handless men) — major evidence for ritual killing and ranked ideology.
- **Decline:** noticeable by early **14th century** (population drop; public architecture reused for residences; old mounds used for ordinary burials); largely deserted by **~1400 CE** as part of broader midwestern population redistribution/reduction (Medieval Warm Period expansion then climate deterioration toward Little Ice Age — Milner synthesis).

## Significance

- Type case for Eastern Woodlands **platform-mound [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]]** and for the distinction **Mississippian ≠ chiefdom** (not all Mississippian sites show strong ranking).
- Anchors comparative complexity debates short of Mesoamerican/Old World states.
- Labor and population arguments (Milner) push back against coerced mega-population readings of Monks Mound.
- Links: [[moundville]], [[spiro]], [[southeastern-ceremonial-complex]], [[chiefly-cycling]], [[eastern-woodlands-chronology]].

- Causes of fourteenth-century decline (climate, warfare, political failure, multi-causal).

## Pauketat 2009 densification (*Cahokia: Ancient America's Great City*)

*(Attributed throughout — [[pauketat-timothy|Timothy Pauketat]], [[pauketat-cahokia-2009]]. The maximalist counterpoint to the Milner layer above; contrasts recorded at [[cahokia-population-urbanism-debate]] — do not silently reconcile.)*

### The "big bang" (Pauketat's central thesis)

Around **1050 CE** (latest radiocarbon dating; the 1054 Crab Nebula supernova's proximity is raised by "some archaeologists" as a possible trigger — Pauketat declines to assert causation), Old Cahokia — a village of 1,000+, the region's largest, with pre-1050 immigration already evidenced by foreign pottery from southern Missouri — was abruptly razed and **New Cahokia** built over it: "the first government-sponsored urban renewal project on the continent north of Mexico." Wittry's Tracts 15A/15B stratigraphy (Old Cahokia houses, 900–1050, beneath New Cahokia construction) is the direct evidence for abruptness. Pauketat's population estimate: **10,000–16,000** in New Cahokia (extrapolated from house counts), with 20,000–30,000 more within fifty miles — comparisons to Uruk-scale Mesopotamian city-states, Moche, Tiwanaku (his interpretive framing).

### Built landscape (Pauketat figures)

- **200+ mounds** across a wider St. Louis–East St. Louis–Cahokia complex; 120+ at Cahokia proper. Destruction history: 26 St. Louis and 45 East St. Louis mounds destroyed in the 19th century (Big Mound down just after the Civil War); the Powell Mound (second-largest) removed by steam shovel in **1930**; only about half the remaining mounds retain original proportions.
- **Grand Plaza:** ~50 acres (elsewhere 1,600+ × 900+ ft — Pauketat's figures are internally inconsistent by ~⅓; both recorded), leveled at foundation; "biggest public space ever conceived and executed north of Mexico" (his superlative).
- **Monks Mound:** >25 million cu ft, ~15 acres at base; ~20 ft high shortly after 1050, ~100 ft within ~150 years (plus a ~30-ft summit building).
- **Sub-Mound 51 borrow pit** (Bareis excavation): >195 ft long, dug ~1050 for plaza fill, refilled with feasting midden in eight strata — Pauketat's per-event estimates run to ~3,900 deer, ~7,900 pots, a million+ charred tobacco seeds: festivals for 10,000+ (his extrapolations).
- **Ridgetop mounds:** roughly a dozen at Cahokia and, with one or two possible exceptions, nowhere else in the world; 9–16 known, dated ~1000–1200s (Mound 72 late 1000s; East St. Louis/Powell/Junkyard 1100s; Mitchell late 1100s–1200s). Mortuary contents from Junkyard Mound (Holder & Wike 1954–55: ~180 individuals, all-female primary burials, one decapitated with head under arm), Moorehead's Rattlesnake Mound, and 1870s newspaper accounts of the destroyed St. Louis/East St. Louis vault mounds. [POSITION] single-lineage resting places vs. Pauketat's "inauguration mounds" of successive rulers via mortuary theater.

### Mound 72 (Fowler excavation 1967–71, detail from Pauketat 2009)

The **beaded burial**: two adult males, the lower on ~20,000 shell beads arranged as a falcon/thunderbird cape. Surrounding pits: a 53-woman pit (52 aged 15–25 plus one "matron"; no skeletal trauma — poisoning, strangulation, or throat-cutting inferred); pits of 19–24 women; four headless, handless men with interlocked arms; **Feature 229** (Rose 1970): 15 higher-status corpses on cedar litters above a false floor covering **39 executed individuals** (avg. age 26.2; three decapitated, one incompletely; two embedded chert points; victims lined on white sand and clubbed sequentially — finger bones dug into the sand indicate death was not instantaneous for some). Grave goods: 15 Cahokia-style [[chunkey]] stones, a copper-clad chunkey stick, ~2 bushels of North Carolina mica, 700+ arrows in two directional bundles. Mound and later burials oriented ~120° azimuth (solstitial alignment). Pauketat reads the whole as staged "political theater" cementing "religion, sex, and rulership" — attributed; the ruler-identification of the beaded-burial male rests on Natchez funerary analogy (flagged analogy; skeletal analysis established only that both were adults).

### Hinterland, immigration, and diaspora

Emerson's **replacement hypothesis** (FAI-270 data): abrupt ~1050 replacement of villages by dispersed farmsteads with temples and sweat lodges. **Halliday site** (~10 mi SE): ~150 structures, 200–300 people, female-associated toolkits, high maize diet, and locally-made Varney-style pottery indicating immigrant potters from SE Missouri/NE Arkansas (Susan Alt: wide-catchment immigration, upland farmers as near-"peasant" surplus producers — isotopic diet evidence, attributed). Upland villages abandoned from ~1150 with Cahokia's decline. **Vacant Quarter** (Stephen Williams's term): depopulated zone from Cahokia (~1350) south to near Memphis (~1400). Pauketat traces possible diaspora routes to the Missouri Bootheel, southern Indiana, and Plains Dhegiha-Siouan and Caddoan peoples (Quapaw, Osage, Pawnee) — explicitly hedged; he argues no single "pure" descendant group exists. Quapaw tribal member Carrie Wilson's proposal (1999) that a 17th-century Quapaw bison robe depicts downtown Cahokia is recorded as a provisional ethnohistoric claim. Pauketat notes the puzzle that no clear Eastern Woodlands oral tradition names Cahokia (unlike Chaco in Puebloan tradition) and speculates deliberate post-Cahokian forgetting.

### Expansion northward and "Pax Cahokiana"

Cahokian-style outposts to ~400 miles north (Aztalan, Trempealeau, WI, 1050–1200 — "colonial outposts or missions," his framing); Gottschall Rockshelter and Picture Cave rock art radiocarbon-dated to the 10th–11th centuries, interpreted via Ho-Chunk Red Horn oral tradition (Radin 1908 recording; Hall/Salzer identification — explicit ethnographic analogy, contested). [POSITION] Cahokian-religion disruption vs. local causes for the end of Wisconsin's Effigy Mound tradition. Pauketat's **"Pax Cahokiana"** hypothesis: post-Cahokian collapse of a regional peace, linked speculatively to 13th–14th-century organized violence (Crow Creek massacre, SD, ~800 dead, 14th century, Emerson's 1978 documentation) — "can only be indirectly credited to Cahokia" (his hedge).

### Excavation history additions (Pauketat 2009)

Henry Marie **Brackenridge** first described Cahokia in detail (1811 visit; 1813 letter to Jefferson): "What a stupendous pile of earth!"; found Trappist monks on the mound later named for them. **Moorehead** (1921) trenched mounds to refute the then-current geologists' claim that they were natural formations; his damaging digs nonetheless won the 1925 state-park purchase. **Preston Holder** (Boas-trained; fired from Washington University in 1957 amid McCarthy-era blacklisting of his Marxist-inflected work — production-context fact) and Joyce Wike dug the Junkyard Mound unfunded in 1954–55 ahead of motel construction. **Wittry** (1960–61) found the Woodhenge (largest circle: 72 posts, 420 ft diameter, solstitial tracking; corroborated by Hall 1963 and Porter's Mitchell-site cypress post). **Fowler** mapped the site and ran Mound 72 (identified via its 120° ridgetop orientation, flagged by Bareis 1965). The **FAI-270 Highway Mitigation Project** (from 1976, the largest salvage-archaeology project in U.S. history per Pauketat) produced the BBB Motor and Sponemann figurine temples (six red-flintclay "goddess" statuettes, ~1100 CE, feminine fertility/ancestor imagery; Emerson's PIMA sourcing shows all red-flintclay carvings were made at Cahokia, with feminine images retained locally and masculine ones exported — his gender-historiography point that male-biased fieldwork over-weighted the hero-twin narrative).

## Pauketat 2007 academic layer (*Chiefdoms and Other Archaeological Delusions*)

*(Attributed — [[pauketat-chiefdoms-delusions-2007]]; the scholarly apparatus behind the 2009 popular layer above.)*

- **The metroplex.** The "central political-administrative complex" comprised three not-fully-discrete sites: Cahokia proper, East St. Louis (fifty pyramids, temples, storage huts, walled compounds), and St. Louis/"Mound City" (twenty-six pyramids) — both satellite groups leveled in the 19th century. Fowler (1975) drew a 13 km² diamond; Pauketat & Lopinot (1997) a conservative 8 km² catchment; Fowler (1997) identified eleven mound-and-plaza groups as possible residential barrios, with uneven craft-debris distributions (spindle whorls, microtools) as support. Pauketat calls the whole "internally differentiated and suggestive of substantial heterarchical complexity."
- **The population argument, mechanics.** Pauketat contends Milner's "low thousands" rests on lengthening the Lohmann phase from 50 to 100 years (halving rates), and predates the Richland-complex upland farming district (Alt) that provisioned the center; corrected, he puts Cahokia proper at **10,000–16,000** and greater Cahokia at 25,000–50,000, with his city definition made explicit: "If a city is a relatively dense concentration of people disposed in such a way as to reveal central organizing principles other than kinship, then Cahokia… was a city, at least for about a hundred years (A.D. 1050–1150)." By his measures Cahokia was 10–16× Moundville's area and ≥10× its population.
- **Yoffee's-criteria application.** Pauketat argues Cahokia meets Yoffee, Fish & Milner's (1999) four early-state criteria point by point (disembedded new capital; ruralization — Emerson's replacement hypothesis, with essentially no pre-Mississippian house directly rebuilt as a wall-trench house by the same family; competing power sources; symbol manipulation) — his application, contested.
- **Mound 72 isotopes.** Sacrificed women's diets were higher in maize and lower in meat than the honored dead (Rose 1999; Ambrose et al. 2003) — a distinct subpopulation; captives, wives, or (Pauketat's suggestion) Richland-complex immigrant farm women.
- **Pax Cahokiana, refined.** Grounded in three claims: Cahokian settlers/objects north to Aztalan–Silvernale–Great Oasis and south to Gahagan–Spiro–Shiloh; violence 1050–1200 "directed at places rather than people" (e.g., ~14–50 storage buildings burned at East St. Louis ~1160); escalating village raiding only after ~1200, when Cahokia "loosened its grip" and fortified towns proliferated from Ste. Genevieve to Memphis. He cautions against conflating "signatures of Cahokian culture with the limits of political control" — direct control was smaller than the influence shadow.

## Historiography

Colonial and nineteenth-century mound myth ([[moundbuilder-myth]]); professional excavation and CRM; late twentieth-century Cahokia as “city” debates; twenty-first-century revisions of demography, wetlands economy, and collapse timing (as of Milner 2021). Maximalist urban synthesis (as of Pauketat 2009) recorded above; the demographic/urbanism dispute is consolidated at [[cahokia-population-urbanism-debate]]. Pauketat 2007 adds that the dispute has "turned political" (citation refusals, blocked collection access) — the historiography is itself contested terrain.
