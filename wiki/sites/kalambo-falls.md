# Kalambo Falls

## Discovery and Excavation History

[[kalambo-falls|Kalambo Falls]] (Tanzania/Zambia border) preserves late [[acheulean-industry|Acheulean]] handaxes and cleavers in ancient lake silts and shore deposits above a ~220 m waterfall. Classic work under J. Desmond Clark made it a reference sequence for south-central African Acheulean and organic artefacts (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Occupation Sequence

Early radiocarbon ages of ~55–50 ka for Acheulean levels were at the method’s extreme and are treated as **minimum ages**; uranium-series dating and regional comparison suggest true ages on the order of **400–300 ka** (as of Phillipson 2005). Phillipson notes Isimila (southern Tanzania) and Kamoa (D.R. Congo) as possibly comparable. Wooden objects from Acheulean levels are among the rare organic finds for the period (as of Phillipson 2005).

Later mode-3/5 industries in the broader region are not the site’s primary load-bearing contribution in Phillipson’s Ch. 3.

## Significance

- Anchors late Acheulean chronology revision (14C minimum → older absolute estimates).
- Exemplifies water-edge settlement and rare wood preservation.
- Links south-central Africa into the pan-African Acheulean–[[sangoan-industry|Sangoan]] narrative.

## Key Debates

Dating method conflict (early 14C vs uranium-series/regional) is the central reliability issue — always state method when citing Kalambo ages.
