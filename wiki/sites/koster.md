# Koster

Deeply stratified Archaic settlement hollow in the Illinois River valley (Illinois, USA). In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is a North American type-case for the rise of [[complex-hunter-gatherers|complex hunter-gatherer]] villages—seasonal then permanent—without Old World-style cereal farming, and later for the Eastern Agricultural Complex.

**Identity caution:** Phase labels and “Koster people” are archaeological constructs, not ethnonyms of historically documented nations.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: excavations from 1969 revealed camping from 8000 BCE, **thirteen superimposed settlements**, and a permanent village by 5000 BCE supporting hunting, plant collecting, and fishing “without fear of hunger.” The sequence is used comparatively with other sedentary foragers (e.g. Uenohara, Skateholm, Damdama) in later chapters.

Lubbock-style seasonal narratives for phase 8 are reconstructions, not ethnographic observation.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Mithen 2003, ch.31.)*

1. **From 8000 BCE:** repeated camping in the hollow; multi-phase buildup.
2. **Phase 8 (~6000 BCE):** multi-hut hamlets occupied spring–autumn for hickory and other nuts, deer, and fishing; abandoned each winter—winter destinations unknown archaeologically.
3. **By 5000 BCE:** permanent village; hunting, plant collecting, and fishing without hunger in Mithen’s summary.
4. **Later phases (6–7):** larger settlement (~dozen huts, ~100 people); canoe trade bringing Great Lakes copper, Gulf shells, Ohio flint; ornaments worn by few; burial inequality—those with ailments in shallow graves near rubbish vs fine physique in headland cemeteries.
5. **Toward farming (~1000 BCE horizon):** Koster-area people farm domesticated descendants of marsh elder, sunflowers, and goosefoot; later adopt Mexican squash, corn, and beans (with possible independent eastern squash domestication).

## Significance

- Flagship Midwestern Archaic sequence for sedentism, storage landscapes, and emerging inequality among foragers.
- Comparative node in global discussions of resource-rich sedentary hunter-gatherers (parallelled by Mithen with Japan, Sweden, South Asia).
- Anchors the independent Eastern Agricultural Complex pathway before full adoption of the Mesoamerican triad.
- Shows long-distance canoe exchange networks in interior eastern North America.

## Key Debates

- How “permanent” early villages were vs multi-season logistic mobility (phase 8 winter absence).
- Interpretation of burial differentiation as hereditary inequality vs achievement-based ranking.
- Timing and independence of eastern squash domestication vs Mexican introductions.
- Whether trade and ornaments imply craft specialization and political hierarchy or smaller-scale prestige competition.
