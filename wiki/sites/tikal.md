# Tikal

## Discovery and Excavation History

Major Petén capital of the Mut kingdom. Classic name *Mut* (“Tikal” is 19th-century; perhaps *ti aka'l* “at the water reservoir”). Intensive Carnegie/University of Pennsylvania and later Guatemalan/international work; [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] synthesize epigraphic dynasty and urban form rather than a single excavation campaign. Artificial reservoirs structure the dry-season city (Scarborough via C&H).

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] unless noted.)*

- **Preclassic:** Late Chicanel masonry structures; Izapan-style wall painting; possible early corbel vaults unproved. Retrospective dynastic founder may belong to the 1st century AD; Burial 85 and Yax Ehb Xook tradition mark early kingship.
- **Early Classic / Teotihuacan horizon:** On 16 January AD 378 Chak Tok Ich'aak I dies the same day Sihyaj K'ahk' (“Fire is Born”) “arrives”; Yax Nuun Ahiin installed; later Sihyaj Chan K'awiil II. Two interpretive schools: military takeover from Teotihuacan (Proskouriakoff/Coggins line) vs elite emulation of Teotihuacan war cult (later majority). Oxygen/strontium work (Lori Wright) finds mostly local elites — tensions with pure invasion models.
- **Hiatus / Calakmul pressure:** AD 562 Calakmul-related conquest in the broader narrative; political eclipse then recovery.
- **Late Classic apogee:** Jasaw Chan K'awiil’s line; Temple I wooden lintel victory over Calakmul’s Yich'aak K'ahk' (August AD 695); Temple IV ~70 m; realm population estimates on the order of ~60,000 (authors’ synthesis — contested scale). Lentz timber study: preferred sapodilla beams before ~AD 741, then inferior logwood — deforestation signal with caveats.
- **Urban form:** “Colloidal” settlement (not Teotihuacan grid); density declines to margins; house compounds around plazas.

## Significance

Type-case of lowland [[classic-maya]] divine kingship ([[kuhul-ajaw]]), dynastic war with [[calakmul]], and reservoir urbanism. Central to Martin & Grube’s hierarchical “Great Game” model as presented by C&H. Primary locus for Thompson-correlation radiocarbon tests on dated lintels.

## Key Debates

- [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] — southern lowland abandonment trajectory.
- Teotihuacan *entrada* of AD 378 — invasion vs emulation ([[teotihuacan]]).
- Population scale (Johnston doubling vs Webster light estimates — field-wide debate).

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]], unless noted. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above; does not overwrite it.)*

### Research history and urban scale

Sharer & Traxler report Tikal (ancient **Mutul**) was unseen by Spanish conquistadors and only discovered by outsiders in the mid-nineteenth century (Méndez and Tut 1848; later Maudslay, Maler, Tozzer/Merwin map, Morley). An airstrip (1951) enabled the University of Pennsylvania Museum **Tikal Project** (1956–70; Shook first director; William Coe last seven years; 113 professional staff; 30+ *Tikal Reports*), which established Maya sites as preindustrial cities with large populations and subsistence/trade networks rather than empty ceremonial centers. IDAEH later worked Group G and the Lost World Pyramid Group.

As of their synthesis: occupation area ~60 km²; earthworks (moat + rubble wall) enclose ~123 km² between bajos; North Acropolis (~100 × 80 m) largely Early Classic funerary temples, royal necropolis from Late Preclassic through Early Classic; Lost World Pyramid (Str. 5C-54) massive Late Preclassic (~80 m square base, >20 m high); sacbeob (Méndez, Tozzer, Maler, Maudslay) link the core; Temples I–IV Late Classic (Temple IV ~70 m; Temple I ~47 m; Temple V ~57 m). They cite Late Classic peak estimates after Rice & Culbert 1990: ~62,000 inside the 120 km² earthwork/bajo boundary, plus ~30,000 rural within 10 km → greater Tikal ~314 km² ~92,000 — on the same order as C&H’s ~60,000 synthesis but with explicit hinterland add-on. Plaza Plan 2 (east-side household shrine over ancestral founder) is reported as a distinctive residential pattern. Twin pyramid groups are treated (after Coggins) as cosmos models for *k’atun*-ending ceremonies.

### Dynasty and the AD 378 regime change

Later kings counted succession from founder **Yax Ehb’ Xook**; Sharer & Traxler place his reign ca. AD 100 with [[el-mirador|El Mirador]]’s decline; dynasty endured nearly 800 years to Stela 11 (AD 869). Burial 85 (headless tall male, North Acropolis axis, ca. AD 100) is identified as likely his tomb. Stela 29 (upper portion) is the earliest dated lowland monument: Long Count 8.12.14.8.15 (AD 292), with Tikal emblem main sign — best evidence of Mutul as independent polity capital.

On the same day AD 378 (8.17.1.4.12): arrival of **Siyaj K’ak’** (“Born of Fire”) and death of 14th ruler **Chak Tok Ich’aak I**. Siyaj K’ak’ titled *ochk’in kaloomte’* (“lord of the west”); Stuart finds a reference at Waka (El Perú) eight days before Tikal (western approach). Positions presented without single adjudication: homeland could be [[kaminaljuyu]] or [[teotihuacan]]; he may have led a returning Tikal faction; or he commanded a force sent by **Spearthrower Owl** to install that king’s son **Yax Nuun Ayiin I** (inaugurated 379; reigned ~379–411; Burial 10). Pre-378 stelae 26 and 39 found broken/mutilated may indicate violence. **Siyaj Chan K’awiil II** (411–456) reasserted dynastic continuity despite the break; Stela 31 (445) synthesizes traditional Maya regalia with Teotihuacan-style warrior imagery of his father.

### Expansion, defeat, and Late Classic revival

Sharer & Traxler argue Tikal influence extended to Río Azul (probable Siyaj K’ak’ reference 393; max growth ca. 390–540) and that archaeological/historical evidence suggests Tikal orchestration of the 426 [[copan]] founding and [[quirigua]] as Motagua subsidiary. Rivalry with [[calakmul]] is framed as commercial (Caribbean–Gulf drainage divide; Río Hondo vs Belize River access) plus ideological claims (Tikal dynastic founding ca. 100; Calakmul older Kan Preclassic heritage).

**AD 562 star war:** Caracol Altar 21 records Tikal’s defeat; Simon Martin argued victor-name outlines match Calakmul’s **Sky Witness** (with [[caracol]] assistance) rather than Caracol’s own king — Wak Chan K’awiil likely captured/sacrificed. Century-long eclipse: dynasty continued (kings 22–25 may have been barred from erecting monuments), probably under tribute; Animal Skull (22nd) broke the Spearthrower Owl patriline while still counting from Yax Ehb’ Xook. **Jasaw Chan K’awiil I** (accession 682) revitalized Mutul by recalling Early Classic glories; Temple I lintels record 695 defeat of Calakmul’s Yuknoom Yich’aak K’ak’ (“bringing down the flint and shield”) and capture of a patron-deity effigy; Burial 116 under Temple I. Son **Yik’in Chan K’awiil** (from 734) defeated Calakmul 736, Waka 743, Naranjo 744. Petexbatun reconquest failed 705 ([[dos-pilas]]). Late rulers: Yax Nuun Ayiin II (from 768); last monument Stela 11 AD 869; realm broken into petty polities claiming Mutul legitimacy; capital abandoned by end of 10th century.

### Economy, ritual, and Terminal Classic decline

East Plaza treated as permanent market at the causeway junction; reservoir water control as elite lever; Tikal middens show increasing deer proportion over time with field-mosaic expansion. Wooden lintels record processions of Naranjo and Waka patron deities after defeat. Authors’ drought scenario: after Stela 24 (810), >60 years without dynastic monuments — possible nine-year drought from ~810; by ca. 850 population dropped ~85% in the capital and ~80% hinterland (reservoir-dependent departure before full famine). Avendaño (1696) may have been first European to see the Classic city when lost returning from [[tayasal]].

## Densification from Martin & Grube 2008

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube, *Chronicle of the Maya Kings and Queens*, 2nd ed. 2008]]; authors [[martin-simon|Simon Martin]] and [[grube-nikolai|Nikolai Grube]]. Complements Coe & Houston and Sharer & Traxler densifications above; does not overwrite them.)*

### Dynasty, peak scale, and founder

Martin & Grube open their Tikal chapter by stating that at its 8th-century peak the city was a dispersed metropolis housing as many as ~60,000 people, with a dynastic succession of at least 33 rulers spanning as long as 800 years. They present Tikal as a survivor of the Preclassic collapse that became a crucible of the new lowland Classic tradition, with a dynasty in place as early as the 1st century AD. Inscriptions, they report, trace Classic dynastic origin to **Yax Ehb Xook** (“First Step Shark”); no contemporary source pinpoints his rule, but average reign-length estimates and a revised count of kings (placing Chak Tok Ich'aak I as 14th rather than 9th) put the founder around AD 100 — a better archaeological fit — and make Burial 85 a viable contender for his tomb. The first contemporary date is Stela 29 (AD 292), which they call the earliest Long Count date in the Maya lowlands.

### Entrada 378, Hiatus 562–692, and dual Mutal conflict

Few events, Martin & Grube argue, transformed the lowlands as much as the arrival of **Sihyaj K'ahk'** (“Fire Born”) at Tikal on 15 January 378, traced via El Perú eight days earlier and interpreted as political takeover rather than neutral “arrival.” Chak Tok Ich'aak I dies the same day (“entering the water”); monuments are destroyed or dispersed; **Yax Nuun Ahiin I** is installed (379) under Sihyaj K'ahk'’s sponsorship, with **Spearthrower Owl** as a second entrada figure and putative Teotihuacan-linked overlord. Sihyaj Chan K'awiil II (411–456) later reinvents the regime as orthodox Maya kingship (Stela 31).

Martin & Grube place the “star war” defeat of Wak Chan K'awiil in 562 — Venus stationary node — with the damaged victor-name making the Snake kingdom the prime suspect (cross-linked to [[caracol]] Altar 21 and [[calakmul]]’s Sky Witness). The Hiatus (AD 562–692) is not a mere “little collapse” tied only to Teotihuacan’s fall: they point specifically to military humbling and the rise of rival Calakmul. Nuun Ujol Chaak faces both Yuknoom the Great’s star war on Tikal (657) and a fraternal/half-brother rival **Bajlaj Chan K'awiil** at [[dos-pilas]] — dual holders of the Mutal title — through the 672–679 “civil war” cycle (Tikal seizes Dos Pilas; Calakmul restores Bajlaj; 679 Dos Pilas victory over Tikal with likely Calakmul aid).

### Jasaw 695, Yik'in wars, Terminal Classic end

Despite the 679 reverse, Martin & Grube argue Nuun Ujol Chaak’s line was not overthrown: son **Jasaw Chan K'awiil I** (from 682) restores Early Classic glory rhetoric and, on 5 August 695, “brings down the flint and shield” of Calakmul’s **Yich'aak K'ahk'** — a turning point for Snake hegemony. Temple I wooden lintels record capture of a Calakmul patron deity (*yajaw muan*); whether the captive on Structure 5D-57 is the Calakmul king remains unclear in the broken caption. Jasaw’s programme revives Teotihuacan war-serpent imagery and marks K'atun-endings with twin-pyramid groups; Burial 116 under Temple I is his tomb.

Son **Yik'in Chan K'awiil** (from 734) is cast as greatest builder and military tactician: Temple IV (largest Classic construction in their account); Calakmul captive on Altar 9 (736); star war on El Perú (743) and on [[naranjo]]’s Wak Kabnal (744), taking Yax Mayuy Chan Chaak (Stela 5). These victories, they argue, break a century-old Calakmul client cordon. Yax Nuun Ahiin II (from 768, 29th from founder) builds enormous twin-pyramid Groups Q and R, yet as El Perú, Naranjo, and Caracol end long dormancies, Tikal’s regional grip seems diminished. After Dark Sun / Stela 24 (810), a ~60-year silence includes unrecorded 10.0.0.0.0 (830); Jewel K'awiil appears only at Seibal (849); satellites Ixlu and Jimbal adopt the once-exclusive Mutal title after 859. **Stela 11 (AD 869)** of Jasaw Chan K'awiil II is Tikal’s final sculpture; by century’s end Martin & Grube describe depopulation, squatter reuse, North Acropolis tomb-mining, and abandonment in the 10th or 11th century.

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel, *A Forest of Kings*, 1990]], Ch. 4–5 and notes. Complements Coe & Houston, Sharer & Traxler, and Martin & Grube densifications above; does not overwrite them. **Nickname note:** 1990 book nicknames retained below — Great-Jaguar-Paw, Smoking-Frog, Curl-Snout, Stormy-Sky, Double-Bird, Shield-Skull, Ah-Cacaw, Lord Water, Jaguar-Paw of Calakmul, etc. Later phonetics revise many of these names (e.g. Ah-Cacaw ≈ Jasaw Chan K’awiil; Curl-Snout ≈ Yax Nuun Ahiin; Smoking-Frog often equated with Sihyaj K’ahk’ in later literature; Great-Jaguar-Paw with Chak Tok Ich’aak I — map with care, do not silently equate without attribution). Battle scenes without surviving pictorial evidence are flagged as NARRATIVE DEVICE.)*

### Late Preclassic North Acropolis and Early Classic dynasty

Schele & Freidel report that in the first century B.C. the North Acropolis expanded with large public buildings and chambered burial vaults; apron moldings, pyramidal platforms, steep stairs, and terraces with painted plaster masks of gods prefigured later state architecture. A Late Preclassic high-status woman’s tomb (wall paintings, rich goods) is presented as among the earliest interments of “patent consequence,” showing ascribed status for noble women despite patrilineal primacy. **Burial 85:** headless, thighless corpse in cinnabar-impregnated bundle with spondylus and stingray spine; green fuchsite portrait-head pectoral wears the Jester God headdress they treat as the crown of kings for the next millennium (missing skull/thighbones possibly retained as relics). North Acropolis elites, they report (citing Haviland 1967 in notes), were larger, more robust, and better dieted than commoners. First-century-B.C. plaza remodeling (North Acropolis renovation; Great/West Plaza paving; East Plaza; repaving) is linked to rivalry with [[el-mirador]] and [[uaxactun]].

Through the first century A.D., Schele & Freidel argue, neither Tikal nor Uaxactun dominated the other; both supported kingship with comparable public architecture and were ready to assume El Mirador’s role when that kingdom disintegrated. Tikal inscriptions claim a single dynasty from Early Classic to ninth-century demise (≥39 successors in their count); historical founder **Yax-Moch-Xoc** reconstructed as ruling ca. A.D. 219–238 (reign-average projection from Mathews/Jones in notes — not the first ruler ever, but the anchoring ancestor later kings recognized). Earliest historical king in portraiture: **Scroll-Ahau-Jaguar** on Stela 29, 8.12.14.8.15 (July 8, A.D. 292). **Moon-Zero-Bird** seated as king on the Leiden Plaque text, September 17, A.D. 320, with captive at feet. Notes flag that dynastic claims of Olmec-era antiquity at Tikal are ideological, not archaeological.

### Great-Jaguar-Paw, Smoking-Frog, and the A.D. 378 war on Uaxactun

**Great-Jaguar-Paw** (ninth successor of Yax-Moch-Xoc in their count; accession window A.D. 320–376) is credited with changing Tikal/Uaxactun destinies and the nature of Maya sacred warfare. On October 21, A.D. 376 he ends the seventeenth katun on fragmentary Stela 39 (waist-down figure with jaguar-paw-shaped executioner’s ax atop a bound captive). Schele & Freidel distinguish traditional captive-taking war from **kingdom-taking conquest**.

**Victory date:** January 16, A.D. 378 = Long Count 8.17.1.4.12 11 Eb 15 Mac — recorded twice at Uaxactun (Stela 5; retrospectively Stela 22) and twice at Tikal (retrospectively Stela 31; Ballcourt Marker in Group 6C-XVI). Primary actors in their reading: high king Great-Jaguar-Paw and **Smoking-Frog**. Uaxactun Stela 5 shows Smoking-Frog as triumphant Tikal *ahau* in full warrior regalia with obsidian-bladed club — first visual of the **Tlaloc-Venus costume** (balloon headdress, spearthrower), which they argue marks “conquest war” and was borrowed from [[teotihuacan]] (POSITION: borrowed costume/rituals; not wiki-voice invasion). The 378 date itself has no detectable astronomical significance in their analysis; planetary timing of battles may have come later under Curl-Snout and Stormy-Sky. Stela 31 retrospective: Smoking-Frog *hom* (“demolished/threw down”) Uaxactun’s buildings; Great-Jaguar-Paw bloodlets from genitals to sanctify victory. Ballcourt Marker records an action that day (probably decapitation of captives) with a decapitation-sacrifice god glyph continuous with older *ahau* practice.

**Political outcome (S&F reconstruction):** Smoking-Frog installed as usurper king at Uaxactun; consistently includes Tikal [[emblem-glyph]] in his name; still at Uaxactun 18 years later (8.18.0.0.0 / July 8, 396, Stela 4); authors are “reasonably sure” he never ruled Tikal as its king. **Curl-Snout** became high king of Tikal September 13, 379 under Smoking-Frog’s sufferance (*yahau* / *u cab* of Smoking-Frog on Stelae 18 and 31). Explicit hypothesis (POSITION): Smoking-Frog brother of Great-Jaguar-Paw; Curl-Snout nephew; Ballcourt Marker *ihtan* “sibling” of “Spearthrower-Owl” equated with Great-Jaguar-Paw. Uaxactun Stela 22 (9.3.10.0.0 / December 9, 504) still commemorates the conquest 126 years later. Victory, they argue, enabled ~180 years of central Petén dominance for Tikal.

**Against Teotihuacan military conquest of Tikal (POSITION):** Imagery has been explained as Teotihuacan military conquest or usurpation by Teotihuacan/Kaminaljuyu lords; Schele & Freidel reject usurpation if their kinship reconstruction holds (unbroken Great-Jaguar-Paw → Curl-Snout → Stormy-Sky). They hold Teotihuacanos were physically present in small numbers for trade/exchange in the fifth–sixth centuries, not military occupation; tassel-headed ambassador-traders with warrior escorts. Curl-Snout (Burial 10) and Stormy-Sky (Burial 48) tombs include significant Teotihuacan-style pots; Problematic Deposit 50 may include high-rank resident Teotihuacanos. Teotihuacan-style imagery in tombs and stelae lasted only through Stormy-Sky’s reign; by A.D. 475 rulers abandoned this self-representation. Pictorial battle reconstruction with hidden atlatl troops is NARRATIVE DEVICE — “Imagine the growing sense of horror…” — not primary evidence. Notes accept Mathews’s conquest scenario for the shared 8.17.1.4.12 date over pure foreigner-arrival/marriage models, while allowing marriage may have followed conquest.

### Post-conquest prosperity and Double-Bird’s silence

After Uaxactun conquest Tikal became, in their view, the largest/most prosperous Early Classic kingdom in the Maya heartland. Mid-Classic kings reworked the North Acropolis to an eight-building pattern; twin-pyramid complex prototype in East Plaza facilitated period-ending rites (complexes unique to Tikal; dating start to late fifth/early sixth century important for katun-festival economy). Double-Bird (21st successor) Stela 17 (9.6.3.9.15 / September 17, 557) was last monument before a long inscribed silence; pre-hiatus stelae deliberately effaced by abrading or shattering.

### Hiatus: Caracol “star-at-Tikal” (562) and impoverishment

Schele & Freidel accept Proskouriakoff’s monument hiatus framing and Willey’s regional-crisis reading, but ground Tikal’s silence in **Caracol** military pressure. Caracol Altar 21 (discovered 1986 ballcourt): **Lord Water** accession April 18, A.D. 553; “ax-war” in the land of Tikal’s *ahau* April 11, 556 (9.6.2.1.11) — not fatal; **star-at-Tikal** war event May 1, 562 (9.6.8.4.2) — treated as usually lethal Tlaloc-Venus war of the type earlier used against Uaxactun. They argue correspondence of Caracol’s victory claim to “all-out destruction” at Tikal shows the claim was not fabrication; extent and duration of political dominance remain uncertain. Archaeological signatures they cite: Caracol-region influence on art and funerary practice; stelae cease; temple building slows; meager grave goods. Speculation: Double-Bird captured and killed; remaining *ahauob* cut off from trade routes. Only one rich tomb under oppression — Burial 195 of the twenty-second successor, never permitted public monuments. In 130 years after defeat only that king left a name, and not in public space. Uaxactun, part of Tikal’s hegemony, ceased monuments at 9.6.0.0.0 for ~200 years — irony that Tikal was undone by the same Tlaloc-Venus war it had waged against Uaxactun ~180 years earlier.

**Envelopment (POSITION):** By mid-Katun 5 Tikal may have been ringed by hostile alliance — [[calakmul]] north (heir of El Mirador’s northern power in their view), Caracol south, [[naranjo]] east, perhaps Yaxchilan west. Cu-Ix of Calakmul vassal ritual at Yaxchilan (537); Naranjo Ruler I accession *u cab* of Cu-Ix (546). Notes: no epigraphic usurper Caracol kings at Tikal, no victory monument as at Naranjo — victors desecrated history and banned public monuments; Ah-Cacaw later cached Stelae 26/31 as restoration of desecrated ancestral history, not because the bloodline was broken. Dos Pilas accepted as independent Tikal offshoot after hiatus with competitive tension (Houston & Mathews position they accept).

### Recovery under Shield-Skull and Ah-Cacaw (695 Calakmul)

Though no stelae 557–692 survive, **Shield-Skull** began ambitious North Acropolis and East Plaza remodeling mid-seventh century — evidence for authors of Caracol’s weakening grip. **Ah-Cacaw** acceded 9.12.9.17.16 (May 6, 682); ~167 cm tall; lived into fourth katun. First major political act: honorably bury desecrated Stelae 26 and 31 (Stormy-Sky’s Uaxactun-victory memorial) within Temples 34 and 33 with termination fires, then raise new Temple 33-1st (~18.8 m) over filled 33-2nd. Revived Twin Pyramid Complex with Stela 30 for end of Katun 13.

**Temple 33 dedication cycle and capture of Jaguar-Paw of Calakmul:** Temple 33-1st finished shortly after 9.13.3.0.0 (March 3, 695); 158 days later Ah-Cacaw took captive King Jaguar-Paw of Calakmul (August 8, 695) — Tlaloc-Venus war style, but timed by 13-katun anniversary of Stormy-Sky’s Stela 31 war event rather than usual Venus mandates (two days after solar zenith passage). Thirteen days later: display/humiliation of Calakmul captives; Structure 5D-57 plaster shows king in Mosaic Monster / Tlaloc Venus-war garb with captive Ah-Bolon-Bakin (ally/vassal, not Jaguar-Paw himself). Twenty-seven days later captives sacrificed in Temple 33 dedication (Temple I Lintel 3); Ah-Cacaw bloodlets from tongue; dedication date = 13-katun anniversary of last date on broken Stela 31. Authors stress Ah-Cacaw preferred historical cycle over Venus for dynastic rebirth. Why Calakmul not Caracol (POSITION): Calakmul–Caracol alliance and earlier encirclement strategy made Jaguar-Paw the target; Naranjo descendants struck south while Ah-Cacaw turned north.

Aftermath in their synthesis: second Calakmul attack taking Ox-Ha-Te; 9.14.0.0.0 second Twin Pyramid (Stela 16 / Altar 5) with Eveningstar headdress; son Ruler B succeeded 9.15.3.6.8 (December 12, 734). Parallel reclamation strategies with Smoking-Squirrel of Naranjo: high-rank captives, massive rebuilding, appeal to ancient orthodoxy (Ah-Cacaw sealing North Acropolis into new triad with Temples 1 and 2). Speculative open question whether Flint-Sky-God K of [[dos-pilas]] delivered Jaguar-Paw — Dos Pilas benefited as Calakmul–Caracol axis broke.
