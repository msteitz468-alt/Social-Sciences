# Uxmal

## Discovery and Excavation History

Largest capital of the Puuc region, northwestern Yucatán. [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler (2006)]] treat Uxmal within their Terminal Classic northern-lowland synthesis rather than as a single-campaign monograph site; architecture (Puuc veneer over concrete hearting), epigraphy of Chan Chak K’ak’nal Ajaw, and settlement/polity arguments draw on multiple projects and comparative Puuc work (including Sayil settlement research by Sabloff & Tourtellot as a regional analogue).

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Sharer & Traxler 2006.)*

- **Environmental setting:** Northern lowlands rainfall usually under 2,000 mm (northwest Yucatán sometimes under 500 mm); almost no surface streams; cenotes principal water sources except in the Puuc, where Maya built *chultuns* (underground cisterns) for dry-season rainwater. Puuc hills sparsely settled before ca. AD 800 for lack of surface water/cenotes; Terminal Classic expansion used fertile soils plus chultuns, with settlement on uplands to free best soils for agriculture. Growth from local increase plus gradual migration from Edzna, Chenes, and Río Bec regions over a century or more, driven by heartland decline.
- **Rise:** New Puuc polities—Uxmal, Kabah, Sayil, Labna—founded, prospered, and declined within roughly 200 years of the Terminal Classic. Oxkintok had dominated a portion of the northern lowlands in the Classic with southern ties; it declined as new Puuc capitals rose (latest Oxkintok monument Stela 9, AD 859).
- **Apogee under Chan Chak K’ak’nal Ajaw:** Uxmal largest Puuc capital; peak territory ~25 km radius; Uaymil likely seaport. Only historically known king Chan Chak K’ak’nal Ajaw (throne late ninth century; dated monuments roughly the decade 895–907) expanded to incorporate Kabah and much of the eastern Puuc; Kabah texts name him; an 18 km *sacbe* links the sites. Stelae depict divine-king warrior paraphernalia; site walls and captive-taking sculpture (Kabah). Emblem glyph identified. Apogee ca. AD 800–1000.
- **Political organization (authors’ argument):** Puuc states not replicas of southern Classic polities; ruling elites modified the system after the southern downfall—power less centralized and personified; more decentralized political and economic systems. Palace distribution and lack of great funerary temples suggest power sharing; some palaces read as “government houses.” Nunnery Quadrangle interpreted as administrative complex (formal entry, elevated northern platform, no domestic features)—king, court, and governing council for tribute and administration rather than pure residential palace.
- **Architecture:** Puuc veneer masonry; northern lowland ball courts at Uxmal and [[chichen-itza]] with vertical walls and stone rings (vs Preclassic sloping-wall courts). Distinctive regional Classic styles include Puuc veneer among others (Peten/Tikal, Usumacinta, Palenque mansard, Copan mosaics, Chichen synthesis).
- **Decline:** By early Postclassic no later styles. Positions on Puuc decline: (A) small-scale ecological replay of southern demise (overpopulation + risky rainfall agriculture)—but northern lake cores seem to rule out decreased rainfall as the Puuc trigger; (B) conflict with growing Chichen Itza regional state. Sharer & Traxler: combination of ecological failure and military onslaught likely. Itza expansion from ~AD 900 incorporated most northern lowlands; neighbors including the Puuc trajectory declined during expansion.
- **Postclassic memory:** Xiu elite house of [[mayapan]] claimed origins from Uxmal (allied with Chel in Mayapan’s *multepal*). Terminal Classic seaborne bulk trade around Yucatán undermined inland heartland polities while sustaining Uxmal and coastal-linked northern centers for a time.

## Significance

Principal type-case of Terminal Classic Puuc florescence: chultun-supported urbanism, veneer architecture, hybrid divine-king / decentralized administration, and the northern zenith concurrent with southern heartland demise ([[classic-maya-collapse-debate]]). Links [[classic-maya]] political reorganization to [[chichen-itza]] expansion and later Mayapan ethnohistory.

## Key Debates

- **Puuc decline causes** — ecological intensification limits vs Chichen military pressure (or both), as positioned in Sharer & Traxler 2006.
- **How “Classic” was Puuc kingship?** — emblem glyph and warrior stelae of Chan Chak K’ak’nal Ajaw vs palace/Nunnery evidence for council-style power sharing; contrast with Chichen art that does not advertise individual divine kings the same way.
- Relation of northern Terminal Classic expansion (Uxmal, surviving [[coba]], etc.) to southern collapse migration streams.

## Historiography

Morley-era Carnegie work mapped and publicized northern sites including routes toward Coba and Calakmul; later ceramic/architectural revision at Chichen Itza re-periodized “florescient” vs “modified florescent” sequences that frame Puuc–Chichen relations. Sharer & Traxler 2006 synthesize epigraphic kingship (one named Uxmal ruler), settlement analogues (Sayil), and multi-causal decline. Source densification: [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006]].
