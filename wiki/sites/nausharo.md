# Nausharo

Settlement on the **Kachi Plain** of Baluchistan (Pakistan), at the foot of the Bolan Pass linking the Quetta/highland route to the greater Indus plain. With [[mehrgarh]] and Pirak, Nausharo forms Kenoyer’s key piedmont sequence for indigenous development into the [[indus-valley-tradition]] ([[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Kenoyer 1998]]).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Later research (after early city-focused campaigns at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa) opened the earlier developmental stages through French Mission work under [[jarrige-jean-francois|Jean-François Jarrige]] and colleagues at Mehrgarh and Nausharo. Kenoyer treats those excavations as overturning simple Near-Eastern-only diffusion narratives for Indus urban origins by documenting long Regionalization-Era sequences on the Kachi Plain.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Kenoyer 1998.)*

- **Geographic setting:** Kachi Plain / Bolan Pass corridor — same piedmont zone as [[mehrgarh]] and Pirak; gateway between Baluchistan highlands and Indus alluvium.
- **Regionalization / Early Harappan (incl. Kot Diji Phase, before 2600 BCE):** Settlement planning on a cardinal grid (large N–S and presumably E–W streets) concurrent with [[harappa]] Period 2, [[kalibangan]], Rehman Dheri, and Kot Diji — planning did not begin only with the large cities.
- **Social hierarchy signals:** Large multi-room houses distinct from smaller structures (with Mehrgarh) read by Kenoyer as class differentiation.
- **Burned levels:** Burning and rebuilding (Period ID rooms dated 2600–2500 BCE in catalogue) — Kenoyer notes (citing Jarrige) that ash layers and fires at Kot Diji/Nausharo need not imply warfare; ceramic continuity can indicate non-replacement of inhabitants.
- **Figurines and pottery (catalogue):** Period IB female figurine type identical to Mehrgarh Period VII (2800–2600 BCE), with red pigment in the hair part (Kenoyer suggests possible analogy to later sindur practice — attributed analogy, not identity of religion). Period ID burned-room group of painted male figurines (including man with infant). Large painted storage jar with humped bull tied to pipal tree (local manufacture despite highland geometric parallels).
- **Early writing-related finds:** Pottery symbols at early levels grouped with Harappa Period 2 as possible Early Indus scripts; terracotta mold for large jars with pre-firing exterior script (possible mold-owner mark).
- **Harappan Phase:** Period III (catalogue c. 2300–2200 BCE) multi-headed hollow animal figurines linked by Kenoyer to multi-animal seal mythology and possible cult puppets; strong ceramic continuity across the urban transition (with Harappa).

## Significance

- Critical **western/piedmont node** for the Regionalization → Integration trajectory of the Indus Valley Tradition.
- Independent evidence that **grid planning and craft/literacy practices** were established outside the great cities before or at the onset of full urbanism.
- Continuity case against invasion-replacement readings of Early Harappan burn horizons ([[indus-polity-debate]], [[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate]] as related warfare literature).

## Key Debates

- Accidental fire vs conflict for burned horizons (Kenoyer favors non-catastrophic readings where ceramic continuity holds).
- Degree of continuity between Mehrgarh and Nausharo communities vs population replacement (four-way non-identity: site sequence ≠ ethnicity ≠ language).
- Whether Early Indus pottery symbols are true precursors of the mature [[indus-script]] (shared sequences vs independent marks — Kenoyer leans toward meaningful continuity).

## Historiography

Interpretation is dominated by the French Mission’s Baluchistan program and by Kenoyer’s use of that sequence to reframe Indus urbanism as indigenous and multi-centric. Absolute dates in Kenoyer 1998 are often phase-level; check Jarrige monographs for full radiocarbon tables when updating.

## Wright (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]], *[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]* 2010, chs. 3, 6, 9, 11.)*

**Pottery seriation (Quivron 2000).** Gonzague Quivron, working at Nausharo, established the contemporaneity of pottery styles across widely dispersed Urban-period Indus settlements through stylistic and stratigraphic seriation, examining hundreds of whole pots and thousands of sherds from stratified deposits — "used this 'uniformity' to establish the contemporaneity of pottery styles among widely dispersed settlements." He identified a two-stage stylistic sequence: an early stage with a "leaflike motif" design register (water weeds/seeds separated by double bands or dots), followed by a stage introducing a checkerboard pattern and hatched leaf motifs, compared across Amri, [[kalibangan]], [[harappa]], Banawali, Cholistan, and [[mohenjo-daro]].

**Kulli-culture/BMAC affiliation shift (Period IV).** At Nausharo Period IV, radiocarbon-dated to roughly 2200–2000 BCE, Kulli-culture (southern Baluchistan) pottery and figurine styles appear — Kulli is described as a politically independent, possibly rival culture contemporary with the Indus (J.-F. Jarrige 1994) — alongside Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex ([[bactria-margiana-archaeological-complex]]) materials, read by Wright as a possible expansion of Kulli influence into northern Baluchistan (Wright 2001). This Kulli/BMAC affiliation shift accompanies broader Post-urban-period BMAC contact documented in small quantities at Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, and elsewhere in Baluchistan and Rajasthan, as Mesopotamian maritime trade and the southern Baluchistan trading outposts of Sutkagen-dor and Sotka-koh were abandoned.

**Faunal and traction evidence.** Sheep at Nausharo remained relatively small in the Urban period, continuing the domestication size-decrease pattern documented at Mehrgarh (Meadow 1989a), in contrast to larger Harappa sheep with kill-off patterns suggesting wool-oriented breeding (unconfirmed). A miniature clay yoke found at Nausharo (Meadow 1996:405) supports evidence for cattle traction elsewhere in the Indus (alongside plowed-field remnants at Kalibangan and cattle-bone traction pathologies at Harappa). Wright also cites Nausharo as a documented instance of "semisedentary/transhumant" pastoralism — seasonal upland/lowland movement — within Richard Meadow's (1996:401) broader continuum-of-mobility framework for Harappan pastoral lifeways, contrasted with sedentary pastoralism at Harappa/Dholavira and inferred nomadic pastoralism on the Ghaggar-Hakra plain. Fish-bone studies (Belcher 2003) at Nausharo, Harappa, Balakot, and [[allahdino]] found freshwater catfish the principal fish exploited at all four sites, likely via seasonal winter backwater fishing.

**Courtyard-house correlation.** Wright groups Nausharo with Harappa, Mohenjo-daro, and Lothal as sites yielding comparable large black storage jars (also found in Oman/UAE, some manufactured in the Indus itself per Mery and Blackman 2005 neutron activation analysis), and notes a broader Kachi-Plain courtyard-house architectural tradition continuous with Mehrgarh's later periods, where household-level storage rooms and courtyard hearths replaced the earlier communal compartmented-building storage pattern.
