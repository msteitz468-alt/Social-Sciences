## Discovery and Excavation History

Kotosh sits at 2,000 m on a tributary of the Río Huallaga, in the Huánuco region of Peru. Although only about 130 m × 95 m in area, it is, in Moore's (2005) description, "one of the most important sites in Andean prehistory." It is one of several nearby Formative centers in the Upper Huallaga drainage, alongside Shillacoto and Waira-jirca (Onuki 1993).

The site was excavated by Seiichi Izumi and Kazuo Terada in the early 1960s (Izumi and Terada 1972). The excavated deposit contains almost 100 superimposed structures spanning six named periods — Mito, Waira-jirca, Kotosh, Chavín, Sajara-patac, and Higueras — dated circa 2500 B.C.E. to circa C.E. 1 (Izumi and Terada 1972:307–12). The dating method in the excerpted material is stated only in terms of period seriation, without further radiocarbon detail specified.

## Occupation Sequence

The earliest, Mito period at Kotosh dates circa 2500–2000/1800 B.C.E. Eleven partial Mito-phase ritual chambers were found, of a basic rectangular form with a single entrance and a split-level floor creating a central, sunken, bench-like space around a well-built hearth, often with a subfloor flue; niches were placed with bilateral symmetry. Tsugio Matsuzawa (1972:129) writes that "the most predominant feature of the constructions of the Kotosh Mito Period is the abundant variety of forms present."

Two major temples were built one atop the other during the Mito period, on a central platform mound Matsuzawa (1972:133) describes as "the focal point of the Kotosh Mito Period": the earlier Templo de las Manos Cruzadas, subsequently covered by the Templo de los Nichitos. The Templo de las Manos Cruzadas had meter-thick walls over 2 m tall, a room measuring 9.3 × 9.5 m, a 1.5 m wide doorway on the south wall leading to a central hearth, symmetrical niches on the east and west walls, and a large, deep niche in the north wall flanked by two crossed-forearm clay reliefs; abundant ash in the deposit indicates extensive burning. The Templo de los Nichitos had a similar plan with more, smaller niches; its western half was destroyed during the later Kotosh-Chavín period. Its exterior dimensions are reconstructed as 9.5 × 8.5 m, with walls 80 cm thick, and Matsuzawa (1972:148) estimates it may have held up to 23 niches if the plan was fully symmetrical.

Kazuo Terada (1972:310) documented a ritual "temple entombment" practice at the site: "the Mito people carefully buried their temples with stones and earth." At the Templo de las Manos Cruzadas, the roof was removed, the crossed-arm reliefs were covered with black sand, the entrance was sealed, and the room was filled with cobbles and covered with red clay before the Templo de los Nichitos was built directly atop it (Bonnier 1995:138–39; Onuki 1993:81).

Moore, following his synthesis of the sequence, notes that Mito-phase Kotosh shows ritual entombment and core continuity, with some continuity persisting into the Waira-jirca phase (small ritual chambers); by contrast, the Kotosh-Kotosh phase shows significant architectural reordering, and Kotosh-Chavín phase construction bears little resemblance to the Mito chambers, partially demolishing the Templo de los Nichitos in the process.

Per Moore's comparative Table 3.3, Kotosh's ritual practice is classified as burned offerings and architectural entombment; permanence as multigenerational; ubiquity as subregional; visibility as personal/consultative.

## Significance

Kotosh is a type site for what Richard Burger and Lucy Salazar-Burger term the "Kotosh Religious Tradition," and Burger (1985a:277) draws a sharp architectural contrast between late Preceramic coastal architecture — open platforms emphasizing public space — and Kotosh-tradition highland architecture, which is more closed and small-capacity, with burnt offerings prominent, suggesting differing ritual and social organization between coast and highlands. In Moore's (2005) broader argument, Kotosh's small, multigenerational, subregional, low-visibility ritual spaces are grouped with sites such as Piruru in a "transitional" category distinct from both the intimate/impermanent Formative sites (e.g., [[real-alto]]) and the large, permanent, public, canonist-associated sites such as [[huaca-de-los-reyes]].

## Key Debates

Two related but distinct disputes attach to Kotosh and its "Mito tradition" neighbors, neither adjudicated by Moore:

- **Demolition versus entombment**: Yoshio Onuki (1993:82–83) distinguishes "demolition-reconstruction," which he argues was more common at other Formative sites, from the deliberate "ritual entombment" documented at Kotosh's Templo de las Manos Cruzadas. Onuki hypothesizes (1993:83, in Moore's translation) that "temple renovation was the first impetus for the sociocultural development that promoted the dynamic process during the late Preceramic and the Formative." Moore calls this "an intriguing hypothesis" but stresses that ritual entombment and simple demolition/renovation must be carefully distinguished as distinct practices.
- **"Mito architectural tradition" versus "Kotosh Religious Tradition"**: Elisabeth Bonnier (1988, 1995; Bonnier and Rozenberg 1988; Bonnier et al. 1985), excavator of the related site of Piruru, defines a "Mito architectural tradition" dated circa 2500 to 2000/1800 B.C.E., ending at the start of the third millennium B.C.E. Burger and Salazar-Burger (1980, 1985), by contrast, propose a "Kotosh Religious Tradition" with continuity of ritual practice extending well into the Early Horizon. The two positions also differ in emphasis: Burger and Salazar-Burger stress the ceremonial hearth itself, while Bonnier stresses the hearth together with the surrounding floor as jointly encoding ritual practice — Bonnier (1995:141) writes that "the very floors of the religious structures function as altars." Moore flags this as a candidate dispute for a dedicated debate page (a source-reliability/interpretive dispute over the periodization of Preceramic-Formative ritual architecture in the Peruvian highlands) but does not adjudicate between the positions; see [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]] for the wider Formative-architecture debate this dispute sits within.
