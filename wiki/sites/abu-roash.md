# Abu Roash

Northernmost classic Memphite pyramid site, ~8 km north of [[giza]], where Djedefre (Khufu’s son and successor) built on a hillock overlooking the Giza plateau. Primary synthesis: [[lehner-mark|Mark Lehner]], *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* (1997).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Abu Roash entered modern literature as a robbed, unfinished-looking stump long associated with dynastic rupture narratives. Flinders Petrie recovered a diorite statue-throne fragment with hieroglyphs reading Men..Ra (most probably Menkaure). From 1995 the Franco Institute / University of Geneva expedition under Michael Valloggia reopened systematic work: accurate baseline measurement (~106.2 m), foundation-bed slope, casing debris analysis, and destruction phasing. As of Lehner’s 1997 writing, that expedition was finding little evidence of Old Kingdom-period destruction—rather Roman-period dismantling. Earlier assignment of the mudbrick Lepsius I pyramid at Abu Roash to late 3rd/early 4th dynasty (Huni?) is noted by Lehner via Swelim but is peripheral to the Djedefre complex.

## Occupation Sequence

### Djedefre’s pyramid

Djedefre interrupted the multi-generational positioning of major monuments at Giza by moving north—placing his pyramid nearer due west of Heliopolis, the sun-cult centre. He is the first pharaoh known to take the title “Son of Re.” Intended side length ~106.2 m; slope debated (evidence for ~48° if casing followed the 12° foundation bed; team suggestion nearer ~52° like Meidum E3/Khufu); theoretical height ~57–67 m. Core stump preserves a natural hillock mid-pyramid (as at Khufu and Khafre). Much granite casing debris; 60° blocks once prompted step-pyramid or mastaba proposals, which Lehner rejects as wrong angles versus known accretion and Shepseskaf slopes.

**Substructure:** return to an earlier concept after Sneferu–Khufu raised chambers: colossal pit ~23 × 10 m and ~20+ m deep; access corridor ~49 m at ~22°35′; scant roofing masonry reminiscent of Djoser-style work at [[saqqara]].

**Complex:** N–S rectangular enclosure—possible first revival of the “Djoser-type” orientation; mortuary temple shifted north of the E–W axis, hastily built of fieldstone walls finished in mudbrick around an open court (workshop-like—perhaps left incomplete at death); boat pit south of the temple; votive pottery cache indicates sustained cult; knoll ~20 m higher than Giza implying a causeway perhaps ~1,700 m. Pyramid base area less than a quarter of Khufu’s; traditional reign length only eight years—Lehner notes a smaller design for an elderly king.

### Cult and later kings

Menkaure may have undertaken restoration of the uncompleted work (Stadelmann, based on Petrie’s statue fragment). A dark purplish quartzite head of Djedefre from the boat pit (Louvre) wears the *nemes*; outward-turning surface behind the headdress is captioned by Lehner as possible beginning of a lion body—“the first Sphinx?”

## Significance

- **Solar kingship title.** First “Son of Re” name element; spatial move toward Heliopolis axis; Lehner links this ideological turn to later solar architecture (including the Sphinx Temple at Giza as first solar-oriented temple associated with an OK pyramid complex).
- **Interruptor of the Giza cluster.** Anchors the three-generation Giza design story by defining what happens when the sequence breaks.
- **Construction history.** Inclined casing return (vs Khufu’s horizontal courses); deep-pit substructure revival; N–S enclosure experiment.
- **Destruction chronology.** Modern work revises “dynastic damnatio” narratives toward Roman stripping.

## Key Debates

- **Succession conflict?** Older suggestion that removal from Giza, monument destruction, and Khafre’s return signal a split among Khufu’s sons is undercut by (1) Djedefre’s cartouche and workers’ graffiti on limestone beams covering Khufu’s boat pit—showing he oversaw his father’s funeral—and (2) Franco-Swiss evidence that major destruction is Roman, not Old Kingdom (Lehner 1997).
- **Final slope and height** (48° vs ~52°).
- **Step pyramid / mastaba proposals** for the stump (rejected by Lehner on angle grounds).
- **Menkaure restoration** (Stadelmann suggestion).
- **Quartzite head as early sphinx** (interpretive caption, not demonstrated body).

## Historiography

Abu Roash’s reputation as a “failed” or “usurped” pyramid is a historiographic product Lehner actively revises with Valloggia’s results and the boat-pit funeral evidence. The site remains thinner in Lehner’s book than Giza or Saqqara—pages p. 120–121 of the catalogue—so this densification should be updated when full Franco-Swiss monographs are ingested. Swelim’s Lepsius I attribution is a separate late-3rd-dynasty claim needing specialist cross-check.
