# Yaxchilan

Usumacinta River capital of a [[classic-maya]] kingdom in what is now Chiapas, Mexico. As of [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]], Yaxchilan and [[piedras-negras]] were the two most powerful Late Classic polities on this highland–lowland trade corridor; Yaxchilan was smaller than Piedras Negras but ultimately conquered its larger rival.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Sharer & Traxler (2006) stress that Yaxchilan is known above all from its inscriptions and carved lintels; archaeology is reported as less complete than at Piedras Negras. After her 1960 Piedras Negras breakthrough, Tatiana Proskouriakoff turned to Yaxchilan and identified death, capture, and captor glyphs—extending the historical reading of Maya texts. Heinrich Berlin’s 1958 Emblem Glyph work used Yaxchilan’s “cleft sky” main sign among early examples of site-unique main signs with *k’uhul* and *ajaw* affixes ([[emblem-glyph]]). Most of what is known of Early Classic Yaxchilan comes from later kings’ retrospective accounts; Sharer & Traxler caution that some of those texts—especially under Bird Jaguar IV—may manipulate dynastic history. Colonial and modern ritual use of Classic ruins by Lakandon Maya (pottery *incensarios*, cave and ruin ceremonies at Yaxchilan) is noted as a later overlay, not a continuous urban occupation.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]] unless noted.)*

- **Preclassic roots; Early Classic dynasty and wars:** Preclassic origins with Early Classic retrospective accounts. Dynasty traced to Yopaat Balam I (founding ~320/359?). Before Itzamnaaj Balam II (681–742) Yaxchilan was a relatively small capital already struggling with Piedras Negras by the 5th century. Early wars with Piedras Negras; Knot-eye Jaguar I captured Tikal lord Aj Balam but later became a captive at Piedras Negras (~521). Yaxchilan records a defeat of Calakmul’s Tuun Kab Hix in 537. A monument hiatus is reported 537–629; monuments carved to create a legacy for Bird Jaguar III where none apparently existed lead Sharer & Traxler to suspect a setback during his reign.
- **Peak under Itzamnaaj Balam II (681–742):** Longevity plus aggression. Reign ~61 years (accession 681, death mid-742); Proskouriakoff’s birth estimate ~647 would make him >90 at death. Almost all buildings and monuments date to the final *k’atun* of the reign; Temple 44 is a late “war memorial.” Piedras Negras records capture of a Yaxchilan *sajal* in 726; Sharer & Traxler suggest a possible boost from Usumacinta trade after a Piedras Negras dynastic shift in 729. Temple 23 (*yotoot* of Lady K’ab’al Xook) carries the famous bloodletting lintels: Lintel 24 shows Itzamnaaj Balam II with a burning torch over kneeling Lady K’ab’al Xook drawing blood with a thorned cord through her tongue—canonical evidence for elite women’s blood sacrifice in royal ritual.
- **Interregnum 742–752 and Bird Jaguar IV:** Ten-year hiatus in the official king list before Bird Jaguar IV. He was not son of principal wife Lady K’ab’al Xook or secondary wife Lady Sak B’iyaan; Stela 10 names his mother Lady Ik’ Skull of [[calakmul]]. Piedras Negras Panel 3’s visitor Yopaat Balam II (749) may be the heir/son of Lady K’ab’al Xook backed by Piedras Negras; no Yaxchilan record survives—if he ruled, Bird Jaguar IV usurped and erased him. Bird Jaguar IV used warfare, local marriage, and an unmatched monumental propaganda program to establish legitimacy after the hiatus (Sharer & Traxler call him, more than any other Maya king, a recognized propagandist).
- **Conquest of Piedras Negras and terminal monuments:** Yaxchilan—though smaller—finally conquered and seemingly destroyed Piedras Negras after a long conflict series. Lintel 10 (808), the latest-known Yaxchilan monument, identifies Piedras Negras Ruler 7 as the *b’aak* (captive) of K’inich Tatb’u Skull III of Yaxchilan. Thereafter riverine decline scenarios (trade realignment, coastal bypass of inland Usumacinta routes) place Yaxchilan among southern capitals that lost economic position by the Terminal Classic.

## Significance

Yaxchilan anchors Usumacinta political history, the epigraphic method (Proskouriakoff after Piedras Negras), and royal bloodletting iconography. It exemplifies Sharer & Traxler’s dictum that size ≠ power: a smaller city defeated a larger neighbor. Dynastic continuity after king loss—when the [[kuhul-ajaw]] institution and legitimacy claims persist—is illustrated by Bird Jaguar IV’s post-hiatus accession (possibly by force). Granting of military authority to subordinates under Bird Jaguar IV is cited among factors that may have eroded royal monopoly on force in the Late Classic war escalation. Emblem-glyph “cleft sky” is a textbook case of Berlin’s site-naming discovery.

## Key Debates

- **Dynastic authenticity of Bird Jaguar III and IV programs:** How much of the Early Classic king list and Bird Jaguar III’s “legacy” monuments is retrospective fabrication vs recoverable history.
- **Yopaat Balam II:** Whether he ever ruled Yaxchilan (Piedras Negras Panel 3 view) and was erased by Bird Jaguar IV, or never held the throne.
- **Boundary with Piedras Negras:** Golden’s settlement patterning defines a well-maintained inter-kingdom boundary; subordinate-site differences are archaeological as well as epigraphic.
- **Trade-network realignment:** Position that Chontalpa/Gulf Coast seaborne routes bypassed inland Usumacinta capitals (Palenque, Piedras Negras, Yaxchilan, Quirigua, Copan), contributing to Terminal Classic decline.
- **Warfare and nonroyal military power:** Authors’ scenario that loss of royal monopoly on military authority (e.g. Bird Jaguar IV granting military roles) was a regional trigger for Late Classic institutional stress.

## Historiography

Yaxchilan’s modern fame rests on carved lintels and Proskouriakoff’s historical method more than on a single long excavation monograph; Sharer & Traxler explicitly note more complete archaeology at Piedras Negras. Because later kings (especially Bird Jaguar IV) are suspected of rewriting dynastic memory, Early Classic narrative must be treated as attributed, multi-sourced political history rather than neutral chronicle. Temple 23 bloodletting scenes became textbook images of [[classic-maya]] royal ritual and women’s ritual agency. Claims about capture of Piedras Negras Ruler 7 (808), the 742–752 interregnum, and size-vs-power comparisons are as of Sharer & Traxler 2006. ## Densification from Martin & Grube 2008

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube 2008]], Yaxchilan chapter. Complements Sharer & Traxler densification above; where king-numbering differs, the authors’ labels are retained in attributed voice.)*

### Setting, ancient name, and early sequence

Martin & Grube place Yaxchilan at the apex of a Usumacinta horseshoe bend, ceremonial core on a man-made river terrace with major structures on karst slopes behind. Anciently *Pa’ Chan* (“broken/split sky”); they note unusual **paired emblem glyphs**. Alfred Maudslay first saw the ruins in 1882. The fabric of early Yaxchilan is largely buried; dynastic knowledge rests on a mid-6th-century lintel king list and a much later hieroglyphic stairway (dates largely following Peter Mathews). Founder **Yopaat Bahlam I** is placed perhaps in 359; Early Classic king lists’ “visitors” are now read as **prisoners**—a rare Early Classic warfare ledger. Moon Skull (before 454) dedicated the “Four Bat Place”; wars with [[piedras-negras]] include capture of its Ruler A and seizure of a *yajaw te’* of Ruler B (~478). **Knot-eye Jaguar I** (Stela 27 at 9.4.0.0.0 / 514) captured nobles from Bonampak, Piedras Negras, and even Tikal (508), yet appears bound on Piedras Negras Panel 12 before 518—authors suggest he survived as a vassal. **K’inich Tatbu Skull II** (526) commissioned the Early Classic king-list lintels and dated captures including a 537 success against the Snake kingdom. A major knowledge gap follows **537–629**. **Bird Jaguar III** (accession calculable ~629) is known almost entirely from retrospective “recreated” monuments; Mary Miller’s position that Yaxchilan’s missing history reflects lengthy subjugation to Piedras Negras is recorded by the authors, who also flag possible [[palenque]] and [[tonina]] influence along the Usumacinta during the vacant years.

### Itzamnaaj Bahlam III (Shield Jaguar the Great) and Lady K’abal Xook

Martin & Grube call the 681–742 king **Itzamnaaj Bahlam III** (Shield Jaguar the Great)—the dominant builder of the city visible today, with his son Bird Jaguar IV. Accession October 681; ~60-year reign; magnificent buildings and monuments largely confined to the final third, little before 723 when he was said to be in his seventies. They link florescence to military-political context: [[piedras-negras]] Stela 8 records capture of one of his *sajal* in 726; authors infer newfound autonomy and downriver traffic revenue after retreat of foreign hegemonies. **Temple 44** (dedicated 732 or shortly after) is his famed war memorial—captures from 681–732, including pre-accession Aj ‘Nik’; the 726 Piedras Negras reverse is omitted but he does not falsely claim victory. Realm along the Usumacinta reached El Chicozapote (near Piedras Negras satellite El Cayo); periodic domination of Lacanha and [[bonampak]]; that area under Tonina overlordship ~715, then Sak Tz’i’ by 726.

Principal queen **Lady K’abal Xook** received extraordinary status. **Temple 23**, dedicated 726, was the first of his major works—ahead of Temple 44—so priorities were ritual renewal and bloodletting/trance communion with tutelary gods, associated with overt Teotihuacan imagery (half-skeletal Mexican-style serpent on Lintel 25). Martin & Grube rank the three lintels among the finest Maya stone carving: **Lintel 24** (709) thorned-cord tongue bloodletting under the king’s torch; **Lintel 25** accession vision (681)—warrior as Teotihuacan Storm God / manifestation of the king as “flint and shield” of Aj K’ahk’ O’ Chaak; **Lintel 26** (724) jaguar helmet. Lintels 24–25 in the British Museum; 26 in Mexico City. García Moll’s Temple 23 floor burials: Tomb 2 likely the king (stingray spines/awls naming both); Tomb 3 a mature woman—possibly the queen, but Temple 24 records her death in 749 and fire ritual at her *muknal*, probably her memorial shrine. Third wife **Lady Ik’ Skull** (Lady Eveningstar), *ix ajaw* from [[calakmul]], not mentioned in his lifetime. He died June 742; subsequent events suggest contested inheritance.

### Interregnum 742–752, Bird Jaguar IV, Bonampak, last dates

Ten years elapsed before **Bird Jaguar IV**’s 752 inauguration—the famed interregnum for which [[proskouriakoff-tatiana|Proskouriakoff]] proposed a struggle so intense no candidate could take the throne. Lady K’abal Xook survived six years. [[piedras-negras]] **Panel 3** names a “holy lord” of Yaxchilan witnessing Ruler 4’s 749 k’atun-anniversary—authors treat him as **Yopaat Bahlam II**, who may have ruled all or part of the interregnum under Piedras Negras ascendancy; no Yaxchilan record survives (expunged if he reigned). On text reliability the authors argue inscriptions are archaeologically robust where testable (e.g. [[copan]]), mutual exclusive victory claims are rare, yet rhetorical purpose and multi-site control remain essential.

Bird Jaguar IV, already 43, son of lesser queen Lady Ik’ Skull, installed himself on round date 9.16.1.0.0 (752). Martin & Grube call him more than any other Maya king a manipulator and propagandist: Stela 11 flap-staff dance with father (741); mother’s bloodletting on her own Stela 35 (replicating Lady K’abal Xook); reset Early Classic king-list lintels; 480-glyph Hieroglyphic Stairway 1 placing himself at the dynasty’s conclusion; repair of early stelae and “legacy” for Bird Jaguar III—some overwriting of earlier texts that may belong to disappeared interregnum kings. Warrior titles (“He of 20 Captives”) made immense capital of mostly lowly victims; last known capture 759 of T’ul Chik, a Piedras Negras *k’inich ajaw*. Major plaza levelling; ≥12 buildings; Temple 21 replicates Temple 23’s programme—quantity over quality. Local **Lady Great Skull** bore successor Itzamnaaj Bahlam IV; foreign wives (Motul de San José) enjoyed prestige. Last date with him June 768 (Lintel 9); Great Skull may have regented the young heir. Temple 33 step blocks show father–grandfather–self ballgame with captives as balls and the only surviving description of the *ox ahal* (“Three Victories”) ballgame myth, naming arenas as far as [[tonina]] and [[copan]].

**Itzamnaaj Bahlam IV** (Chel Te’ Chan K’inich) in power by 769; Hieroglyphic Stairway 5 lists captures to 800. Control of [[bonampak]]–Lacanha strong; a Yaxchilan royal lady (probably his sister) married mural patron Yajaw Chan Muwaan; Bonampak murals—finest Precolumbian mural narrative—involve him (central lintel image; he oversees Bonampak accession 790; joint 787 campaign vs Sak Tz’i’). Shortly after 800, **K’inich Tatbu Skull IV** left one crude Lintel in Temple 3 (808 *wayibil* of patrons)—last recorded star-war and capture events of the Classic; final passage names Piedras Negras **Ruler 7** as his *baak*. Large-scale occupation ceased soon after; later Lacandon pilgrimage with incense-burners in standing buildings.

### Lady Xoc, Temple 23, and Shield-Jaguar’s compromise

Schele & Freidel make **Temple 23** (Lady **Xoc** / later K’abal Xook) the political pivot of Late Classic Yaxchilan under **Shield-Jaguar** (Itzamnaaj Bahlam / Itzamnaaj Bahlam III “the Great”). Three lintels:

| Lintel | Scene (as of S&F 1990) |
|---|---|
| Center (accession vision) | Sole pictorial protagonist is Lady Xoc; her bloodletting materializes founder **Yat-Balam** to sanction Shield-Jaguar’s accession—he is named but not portrayed; no other pictorial accession of his is known |
| Lintel 24 (left) | Lady Xoc pulls thorn-laden rope through tongue; Shield-Jaguar holds torch; ~28 years after accession scene; timed to Jupiter–Saturn hierophany (28 Oct **709**), 62 days after a son’s birth (24 Aug 709) |
| Right door | Lady Xoc (mouth seeping blood) helps Shield-Jaguar dress for war—cotton armor, flint knife; she holds flexible shield and jaguar helmet; captives for dedication ~724 or 726 |

Depicting a woman as principal ritual actor is unprecedented at Yaxchilan and almost unknown in Maya monumental art. Authors claim no other representation shows a thorn-lined rope in the tongue wound—extraordinary severity. The child of the 709 birth/hierophany was born to **Lady Eveningstar** (Lady Ik’ Skull; “Lady Ahau of Calakmul” on Stela 10), not Lady Xoc—who was at least middle-aged. Yet Shield-Jaguar never mentioned Lady Eveningstar on his own monuments; late monuments aimed to secure support for her son **Bird-Jaguar**.

**Lintel 23** reconstructs Lady Xoc’s kinship: daughter of Shield-Jaguar’s mother’s father’s sister—maternal first cousin of his mother, his maternal first cousin once removed; her father’s powerful patriline is suppressed in the public record in favor of her mother’s people already allied to the royal house. **POSITION:** Temple 23 (begun ~723 when Bird-Jaguar was ~13) was Shield-Jaguar’s grand compromise—honor Lady Xoc and local alliances while building support for the foreign (Calakmul) heir. Of three Temple 23 events, authors argue the hierophany bloodletting was the focus of Shield-Jaguar’s political message; Bird-Jaguar later centered legitimacy strategy around this building and event.

### Late Shield-Jaguar, flapstaff, and 9.15.10.0.1 bloodletting

Shield-Jaguar fathered Bird-Jaguar at age 61; in his eighties celebrated Temple 44 victories; at 84 still took a captive. **Flapstaff** ritual (human-high staff with T-shaped flapped cloth, usually after summer solstice): first display **27 June 736** (age 88) on Stela 16 before Temple 41; father–son joint flapstaff **26 June 741** on Stela 11 (erected retrospectively by Bird-Jaguar) as heir legitimization. Five days after 9.15.10.0.0 (**1 July 741**), a multiple bloodletting recorded three times, pivoting thematically around Temple 23: Lintel 14 (Temple 20)—**Lady Great-Skull-Zero** and brother **Lord Great-Skull-Zero** (cahal lineage) as bloodletters; Temple 21 stela—Lady Eveningstar bloodletting equating her with Lady Xoc; Lintel 39—Bird-Jaguar himself as “fish-in-hand” vision actor. *(Multi-page dawn vignette inventing monologues and choreography is NARRATIVE DEVICE; underlying ritual elements restated from monuments.)* Shield-Jaguar dies **19 June 742**; Bird-Jaguar at 32 begins campaign for office.

### Ten-year interregnum and Bird-Jaguar’s accession campaign

**ATTRIBUTED/POSITION:** Circumstances kept the throne empty ten years though a competent legitimate heir (Bird-Jaguar) was available; no other accessions appear—“winner’s version of history.” Authors surmise he needed those years to defeat rivals (possibly sons/grandsons of Lady Xoc). Two political problems after delay: (1) public recognition of mother’s status and equality with Lady Xoc; (2) forging alliances among noble *cahal* families to force accession—via temples/lintels and marriage into allies’ lineages.

Interregnum timeline (as of S&F 1990): ballgame **21 Oct 744** on Structure 33 steps (captive bound as ball; flanking Shield-Jaguar and grandfather 6-Tun-Bird-Jaguar; cahalob playing); period ending 9.15.15.0.0 (**4 June 746**) credited to dead Shield-Jaguar *u cab* “in the land of” Bird-Jaguar (de facto kingship reading); solo flapstaff **25 June 747**; Lady Xoc dies **3 April 749**; Stela 11 Chac-Xib-Chac sacrifice scene **4 June 750** with dual accession formulas (*hok’ah ti ahauel* / *chumwan ti ahauel*); Lady Eveningstar dies **13 March 751**. Remaining barriers: prestigious captive and male heir. Capture of **Yax-Cib-Tok** (**10 Feb 752**)—a *cahal* of an unidentified king—and birth of **Chel-Te-Chan-Mah-Kina** to Lady Great-Skull-Zero (**18 Feb 752**); crowned **75 days later** (3 May 752). Capture recorded at Temple 41 (father’s war locus) and Lintel 16 (Temple 21). Temple 21 Lintel 17: foreign wife **Lady Balam of Ix Witz** bloodletting parallels Lady Xoc acknowledging co-wife’s son. Only surviving accession picture: Temple 33 Lintel 1—Bird-Jaguar with GII scepter; Lady Great-Skull-Zero holds bundle, will let blood as Lady Xoc did for Shield-Jaguar.

### Architecture as political media; *cahalob* on the public stage

First-phase building program rewrites the legitimacy landscape around Temple 23: Temple 22 (dedicated **12 May 752**, nine days after accession) resets Early Classic lintels including Chan-Ah-Tz’i dedication by Moon-Skull (454)—evidence of pre-accession power; Temple 12 resets 1st–10th kings and Ta-Skull accession 526; Temple 24 records deaths of grandmother, Shield-Jaguar, Lady Xoc, Lady Eveningstar; Temples 20–21 replicate and extend Lady Xoc’s program; Structure 13 frames Lady Eveningstar’s bundle rite one day after Lintel 24—“retrospective history at its best.” Second phase (Temples 33, 1, 42): dual-location repetition of key scenes so multiple nobles gain prestige of permanent public history with the king.

**Cahalob** (subordinate nobles; phonetic reading disputed even in 1990 notes—convenient label): traditionally rarely depicted on the same public monuments as the high king; before Bird-Jaguar, public stelae/lintels normally showed only kings and occasionally family. Bird-Jaguar is cast as first to exalt *cahalob* beside him publicly. Paradigmatic co-performer **Kan-Toc**: dual lintels 40 days after accession (Lintel 5 foreign wife vs Lintel 42 Kan-Toc with battle ax); capture of **Jeweled-Skull** (most famous of Bird-Jaguar’s lifetime) shows near-equal compositional space with Kan-Toc. **POSITION:** he “sold” royal prerogatives for *cahal* loyalty—“dangerous and eventually debilitating” precedents; alliance fragile, had to be re-performed continually.

Heir strategy deliberately differs from father’s foreign-wife problem: heir Chel-Te from internal *cahal* wife Lady Great-Skull-Zero so the son would not face the internal-lineage opposition that kept Bird-Jaguar off the throne. No formal heir-designation rite (unlike Palenque Cross Group or Bonampak murals)—instead repeated co-performance of heir with important *cahalob* (Temple 54 maternal-uncle *yichan* scenes; fifth-anniversary bird-scepter with Chel-Te on Temple 33, 7 April 757; Temple 55 father–son Manikin Scepters 766). Subordinate-town *cahal* **Tilot of La Pasadita**: lintels with king, captive, and heir. Final monument Lintel 9 (**20 June 768**): Great-Skull-Zero conducts flapstaff with Bird-Jaguar, named *yichan ahau* (“brother of the mother of the heir”)—strategy worked; Chel-Te took the throne as namesake Shield-Jaguar.

### Piedras Negras diplomacy and structural risk

During interregnum, Piedras Negras Lintel 3 (retrospective) records “Jaguar of Yaxchilan” witnessing Ruler 4’s 20-tun anniversary **31 July 749**—authors most likely identify Bird-Jaguar as de facto king. Named appearance **20 Oct 757**: Bird-Jaguar conducts bundle rite for Piedras Negras heir designation (Ruler 5); never recorded at Yaxchilan—reciprocal heir-support reading. **POSITION:** succession problems not novel (Pacal/Chan-Bahlum also struggled), but sharing the public monumental forum with political allies was new in Classic history and spread on the Usumacinta; primogeniture fails when ambitious offspring of multiple marriages compete; *ahauob* and *cahalob* are ranked elite tiers whose clustering around rival royal branches is visible court politics.
