## Discovery and Excavation History

Khok Phanom Di is a mound site of roughly 5 hectares, now about 22 km inland from the
Gulf of Siam but situated on a former estuarine/mangrove/tidal-flat coastline at the
time of occupation. It was excavated over a seven-month season in 1985 by a team that
included Charles Higham (Higham et al. 1987). The excavation opened a 10×10 m square
under a steel roof — a departure from the narrow trenches typical of earlier regional
excavation — specifically to recover complete burials and their spatial patterning
rather than isolated finds. Natural soil was reached at a depth of 6.8 m (Higham 1989).

A supporting pollen-core sequence from the surrounding area produced eight radiocarbon
dates suggesting the earliest acceptable evidence of human settlement along the ancient
shoreline at roughly 4710–3960 BCE, inferred from a charcoal spike and a rise in grass
and rice-field-weed pollen in one spectrum. Higham (1989) infers that earlier,
ancestral coastal settlements probably exist nearby, now buried under marine clay.
Khok Phanom Di itself, based on 18 available radiocarbon dates, was occupied roughly
2000–1400 BCE, a cultural and pollen-core sequence spanning about 3,300 years overall
(as of Higham 1989).

## Occupation Sequence

Excavation revealed three major stratigraphic zones (Higham 1989):

- **Zone A** (earliest, roughly 3 m thick): complex lensed ash, occupation debris,
  shell midden, and red-soil deposits with numerous hearths and postholes, containing
  104 inhumation burials. The earliest six burials (3 adults, 1 child, 2 infants) were
  shallow, largely without grave goods (one exception, a shell bead), heads oriented
  east; infant grave 151 was covered in red ochre and wrapped in an unwoven bark-cloth
  fabric, a treatment that became standard in later Zone A burials. The remaining 98
  Zone A burials were set in rows and superimposed clusters, with a strong predominance
  of infant burials (52 infants, 5 children, 30 adults). No adults in Zone A were
  buried with bone bracelets, unlike infants; the two richest adult graves (burials 72
  and 91) held multiple pots, burnishing stones, and shell beads.
- **Zone A→B transition**: a major stratigraphic break with a 40–50 cm hiatus in
  burials. The infant-burial proportion fell from about 55% in Zone A to about 30% in
  Zone B, and grave goods expanded to include clay anvils, large shell bracelets, shell
  discs, shell ear ornaments, fish hooks, and turtle-carapace "breastplates." East
  orientation persisted.
- **Zone B**: the earlier undifferentiated occupation-area burial pattern gave way to a
  dedicated cemetery with a sharp wealth dichotomy. Four outstandingly rich graves (a
  woman and three infants) appear; two women with moderately rich goods were interred
  beneath a raised rectangular structure with clay-wall foundations, itself later cut
  through by another burial. A row of burials with surrounding postholes indicates an
  above-ground mortuary structure in front of this platform, and shell middens and
  ash/charcoal spreads abutting the graves suggest funerary feasting. Only men were
  accompanied by turtle-shell breastplates, while some of the richest and most
  elaborate burials belonged to women. Common ritual elements across Zones A and B were
  east orientation, red ochre, and pots/shell beads as offerings. The single richest
  interment — Burial 15, an adult woman in her late thirties, the volume's frontispiece —
  lay in a large grave beneath a pyramid of clay potting cylinders topped with pots, with
  roughly 120,000 shell-disc beads, large I-shaped bead necklaces, two horned shell discs,
  a shell bracelet, and a potter's clay anvil with burnishing pebbles at her feet; an
  adjacent contemporary infant grave was nearly as richly furnished, including a miniature
  potter's anvil. The concentration of potting equipment in the richest graves ties status
  at the site to skilled ceramic production.
- **Zone C** (uppermost, roughly 1 m, darker soil, rich in pottery, animal bone, and
  pottery-making tools): contained no burials. The final Zone B burial was cut just
  below the stratigraphic shift into Zone C; marine shell and crab remains cease at
  this point, mammalian fauna becomes common, stone bracelets are favored, pottery
  continues to be made locally, stone adzes remain common, and there is no evidence of
  metallurgy at this stage. Higham (1989) correlates this shift with a lowering of sea
  level and an accompanying change in subsistence and exchange.

Material culture was fully developed from the earliest levels: pottery was made
on-site throughout occupation (clay anvils, burnishing stones, and clay
cylinders/pre-forms occur from the basal level, with bonfire firing attested by ash
spreads); cord-marked wares dominate the Zone A occupation layers, but most grave-good
pottery is lustrous black burnished ware with incised decoration, with no apparent
developmental "learning stage." Fishing gear (clay net weights, harpoons, fish hooks)
is present from the earliest contexts, and local shell-jewelry manufacture (drilled
disc beads) is attested by shell debris and grave goods. Stone adzes are present from
earliest occupation — a cache of ten in the earliest level — sourced from multiple
exotic stone types, evidencing participation in an exchange network; many are worn from
repeated resharpening.

Rice is represented by husk fragments in occupation layers and by chaff impressions and
chaff temper in pottery clay (Zones A and B). Higham (1989) states explicitly that "it
is not known whether the rice comes from a wild or domestic plant, or perhaps from
both" — the wild/domestic status remains an unresolved position, not a settled finding.
A larger rice sample was, as of 1989, still under microscopic analysis (overseen by
Thompson) with no report yet available. The Zone A/B faunal assemblage includes small
amounts of pig and dog bone within an overwhelmingly aquatic assemblage; the domestic or
wild status of these animals at Khok Phanom Di specifically is not resolved in the
available material.

## Significance

Khok Phanom Di is central to Higham's (1989) argument, developed across *The
Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia*, that the site evidences "domestication…not
necessarily of plants or animals, but rather of people" (attributed) — that is,
sedentism itself (permanent co-residence, house and ritual structures, ancestral
continuity across several centuries of deposit) as the primary process under study,
independent of whether plant or animal domestication can be confirmed. Higham states it
is "immaterial" to this argument whether the rice was cultivated or whether animal
behavior was being modified toward herd maintenance (attributed). In support, Higham
cites Oka and Morishima (1971) (attributed) that regular harvesting of wild rice alone
modifies seeding rate and spikelet number within five generations — a caution against
inferring domestication from harvesting behavior or morphology alone. He also notes,
**as an explicit ethnographic analogy rather than direct evidence**, that historically
documented Tonle Sap peoples harvested wild floating rice by boat (Delvert 1961); this
comparison is flagged here as an analogy used to caution against assuming harvesting
implies domestication, not as evidence about Khok Phanom Di itself.

Higham (1989, attributed) applies Price and Brown's (1985) criteria for hunter-gatherer
"complexity" — dense co-residing population, large settlement size, permanent houses,
differential wealth evidence, energy expended in burial ritual with patterned interment
location, and permanent single-site occupation — to classify Khok Phanom Di as a
[[complex-hunter-gatherers|complex hunter-gatherer]] community, while stating a
preference for interacting cultural/environmental variables over single "prime mover"
explanations such as population pressure. He also applies Yesner's (1980) model for
maritime hunter-gatherers (attributed): high biomass and resource predictability,
scarcity of optimal coastal enclaves producing high territoriality, reduced constraints
on population growth (both young and old can collect shellfish), high population
density, and a need for plant supplements to offset a low-vitamin marine diet.

Elsewhere in the same volume, Higham (1989, attributed) frames the later, rich Zone B
burials as evidence of a ranked social system sustained by coastal resource wealth
rather than agricultural surplus, contrasting sharply with mobile interior foraging
bands, and notes — as a tentative, non-causal observation — that the orientation of
Khok Phanom Di's raised mortuary platform, built roughly 3,500 years ago, resembles that
of the much later sacred precincts of Isanapura. This forms part of Higham's broader
synthetic thesis that mainland Southeast Asian prehistory and early history show
underlying continuity in competitive status display — from Khok Phanom Di-era ritual
and exchange through to Angkorian god-kingship — with rice intensification framed as a
later consequence rather than an early driver of complexity; Higham states that the
first major intensification of agricultural methods probably came later still, with the
formation of centralized [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]] around 500–1 BCE, explicitly outside the
Khok Phanom Di period.

## Key Debates

The wild-versus-domestic status of Khok Phanom Di's rice is explicitly unresolved in
Higham (1989) — a position, not a finding — and stands in contrast to the flotation-
recovered grains at [[ban-na-di]], which specialists (Chang and Loresto 1984) judged
cultivated. Higham's fission/circumscription/ranking model — drawing on Johnson's
(1982) concept of "scalar stress" (attributed) — proposes that sedentary population
growth at Khok Phanom Di triggered scalar stress, to which the initial response was
fission: emigrant groups colonizing marginal inland tributary-valley locations such as
[[ban-chiang]], Non Kao Noi, and [[non-nok-tha]]. Once coastal enclaves and marginal
hinterland were filled, Higham proposes options narrowed toward increased ranking and
ritual, with increasing wealth-goods disparity, differential burial investment,
restricted valuables distribution, and habitat modification (marsh clearance
encouraging rice) offered as testable correlates. This entire model is an attributed
interpretive framework, not a wiki-voice conclusion.

## Historiography

Higham's (1989) reading of Khok Phanom Di is explicitly processual in orientation,
applying externally sourced complexity criteria (Price and Brown 1985) and ecological
models (Yesner 1980) to classify and explain the site's social organization, and
drawing on Johnson's (1982) scalar-stress framework and Dalton's (1977) work on
"primitive valuables" in ethnographically documented societies as an interpretive lens
for wealth disparity — all attributed frameworks Higham applies to prehistoric data,
not evidenced facts about Khok Phanom Di itself. The excavation methodology (open
10×10 m area under a steel roof, aimed at recovering complete burial populations and
spatial patterning) represents a methodological advance over earlier narrow-trench
excavation in the region and improves the reliability of the mortuary and stratigraphic
record relative to older regional excavations. As of Higham (1989), rice-remains
analysis was still in progress (Thompson), leaving the wild/domestic question open; no
later reassessment is captured in this ingest.
