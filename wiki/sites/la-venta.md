## Discovery and Excavation History

After the decline of [[sites/san-lorenzo]], Olmec political power passed to La Venta, an island site in a coastal swamp in Tabasco near the Tonalá River, roughly 18 miles (29 km) inland, comprising about 2 square miles (5 sq km) of dry land with a main complex stretching 1.2 miles (1.9 km) north-south (Coe & Koontz 2013). It was first excavated by Matthew Stirling for the Smithsonian Institution, with subsequent work by the University of California; the site is now largely destroyed by petroleum operations. Rebecca González-Lauck's later work on the south side of the Great Pyramid — a 110 ft (34 m) clay structure once thought to imitate a volcano — showed it was in fact a rectangular pyramid with stepped sides and inset corners.

## Occupation Sequence

Radiocarbon dates (calibrated) place La Venta in the Middle Preclassic, roughly 1200–400 BCE, contemporary in part with the terminal phases of San Lorenzo (Coe & Koontz 2013). William Rust's survey and excavation documented dense occupation of the surrounding river-levee zone from 1750 BCE, with incipient agriculture in the region possibly dating to the fifth millennium BCE; his survey of the island itself showed a substantial town rather than an empty ceremonial precinct.

La Venta was deliberately destroyed around 400–300 BCE, at the end of the Middle Preclassic: 24 of its 40 sculptured monuments were intentionally mutilated. The site nonetheless retained cult significance afterward — Late Preclassic pottery offerings continued to be deposited, and even a Spanish olive jar from the early Colonial period was found in the latest cache (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Significance

La Venta yielded four colossal heads. Stela 3 (the "Uncle Sam" stela) depicts two elaborately garbed men, one with an aquiline nose and goatee, with were-jaguar figures overhead; Altar 5 shows a central figure holding a jaguar-baby with four subsidiary figures each holding were-jaguar infants. Three rectangular serpentine mosaic pavements, roughly 15 by 20 ft (4.5 by 6 m) and composed of about 485 blocks each, form abstract jaguar-mask designs and were later deliberately covered with clay or adobe as offerings. A group of six jade/serpentine celts accompanied sixteen standing serpentine and jade figurines arranged as a tableau, along with jade ear flares and pendants and concave mirrors of magnetite or ilmenite polished to optical precision — experiments show these mirrors can start fires and project images "like a camera lucida" (Coe & Koontz 2013).

The Mound A-2 tomb, roofed and surrounded by basalt columns with a limestone floor, contained the remains of two juveniles wrapped and coated in vermilion along with jade offerings; a nearby sandstone "sarcophagus" held only clay fill. Coe & Koontz offer, in attributed voice, the speculation that "the children or infants in the tomb were monstrosities who to the Olmecs may have resembled were-jaguars."

Robert Heizer calculated that La Venta's elite center required a hinterland population of at least 18,000, with construction of the main pyramid alone requiring roughly 800,000 person-days. Heizer and Philip Drucker's hypothesis that the nearest arable land, between the Coatzacoalcos and Tonalá rivers, supported the site is contested by Coe & Koontz as "relatively poor and eroded, making the proposition unlikely"; they instead favor rich river-levee agriculture on the San Lorenzo model — a live disagreement over the site's subsistence base, recorded here as a position rather than adjudicated.

Nearby, the Chiapa de Corzo royal tomb (in the Grijalva Depression, excavated 2010 by Bruce Buchand and Lynneth Lowe) contained a middle-aged male accompanied by two human sacrifices, pyrite and mother-of-pearl tooth inlays, over 1,000 jade beads matching La Venta's style, and hematite-powder coating; Coe & Koontz suggest, in attributed voice, that "in all likelihood this potentate was a satrap of La Venta," evidence for the settlement's regional political reach.

## Key Debates

La Venta's status as the Olmec heartland's central authority feeds the [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]]: Coe & Koontz hold that "there is now little doubt that all later civilizations in Mesoamerica, whether Mexican or Maya, ultimately rest on an Olmec base," a position contested by revisionists who point to sites such as Teopantecuanitlan in Guerrero as evidence against Gulf Coast primacy.
