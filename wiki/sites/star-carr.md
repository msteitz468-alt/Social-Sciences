# Star Carr

Early Mesolithic lakeside settlement in the Vale of Pickering, Yorkshire (England). In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is “where the Mesolithic effectively began”—one of the earliest-known Mesolithic settlements, the first European site of any period to be radiocarbon-dated, and the excavation that shifted British Mesolithic studies from tool catalogues to lifestyle and environment.

**Identity caution:** Early Mesolithic cultural similarity across northern Europe (Mithen’s “virtual identity” at the time of Star Carr) is a material-culture observation, not a single people or language.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: **Grahame Clark** excavated 1949–51. Waterlogged preservation of bone, antler, and wood was unmatched by any other British Mesolithic site then known. Clark had pioneered British Mesolithic tool classification in the 1930s but only with Star Carr turned fully to lifestyle and environment—catching up with Danish shell-midden archaeology (køkkenmøddinger studied since the 1850s). Finds included deer-skull “masks,” barbed antler points of varied design, and a wooden paddle (no canoe recovered).

**Reanalyses:** Clark took abundant antler as winter occupation; **Legge and Rowley-Conwy (1985)** tooth-eruption study indicated most deer killed May–June (early summer). **Petra Dark**’s peat microstratigraphy documented ~80 years of annual lakeside reed clearance after ~9000 BCE, a gap, then return ~8750 BCE for another century.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Mithen 2003, ch.15; Clark; Dark; Legge & Rowley-Conwy.)*

- **Summer base** (revised seasonality): hunting red deer plus wild boar, roe deer, elk, aurochs; plant gathering, fowling, likely canoe fishing; antler cached for barbed points.
- **Landscape management:** annual burning of dense lakeshore reeds to improve access, view, and browse for deer—microstratigraphic sequence of clearance, hiatus, and return.
- **Material culture:** barbed points; deer masks interpretable as hunting disguise rather than (or as well as) dance costumes; wooden paddle.
- **Regional setting:** post-Younger Dryas woodland succession (birch, willow, pine, then oak, elm, lime, alder); red deer, roe deer, and wild boar as preferred Mesolithic prey after megafaunal losses.
- Later Mithen chapters use Star Carr as a baseline of early Mesolithic cultural commonality before late Mesolithic regionalisation of styles.

## Significance

- Foundational site for British and European [[mesolithic]] research programs and for the introduction of [[radiocarbon-dating]] to European prehistory.
- Exemplar of waterlogged organic preservation enabling lifestyle reconstruction beyond lithic catalogues.
- Case for anthropogenic fire management of wetland edges in early Holocene forager economies.
- Historiographic hinge: Victorian periodization (Lubbock’s *Prehistoric Times* 1865 lacked a Mesolithic category; Steenstrup vs Worsaae on Danish middens) vs twentieth-century recognition of a distinct Mesolithic.
- [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) cites Star Carr as a pioneering "site economy" excavation integrating faunal, botanical, and [[pollen-analysis|pollen]] evidence — an origin point for [[environmental-archaeology|environmental archaeology]].

## Key Debates

- **Seasonality:** Clark’s winter base vs Legge & Rowley-Conwy early-summer kills—classic reanalysis episode. Cunliffe (2013) adds that annual movement patterns were more complex than simple spring dispersal / autumn return, especially early Mesolithic while vegetation was still adjusting (position).
- Function of deer-skull masks / frontlets: hunting disguise (Mithen and others) vs headdress for stalking **and/or** ritual propitiation and spiritual mediation at a preferred special place (Cunliffe 2013).
- How far Star Carr represents a widespread Maglemosian-type world vs a uniquely preserved lake-edge locale — Cunliffe notes several Flixton/Pickering lake sites, none comparable to Star Carr’s intensity (finding).
- Relation of early Mesolithic inland lake sites to lost coastal and [[doggerland]] landscapes (Colinda harpoon older than Star Carr types by ~2,000 years in Mithen’s account).
- **Freshwater fish “taboo” hypothesis:** absence of fish bones at Star Carr and Thatcham as possible dietary taboo for some communities (Cunliffe’s hint; not demonstrated in wiki voice).
- **Fire → biomass → proto-domestication chain:** burning and supplementary feed as first stage toward red-deer domestication (Cunliffe position; interpretive).

## Britain Begins densification (Cunliffe 2013)

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* 2013, Ch. 4; densifies without replacing Mithen/Clark reanalysis content above.)*

### Chronology and regional setting

On present evidence Cunliffe places the earliest British [[mesolithic]] sites in the **ninth millennium BC**: Thatcham (Kennet) from ~9000 BC; **Star Carr 8770–8460 BC**; Cramond near Edinburgh ~8500 BC; no evidence yet that Ireland (already an island after ~9000 BC separation in his sea-level narrative) was occupied at that time (attributed). Star Carr sits in the Vale of Pickering as Britain’s best-known Mesolithic site in his synthesis — originally excavated 1949–51, with waterlogged preservation.

Lake-core reading he reports: activity began ~**8770 BC** and continued ~**80 years**; after a **century of inactivity** the site was brought back into use and visited regularly for ~**130 years** until ~**8460 BC** (attributed). This two-phase structure aligns with the clearance–gap–return pattern already noted from Petra Dark’s peat microstratigraphy (Mithen synthesis above), with absolute spans as Cunliffe states them.

Star Carr is **one of several Mesolithic sites** around the former Flixton/Pickering lake; none compare with it, and all vary in location and intensity. For a good part of its duration, Cunliffe holds, Star Carr was the **habitual point of reference** to which people returned within a larger community land-use pattern (attributed).

### Platform, fauna, and material assemblage

- **Setting:** camp close to the edge of former Lake Pickering; birch brushwood platform laid near the lake edge for stability; reed swamp margin (attributed).
- **Prey:** red deer principal catch, then roe deer, elk, aurochs, and pig; bear, badger, fox, and wolf for pelts; stork and crane taken; **domesticated dog** present (attributed).
- **Freshwater fish absent** as at Thatcham — Cunliffe hints that eating freshwater fish may have been taboo for at least some communities (position, not finding).
- **Frontlets:** twenty-one thinned, perforated red-deer skull frontlets with antlers, possibly tied as headdresses for stalking (attributed report of interpretation).
- **Barbed points:** 101 in all (mostly red-deer antler), with on-site blank production — exceptional versus ones or twos on other sites — indicating a special human–red deer relationship (finding + interpretive).
- **Other gear and ornaments:** elk-antler mattocks; stone and amber beads; perforated bears’ teeth in unparalleled quantities (attributed).

### Fire management and mobility

Cunliffe reports ample evidence of **fire to clear vegetation** as prelude to both occupation phases and in upland pollen sequences. Burning can increase ungulate biomass up to tenfold; piles of feed could habituate red deer — which he treats as the first stage toward domestication (attributed position). Annual movement, he concludes, was more complex than simple spring dispersal and autumn return, especially while early Holocene vegetation was still adjusting; group size, strategies, and territories varied with over-exploitation, population pressure, and local hostility — yet land and sea remained productive enough for plentiful varied diet and leisure (position).

### Ritual and spiritual reading

Cunliffe goes further than pure economic reconstruction:

- Deposition of frontlets, barbed points, and carcasses in the lake may have been an act of respect / propitiation of the beast’s spirit (position).
- Elk-antler mattocks, beads, and perforated bear teeth in unparalleled quantities **strongly suggest** a significant spiritual role — a preferred special place for mediating with powers controlling nature (position).
- In a later passage he argues the evidence implies complex behaviour around a close relationship with **red deer (and probably elk)**, with possible placation of the animal’s spirit and ritual enactment at the waterside camp (position).
- He places this within a broader Mesolithic worldview claim: communities would not have drawn sharp distinctions between secular and religious, physical and spirit worlds (theoretical; see [[mesolithic]]).

These are **attributed interpretive theses**, not wiki-voice findings. They sit alongside the hunting-disguise reading of masks and the seasonality reanalyses already filed above.

### Place in the early Holocene map

Cunliffe embeds Star Carr within eastern/plain connectivity that once included [[doggerland]] and within a Britain where the earliest known Mesolithic also includes Thatcham and Cramond; western Atlantic colonization and Ireland’s later seventh-millennium start are separate corridors in his dual-route model (see doggerland and mesolithic pages).
