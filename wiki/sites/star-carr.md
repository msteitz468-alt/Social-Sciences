# Star Carr

Early Mesolithic lakeside settlement in the Vale of Pickering, Yorkshire (England). In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is “where the Mesolithic effectively began”—one of the earliest-known Mesolithic settlements, the first European site of any period to be radiocarbon-dated, and the excavation that shifted British Mesolithic studies from tool catalogues to lifestyle and environment.

**Identity caution:** Early Mesolithic cultural similarity across northern Europe (Mithen’s “virtual identity” at the time of Star Carr) is a material-culture observation, not a single people or language.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: **Grahame Clark** excavated 1949–51. Waterlogged preservation of bone, antler, and wood was unmatched by any other British Mesolithic site then known. Clark had pioneered British Mesolithic tool classification in the 1930s but only with Star Carr turned fully to lifestyle and environment—catching up with Danish shell-midden archaeology (køkkenmøddinger studied since the 1850s). Finds included deer-skull “masks,” barbed antler points of varied design, and a wooden paddle (no canoe recovered).

**Reanalyses:** Clark took abundant antler as winter occupation; **Legge and Rowley-Conwy (1985)** tooth-eruption study indicated most deer killed May–June (early summer). **Petra Dark**’s peat microstratigraphy documented ~80 years of annual lakeside reed clearance after ~9000 BCE, a gap, then return ~8750 BCE for another century.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Mithen 2003, ch.15; Clark; Dark; Legge & Rowley-Conwy.)*

- **Summer base** (revised seasonality): hunting red deer plus wild boar, roe deer, elk, aurochs; plant gathering, fowling, likely canoe fishing; antler cached for barbed points.
- **Landscape management:** annual burning of dense lakeshore reeds to improve access, view, and browse for deer—microstratigraphic sequence of clearance, hiatus, and return.
- **Material culture:** barbed points; deer masks interpretable as hunting disguise rather than (or as well as) dance costumes; wooden paddle.
- **Regional setting:** post-Younger Dryas woodland succession (birch, willow, pine, then oak, elm, lime, alder); red deer, roe deer, and wild boar as preferred Mesolithic prey after megafaunal losses.
- Later Mithen chapters use Star Carr as a baseline of early Mesolithic cultural commonality before late Mesolithic regionalisation of styles.

## Significance

- Foundational site for British and European [[mesolithic]] research programs and for the introduction of [[radiocarbon-dating]] to European prehistory.
- Exemplar of waterlogged organic preservation enabling lifestyle reconstruction beyond lithic catalogues.
- Case for anthropogenic fire management of wetland edges in early Holocene forager economies.
- Historiographic hinge: Victorian periodization (Lubbock’s *Prehistoric Times* 1865 lacked a Mesolithic category; Steenstrup vs Worsaae on Danish middens) vs twentieth-century recognition of a distinct Mesolithic.
- [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) cites Star Carr as a pioneering "site economy" excavation integrating faunal, botanical, and [[pollen-analysis|pollen]] evidence — an origin point for [[environmental-archaeology|environmental archaeology]].

## Key Debates

- **Seasonality:** Clark’s winter base vs Legge & Rowley-Conwy early-summer kills—classic reanalysis episode.
- Function of deer-skull masks (hunting disguise vs ritual costume).
- How far Star Carr represents a widespread Maglemosian-type world vs a uniquely preserved lake-edge locale.
- Relation of early Mesolithic inland lake sites to lost coastal and Doggerland landscapes (Colinda harpoon older than Star Carr types by ~2,000 years in Mithen’s account).
