# Cantona

Fortified Epiclassic center in the Oriental basin of northern Puebla, built atop a volcanic flow using mortarless cut-stone construction. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], as described by chief excavator Ángel García Cook, Cantona had a significant Classic-period occupation — unusual among Epiclassic centers besides [[sites/cholula]] — and controlled the Oyameles-Zaragoza obsidian source roughly 6.25 miles (10 km) away.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Excavation and survey by Ángel García Cook established that, around AD 600, the site was suddenly filled with walled walkways and controlled-access residential compounds, distinct from the Teotihuacan-style apartment compounds found elsewhere; the acropolis's sacred architecture had restricted access, and the whole site was fortified, including a moat at its most vulnerable point. Cantona was occupied as a fortified center until abandonment around AD 1000.

## Occupation Sequence

Cantona has 24 identified I-shaped masonry ball courts, 18 believed to have functioned simultaneously — one of the highest concentrations of ball courts known at a single Mesoamerican site (see [[concepts/mesoamerican-ballgame]]).

## Significance

Cantona illustrates the militarized, fortified character of several Epiclassic central Mexican polities following the decline of Teotihuacan, and its obsidian-source control situates it within the broader Epiclassic reorganization of exchange networks discussed at [[sites/teotihuacan]] and [[sites/xochicalco]].

## Key Debates

Cantona's chronology bears on the wider Epiclassic question of what replaced Teotihuacan
(see [[phenomena/societal-collapse]]): unlike most Epiclassic centers it had a substantial
*Classic*-period occupation contemporary with Teotihuacan, so whether it is best read as a
successor polity or a longstanding rival that outlasted the metropolis is unsettled (as of
[[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz 2013]], reporting García Cook). Its
extreme concentration of ball courts also feeds the debate over the
[[concepts/mesoamerican-ballgame|ballgame]]'s political and ritual functions.
