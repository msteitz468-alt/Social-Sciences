# Casas Grandes (Paquimé)

Casas Grandes (Paquimé), in Chihuahua near the New Mexico border, is dated to c.
1200–1475 per Charles Di Peso's excavations and was central to Post-Classic
Mesoamerica–Southwest interaction (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Charles Di Peso's excavations established the site's chronology and its combination of
Southwestern and Mesoamerican architectural traits.

## Occupation Sequence

By the Post-Classic, Southwest US complex cultures continued Mesoamerican contact begun
in the Epiclassic. Theobromine (a chocolate biomarker) found in ceramic vessels at
Pueblo Bonito, Chaco Canyon, evidences Mesoamerican chocolate reaching the Southwest by
the eleventh century; copper bells were imported from West Mexico to the Southwest
around the same time or slightly earlier, and tropical bird feathers (especially scarlet
macaw) likely moved north alongside chocolate, as turquoise moved south.

Casas Grandes combined Southwestern-style apartment houses with Mesoamerican
features — platform temple mounds, I-shaped ball courts, and Feathered Serpent cult
iconography — plus warehouses of Southwestern minerals, notably turquoise.

## Significance

Coe & Koontz infer that the regional elite consumed large quantities of turquoise
locally, while a substantial share moved south, feeding growing Tarascan and broader
Mesoamerican demand (see [[cultures/tarascan-kingdom]]). The site is a key node for
documenting the northern extent of Post-Classic Mesoamerican exchange networks.
