# Uruk

Type urban site of the [[uruk-culture|Uruk]] period (southern Mesopotamian alluvium; modern Warka). Primary teaching case in [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 8 for pristine [[state-formation]] and the [[urban-revolution]] in Southwest Asia. Mid-2000s textbook chronology and models only — specialist monographs supersede details.

## Discovery and Excavation History

W&O (2007) present Uruk as the classic locus of southern Mesopotamian urbanization rather than as an excavation-history monograph. They emphasize continuous temple architecture from ‘Ubaid into Uruk: a major temple at Uruk after ~4000 BCE sits on the same locus as an ‘Ubaid temple (religious continuity). The White Temple on a ~12 m platform (c. 3200–3100 BCE) continues the Eridu temple plan. Early administrative tablets and sealings from Uruk (c. 3400 BCE; ~1500 signs, language not securely Sumerian as of their account) anchor discussions of writing’s economic role. Detailed trench histories, expedition names, and post-2007 reanalyses require specialist literature.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 8; mid-2000s consensus narrative.)*

- **‘Ubaid precursor:** Southern alluvial farming (barley, vegetables/dates, cattle/sheep/goats) with small-scale irrigation; for most of ‘Ubaid, little clear differential access to wealth/power/prestige. Temples on platforms become ubiquitous; by late ‘Ubaid the style zone is wide and the potter’s wheel likely appears.
- **Uruk period (c. 4100–3000 BCE, with Jemdet Nasr):** Framed as the era of **primary state formation** in SW Asia, culminating in cities, warfare, writing, social hierarchies, and advanced arts/crafts — the “complete checklist of [[civilization]]” in textbook language (as of W&O 2007).
- **Demographic trajectory (Adams via W&O):** Ancient Uruk/Warka may have held ~10,000 people by ~3800 BCE with a loosely integrated hinterland; by ~3000 BCE rapid growth to ~50,000 behind substantial defensive walls, read as immigration or forced transfer as rural settlements were abandoned.
- **Material signatures of administration:** Ubiquitous **bevel-rimmed bowls** define ceramic phases (Johnson); interpretations include ration bowls for laborers (Nissen: capacities match textual daily rations), bread molds, or temple votives — mass mold production and possible temple/government commodity roles mark early steps toward intrusive state organization. Mid–late 4th millennium: many craft specialists and centralized pottery production; cylinder seals and tablets from the 5th/4th millennia onward.
- **Fortification and peer polities:** Toward the end of Uruk, almost all major settlements fortified; later texts (c. 2600 BCE) detail inter-city conflict. State emergence is framed as a **network** of polities (Nippur, Nineveh, Uruk regions — Wright & Johnson), not a single isolated case.
- **Early Dynastic Sumer (c. 3000–2350 BCE):** Roughly 13 usually autonomous city-states sharing one cultural tradition (ziggurats/platforms, potter’s wheel, wheeled carts, metal-working, sailboats, writing). Writing’s early role is largely economic (storage, water, land, ritual). The late Uruk “Standard Professions List” ranks officials and professions by power/prestige.
- **Imperial aftermath (context):** Sargon of Akkad c. 2350 BCE ends the multipolar Sumerian city-state pattern; Ur III, Babylon/Hammurabi, and later empires reorganize the fabric — beyond the core Uruk-site narrative but part of the textbook sequence.


### Adams settlement-survey layer (1981)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]]'s *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]* maps Uruk as the southern **primate** city of the Uruk–ED I system (study: [[adams-heartland-of-cities-study]]). As of Adams 1981:

- Early Dynastic I occupied area ~**400 ha**, order of **40,000–50,000** persons (using ~100 persons/ha; monumental/open-space and wall-density uncertainties noted).
- Around Uruk at ED I peak, ~**81%** of recorded settlement area in sites >10 ha — key figure in Adams's claim of the "world's first predominantly urbanized society."
- Rank-size: southern primacy throughout the Uruk period, more pronounced early; later growth of smaller towns relative to Uruk; combined N+S ED I plot understates late primacy when hinterland hierarchy collapsed into the center.
- Prior Warka survey (Adams & Nissen 1972): 466 sites reused in Heartland numbering for the SE sector.

All figures date-stamped to Adams 1981; specialist reanalysis may revise areas and subphases.

## Significance

- Type case for comparative [[complex-society]] / primary-civilization teaching: southern Mesopotamia as one of the classic [[pristine-state]] regions.
- Anchors debates on whether irrigation, population pressure, warfare, or multi-causal combinations drive urban takeoff. W&O (following Adams) argue specialization, monuments, settlement hierarchy, and architectural/mortuary stratification appear **before** significant irrigation expansion; complex integrated canals centuries after first complex societies — irrigation more result of urbanism than cause. Population increases generally **followed** rather than preceded core Urban Revolution processes (Adams via W&O).
- Algaze models southern ecological/trade advantage and Uruk expansion as lowland core incorporating resource-rich peripheries (styles into W. Iran, Syria, even Nile Delta); Pollock critiques models that treat Uruk peoples as passive, urging internal daily-life conflict and agency (as of W&O 2007).
- Writing, ration systems, and craft specialization at Uruk feed [[craft-specialization]] and [[urban-revolution]] comparative checklists.

## Key Debates

- **Irrigation / Wittfogel vs multi-causal sequences** — W&O deny irrigation as primary cause for SW Asian complexity (see above).
- **Algaze world-systems expansion vs Pollock agency-focused critiques.**
- **Wright & Johnson three-level administration** as operational definition of the state; rank-size plots as empirical pattern without strong theory (Johnson via W&O).
- **Adams:** urbanization imposed by a small politically conscious superstratum with military/economic motives; cities as adaptations to periodic shortages (storage/defense nodes, religious and/or coercive integration).
- **Ethnicity of “Sumerians”** — W&O treat language/origin puzzles as non-explanatory of achievements (“people are constants in the equations of cultural evolution”).
- Specialist chronology, Eanna precinct phasing, and post-2007 tablet/linguistic work not carried here; do not equate archaeological [[uruk-culture]] with a single people, language, or genetic population without attributed argument.

### Service (*Origins of the State*, 1975, attributed)

Elman Service (1975) read the Uruk-period urban explosion as evidence for secular-political rather than purely economic drivers of urbanism (see [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]] and the [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]]). Quoting Adams, he held that Uruk's growth was "explosively rapid" and accompanied by "profound structural changes" — massive fortifications, palaces, and political hierarchies displacing temple centrality — achieved through rural abandonment and the forcible relocation of villagers into the city, not organic accretion (as of Service 1975). He traced the mechanism back to the Ubaid-period temple, which functioned simultaneously as sanctuary, storehouse, and redistributive center, the same "governance by benefit" logic he identified cross-culturally. Service explicitly rejected Wittfogel-style hydraulic determinism and Childe's surplus-equals-exploitation account, following Frankfort's counter-argument that Mesopotamian farming required only seasonal labor. (Source: [[service-origins-state-1975]].)

### Adams (*The Evolution of Urban Society*, 1966, attributed)

In *[[adams-evolution-urban-society-1966|EUS]]*, Protoliterate Uruk is the **theocratic-polity** type-site for the Mesopotamian sequence: temple *en*, Eanna redistribution and accounting, writing under theocratic auspices, and a style/monumentality plateau equivalent to Classic [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] on Adams's homotaxial correlation ([[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]]). Late Protoliterate militarism and Early Dynastic palace/*lugal* developments mark the militaristic turn; Akkadian trade-and-tribute organization is the terminal conquest-state correlate of the [[aztec-empire|Aztec]] realm. Surplus is institutional gross deployable wealth concentrated at temple/palace centers (ration systems; composite multi-zone subsistence), not automatic rural efficiency. Mid-1960s synthesis — absolute dates and settlement figures superseded. See [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]].

*Source depth: textbook synthesis (as of [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]]) + Adams 1966 comparative layer.*

### Roux (*Ancient Iraq*, 1992, attributed)

[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] describes Uruk (biblical Erech, modern Warka) as
lying roughly midway between Baghdad and Basrah near Samawa, occupying some 400
hectares and occupied continuously from Ubaid to Parthian times; excavated by German
archaeologists intermittently since 1912. Uruk formed from the **merger of two
originally separate towns**, 800 m apart: **Kullaba**, cult center of the sky-god An
(Anu), and **E-Anna** ("House of Heaven"), the main seat of the love-goddess Inanna
(Ishtar). E-Anna's remains include a mud-brick **ziqqurat built by Ur-Nammu** (c.
2112–2095 BCE, Roux's dates) atop a Jemdat Nasr-period platform temple; German
excavators found at least seven adjacent or superimposed temples and cultic
installations from the second half of the Uruk period, and a 20-m excavation well
reached virgin soil, giving a full stratigraphic sequence founded in the Ubaid period.
Archaic E-Anna temples share Ubaid-period Eridu's architectural plan (buttressed
façade, long cella with flanking small rooms, doors on the long side), read by Roux as
evidence of continuity of architectural and cultic tradition. Prof. H. Lenzen
suggested that E-Anna's **paired temples** were dedicated to both Inanna and her
consort Dumuzi (attributed, named scholar). Among the notable structures Roux
describes: a lowermost-level temple on limestone foundations (87 × 33 m); the "mosaic
building," a courtyard with eight mud-brick columns faced with painted terracotta-cone
mosaic; the "Red Temple"; and the Anu ziggurat, where six successive temples were built
into a platform ~15 m high, capped by the well-preserved late-Uruk-period "White
Temple."

Writing **first appears (pictographic tablets) at the end of the Uruk period, c. 3300
BCE**, in the archaic temples of E-Anna — Roux's date for this event, distinct from but
compatible with the ~3400 BCE tablet/sealing dating given above from Wenke & Olszewski.
The **Warka Vase** and **Warka Mask** ("Lady of Uruk") — both Jemdat Nasr-period,
called unrivaled for their era — come from Uruk (see [[uruk-culture]] and
[[jemdat-nasr]]). By the Early Dynastic period Uruk had grown, per Roux, into a huge
metropolis of 400+ hectares and an estimated 40,000–50,000 inhabitants, broadly
consistent with the Adams settlement-survey figures above; Uruk's roughly 9.7-km-long
wall (still traceable at the Warka ruins) is popularly attributed to the (partly
legendary) king **Gilgamesh**, fifth ruler of the First Dynasty of Uruk per the
[[sumerian-king-list]] — see [[mesopotamian-religion]] for the Gilgamesh epic cycle and
[[sumerian-king-list]] for Roux's historicity judgment on the king himself ("today
there are good reasons to believe that a king of that name actually ruled over Uruk,
though definite proof is still lacking").

### Postgate (*Early Mesopotamia*, 1994, attributed)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] treats Uruk as the **archetype of
early Mesopotamian urbanism and the origin point of the written record**: the earliest
attested true writing (the Eanna IV tablet corpus, in which some pictographic signs can
be matched to earlier token prototypes) is, as of current excavation, documented **only**
at Uruk — a fact Postgate flags carefully as reflecting the current state of
excavation coverage rather than a settled claim about writing's sole point of origin
("arguments from silence are particularly perilous" given how few contemporary sites
have been dug). At least ~2,000 distinct signs are attested in the Eanna IV corpus,
mostly recognizably pictographic; the sign repertoire standardizes in the following
Eanna III phase, and the same shared sign-corpus turns up at Jemdet Nasr and Uqair,
showing an accepted, shared writing system in place across South Mesopotamia early on.
Writing at Uruk in this earliest phase is entirely institutional/temple-administrative —
the first evidence of writing outside a clear institutional context (small numeral
tablets from Jebel Aruda and Habuba Kabira) is later and plausibly commercial. Postgate
also reads the Uruk temple sequence as evidence **against** a sharp ethnic-replacement
narrative for the Ubaid-to-Uruk transition: excavation beneath the Anu ziggurat found an
Uruk-period temple built directly over an earlier Ubaid-period temple on the same spot,
continuity he takes as indicating the major Uruk-period cities are "in part at least an
indigenous development," consistent with his broader caution (see [[sumerians]])
against inferring ethnic intrusion from a material-culture shift. Late Uruk cylinder
seals from the Eanna VI level show the first figurative/narrative art with apparent
political content — bound captives, "smiting" scenes, a hat-and-skirt-clad ruler figure
hunting lions and standing beside a goddess — but Postgate cautions "we still lack the
critical written sources to allow us to push our identifications further." (As of
[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)
