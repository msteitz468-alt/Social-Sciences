# Eynan (Ain Mallaha)

Open-air [[natufian-culture|Natufian]] base-camp on the shore of the former Hula Lake / marsh system in the Upper Jordan Valley (Israel). One of the type settlements for Natufian architecture, burial, and long-term occupation; excavated first by Jean Perrot and later in a multi-year French–Israeli program directed by [[valla-francois|François R. Valla]] with Hamoudi Khalaily and others.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Perrot’s mid-century work established Mallaha as a flagship open-air Natufian village. Renewed excavations (1990s–2000s) produced dense spatial, geoarchaeological, faunal, and artifactual datasets reported in *Mitekufat Haeven* and synthesized across multiple chapters of [[bar-yosef-valla-natufian-foragers-2013|Bar-Yosef & Valla 2013]] (Valla et al. Structure 215-228; Samuelian Structures 200/203; Bocquentin graves; Khalaily & Valla obsidian; Valentin et al. knapping; March fire structures; Simmons avifauna; Le Dosseur & Maréchal bone ornaments; Ashkenazi habitats).

## Occupation Sequence

Early through Final Natufian occupation with multi-phase architecture, floors, and burials. Absolute chronology depends on sparse radiocarbon and regional schemes (see Grosman in the 2013 volume). Seasonality and duration of house occupation remain debated (Valla 1998; Bar-Yosef & Valla preface 2013).

## Significance

Anchors the “core area” (Mount Carmel–Galilee–Upper Jordan Valley) model of Early Natufian complexity — substantial architecture, burials, art, groundstone, and bone industries together. Provides high-resolution Final Natufian domestic and mortuary data; exotic obsidian documents long-distance connections.

## Key Debates

- Sedentism vs. seasonal mobility at a wetland-edge base-camp.
- Core-area distinctiveness vs. multi-regional Early Natufian (Belfer-Cohen & Goring-Morris) — [[natufian-origins-core-periphery-debate]].
- Commensalism and occupation intensity (Weissbrod et al. 2013).
- YD expression at Hula wetlands (Ashkenazi: flora/fauna do not support cold-dry YD reading) — [[natufian-younger-dryas-debate]].

## Densified findings from Bar-Yosef & Valla 2013

*(Attributed to chapter authors in [[bar-yosef-valla-natufian-foragers-2013]].)*

- **Structure 215-228 (Final Natufian):** Valla et al. spatial analysis — not a standard habitation unit; specialized/non-residential interpretation relative to residential ovals.
- **Structures 200 & 203 (Samuelian):** closed and open oval residential complexes; floors of accumulation; light superstructures.
- **Graves (Bocquentin et al.):** field anthropology of interstratified floors and burials (e.g. H156 coffin taphonomy); co-mingling of dead and living spaces.
- **Obsidian (Khalaily & Valla):** N=386 pieces, predominantly Cappadocian Göllü Dağ East — long-distance exchange into Final Natufian Eynan.
- **Knapping (Valentin et al.):** Early Natufian shelter 131 — four debitage objectives (bladelets/microbladelets/flakes/microflakes).
- **Fire structures (March):** geochemical diversity of hearths; mixed animal+plant lipids; fecal markers (dung-as-fuel hypothesis); no dairy isotopic range in sampled contexts.
- **Avifauna (Simmons):** human bird exploitation; Early Anatidae-heavy → Final diversification (mallard decline; coots/grebes rise); wintering waterfowl.
- **Bone ornaments (Le Dosseur & Maréchal):** 285 pieces; gazelle-phalanx pendants dominate; ornaments leave graves by Late/Final; lifetime use-wear.
- **Habitats (Ashkenazi):** ~6 km multi-habitat catchment; spring thermal refuge.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003, chs. 3–6; ‘Ain Mallaha naming as in Mithen; calendar BC.)*

- **Regional environmental frame:** Hula basin 16.5 m pollen core (20 km N of Lake Galilee) reaches LGM sediments — rare deep west Asian vegetation sequence documenting woodland→steppe→desert gradient (attributed, ch. 3).
- **Early Natufian type village (~12,300–10,800 BC):** planned year-round settlement in Mediterranean oak woodland; Bar-Yosef “point of no turning back” exemplar (attributed).
- **Architecture:** subterranean floors, low drystone walls, brush/hide roofs; Perrot 1954 dwelling 131 — post-holes, hearths with bird bones, flint, grooved straighteners, basalt mortars, sickle-gloss blades (wild wheat/barley stems) (attributed).
- **Plant processing:** boulder-proportion basalt mortars; acorns, almonds, etc. at far larger scale than LGM Ohalo/Azraq camps (attributed).
- **Dog burial:** elderly woman with puppy, hand on body — persuasive early true dog (size-reduced) (attributed).
- **Exchange and style:** dentalium bead-making; Nile-origin bivalve ≥500 km; lunates as fashion signature; jewellery raw materials differ from Hayonim (attributed).
- **Village biology:** Mallaha vs Hayonim — height and third-molar agenesis differences read as rare intermarriage (attributed).
- **Henry prestige model:** dentalium from Negev steppe foragers in exchange for cereals/nuts/meat; burial as vault limiting shell circulation (position).
- **Sedentism proxies:** Lieberman multi-season gazelle cementum; Cope male-biased large-gazelle culling → size reduction between cave and terrace dumps (attributed).
- **YD aftermath (~10,800 BC):** abandoned/ramshackle settlement; secondary reburial gatherings of mobile Late Natufians recreating ancestral village life temporarily (Lubbock-framed reconstruction grounded in secondary-burial evidence) (attributed).
- **Valla village-origins model:** extend Kebaran winter aggregations year-round (via Mithen) (position).

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]]; Perrot 1966; Baruch and Bottema 1991.)*

Roberts uses Ain Mallaha as a **site-catchment** case study of a broad-spectrum, pre-agricultural Natufian economy (occupied ~13,000 years ago), sited near a spring at the junction of former Lake Huleh, valley marshland, and oak-parkland uplands, with only ~15% of the 5 km catchment potentially arable. Lake Huleh pollen (Baruch and Bottema 1991) confirms denser-than-modern oak parkland supplying acorns and wild cereals; lake stratigraphy and shoreline gravels confirm a larger, deeper lake with abundant wildfowl, turtles, and freshwater shellfish (fish hooks, net weights attested). The contrast with the specialized farming catchment of [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] illustrates how the switch to agriculture required new resource types and new (alluvial) site locations.
