## Discovery and Excavation History

La Mojarra Stela 1 was discovered accidentally in November 1986 in the Acula River, in the Veracruz lowlands between [[sites/tres-zapotes]] and Cerro de las Mesas (Coe & Koontz 2013). The monument is a four-ton basalt stela carved with an imposing standing figure in Izapan style, wearing a towering bird-monster/shark headdress. Excavators/recoverers are not detailed in the extracted material; recorded here as [[unknown]] pending further ingest.

## Occupation Sequence

The stela carries two Long Count dates: 8.5.3.3.5, corresponding to 21 May CE 143, and 8.5.16.9.7, corresponding to 13 July CE 156 — placing the monument and its associated script toward the end of the Late Preclassic (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Significance

La Mojarra Stela 1 bears a hieroglyphic text of roughly 400 signs arranged in 21 columns, described by Coe & Koontz as the longest known Mesoamerican inscription at the time of their writing. Its script matches that found on the Tuxtla Statuette and has been dubbed "Isthmian" by specialists — see [[concepts/isthmian-script]] for the script itself and the contested Justeson–Kaufman decipherment proposal.

## Key Debates

The date and script of La Mojarra Stela 1 bear on the [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]] insofar as the Isthmian writing tradition and Long Count calendar it exemplifies sit at the historical junction between Olmec, Epi-Olmec, and Izapan/early Maya traditions (see [[sites/izapa]], [[concepts/long-count]]).
