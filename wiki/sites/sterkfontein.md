## Discovery and Excavation History

Robert Broom began work at Sterkfontein in 1936 and recovered a partial adult australopithecine skull almost immediately. In 1947–48, assisted by John T. Robinson, he recovered a partial skeleton that included a pelvis demonstrating bipedalism beyond doubt, and a nearly complete cranium known as "Mrs. Ples." The site has been worked intermittently since, and — as at the other southern African cave sites — the absence of volcanic material means no reliable substrate exists for radiometric dating; chronology rests mainly on faunal correlation, supplemented at Sterkfontein by electron spin resonance and paleomagnetic results. This dependence on faunal and indirect dating methods, rather than direct radiometric dates, is a standing reliability caveat for the whole southern African australopithecine sequence (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Occupation Sequence

The Sterkfontein Formation comprises roughly 20 m of cave-fill deposit divided into six successive members (1–6). Member 4, the "type site" for much of the classic australopithecine collection, probably accumulated between 2.8 and 2.3 million years ago, perhaps mainly around 2.5 Ma — a date corroborated by an electron spin resonance estimate of 2.1 ± 0.5 Ma and by paleomagnetic results. Member 2 produced "Little Foot," four articulating ankle and big-toe bones combining human and apelike features (notably a divergent big toe), potentially more than 3 million years old and assigned to [[australopithecus|Australopithecus africanus]] or its immediate ancestor. Member 5 (roughly 2–1.4 Ma) contains an [[oldowan-industry|Oldowan]] artifact assemblage (about 2,800 pieces, no bifaces) overlain by an Early [[acheulean-industry|Acheulean]] assemblage (about 635 pieces, postdating 1.7 Ma), alongside roughly 20 specimens attributed to [[homo-habilis|Homo habilis]], H. ergaster, or both.

## Significance

Sterkfontein anchors the southern African record of [[australopithecus|Australopithecus africanus]] and documents, through Members 4 and 5, the transition from a hominin-only faunal assemblage to one accompanied by stone tools and early Homo. "Mrs. Ples" and "Little Foot" are among the most complete australopithecine specimens known from the region, and the site's stratified member sequence gives it a comparatively fine-grained (if indirectly dated) chronology relative to other South African cave sites.

## Key Debates

Which hominin made the Member 5 Oldowan and Early Acheulean tools is contested. Klein argues that Homo produced most if not all of the stone tools found at Swartkrans, Sterkfontein, Olduvai Gorge, and other sites where [[australopithecus|Paranthropus]] and Homo overlapped, based largely on the disproportionate (roughly 97%-to-3%) ratio of robust-australopithecine to Homo fossils at such sites and on Paranthropus's apelike thumb-metacarpal morphology (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]); the attribution remains circumstantial rather than directly demonstrated.

Sterkfontein is discussed in [[klein-human-career-1999]] ([[klein-richard]]) alongside the neighboring sites of [[hadar]] and [[olduvai-gorge]] as part of the southern and eastern African australopithecine record.

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*Attributed — Sahnouni, Semaw & Rogers (Ch. 22), Domínguez-Rodrigo (Ch. 21), Foley (Ch. 20), Connah (Ch. 2) in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]; **as of handbook ~2012**.*

Sahnouni et al. contrast southern African stone-tool geography: the **Oldowan is restricted to Sterkfontein and Kromdraai B**, whereas the **Acheulean is widespread** across southern Africa. Sterkfontein Valley may hold possible Early Acheulean 1.7–1.4 mya with few diagnostics; absolute dates remain sparse relative to East African radiometric sequences. Domínguez-Rodrigo includes Swartkrans (South) among Oldowan sites; Maker authorship of Member 5 tools remains open (Homo vs contemporaneous robust australopithecines — **position**, not identity). Connah situates South African australopithecine discoveries (Dart, Broom; acceptance only late 1940s) in the colonial/external-funding history of African palaeoanthropology. Four-way non-identity: cave fill, industry labels, and fossil taxa are separate evidence classes; do not equate “Sterkfontein culture” with a people or language.
