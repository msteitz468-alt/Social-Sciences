# Stonehenge

Late-Neolithic to Early-Bronze-Age monument-complex on Salisbury Plain, Wiltshire,
England. Stonehenge is the anchor case of [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Renfrew's
*Before Civilization*]] (1973): the site whose calibrated radiocarbon dates Renfrew used
to argue that a diffusionist ("[[ex-oriente-lux|ex oriente lux]]") derivation from
Mycenaean Greece is untenable and that the monument is an indigenous British achievement.
*[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* (2013) places it in a third-millennium
Wessex ritual landscape of mega-henges, cursus monuments, and sequential remodelling
over ~1.5 millennia (attributed, Cunliffe 2013).

**Clark 1961 (pre-calibration frame).** *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory]]* treats Stonehenge as "in many respects the most outstanding monument of barbarian society in Europe": initial horseshoe ditches and ritual-pit settings attributed to recently acculturated British secondary Neolithic groups; later Wessex Bronze Age elaboration linked to Mycenaean trade contacts in the mid-second millennium B.C. (attributed, Clark 1961 chs. 6–7). That Mycenaean-stimulus reading is exactly the short-chronology diffusionist position Renfrew later overturned with calibrated C-14 — keep Clark's account as a **period position**, not current consensus.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The three-phase construction sequence familiar in mid-twentieth-century literature was
established by the stratigraphic excavations of **Stuart Piggott** and **Richard Atkinson**
in the 1950s (attributed, Renfrew 1973). Excavation quality here is modern single-context
work relative to antiquarian digging; Renfrew's chronology rests on antler-pick radiocarbon
samples from securely stratified contexts.

**Antiquarian frame (Cunliffe).** Cunliffe reports John Aubrey’s view that
Avebury/Stonehenge-class stone circles beyond Roman/Phoenician/Danish reach were British
Druid temples, and William Stukeley’s 1719–24 plans of Stonehenge and [[avebury]], later
turned to Druidic fantasy in *Stonehenge* (1740) and *Avebury* (1743) (attributed,
[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]]). The **Aubrey Holes** are named after the antiquary who
first noted them (as of Cunliffe 2013).

**Mesolithic prehistory of the place.** Monitoring of Stonehenge car-park construction found
a row of three (possibly four) large post-holes 10–14 m apart that held pine timbers up to
0.8 m diameter, set >1 m deep in a pine–hazel woodland clearing — Cunliffe treats these as
remains of a special place nearly 10,000 years ago (attributed finding; Mesolithic monument
scale, not continuity of cult into the Neolithic).

## Occupation Sequence

### Piggott–Atkinson three-phase frame (as of Renfrew 1973)

Three main phases (attributed to the Piggott–Atkinson reconstruction, as of Renfrew 1973):

- **Phase I** (late Neolithic): a bank-and-ditch circle ~100 m across, an entrance causeway
  to the NE, the outlying **Heel Stone** roughly aligned on midsummer sunrise, possible
  Station Stones, and 56 **Aubrey Holes** — pits mostly containing cremated human bone, i.e.
  a cremation cemetery. Dated by associated pottery.
- **Phase II**: erection of the **bluestones** (transported from Wales) in a double circle;
  Beaker pottery marks a new cultural tradition at the very end of the Neolithic.
- **Phase III**: erection of the great **sarsen** circle with lintels and an inner horseshoe
  of five **trilithons** (sarsens probably quarried near Avebury ~40 km away; heaviest stones
  ~50 tons); the bluestones were later re-set inside the sarsen ring.

Atkinson calculated the sarsen phase required on the order of ~1,100 men to haul the heaviest
stones and ~200 to raise an upright, with lintels lifted by earth ramp or timber crib —
labour "not beyond the competence of neolithic communities" (attributed, Renfrew 1973). The
central rotunda (Stonehenge IIIa) is captioned by Renfrew as built c. 2000 BCE.

### Cunliffe 2013 — landscape setting and eight-stage model

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]]; “contentious in part” in Cunliffe’s own
phrasing; reflects continuous remodelling rather than a frozen finished temple.)*

**Henge type.** Cunliffe defines henges as enclosing earthworks with ditch and bank piled
outside, usually circular, one or more entrances, internal posts/stones/pits/burials; size
from Coneybury (~2 ha) to Durrington Walls (~12 ha). Stonehenge is the archetypal henge
from which the type-name derives: stone settings occupy the centre of a much larger circular
enclosure built around **3000 BC** (attributed).

**Simple sequence (Cunliffe summary):** began c.3000 BC as circular earthwork + circle of
pits just inside the ditch, possibly once taking **bluestone** uprights; several centuries
later a central bluestone arc; remodelled when **sarsen trilithons** were erected c.2500 BC
(attributed). Solar axis: midsummer solstice sunrise and midwinter solstice sunset
(attributed — Cunliffe’s solar-calendar framing for henges generally).

**Eight-stage model** (recent research as reported by Cunliffe 2013):

1. **c.3000 BC** — henge bank and ditch; Aubrey Holes within the ditch line, possibly for a
   circle of Preseli (SW Wales) bluestones.
2. **c.2900–2600 BC** — bluestone rearrangement; large timber structure.
3. **~2500 BC** — sarsen circle and trilithons erected; bluestones rearranged again.
4. **c.2450–2210 BC** — large central pit.
5–6. **Twenty-second–nineteenth centuries BC** — further bluestone rearrangements to central
   oval then horseshoe (the form now visible).
7–8. **Nineteenth–sixteenth centuries BC** — circles of holes dug around the standing stones.

Narrative: sequential remodellings over **~1.5 millennia** while other sites in the
surrounding ritual landscape were built, remodelled, and abandoned (attributed).

**Preseli bluestones.** Cunliffe stresses that the same interactive third-millennium society
conceived and achieved moving massive bluestones — **possibly an existing stone circle** —
from the Preseli Mountains to the Wessex chalk (attributed). Package of stones ≠ ethnic
migration.

**Wider landscape (functionally linked through the third millennium, per Cunliffe):**
Durrington Walls mega-henge and **Woodhenge**; Avenue to the Avon; Coneybury; **Cursus**
(2.7 km) and Lesser Cursus (400 m) to the north — cursus form spreads as causewayed floruit
ends in the thirty-sixth century, suggesting procession vs static assembly (attributed). Five
chalkland clusters each dominated by a mega-henge (Mount Pleasant, Knowlton, Durrington
Walls, Marden, [[avebury]]); major Neolithic monuments later became foci for Late Bronze Age
barrow cemeteries (attributed caption). Stonehenge Cursus is part of that early linear
monument horizon.

**Grooved Ware and Beaker.** Grooved Ware (flat-bottomed, line/stab decoration; ~1000 years
from thirty-second/thirty-first century) is invariably associated with henges; elite-ceramic
hypothesis is unproven for lack of non-ritual third-millennium excavations (attributed).
Beaker pottery at traditional monuments need not mean more than continued practice;
Cunliffe notes later bluestone rearrangements as possible constructive interest by new
elites/ideologies, while sarsen circle/trilithons c.2500 would be slightly early for Beaker
involvement if that date holds (attributed). Early Bronze Age elite burial south of the
monument includes **Bush Barrow** (gold lozenges, bronze daggers — attributed as Wessex
elite burial, not a separate “race”).

## Significance

**The dating argument (Renfrew).** Traditional archaeology (Piggott 1938; Atkinson 1956) tied
Stonehenge III to the [[wessex-culture|Wessex culture]] and, through a supposed
Mycenaean-form dagger carving found in 1953, to Mycenaean Greece (~1500 BCE), with Atkinson
even speculating the architect might have been Mycenaean. The first radiocarbon date (1959)
was 1720 b.c. ± 150 (uncalibrated), broadly consistent with that reading.

Renfrew argued that **tree-ring calibration** overturns it: Atkinson's uncalibrated
17th-century-b.c. estimate calibrates to roughly **2100–1900 BCE calendar years — before the
rise of Mycenaean civilization** — so "the whole concept and its execution must have been of
local, British inspiration" (attributed, Renfrew 1973). Renfrew retained Atkinson's separate
inference that mobilising such labour "implies the concentration of political power … in the
hands of a single man," reading it as evidence for an **indigenous
[[chiefdom]]**, not a foreign architect: "I am more optimistic about the possible
achievements of 'mere barbarians'." This is the load-bearing case of the wider
[[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]].

**Cunliffe’s connectivity frame.** After formative Neolithization (4200–3800 BC), the period
3800–2500 BC sees intensified indigenous development with cultural similarity and free flow
of ideas of which Stonehenge and Avebury are emblematic; ~2500 BC Beaker-related
pan-European mobility and metals change the picture (attributed, Ch.6 framing). Henge
ubiquity: one could not travel far without a sacred location; “the entire landscape seemed
to belong to the gods” (attributed theoretical flourish). Earliest henges currently Orkney
(end fourth millennium); southern phase-1 sites including Stonehenge ~3000 BC; origins left
open (attributed, as of 2013). Some writers propose Orkney-first belief systems (henges +
Grooved Ware) spreading south; Cunliffe requires more precise chronology (attributed).

**Astronomy.** Gerald Hawkins (*Stonehenge Decoded*, *Nature* 1963) proposed solar and lunar
alignments (including Station-Stone alignments on the lunar-standstill extremes of the
18.6-year cycle), reached independently and earlier by C. A. Newham (1964). Atkinson's review
"Moonshine on Stonehenge" attacked Hawkins's method as "tendentious, arrogant, slipshod and
unconvincing," but Renfrew records that "at least some of the alignments are now widely
accepted" and that the site functioned "indeed [as] an observatory" (attributed). A. Thom's
survey of British stone circles argued for geometric precision, a standard "megalithic yard,"
and clustered solstice/lunar alignments (attributed; contested). Cunliffe adds the henge-axis
midsummer sunrise / midwinter sunset framing without endorsing full “observatory”
instrumentalism (attributed).

## Key Debates

- [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]] — the "Wessex without Mycenae" argument.
- The astronomical claims (Hawkins, Thom) — recorded as attributed and contested.
- Eight-stage phasing detail (contentious in part, as of Cunliffe 2013) vs classic three-phase
  Piggott–Atkinson scheme — both record continuous multi-century remodelling.
- Beaker-period agency at sarsen vs later bluestone stages (Cunliffe 2013 caution).
- Orkney-first vs open origins for henges/Grooved Ware (position, not settled).

## Historiography

Renfrew's reinterpretation depends entirely on the calibration of
[[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] via [[dendrochronology|tree-ring]] chronology; his
theoretical lens is the emerging [[processual-archaeology|"new prehistory"]] and its
[[chiefdom]] models. Comparable monuments quantify the labour scale: Durrington Walls henge
~900,000 man-hours (Wainwright); Silbury Hill (cal. ~2500 BCE, 40 m high) ~18 million
man-hours built in one episode (Atkinson) — all attributed, as of Renfrew 1973.

Cunliffe 2013 synthesizes post-processual-era landscape archaeology with Bayesian
radiocarbon narratives: Stonehenge as one node in a Wessex mega-henge network, bluestone
transport as achievement of an interactive insular society, and multi-stage remodelling over
1.5 millennia rather than a single finished “temple of the Druids.” Theoretical lens is
Atlantic/continental **connectivity**, not Mycenaean diffusion. Do not equate the
[[wessex-culture|Wessex culture]] Early Bronze Age burial package with the Neolithic henge
builders as one people (four-way non-identity).

## Facing the Ocean densification (Cunliffe 2001)

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry]], *[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Facing the Ocean]]* 2001, Chs. 4–5; an earlier, independent Cunliffe synthesis than *Britain Begins* 2013, cited separately.)*

**Mesolithic posts, detail.** Cunliffe (2001) reports the car-park pits as a row of three large pits (each 1.5–2 m diameter, up to 1.3 m deep) that held upright pine-tree trunks ~0.75 m diameter; dating placed their erection in the second half of the 8th millennium BCE, with pollen evidence of a birch-and-pine forest landscape at the time. He calls these "the first truly monumental structure of the Mesolithic period known to us" — a stronger and earlier claim than a merely "special place," consistent with the [[mesolithic]] page's cemetery-and-monument argument for the period.

**Phase 1 and the Wessex henge network.** Stonehenge's first phase (c. 3000 BCE), per Cunliffe (2001), was an early henge: a circular ditch 110 m in diameter with an internal bank and a circle of 56 large pits thought to have held upright timbers — broadly contemporary with, and similar to, the Orkney henges (see [[megalith]]). Five extensive Wessex ritual complexes, each centred on a massive henge (Avebury, Marden, Durrington Walls, Knowlton, Mount Pleasant), were built around 2500 BCE — a half-millennium later than the Orkney henges, but likely culminating a tradition rooted earlier in the region. Cunliffe (2001) argues that Grooved Ware pottery and the henge "ritual package" may have developed in Orkney c. 3200–3100 BCE and spread rapidly (~1,000 km) to eastern/southern Britain, implying belief systems could travel this way — he presents this as a plausible reading, not settled fact, matching the "Orkney-first" position already recorded above from the 2013 synthesis.

Source: [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001]].

## Bradley 1998 — persistence of memory (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* uses Stonehenge as the Ch. 6 exemplar of monumental continuity: same basic circular groundplan across ~1,500 years while alterations refer to the surrounding landscape.

**Four-phase sketch (Bradley after then-current literature, incl. Cleal et al. 1995):**

1. **~3000–2900 BC:** Precisely circular enclosure, internal bank, external ditch (possibly ~60 segments); late causewayed-enclosure form; cattle bones (some century+ old) at main entrances; antler picks; built as local long-barrow tradition ended.
2. **To ~2400 BC:** Chalk levelled into ditch; Grooved Ware deposits; many human cremations in ditch tops, bank, perimeter, and phase-1 posthole fills (with bone pins, occasional macehead) — better as offerings around a changing earthwork than as a “cremation cemetery” alone; timber circle(s) and elaborated entrances paralleling Durrington Walls / Woodhenge; adapts older earthwork toward henge tradition and wider British network.
3. **Stone phase (~2500–1600 BC):** Successive stone structures; sarsen circle with lintels + internal horseshoe open to northern entrance; mortice-and-tenon as carpentry reference; Avenue to Avon on midsummer sunrise; skyline barrows visible from inside (Fig. 41); stone settings as permeable screen linking interior ceremonies to surrounding barrows.
4. **Final (~1600–1500 BC):** Concentric pit rings (possibly unfinished); axe and dagger carvings on sarsens (~1500 BC types); association paralleling richest nearby burial **Bush Barrow**.

**Thesis:** Sequence shows massive continuity against drastic social change; public ritual assimilates change to existing practice (Bloch-style formalisation; “Theatre of Memory”). After ~1500 BC few large purely public-ritual monuments of this kind remain. Cross-links: [[henge]], [[causewayed-enclosure]], [[ritual-landscape]], [[social-memory]], [[bradley-richard]].
