# Stonehenge

Late-Neolithic to Early-Bronze-Age monument-complex on Salisbury Plain, Wiltshire,
England. Stonehenge is the anchor case of [[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Renfrew's
*Before Civilization*]] (1973): the site whose calibrated radiocarbon dates Renfrew used
to argue that a diffusionist ("[[ex-oriente-lux|ex oriente lux]]") derivation from
Mycenaean Greece is untenable and that the monument is an indigenous British achievement.

**Clark 1961 (pre-calibration frame).** *[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory]]* treats Stonehenge as "in many respects the most outstanding monument of barbarian society in Europe": initial horseshoe ditches and ritual-pit settings attributed to recently acculturated British secondary Neolithic groups; later Wessex Bronze Age elaboration linked to Mycenaean trade contacts in the mid-second millennium B.C. (attributed, Clark 1961 chs. 6–7). That Mycenaean-stimulus reading is exactly the short-chronology diffusionist position Renfrew later overturned with calibrated C-14 — keep Clark's account as a **period position**, not current consensus.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The three-phase construction sequence was established by the stratigraphic excavations of
**Stuart Piggott** and **Richard Atkinson** in the 1950s (attributed, Renfrew 1973).
Excavation quality here is modern single-context work, a reliability advance on antiquarian
digging; Renfrew's chronology rests on antler-pick radiocarbon samples from securely
stratified contexts.

## Occupation Sequence

Three main phases (attributed to the Piggott–Atkinson reconstruction, as of Renfrew 1973):

- **Phase I** (late Neolithic): a bank-and-ditch circle ~100 m across, an entrance causeway
  to the NE, the outlying **Heel Stone** roughly aligned on midsummer sunrise, possible
  Station Stones, and 56 **Aubrey Holes** — pits mostly containing cremated human bone, i.e.
  a cremation cemetery. Dated by associated pottery.
- **Phase II**: erection of the **bluestones** (transported from Wales) in a double circle;
  Beaker pottery marks a new cultural tradition at the very end of the Neolithic.
- **Phase III**: erection of the great **sarsen** circle with lintels and an inner horseshoe
  of five **trilithons** (sarsens probably quarried near Avebury ~40 km away; heaviest stones
  ~50 tons); the bluestones were later re-set inside the sarsen ring.

Atkinson calculated the sarsen phase required on the order of ~1,100 men to haul the heaviest
stones and ~200 to raise an upright, with lintels lifted by earth ramp or timber crib —
labour "not beyond the competence of neolithic communities" (attributed, Renfrew 1973). The
central rotunda (Stonehenge IIIa) is captioned by Renfrew as built c. 2000 BCE.

## Significance

**The dating argument.** Traditional archaeology (Piggott 1938; Atkinson 1956) tied
Stonehenge III to the [[wessex-culture|Wessex culture]] and, through a supposed
Mycenaean-form dagger carving found in 1953, to Mycenaean Greece (~1500 BCE), with Atkinson
even speculating the architect might have been Mycenaean. The first radiocarbon date (1959)
was 1720 b.c. ± 150 (uncalibrated), broadly consistent with that reading.

Renfrew argued that **tree-ring calibration** overturns it: Atkinson's uncalibrated
17th-century-b.c. estimate calibrates to roughly **2100–1900 BCE calendar years — before the
rise of Mycenaean civilization** — so "the whole concept and its execution must have been of
local, British inspiration" (attributed, Renfrew 1973). Renfrew retained Atkinson's separate
inference that mobilising such labour "implies the concentration of political power … in the
hands of a single man," reading it as evidence for an **indigenous
[[chiefdom]]**, not a foreign architect: "I am more optimistic about the possible
achievements of 'mere barbarians'." This is the load-bearing case of the wider
[[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]].

**Astronomy.** Gerald Hawkins (*Stonehenge Decoded*, *Nature* 1963) proposed solar and lunar
alignments (including Station-Stone alignments on the lunar-standstill extremes of the
18.6-year cycle), reached independently and earlier by C. A. Newham (1964). Atkinson's review
"Moonshine on Stonehenge" attacked Hawkins's method as "tendentious, arrogant, slipshod and
unconvincing," but Renfrew records that "at least some of the alignments are now widely
accepted" and that the site functioned "indeed [as] an observatory" (attributed). A. Thom's
survey of British stone circles argued for geometric precision, a standard "megalithic yard,"
and clustered solstice/lunar alignments (attributed; contested).

## Key Debates

- [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]] — the "Wessex without Mycenae" argument.
- The astronomical claims (Hawkins, Thom) — recorded as attributed and contested.

## Historiography

Renfrew's reinterpretation depends entirely on the calibration of
[[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] via [[dendrochronology|tree-ring]] chronology; his
theoretical lens is the emerging [[processual-archaeology|"new prehistory"]] and its
[[chiefdom]] models. Comparable monuments quantify the labour scale: Durrington Walls henge
~900,000 man-hours (Wainwright); Silbury Hill (cal. ~2500 BCE, 40 m high) ~18 million
man-hours built in one episode (Atkinson) — all attributed, as of Renfrew 1973.

## Braidwood 1959 — Wessex visual trace

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]*.)* In the Britain-as-sample end-of-prehistory chapter, final-form Stonehenge is the great visual trace of **Wessex** achievement after Beaker and related arrivals — a period narrative for European metal-age sequences, not a site monograph.
