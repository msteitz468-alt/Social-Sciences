# Maquixco Bajo (TC-8)

Middle Horizon (Classic, Teotihuacan-period) rural village on the piedmont 5 km west of [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] — the first substantial excavation of a rural Teotihuacan community, and [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|Sanders, Parsons & Santley's (1979)]] type case of the **peasant village** inside an urban-centered state.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Located in the 1960 Teotihuacan Valley survey; excavated 1961–62 by the Teotihuacan Valley Project (three adjacent house compounds plus the detached ceremonial complex). Pre-flotation-era methods — no botanical macrosamples; the faunal sample is 347 fragments — a stated limit on the subsistence reconstruction.

## Occupation Sequence

Per the source: founded as a dozen dispersed houses in First Intermediate Three, growing into a compact, formally planned rectangle (~200 × 400 m) peaking in the Late Middle Horizon at an estimated 140–150 families (500–600 people, ~6,000/km²). Some 20 residential mounds held Teotihuacan-style multi-apartment houses (10–15 nuclear-family apartments in the excavated mounds 1–2, ~40–60 residents per house) around central courts with altar platforms; a small plaza carried a talud-tablero temple and civic buildings.

## Significance

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) read the site as three nested social levels — apartment/nuclear family, house/lineage, village — functionally comparable to the later Aztec [[calpulli]], and as a **peasant** community in Wolf's (1966) sense: surplus-producing (especially maguey products, per ~300 worn rasping scrapers), taxed by the city, cultivating piedmont lands while the urban center apparently controlled the alluvial plain. The obsidian assemblage (9,121 blades, 39 cores, no workshop debris) implies cores came from Teotihuacan with only blade-striking done locally, and skilled construction elements (plaster, talud-tablero, balustrades) suggest contracted urban masons — both read as economic dependence on the city. Architecture is a scaled-down, inferior version of urban compounds (e.g., Yayahuala), yet portable goods show weak urban–rural rank differences, complicating simple hierarchy. The contrast with [[loma-torremote|Loma Torremote]] carries the book's tribal-village → peasant-village argument about [[state-formation]] (see [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]).

## Key Debates

The initial identification of the finest house (mound 4) as the village headman's was revised by the authors themselves; an earlier Sanders (1966) warfare/cannibalism reading of some deposits was also reinterpreted in the 1979 synthesis. Lineage identifications rest on architectural inference plus Aztec/modern ethnographic analogy, flagged as such in the source.
