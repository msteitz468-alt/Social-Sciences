## Discovery and Excavation History

Huaca Sipán (Huaca Rajada) in the Lambayeque drainage yielded, in excavations directed by Walter Alva, what [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]] calls the richest unlooted tombs scientists have discovered in the Western Hemisphere. Scarre ed. 2013 credits Alva and Christopher Donnan (1993) with demonstrating that the Moche "Presentation Theme" of elite combat, captive sacrifice, and blood offering corresponded to real offices and ceremonies: the principal tomb held a "Warrior Priest" with costume, headdress, sacrificial chalice, and a spotted dog matching the iconography. The site is a multi-level adobe funerary platform complex, not a residential capital on the scale of Cerro Blanco (Huaca del Sol / Huaca de la Luna).

## Occupation Sequence

As of Moseley 2001:

- Phase I–II Moche ceramics are rare or absent in Lambayeque; **Phase III predominates at Sipán**, with mixed phase attributes — the warrior-priest tomb dated **AD 290**.
- Opulent burials represent succession of office-holders; art defined ranks of a religious/political hierarchy.
- Sipán was the center of a powerful Moche *señorío* that **did not march in lockstep** with the Cerro Blanco (Moche Valley) capital even though elite ideology was shared — key evidence in Moseley’s northern-confederation reading of Moche political organization.
- Parallel: in the Jequetepeque Valley, Donnan excavated graves of women bedecked as the presentation-theme **priestess**; together Sipán warrior priests and Jequetepeque priestesses instantiate the sacrifice/presentation cast of characters.

**Presentation/sacrifice theme (attributed iconography–tomb match):** prisoners’ throats cut, blood collected in a goblet, paraded by priestess and owl-headdress figure to the warrior priest for consumption; funerary goods include the presentation goblet, uniforms/headgear matching painted scenes, and the spotted dog companion of the warrior priest in art.

## Significance

- Landmark case of **independent convergence** between iconographic evidence class and excavated mortuary assemblage — turning "mythic" Moche painted narratives into attested ritual roles.
- Anchors the **northern Moche** as wealthy, ideologically Moche, but politically not necessarily a province of a single Cerro Blanco state (Moseley’s impression: powerful northern señoríos in loose confederation via kuraka ideology).
- Demonstrates wealth-finance monopolization by kuraka elites (precious metal, fine costume) justifying demigod-like office.

## Key Debates

- **Single Moche state vs confederation:** Sipán’s Phase-III mixed attributes and absence of southern-style intrusive administrative centers in northern valleys support the confederation/indirect-rule side; southern monumental centers (Panamarca, Huancaco, Pampa de Los Incas) support stronger Cerro Blanco hegemony for Chicama–Huarmey in Phases III–IV. Recorded as open dispute on [[moche-culture]] — no wiki-voice adjudication.
- **How far "office" equals "divine kingship":** Moseley treats high office-holders as probably viewed as demigods via special creation myths; that is attributed ideology reconstruction.

## Historiography

Alva’s salvage/scientific excavation after looting threats made Sipán a global reference point for Andean archaeology and museum presentation. Donnan’s thematic analysis of Moche art provided the interpretive key that the tombs "solved." Collection coverage: Scarre handbook + Moseley 2001; full Alva/Donnan monographs not yet ingested as primary sources.
