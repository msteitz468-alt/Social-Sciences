# Jarmo

Early village-farming settlement on a bluff above a stream in the **hill country east of Kirkuk** (Iraqi Kurdistan) — type-case for [[braidwood-robert|Braidwood]]’s **[[hilly-flanks|hilly-flanks]]** / primary village-farming community model in *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* (3rd ed. 1959).

## Discovery and Excavation History

The Iraq Directorate General of Antiquities suggested the site in **1948**; Braidwood’s team had conducted **three seasons** by the 1959 edition. Depth of deposit ~**27 feet**, ~**a dozen layers** of architectural renovation, treated as a **“one period”** site: assemblage essentially constant with few late additions. Area ~**four acres**. Multidisciplinary staffing (including zoologist **Reed**) is part of the site’s historiographic importance — early institutionalization of [[paleoethnobotany]] / [[zooarchaeology]] in farming-origins work.

Excavation quality and full specialist reports are not reconstructable from the popular book alone; treat 1959 claims as Braidwood’s public synthesis, not the final field monograph.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Braidwood 1959.)*

| Domain | Report |
|---|---|
| Architecture | Hall-mark of the village proper = architectural permanence; houses of *touf* (puddled mud); rough order of ~20 houses / ~150 people |
| Plants | Barley + two wheats; sickles, querns/mortars, parching ovens, stone bowls; wheat morphologically intermediate between wild and fully bred |
| Animals | Domesticated **goat** affirmed; sheep, cattle, pig, horse, dog — Reed not convinced of domestication from bones alone |
| Other food | Large quantities of land snails |
| Pottery | Portable pottery only in the **upper third** of the deposit |
| Lithics | Strong continuity of flint blades and microliths |
| Trade | Obsidian from sources Braidwood places **more than 300 miles** away |
| Figurines | Unbaked clay, including pregnant female forms |
| Radiocarbon | **Twelve** determinations; “most reasonable cluster” averages **~6750 ± 200 B.C.**; full scatter runs **3250–9250 B.C.** — he flags the extremes as “completely unreasonable.” **As of 1959, uncalibrated early series; not wiki-voice chronology.** |

Braidwood places a **gap** between the Karim Shahir–M’lefaat–Zawi Chemi ([[karim-shahir|Karim Shahir]]; Zawi Chemi near [[shanidar]]) incipient group and Jarmo; a few dates might imply ~2,000 years (Zawi Chemi 8900 ± 300 B.C. vs Jarmo cluster) but he doubts the gap was that long.

## Significance

Anchors the **primary village-farming community** era in Braidwood’s stage scheme; pairs with [[jericho|Jericho]] in mid-century textbooks as early farming type-cases; empirical face of the [[hilly-flanks]] thesis and of the [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]]; later work revises dates, botanics, and the single-nuclear narrative.

## Key Debates

[[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]]; whether pre-pottery villages imply food production; reliability of the early Jarmo radiocarbon series; multi-regional vs nuclear Near East primacy. Related: [[neolithic-revolution]], [[agricultural-revolution]], [[incipient-cultivation]], [[nuclear-area]].

## Historiography

Known largely through Braidwood program publications and textbook reception; this page’s first densification is from the 1959 popular synthesis. Full excavation-report ingest will supersede assemblage and date detail. Source: [[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959]].

### Roux (*Ancient Iraq*, 1992, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]].)* Roux locates Jarmo near Chemchemal in Iraqi Kurdistan, excavated by R. J. Braidwood (University of Chicago) across seasons in 1948, 1950–51, and 1955: a 7 m mound with 16 occupation layers, 11 of which are pre-ceramic ("aceramic Neolithic"). He reports square, multi-room pressed-mud (pisé) houses, mud ovens, clay basins, bone spoons and needles, stone spindle whorls (evidence of weaving), microlithic and full-size flint/obsidian blades including bitumen-hafted sickles, limestone tools (axes, celts, querns, mortars, pestles, vessels), carbonized wheat and barley grain, and animal bone 95% domesticated (sheep, cattle, pig, dog), alongside lentils, peas, vetches, and acorns. Sub-floor burial is attested, along with clay animal figurines and a "steatopygous, pregnant woman" figurine (a fertility interpretation, attributed).

**Dating supersession (Chronological Uncertainty Protocol):** Roux notes pre-ceramic Jarmo was initially radiocarbon-dated, via snail shells, to c. 4750 BCE; further charcoal testing produced older dates, and Roux favors c. 6750 BCE as "more likely" — an explicit within-the-literature revision of the site's absolute chronology, illustrating the fragility of early radiocarbon dating (Roux elsewhere notes the Libby half-life of 5,568 years used in this era's determinations has since been superseded by 5,730 years). Roux groups Jarmo with Ali Kosh, Tepe Guran (Iran), Hacilar (Anatolia), and Jericho (Palestine) as among the earliest agricultural communities of Western Asia, noting Jericho's atypical monumental fortification distinguishes it from the otherwise small, unfortified villages of this group.
