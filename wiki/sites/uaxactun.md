# Uaxactun

## Discovery and Excavation History

[[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler (2006)]] report Uaxactun (~40 km north of [[tikal]]) was named by Sylvanus G. Morley for Stela 9’s 8 Bak’tun [[long-count|Long Count]] date (AD 328), then the earliest known Maya date in his catalog. The Carnegie Institution of Washington’s 1926–37 project was, they state, the first comprehensive archaeological investigation in the central lowlands (field directors and specialists including Frans Blom, Oliver and Edith Ricketson, A. L. and Robert Smith, Edwin Shook, and Robert Wauchope). Discoveries they highlight include Structure E-VII-sub (first major Preclassic lowland temple excavated), the Group E astronomical assemblage that became the type “E Group,” early house-mound settlement survey, Wauchope’s household archaeology, and Robert Smith’s pottery sequence (Mamom–Tzakol–Tepeu) that long scaffolded lowland ceramic chronology.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Sharer & Traxler 2006 unless noted.)*

- **Late Preclassic:** Group H major sacred-mountain architecture later buried under Early Classic fill. South Plaza platforms carried six eastern buildings with Late Preclassic plaster masks; Str. H-Sub-3 bore *witz* (mountain deity) masks identifying the building as a sacred mountain; Sub-10 gateway combined *ajaw* masks and ruler figures on throne symbols with smoke scrolls (paralleling Tikal 5D-Sub-10) alternating with woven mat (*pop*) panels—kingship symbols shared between highlands and lowlands. E-VII-sub: Late Preclassic plastered platform with 16 masks and thatched summit posts. Late Preclassic E Group at Uaxactun belongs to the El Mirador Basin architectural package (triadic pyramids, vaulted masonry, ball courts, E Groups) spreading into the central lowlands; similar triadic forms at Cerros, Nohmul, Lamanai, Tikal, and Uaxactun—none matching [[el-mirador]]’s scale.
- **Early Classic monuments (AD 328–416):** Six monuments in this span; Stela 9 earliest (AD 328); Stelae 18–19 among earliest known k’atun-ending monuments (AD 357). Neighboring early monument centers (Balakbal, Uolantun, El Zapote, Yaxha, Xultun) form an equidistant early-capital landscape; Sharer & Traxler treat it as likely that Tikal eventually defeated local competition, with many near-Tikal sites ceasing monuments thereafter.
- **AD 378 Siyaj K’ak’ horizon:** Same day AD 378 (8.17.1.4.12): arrival of Siyaj K’ak’ (“Born of Fire”) and death of Tikal’s fourteenth ruler Chak Tok Ich’aak I, recorded on monuments at both Tikal and Uaxactun. Scheie & Freidel (as reported by Sharer & Traxler) concluded Uaxactun Stela 5 (Central Mexican warrior with atlatl and obsidian-blade club) and Structure B-VIII multiple burial (two adult women—one pregnant—child, infant) commemorate conquest of Uaxactun by Siyaj K’ak’ and slaughter of the defeated king’s family; a Group B mural may record a related event. Siyaj K’ak’ titled *ochk’in kaloomte’* (“lord of the west”); Stuart finds a reference at Waka (El Perú) eight days before Tikal.
- **Classic urban density:** Ricketson house-mound survey indicated settlement density beyond pure swidden support. As of Sharer & Traxler 2006 (summarizing Rice & Culbert 1990), Uaxactun structure densities fall well below central [[copan]] and most Classic cores (most Classic sites ~200–450 structures/km²).
- **Destinies with Tikal:** Authors state destinies intertwined with Tikal for roughly a millennium of shared Preclassic–Classic trajectories.

## Significance

Type-site for (1) Carnegie-era central-lowland method (ceramics, households, settlement survey); (2) E Group astronomical architecture named from Group E; (3) early Long Count barometer historiography (Morley’s “Old Empire” start date later revised by Tikal Stela 29 to AD 292); (4) the AD 378 Teotihuacan-related *entrada* narrative as it played out at a Tikal satellite. Anchors links among [[classic-maya]] divine kingship ([[kuhul-ajaw]]), Preclassic Mirador-package architecture, and the Tikal political landscape.

## Key Debates

- **AD 378 conquest interpretation:** Scheie & Freidel’s Uaxactun conquest/slaughter reading of Stela 5 and B-VIII (as reported by Sharer & Traxler) remains an attributed interpretive position within the broader Tikal–[[teotihuacan]] *entrada* dispute (military takeover vs elite emulation).
- **Morley Long Count barometer:** Sharer & Traxler recount Morley’s use of stelae/Long Count as a barometer of the “Old Empire” (Uaxactun Stela 9 once as start; peak at 9.18.0.0.0 / AD 790; last dates shifted later to [[tonina]] 909). They argue end of Long Count monuments is one symptom of multi-century decline of divine kings, not demise of civilization—[[el-mirador]] rose and fell without Long Count monuments.
- [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] — Petén abandonment trajectories in which Uaxactun participates as a secondary central-lowland capital.

## Historiography

Uaxactun’s scholarly role outruns its political scale: Carnegie 1926–37 made it the laboratory where lowland ceramic periods, E Groups, Preclassic temples, and household archaeology first cohered as a research package. Morley’s naming and stela catalog embedded the site in “Old Empire” chronology later rewritten by Tikal’s earlier dates and by modern epigraphy. Scheie–Freidel narrative history and Stuart’s *entrada* philology re-centered Uaxactun as a witness to Early Classic interregional politics rather than a pure ceramic type-site. Source densification for this page: [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006]]; excavation quality is Carnegie-era (pre-single-context modern standards but systematically recorded for its generation).

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel, *A Forest of Kings*, 1990]], Ch. 4 and notes. Later phonetics revise many names; Smoking-Frog is often equated in later literature with Sihyaj K’ahk’, but S&F 1990 present him as Tikal *ahau*/war chief installed at Uaxactun, not as the full modern *entrada* package. Battle dramatization without surviving pictorial scenes is NARRATIVE DEVICE.)*

### Late Preclassic florescence: E-VII-Sub and Group H

Schele & Freidel report that over the same centuries as [[tikal]]’s North Acropolis expansion, Uaxactun underwent a comparable Late Preclassic florescence. **Temple E-VII-Sub** is treated as a typical expression of Late Preclassic kingship, not an anomaly — monumental plaster-mask architecture continuous with the broader lowland package also seen at [[cerros]] and early Tikal. Deep within and beneath the South Plaza of **Group H** lies what they call the largest program of Late Preclassic monumental masks yet discovered (as of 1990). Structure **H-Sub-3** façade is read as modeling the sacred living mountain (**Witz**) rising from primordial waters with Vision Serpent — parallel to but different from Cerros / E-VII-Sub sun–Venus celestial-cycle programs. Group H **Sub-10** gateway carried stucco jaguar *ahau* masks, mat (*pop*) signs, and stucco royal portraits amid blood scrolls — comparable to Tikal 5D-Sub-10-1st; authors see Late Preclassic kings memorializing themselves for the first time on main temple axes, prototype of Classic stelae placement. Through the first century A.D., they argue Tikal and Uaxactun entered the Classic as full equals, both ready to assume [[el-mirador]]’s role when that kingdom disintegrated — and, being less than twelve miles apart, “perhaps too closely situated for both of them to become kingdoms of the first rank.”

### Early Classic monuments before 378

Earliest surviving monument: **Stela 9**, 8.14.10.13.15 (April 11, A.D. 328). **Stelae 18 and 19** in Group E are, in their account, the earliest surviving public katun-ending celebrations; captive-at-feet imagery recurs; they suggest Tikal as the likely victim source in that peer-raiding phase. Traditional Maya war is framed as captive-taking for sacrifice and prestige, not kill-to-conquer.

### A.D. 378 subordination: Smoking-Frog and the Tlaloc-Venus conquest

Schele & Freidel’s core Chapter 4 thesis: competition with Tikal was resolved violently on **January 16, A.D. 378** (8.17.1.4.12 11 Eb 15 Mac) by a new type of kingdom-taking war they call **Tlaloc-Venus war** / “star wars,” with imagery and method borrowed from [[teotihuacan]] (POSITION — cultural-political thesis about elites and ritual, not a population-replacement claim). The date is recorded twice at Uaxactun (Stela 5; retrospectively Stela 22) and twice at Tikal (Stela 31 retrospective; Ballcourt Marker) — rare non–period-ending multi-site date.

- **Uaxactun Stela 5:** single primary visual of the event; **Smoking-Frog** as triumphant leader of Tikal forces, named *ahau* of Tikal on the reverse, full warrior regalia with obsidian-bladed club; first visual representation of Tlaloc-Venus costume (balloon headdress, spearthrower) radically different from earlier Maya *ahau* war dress — marks “conquest war.”
- **Actors (S&F reconstruction):** high king **Great-Jaguar-Paw** of Tikal and Smoking-Frog (war chief; Tikal [[emblem-glyph]] in name; never Tikal’s own high king in their reading). Stela 31 retrospective at Tikal: Smoking-Frog *hom* (“demolished and threw down”) Uaxactun’s buildings; Great-Jaguar-Paw bloodlets to sanctify victory. International symbolism grafted onto orthodox Maya decapitation sacrifice functioned, they argue, as propaganda enabling Smoking-Frog’s installation as **usurper king at Uaxactun**.
- **Aftermath:** Smoking-Frog still at Uaxactun 18 years later on 8.18.0.0.0 (July 8, 396) with Stela 4 next to the conqueror portrait; oversaw nephew **Curl-Snout**’s Tikal accession September 13, 379; may have ruled the combined kingdom for ~18–26 years, perhaps marrying into Uaxactun’s ruling family (hypothesis). Curl-Snout is *yahau* / *u cab* of Smoking-Frog in Tikal texts.
- **Stela 22 (9.3.10.0.0 / December 9, 504):** 126 years after conquest, a Uaxactun ruler still celebrated the victory with day 11 Eb and the same *hom* verb — glory by association for Smoking-Frog’s progeny.
- **Temple B-VIII group burial (notes POSITION):** Stela 5 set with Temple B-VIII stairs; Smith (1950) found a unique multi-body tomb (pregnant woman, second woman, child, infant) built with the substructure — Schele & Freidel interpret as wives/children of the defeated Uaxactun king, killed and sealed in the victory temple to end the local royal line. This is their interpretive position on a Carnegie-era find, not an independent forensic proof.
- **NARRATIVE DEVICE:** Pictorial battle scenes do not survive; dramatized atlatl-surprise combat (“Imagine the growing sense of horror…”) is imaginative reconstruction, not primary evidence. Theological stakes of conquest (hold the enemy’s sacred portal; cut defeated from ancestral guidance) are similarly reconstructed narrative framing.

### Hiatus with Tikal’s 562 defeat

When Caracol’s star-at-Tikal war (May 1, 562) broke Tikal’s dominance, Schele & Freidel report Uaxactun also lost kingship as part of Tikal’s hegemony: leaders ceased erecting monuments at 9.6.0.0.0 and did not resume for about two hundred years — the same Tlaloc-Venus war type inverted against the 378 conqueror’s house.
