# San José Mogote

## Discovery and Excavation History

Major Early Formative village in the **Valley of Oaxaca** (Etla arm), principal focus of the University of Michigan Oaxaca project directed by [[flannery-kent-v|Kent V. Flannery]]. Featured throughout *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* (1976; study [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]]) as the large village that forced multi-scalar sampling questions (Flannery cites ~40 acres of Early Formative houses as a research problem). 1969 season included San José Mogote houses alongside Winter’s sampling of the hamlet near Santa María Atzompa ([[tierras-largas]]). Intensive surface work and residential-area excavation (Areas A and C among them) underwrite the volume’s household, community, exchange, and ritual chapters.

## Occupation Sequence

Early–Middle Formative village with residential wards, [[household-cluster|household clusters]], and public/ritual facilities discussed in the 1976 volume; later Zapotec trajectory treated in subsequent Flannery & Marcus work (not fully covered by the 1976 village volume alone).

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996)

Densified from [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996]]. *(Attributed throughout.)*

### Rosario paramount center

At Rosario peak: est. **60–65 ha**, ~**1000** persons; seven elite-residence areas defined by decorated fine gray ware; “downtown” public + elite zone ≥42 ha. Apex of three-arm valley hierarchy (Etla ~2000 total) with satellites [[fabrica-san-jose]], [[tierras-largas]], frontier [[huitzo]].

### Mound 1 architecture and warfare

Structures **19 / 28 / 14**: multi-stage stone/adobe platforms; Structure 28 lime-plastered temple with dedicatory corner vessels and obsidian bloodletting tool — **burned** with vitrified daub (raid interpretation; experimental burning cited). Monument 3 threshold slab: naked sacrificed captive named **“1 Earthquake”** (dot + Xoo glyph) — authors’ five inferences include 260-day calendar, archaic Zapotec day-name, heart sacrifice, raiding pattern by ~600 BCE, and “trod upon” placement. Writing born in **chiefly competition** (their theoretical claim). Circular Structure 31 (~6 m diameter) — function unknown.

### Elite residences and Tomb 10

Structures 25/26/30 adobe compounds above burned temple ruins; Tomb 10 two-chambered masonry tomb (antechamber tradition); emptied on abandonment; eleven imported-obsidian points in ochre left behind. High-ranking woman Burial 60 with jade and tabular cranial deformation.

### End of Rosario / synoikism participation

Core 40-ha ceremonial/elite zone **all but abandoned** at end of Rosario; half of non-continuing Rosario villages in southern Etla — principal evidence for SJM-led participation in [[monte-alban]] [[synoikism]]. Later MA II ritual deposits and noble tomb (Tomb 4 war death) show continued but secondary importance; Classic beakers name founders Lord **1 Jaguar** and Lady **2 Flower** commemorated here.

## The Cloud People (Flannery & Marcus, eds., 1983)

In [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983), San José Mogote is the anchor of the pre-Monte Albán [[zapotec-civilization|Zapotec]] sequence (all dates uncalibrated radiocarbon):

- **Oldest Oaxaca ceramics.** The Espiridión complex (ca. 1900–1400 BCE, inferred) underlies the Tierras Largas phase; [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] argued early vessel shapes derive from cross-sectioned bottle gourds rather than foreign ceramic diffusion.
- **Early regional center.** Already a plastered-public-building village of ~147 persons in the Tierras Largas phase, San José Mogote exploded to a 70-ha, ~700-person center in the San José phase — one of the largest Early Formative sites in highland Mesoamerica, participating heavily in the "Olmec" interaction sphere (magnetite mirrors, marine shell) that the contemporaneous Mixteca Cruz phase largely did not.
- **Chiefdom, not state.** [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] and Marcus read the Rosario-phase elite compound and Tomb 10 atop Mound 1 as a "ranked but not yet stratified" society — a [[chiefdom]] — and explicitly declined to demonstrate a multilevel administrative hierarchy in the valley before Monte Albán II.
- **Earliest writing.** Rosario-phase Monument 3 ("1 Earthquake") carries the region's oldest [[zapotec-writing|Zapotec calendric glyphs]] and Marcus's reading of a sacrificed captive.
- The site was eclipsed as the valley's leading center by the founding of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] at the start of Period I.

### From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — size, growth, contrast with Tierras Largas

All figures and interpretations attributed to Winter, Marcus, Flannery, and named contributors in Flannery ed. 1976.

**Late Tierras Largas phase:** Marcus (sample of 10 sites) treated virtually all sample sites as hamlet size (most 5–10 households); largest site San José Mogote probably ≤3 ha with ≥300 m² (~0.03 ha) public buildings — public buildings absent at Tierras Largas this phase and unknown elsewhere in the period sample.

**Late San José phase (1000–850 B.C.):** sample of 10 sites — nine hamlets 0.5–2.0 ha; San José Mogote **~20 ha** (~10× next largest). Extrapolating Winter’s households/ha from Tierras Largas: Marcus estimated **80–120 households** for SJM (vs Tierras Largas 8 households on 1.59 ha); estimate does not allow for substantial public buildings. Plog’s Etla block average site size drops from 8.65 ha to **0.92 ha excluding San José Mogote**.

**Guadalupe/Rosario (850–500 B.C.):** discontinuous size distribution continues; SJM reaches **~40 ha**, still greater than 10× next largest. Winter (via Flannery): ~100–140 households / 500–700 persons by ~550 B.C. (some public buildings). Public buildings: large indeterminate area of SJM; Huitzo ≥13% of 2.7 ha stone architecture; Tierras Largas no conclusive public buildings (possible elite residence at center).

**Growth trajectory (Winter Ch. 8):** late Tierras Largas phase only slightly larger than the hamlet Tierras Largas; by 1150 B.C. growing enormously; by **900 B.C. ~10× Tierras Largas**; by **550 B.C. ~15×**; more internally differentiated; substantial growth via proliferation of public buildings; second peak in Proto-Classic then slow decline to abandonment (Fig. 8.7). Winter argued SJM grew to **more than 20×** its original size while Tierras Largas showed essentially **no growth in households** across seven Early–Middle Formative subphases. Nearly **half** of Valley of Oaxaca regional population growth 1500–500 B.C. he attributed to growth of this single large nucleated village (~5 ha/century for ~700 years). Growth–climax–decline, he suggested, may fit large nucleated villages (SJM, San Lorenzo, Chiapa de Corzo), not Formative hamlets that plateau at modal 8–12 households.

## Significance

Type case for Formative highland village complexity short of urban Teotihuacan-scale centers; load-bearing for [[household-archaeology]], community zoning, exchange, and ritual chapters of the 1976 program. Regional consumer / largest site in Etla linear Atoyac system (Flannery Ch. 6); ceremonial-civic services hypothesized for smaller villages (Plog gravity-model interaction; Winter & Pires-Ferreira pooling).

## From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — households, exchange, ritual, surface/catchment

### Households and residential structure

Flannery & Winter used ten houses at San José Mogote (plus other valley sites) for activity-area typology: universal household activities (grinding, storage, maize, braziers) plus local specializations (ground-polished **celt** manufacture at several wards ~1150 B.C.; shell ornament production nearly every extensively excavated 1150–850 B.C. house at SJM and Tierras Largas). House 2 Area C (~1000 B.C.) is a mapped floor assemblage exemplar; provisional male/female work-area patterning (chert/shell left of door, brazier/food right). Flannery proposed large-village unit sequence: house → [[household-cluster]] → courtyard group → **residential ward** → community — 8–12-household hamlets may equal one ward at SJM scale. Pyne (Ch. 9): six extensively excavated EF houses/clusters at SJM (Houses 2 and 9 west edge; Household Clusters C1–C4 east); several households positively associated with **fire-serpent** motif and negatively with were-jaguar; high fire-serpent houses also more *Spondylus*, pearl oyster, mica, magnetite; adult male burial with fire-serpent cylindrical bowl near Houses 2–9.

### Exchange

Pires-Ferreira & Flannery: Early Formative obsidian moved at every-household level; shell at regional craft-specialist level (SJM/Tierras Largas part-time shell workers; finished ornaments elsewhere); magnetite mainly elite-to-elite — Flannery (1968) residential ward at SJM producing small flat iron-ore mirrors; Coe found identical at San Lorenzo. Winter & Pires-Ferreira: Area A Household Clusters C1–C4 show remarkably **uniform** obsidian source proportions during San José phase → tentative **pooling** before distribution (Area C sample too small); Area A also stratigraphic craft continuities (shell, mica, magnetite mirrors) over four “generations.” Early Formative Tierras Largas lacked pooling and had different source %, suggesting it did not get obsidian from SJM despite probable ceremonial-civic dependence. Prismatic blades from Barranca de los Estetes / Zinapécuaro ~1000 B.C. associated with reduced inter-household source variation. Plog assigned SJM **280 households** (vs standard 10 for 1–3 ha villages) in gravity-model interaction analysis of Atoyac Yellow-white design elements; SJM–Fábrica San José highly similar despite temporal caveats; hypothesized competing ceremonial-civic center with Huitzo and service hub for smaller villages.

### Ritual / public architecture

Public buildings present from Late Tierras Largas phase onward (Marcus); substantial proliferation as community grew (Winter). Drainage ditches and bedrock cistern under Houses 16–17 noted by Winter among Formative feature types. Olmec-style motif associations with specific residential areas (Pyne) rather than site-wide uniformity. Nested catchment (Flannery Ch. 4): 2.5-km circle ~76% high alluvium; mountains beyond 5 km for pine; exotics possibly over 200 km (Pacific shell, Gulf mussels, jadeite/nephrite) — ceremonial and exchange goods beyond local walking-distance resources.

### Surface collection and regional position

Plog Etla intensive survey: highest site density per km² among three Oaxaca blocks; SJM dominates size statistics. Flannery linear-stream analysis: Late San José six-site string with ~5 km spacing; SJM land allotment mid-line partition ~±1430 ha alluvium; major ford may have been at San José Mogote; first-settlement / regional-center role in conjectured Etla colonization rules. Surface-defined site limits and intensive pickup figure in community sampling arguments throughout the volume; large EF extent made total house excavation impossible — multi-scalar sampling is a research necessity, not a preference.

## Key Debates

Scale and political status of Formative San José Mogote relative to Olmec interaction and later Monte Albán state formation — treat as attributed positions on later sources; this page densifies from [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]] first. Winter’s discontinuous growth (one nucleated giant vs stable hamlets) challenges uniform village life-cycle models. Whether Early Formative SJM was already a redistributive elite center (obsidian pooling, magnetite ward) or still largely household-reciprocal remains an attributed reading of Winter & Pires-Ferreira, not a settled wiki-voice fact.

## Ancient Oaxaca, 2nd ed. (Blanton et al. 2022)

From [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022]]; interpretive claims attributed to Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas 2022.

- **Scale.** ~1,000 people by ca. 1000 BC (San José phase, ~10× the next community; 79 ha head village of an eleven-cluster head-and-satellite highland pattern); Rosario population still "only a little over 1,000" — est. ~132 per their Rosario table for the immediate mound zone reading, four times larger than next-largest Yegüih, apex of a two-level Etla hierarchy. Nine Rosario mounds; principal platforms up to 28 m on a side with half-ton stone-block stairways.
- **Against a San José–phase chiefdom.** Blanton et al. argue the earth/sky (fire-serpent/earthquake) **dual symbolism** — found only in male burials and mapped onto the community's two halves — marks a moiety-like [[dual-organization]] with power-sharing (ethnographic analogy: Western Mixe dual-barrio office rotation, Beals 1945; flagged as analogy), not ranked unilineal descent groups: unilineal systems typically show many symbolic groupings, and elite households still did ordinary subsistence and craft work (contra labor-exempt chiefly elites). Wealth is instead tied to exchange-network centrality, big-man-fashion (Kapauku, Plains analogies — flagged). By the **Rosario phase** — elite adobe residence with two-chambered stone tomb (earliest of the later standard Zapotec elite type) replacing communal ritual space on Structure 19/28's hill, a burned temple (termination ritual vs. conflict unresolved), Monument 3 — they allow "chiefdom" as a label, with descent-ranking unconfirmed. Marcus & Flannery's fuller five inferences from Monument 3 (260-day calendar by Rosario; Xòo day-name linking to archaic Zapotec; heart sacrifice; captive nudity; trodden placement) are recorded as attributed.
- **After the founding.** The site declined sharply once [[monte-alban]] was established; see [[monte-alban-founding-debate]] for whose elite (if anyone's) led the move.
