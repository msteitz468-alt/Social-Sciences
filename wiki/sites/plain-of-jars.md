## Discovery and Excavation History

The Plain of Jars is an Iron Age cremation-cemetery landscape of large stone burial jars and menhirs in the Lao uplands (Xieng Khouang plateau), first systematically documented by Madeleine Colani (1935). Higham (2002) treats the complex as an upland mortuary tradition contemporary with lowland Iron Age chiefdoms on the Khorat Plateau and in Bac Bo, and notes that glass and metal finds paralleling Khorat Basin sites recently allow dating and cultural affiliation of the upland groups (as of Higham 2002; R4-031).

The most impressive site Higham reports is Phon Savanh (Ban Ang) at over 1,000 m elevation: more than 250 stone jars. Colani identified a prehistoric crematorium on a central hill and ascribed a richer jar group on a raised area to a ruling group. Recent excavations (as of Higham 2002) found pottery vessels and graves with comminuted bone under a decorated stone slab. The Pu Keng quarry lies about 9 km distant (Sayavongkhamdy and Bellwood 2000) (R4-032).

## Occupation Sequence

Higham places material parallels with the Khorat Iron Age and assigns a date region of roughly 300 BC–AD 300, consistent with the finds (as of Higham 2002; R4-033). Offerings reported at Phon Savanh include glass and carnelian beads, coastal cowries, bronze helices/bells/bracelets, and iron knives, arrowheads, and spearheads. Clay mould fragments indicate local bronze casting; iron slag indicates local iron working (R4-032). One bronze figurine recalls Dong Son forms — association via exchange or stylistic affinity, not identity of polity (Higham 2002).

**Origins hypothesis (attributed).** Higham suggests the origin of Plain of Jars cremation cemeteries “might also relate to Sa Huynh expansion” — a speculative link to the jar-burial coastal tradition of [[sa-huynh-culture]], not a demonstrated population movement (R4-031). Keep this as `associated_with` / hypothesis, not succession identity.

**Exchange-route model (Colani via Higham).** Colani may be correct, Higham argues, that these people controlled exchange routes between southern China, Bac Bo, and burgeoning Khorat chiefdoms, and that salt trade (uplands still a major salt source) funded exotic imports (R4-033). This is theoretical synthesis, not a proven economic reconstruction.

## Significance

The Plain of Jars documents Iron Age complexity in the Lao uplands beyond both Han imperial boundaries and the Mun–Chi moated-settlement heartland. Local metalworking plus coastal and lowland exotics show the uplands as active nodes, not empty margins, in first-millennium BC–early first-millennium AD exchange. The complex is `associated_with` [[dong-son-culture]] drum/figurine parallels, [[sa-huynh-culture]] jar-cremation practice (speculative), and Khorat Iron Age centres including [[noen-u-loke]] via shared glass/metal types (as of Higham 2002).

## Key Debates

- **Sa Huynh expansion vs independent upland tradition** — Higham’s expansion hypothesis is weak and comparative; cremation jar burial is not unique enough to force cultural identity.
- **Salt/exchange control** — Colani’s model remains plausible but under-tested archaeologically as of Higham 2002.
- **Construct status of “Plain of Jars culture”** — the landscape is a site complex / mortuary phenomenon; do not silently equate jars with a single people, language, or genetic population (as of Higham 2002).

## Historiography

Colani’s 1930s colonial-era survey and excavation established the basic inventory (stone jars, menhirs, crematorium interpretation, status differentiation among jar groups). Higham 2002 re-situates the complex within a regional Iron Age of chiefdoms, iron, glass, and maritime–overland exchange, using later Khorat radiocarbon and typological anchors.
