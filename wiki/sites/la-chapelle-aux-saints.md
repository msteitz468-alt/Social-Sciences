## Discovery and Excavation History

La Chapelle-aux-Saints (Bouffia Bonneval), southwestern France, was excavated as part
of a cluster of French discoveries between 1908 and 1921 (alongside Le Moustier, La
Ferrassie, and La Quina) that removed lingering doubt about [[neanderthals]] as a
genuine fossil human type, following the earlier, more disputed finds at Engis (1829–30),
Forbes' Quarry, Gibraltar (1848), and the founding Feldhofer Cave discovery (1856)
(Klein, [[klein-human-career-1999]]). Specific excavators are not named in the source
consulted for this page; see [[unknown]].

## Occupation Sequence

Precise chronometric dates for the La Chapelle-aux-Saints skeleton are not given in the
source consulted for this page; see [[unknown]]. The find falls within the broader
reliably dated Neanderthal fossil range of roughly 127–50/35 ka established elsewhere
in Klein's synthesis.

## Significance

La Chapelle-aux-Saints yielded the celebrated "old man" skeleton, which shows severe
periodontal disease, tooth loss, and osteoarthritis at an estimated age of forty or
younger — pathology Klein reads, together with comparable cases such as Shanidar 1, as
evidence that severely incapacitated Neanderthal individuals depended on "substantial
help and care from their comrades" to survive as long as they did (Klein 1999). The
"old man" became, historiographically, the type specimen around which the popular and
scientific image of the stooped, brutish Neanderthal was originally built — a
reconstruction later revised once the individual's severe arthritis was recognized as
the source of the stooped posture rather than a normal Neanderthal trait.

Burial pits have been reported at La Chapelle-aux-Saints, as well as at the related
sites of La Ferrassie (which held eight graves, described by Klein as "a veritable
cemetery") and Le Roc de Marsal.

## Key Debates

Whether the La Chapelle-aux-Saints burial pit reflects deliberate ritual interment or
simple practical corpse disposal is part of the wider Neanderthal-burial dispute: no
Mousterian grave, in Klein's skeptical reading, contains grave goods clearly
distinguishable from the surrounding artifact and bone assemblage, so accidental
incorporation into fill cannot be excluded, and Mousterian graves generally are far
simpler than securely ritual Upper Paleolithic burials. This dispute is recorded on
[[modern-human-origins-debate]] rather than adjudicated here.
