## Discovery and Excavation History

Laetoli, roughly 45 km south of [[olduvai-gorge]], was technically the first site in eastern Africa to yield australopithecine fossils — a canine tooth in 1935 and a maxillary fragment in 1939 — but their significance went unrecognized until systematic excavations led by Mary Leakey from 1974 to 1979 recovered material from as many as 23 individuals (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Occupation Sequence

The Laetoli hominid fossils are bracketed by radiopotassium dates between 3.76 million years ago (below Tuff 1) and 3.46 million years ago (Tuff 8). Laetoli Hominid 4, a mandible, is the holotype for [[australopithecus|Australopithecus afarensis]]. The Laetoli footprints, preserved on a paleosurface within Tuff 7 (the "footprint tuff"), are attributed to A. afarensis and show a heel strike, a nondivergent big toe, and a distinct arch. No stone artifacts occur in the Laetolil Beds; Acheulean and Developed Oldowan artifacts appear only later, in the overlying and unconformable Olpiro Beds, dated to roughly the time of Olduvai Bed II (after 1.7 Ma).

## Significance

Laetoli provides both the type specimen for Australopithecus afarensis (alongside the [[hadar]] material) and the earliest direct trace evidence for hominin bipedal locomotion in the form of the footprint trail, independent of skeletal anatomy.

## Key Debates

Whether the Laetoli footprints reflect a fully modern human striding gait is contested. Some researchers argue the footprints' heel strike, nondivergent big toe, and arch imply a gait "essentially indistinguishable from that of modern people," while others argue this is difficult to reconcile with the somewhat apelike foot and hip anatomy of the Hadar A. afarensis specimens and propose instead a "bent-hip, bent-knee" gait more like a chimpanzee walking on two legs (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]). Klein presents both positions without adjudicating between them.
