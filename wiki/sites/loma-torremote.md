# Loma Torremote

Middle Formative (First Intermediate Phase Two) nucleated village in the Cuautitlan region, northwestern Basin of Mexico — the Basin's key excavated window on pre-state village organization, and the case on which [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979)]] built their model of ranked, lineage-organized "tribal" society before [[state-formation|state formation]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Found in 1974 under imminent threat of destruction; salvage excavated August 1974 – February 1975 by INAH with Penn State personnel (Rosa Reyna and Robert Santley): intensive surface survey, 22 stratigraphic pits, and one large lateral exposure of three adjacent house compounds. A modern salvage excavation with good horizontal control — far above the Basin's prior Formative-residence record (none published before this project).

## Occupation Sequence

As reported in the source: occupation from Early Horizon Two through First Intermediate Two B, peaking in Phase Two A (Atlamica subphase, ~650–550 BCE) as a ~30-ha nucleated village of an estimated 400–475 households (~2,000–3,000 people at maximum). The settlement unit was the walled adobe **house compound** (wattle-and-daub residence, patios, garden, bell-shaped storage pits), grouped into **compound clusters** of 3–6 sharing walls; the excavated compounds were continuously occupied and rebuilt 5–7 times over roughly a century.

## Significance

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) read the excavation as a stratified record of household evolution: compounds shifting from nuclear- to extended-family scale; one compound (A-1) becoming progressively **ranked** — double the per-capita roofed space, the cluster's only ceremonial shrine sequence, twice the ceramic inventory, a monopoly on obsidian core preparation (blades distributed to neighbors), and 2.2× the storage capacity — interpreted, with explicit ethnographic analogy to lineage societies (Fried 1967), as the local head of a ramage-like lineage acting as "an agent of local redistribution." The village anchors their argument that Phase Two Basin society was organized as ranked lineages/[[chiefdom|chiefdom]]-like polities, the baseline for the tribal-vs-peasant contrast with [[maquixco-bajo|Maquixco Bajo]]. Burial-vs-surface-density comparisons here also calibrated the survey's population-estimation method ([[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]).

## Key Debates

The ramage/lineage reading is analogy-dependent (the authors flag it); whether supracluster ("barrio") organization existed at the site is called "a moot point" in the source.
