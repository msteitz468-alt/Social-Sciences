# Dos Pilas

Late Classic capital of the Petexbatun kingdom in the Pasión zone of the Maya lowlands, Guatemala. As of [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]], Dos Pilas was founded from [[tikal]] in AD 629, later flipped to [[calakmul]] vassalage, inflicted major defeats on Tikal despite small size, and was destroyed by endemic warfare in 761—the most dramatic pre-800 instance of heartland collapse by violence.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Sharer & Traxler (2006) report investigation by a large-scale Vanderbilt University project led by Arthur Demarest, Héctor Escobedo, and Stephen Houston. The Pasión zone has ~25 sites, ≥12 with [[emblem-glyph|emblem glyphs]], including Altar de Sacrificios and Seibal (earlier Harvard Peabody work). Dos Pilas itself covers about 1 km² with three monumental complexes on an east–west axis recalling Preclassic [[nakbe]] and [[el-mirador]] layouts: Main Group (west), Murciélagos Group (royal palace over a cave system), and El Duende Group. Fortification archaeology—hastily built concentric palisades using stones ripped from the royal palace, including a hieroglyphic staircase—documents the 761 fall. Sister capital Aguateca (Takeshi Inomata) preserves a sudden ca. AD 800 palace abandonment and burn with floors still holding artisan, scribal, and domestic materials.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]] unless noted.)*

- **Foundation 629 from Tikal:** Plausible reconstruction: Tikal’s K’inich Muwaan Jol II and son B’alaj Chan K’awiil—who was apparently dispatched in 629 to found a new capital at Dos Pilas, ~120 km southwest of Tikal. Prior Petexbatun had been ruled from several small Early Classic capitals. After B’alaj Chan K’awiil established control, the new kingdom signaled origin by using Tikal’s Mutul [[emblem-glyph]] as its own. As founder he would have been only about four when he “arrived.” One of Tikal’s monument-poor rulers (numbers 23–25, ca. 628–682), K’inich Muwaan Jol II, is known from Dos Pilas texts.
- **648 Calakmul conquest and side-switch:** Beginning 648 (~19 years after founding), Dos Pilas texts record in rapid order a Calakmul attack, death of a Tikal *ajaw*, and B’alaj Chan K’awiil’s acknowledgment he was now *yajaw* (vassal) of Yuknoom the Great ([[calakmul]]). After defeating Tikal’s colony, Yuknoom induced B’alaj Chan K’awiil to switch sides—splitting Tikal’s royal house. Nuun Ujol Chaak (first clearly mentioned 657), quite likely B’alaj Chan K’awiil’s brother or close kin, ruled at Tikal under Calakmul pressure during the hiatus.
- **Tikal–Dos Pilas war cycle 672–679:** In 672 Tikal under Nuun Ujol Chaak attacked Dos Pilas and forced B’alaj Chan K’awiil into exile; for five years Tikal regained the former vassal. In 677 Calakmul drove Tikal from Dos Pilas, restoring B’alaj Chan K’awiil. In 679 B’alaj Chan K’awiil led Dos Pilas to a great victory over Tikal (likely with Calakmul aid); Dos Pilas texts describe “pools of blood” and “piles of heads.”
- **Marriage alliances and Naranjo restoration:** Principal wife from Itzan nobility (~25 km NW); son Itzamnaaj Balam became heir. A second marriage produced Lady Six Sky, sent to [[naranjo]] in 682 to restore its devastated royal dynasty under apparent Calakmul sponsorship—female-line dynastic restoration within the [[kuhul-ajaw]] system.
- **After Calakmul’s 695 reverse:** In 682 B’alaj Chan K’awiil traveled to Calakmul for the 9.12.10.0.0 period ending with Yuknoom the Great; returned 686 to witness accession of Yuknoom Yich’aak K’ak’ (Dos Pilas Stela 13). Succeeded briefly by son Itzamnaaj Balam (dates unclear); brother Itzamnaaj K’awiil accession 698. Tikal’s 695 victory over Calakmul forced Petexbatun to defend its own interests. Significant victory over Tikal in 705 by commander Ucha’an K’in Balam (later Ruler 3)—Jasaw Chan K’awiil’s attempt to reconquer Petexbatun failed.
- **Maximum extent under Ruler 3 and K’awiil Chan K’inich:** Ruler 3 (Ucha’an K’in Balam; royal name unclear) accession 727, possibly regent for minor heir K’awiil Chan K’inich (Panel 19 bloodletting scene with Ruler 3, queen, and Calakmul “guardian of the prince”). Aguateca established as second capital. In 735 conquered Seibal; Yich’aak Balam portrayed under feet on Aguateca Stela 2. Marriage to a Cancuen royal sealed highland-trade alliance (~55 km S). Petexbatun at maximum extent controlled much of the territory between the Pasión and Chixoy Rivers. Ruler 3 died 741 (Aguateca Stela 1).
- **Fall 761 and fragmentation:** Tamarindito inscriptions refer to defeat and exile of K’awiil Chan K’inich in 761. At Dos Pilas concentric palisades were hastily erected over symbols of power (including a hieroglyphic staircase), using stones ripped from the royal palace; Murciélagos throne smashed; defenders held out then overrun; site abandoned. Kingdom broke into petty states, some rulers still claiming *k’uhul mutul ajaw*—divine Tikal king. Aguateca palace destroyed ca. 800; Punta de Chimino’s massive moated defenses (after ca. 760) mark a last stronghold; endemic warfare made life impossible and most of the Petexbatun was abandoned. Seibal later reasserted power after Dos Pilas’s destruction ~AD 800.

## Significance

Dos Pilas is the textbook case that size ≠ power in Classic politics: a small capital defeated [[tikal]] with [[calakmul]] aid and used the Mutul emblem while fighting its parent house. It is also the sharpest archaeological demonstration of Late Classic endemic warfare as a collapse trigger—palisades, smashed thrones, sister-site burn deposits—with little environmental degradation or malnutrition evidence in the Petexbatun as of Sharer & Traxler’s synthesis. Dynastic practice (Calakmul *yajaw* status; Lady Six Sky’s restoration of Naranjo) shows how the [[kuhul-ajaw]] institution could be exported, subordinated, and restored across polities. Layout continuity with Preclassic east–west cosmograms links a Late Classic war capital to earlier lowland urban design.

## Key Debates

- **Founding intent:** Cadet colony of Tikal vs more complex local dynamics; age-at-arrival of B’alaj Chan K’awiil and reliability of later Dos Pilas narratives.
- **Calakmul orchestration:** Degree to which Petexbatun wars were Kan-strategy instruments vs local agency after 695.
- **Warfare as cause vs symptom of collapse:** Petexbatun as authors’ regional case where eighth-century warfare, not drought or soil failure, destroyed the social fabric by ~800; origins may include Calakmul–Tikal aftermath and elite rivalries.
- **Emblem-glyph identity:** Petexbatun use of Tikal’s Mutul glyph and post-761 “petty Mutul kings”—what “Tikal” means when cadet houses claim the same title.
- **Seibal afterlife:** Terminal Classic revival under Ucanal–Caracol sponsorship after Dos Pilas’s fall vs continuity of Pasión commercial geography.

## Historiography

The Vanderbilt Petexbatun Project made Dos Pilas/Aguateca central to 1990s debates on Classic collapse by warfare. Epigraphy (Houston and others) and fortification archaeology converge more tightly here than at many capitals: texts of 648–679 and 761 align with burned, hastily fortified deposits. Inomata’s Aguateca “freeze-frame” palace assemblages are among the best windows on Late Classic elite craft and domestic life. Claims about “pools of blood” victory rhetoric, Lady Six Sky’s Naranjo mission, and little malnutrition evidence are as of Sharer & Traxler 2006 and should remain attributed.
