## Discovery and Excavation History

Asana is an open-air Archaic site in the southern Peru highlands, excavated by Mark Aldenderfer (Aldenderfer 1989, 1990, 1991, 1993a, 1998). The site preserves superimposed structures across three broad phases spanning the Archaic period: Asana I (Early Archaic, 10,500–9,800 B.P.), Asana IV (Middle Archaic, ca. 9,800 B.P.), and Asana VI (Late Archaic, ca. 5,000–4,400 B.P.) (Aldenderfer 1993a:14). Moore (2005) reports these radiocarbon-based phase dates as B.P. figures without stating a calibration status; the calibration status is unstated in Moore 2005 and should be treated as likely uncalibrated pending verification.

## Occupation Sequence

Asana I dwellings comprised at least two oval structures with 4.2–5 m² floor area, indicated by post molds and unprepared floors, with small internal hearths interpreted as for warmth; cooking activity occurred outside the structures, indicated by external ash, fire-cracked rock, and burned bone concentrations.

Asana IV dwellings were slightly larger (4.1–5.9 m²), with floors of puddled white clay or a clay/sand mix and a post-mold pattern suggesting a lightweight pole frame covered by brush or hides. Shallow ash-filled hearths inside these structures are interpreted by Aldenderfer as associated with sleeping rather than cooking; extramural activity areas are indicated by hearths, ground-stone tools, faunal remains, and lithic debris.

By Asana VI (Late Archaic), architecture changed markedly: two feature classes are present, dwellings and enclosures. Dwellings were oval or rectangular with unprepared floors and roughly twice the floor area of earlier dwellings (8.4–12 m²). Enclosures were rectilinear arrangements of small post molds with prepared clay floors.

## Significance

Aldenderfer (1990, 1991, 1993a, 1993b) interprets the Asana VI enclosures, associated offerings, and a possible altar stone as evidence of Archaic ritual practice — among the earliest candidate ritual architecture in the region. Moore (2005) also uses Asana in a soundscape reconstruction (his Figure 2.19, based on Aldenderfer 1993a), grouping it with [[paloma]] and Acha-2 as sites where dwelling spacing allowed conversation to flow house-to-house while whispers remained private. Moore interprets this acoustic pattern as consistent with egalitarian social organization: no expectation of hoarding, storage, or status-item accumulation, and conflict resolution through fission, violence, or talk rather than institutionalized hierarchy.

## Key Debates

No named debate page currently treats Asana specifically. The interpretation of the Asana VI enclosures as ritual space (Aldenderfer) versus purely domestic reuse is noted by Moore as an attributed interpretive claim rather than a settled finding. The soundscape-based egalitarianism argument is part of Moore's broader argument, previewed in his introduction, that Formative Period monumental architecture (see [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]]) marks a later, distinct social transformation from the Archaic pattern seen at Asana.
