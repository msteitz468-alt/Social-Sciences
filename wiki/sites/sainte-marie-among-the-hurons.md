## Discovery and Excavation History

Sainte-Marie among the Hurons was excavated in two campaigns of markedly unequal documentary quality, a fact Bruce Trigger (*The Children of Aataentsic*, 1976; 1987 reprint) treats as itself a reliability concern for interpreting the site. Kenneth E. Kidd excavated 1941–1943 and produced a detailed, published report that Trigger calls "one of the key publications of historic site archaeology in North America." Wilfrid Jury excavated 1947–1951 but never published a detailed report; his reconstruction nonetheless became the basis for the Ontario government's public reconstruction of the site. (FINDING/SOURCE-RELIABILITY, attributed)

The two excavators' interpretations of the European Compound conflict on several points: wall construction, building layout, and — most visibly — the function of a stone "lock system," which Kidd interprets as a storage pit and Jury as canal locks. Trigger sides with Kidd, citing W. A. Russell's 1965 critique that the proposed locks are structurally implausible and too short to have accommodated canoes, and states that Kidd's interpretation "deserves more careful consideration than Jury['s]." (SOURCE-RELIABILITY/POSITION, attributed) This is a genuine dispute over the reliability of two 20th-century archaeological reconstructions of the same standing walls rather than a live theoretical debate; see Key Debates below.

## Occupation Sequence

**Founding (1639).** Jérôme Lalemant, superior of the Huron mission from August 1638, proposed abandoning the Jesuits' dispersed residence within existing Huron villages in favor of a single, permanent, French-style mission headquarters independent of any Huron settlement — modeled explicitly on the Jesuit "reduction" settlements founded in Paraguay among the Guaraní (1609) and on the Sillery reserve near Quebec, founded in 1638 for the Montagnais. (FINDING, attributed) Construction began in summer 1639 on the east bank of the Wye River between Mud Lake and Georgian Bay, on land belonging to the Ataronchronon; only a single building went up the first year. Jury identified possible remains of a Huron-style house (roughly 40 by 20 feet) beneath later French construction, though Trigger flags this identification as uncertain and never published in detail. In autumn 1639 the Jesuits abandoned their Ossossane residence, and Lalemant divided Huron country into four (later five, adding the Tionnontate) mission districts operating from central points, with Sainte-Marie as headquarters for the Ataronchronon; the new system began operation on 1 November 1639.

**Personnel and construction, 1640–1649.** French personnel in Huron country grew from roughly 13 priests and 14 lay assistants before 1640 to a peak of about 18 priests and 46 lay assistants in the winter of 1648–49; Sainte-Marie at times sheltered nearly as many Europeans as Quebec had held in 1629. Personnel comprised priests; lay brothers (barred from carrying arms, developing specialized trades such as tailor, blacksmith, gardener, and cook after 1640, never more than four at a time); *donnés* — unpaid lay volunteers who forwent wages and trading rights, growing from two in 1641 to twenty-three in 1648, the first being Robert Le Coq (contract 23 December 1639); hired workmen (paid, with trading rights); and soldiers (sent 1644–45, 1648–49, 1649–50). Of the twenty-four priests who served the Huron mission from 1634, nineteen died there or remained until the mission ended in 1650. Several *donnés* and workmen — Pierre Boucher, Guillaume Couture, Charles Le Moyne, Médard Chouart Des Groseilliers — went on to prominent careers as colonial officials, diplomats, and fur traders, which Trigger reads as evidence the mission functioned as a training ground in Indigenous-relations skills. (FINDING, attributed)

Construction proceeded in stages: a European-style dwelling by 1640; a Huron-style longhouse for Indian devotions and a chapel by 1641; a hospital, kept separate from the priests' quarters and admitting women, before autumn 1642; a public church and consecrated cemetery by autumn 1642. Fortification funding traces to Cardinal Richelieu's 1642 grant, though explicit references to actual fortifications date only to spring 1649. Excavation showed the site was roughly 800 feet long and over 200 feet wide at its north end, divided into a southern "Indian Compound" and a northern "European Compound," separated by a palisade and ditch. The Indian Compound held a longhouse for Huron visitors (later Christian Indians), two European-style buildings in colombage pierroté construction that Jury identified as a hospital and a church (70 by 27 feet), and an adjacent cemetery with twenty Indian graves showing mixed Christian (rosaries) and traditional (grave goods, wampum, dog remains) burial practice, including one coffin combining a Christian-style flexed burial with a native bundle burial — material evidence, per Jury and Jury (1954), that the Jesuits had not fully suppressed Huron mortuary custom even within a mission cemetery. (FINDING, attributed)

**Reduction plan and its failure.** Lalemant's 1641 plan to draw Christian Huron families to reside permanently at Sainte-Marie in a Paraguay-style reduction failed against the strength of existing Huron village and clan-segment ties; by 1643 the Jesuits instead re-established or expanded residences within existing villages (Ossossane, Teanaostaiae, Scanonaenrat, Taenhatentaron, and, after Contarea's 1647 abandonment, a new Attignawantan mission at Sainte-Magdelaine). Mission-to-village assignments shifted year to year according to congregation size and administrative convenience rather than Huron tribal organization. Trigger argues (against Kidd's suggestion that Sainte-Marie was meant as a "model farm" for acculturating the Huron to European agriculture, animal husbandry, and pottery) that no evidence shows such a policy was ever implemented; the primary purpose of transplanting French material culture, in Trigger's reading, was to make the Frenchmen's own lives easier and safer, not — in the 1640s — active acculturation. (TRIGGER-CLAIM, attributed)

**Subsistence economy.** Sainte-Marie's economy centered on maize as the main crop from 1640, with possible wheat, barley, and oats; European pigs from about 1644; cattle (calves 1646, a heifer 1648) providing pork, beef, and milk by 1649; wine from wild grapes from 1637; and on-site blacksmithing producing nails, trade axes, and gun repair. Lacking horses or oxen, Jesuit farming remained hoe-based/horticultural, like Huron farming, rather than plow agriculture. (FINDING, attributed)

**Fortification, abandonment, and Sainte-Marie II.** By 1649 the Wye-River site was fortified with French-built towers and musketeer positions, considered impregnable per the missionary Bressani. Following the March 1649 Iroquois destruction of Taenhatentaron (St. Ignace) and St. Louis — in which Jean de Brébeuf and Gabriel Lalemant were captured, tortured, and killed — and the subsequent flight and self-destruction of all remaining Huron villages within two weeks, the Jesuits themselves abandoned and burned Sainte-Marie on 14–17 May 1649 to deny its use to the Iroquois or the Dutch, after loading a raft with livestock, supplies, and portable objects, including a cannon, for the move to Gahoendoe (Christian Island) in Georgian Bay, at the invitation of Huron headmen who promised even traditionalists there would convert. A new fortified Sainte-Marie II was built on Gahoendoe: a stone fort roughly 120 feet square with bastions, sited over 100 feet from shore, with a stone-lined cistern about 9 feet square, its masonry finished by the end of summer 1649. (FINDING, attributed)

Roughly 300 families were on Gahoendoe when the Jesuits arrived; drought damaged crops, and although over 100 cabins were eventually built and population was estimated (probably exaggerated, per Ragueneau) at 6,000–8,000, a figure Trigger considers more plausibly a few thousand, the island's food supply from natural resources proved inadequate. Famine conditions during the winter of 1649–50 led to eating moss, bark, and fungus, and — in cases Trigger reports as done secretly given the "abhorrence of cannibalizing kin" — to disinterring and eating the bodies of the dead. More than 1,400 baptisms occurred between mid-March and August 1649, and roughly 1,600 more by March 1650, mostly on Gahoendoe. The remaining Huron abandoned Gahoendoe in spring 1651, eventually reaching Quebec.

## Significance

Sainte-Marie anchors Trigger's account of the transformation of the Jesuit mission from a network of residences embedded in existing Huron villages to an independent, fortified European enclave — and of the failure of the Paraguay-style reduction model to take root among a tribally organized society with strong village and clan-segment loyalties. Its excavation (principally Kidd's) is treated by Trigger as a model of historic-site archaeology, and the site's material record (the Indian Compound cemetery in particular) provides direct evidence of religious syncretism that documentary sources alone understate.

## Key Debates

**Kidd/Jury reconstruction dispute.** As described above (Discovery and Excavation History), Trigger treats the disagreement between Kidd's published, detailed excavation report and Jury's unpublished reconstruction — used as the basis for the public reconstruction of the site — as a source-reliability dispute over 20th-century archaeological interpretation, centered on the "lock system," wall construction, and building layout. He sides with Kidd, citing Russell's 1965 structural critique of Jury's canal-lock reading.
