# Hemudu

Mid-Holocene wet-rice farming settlement on Hangzhou Bay (Hang-Chou Bay in Mithen’s spelling), lower Yangtze, China. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it exemplifies mature Yangtze delta rice agriculture with sophisticated wooden architecture, still mixed with hunting, fishing, and gathering.

**Identity caution:** Hemudu is an archaeological culture/site label in broader literature; do not equate occupants with later ethnic or linguistic groups (e.g. “proto-Austronesian”) without separate attributed argument. This page follows Mithen’s site-level description only.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: excavated in the **1970s**. Waterlogged conditions preserved wooden architecture and organic tools. Mithen places Hemudu in a Yangtze rice-and-pottery narrative alongside middle-Yangtze sites (Pengtoushan, Bashidang, etc.) treated elsewhere in the same chapters.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Mithen 2003, ch.39.)*

1. **Regional prelude (~6700 BCE):** Yangtze delta settlements harvest **wild** rice only; the idea of sowing on the flood plain and tending seedlings has not yet arrived from upstream middle Yangtze. When flood-plain cultivation spreads, coastal wetlands become ideal for rice-farming expansion.
2. **Hemudu village (~5000 BCE):** houses more than 20 metres long, built with wooden planks using **mortise-and-tenon joinery** and raised on piles; **spades made from water-buffalo scapulae**; domestic water buffalo and pigs; large rice quantities implying **nursery transplanting**; ornate pottery; economy still mixed with hunting, fishing, and gathering.
3. **Pottery–rice coupling (theoretical in Mithen):** near-simultaneous pottery and rice cultivation unlikely coincidence—vessels for steaming/boiling (cooked rice in pot at Hemudu); pottery enabled storage/cooking that encouraged further cultivation, analogous to Fertile Crescent grinding stones for wheat/barley.

## Significance

- Classic lower-Yangtze wet-rice Neolithic site with exceptional wooden architecture.
- Demonstrates transplanting-level rice agriculture by ~5000 BCE in the delta, downstream of earlier middle-Yangtze experiments.
- Case for mixed farming–foraging economies rather than abrupt pure agricultural replacement.
- Comparative node for independent East Asian agricultural pathways vs SW Asian packages.

## Key Debates

- Timing of wild-to-domestic rice transition in the delta vs middle Yangtze (Mithen: idea spreads from upstream).
- Whether pottery co-evolution with rice is causal or parallel (theoretical).
- Degree of sedentism and social complexity implied by long pile houses (not fully developed in the 2003 Hemudu paragraphs).
- Later linguistic/genetic associations with Hemudu culture—out of scope for this Mithen-only densification; keep four-way non-identity.
