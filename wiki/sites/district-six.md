# District Six

Inner-city Cape Town neighborhood, built up after slave emancipation (1838) partly with invested compensation money, named in 1867, and razed under apartheid's Group Areas legislation — now the emblematic South African case of forced removal, contested heritage, and memorial archaeology. Hall (2000) treats it as the culminating case of his "archaeology of the modern world": a landscape where the colonial past is actively recreated in present identity politics.

## Occupation Sequence: History and Destruction

A cosmopolitan "doorstep dormitory" for inner-city industry — West Indian, Coloured, Scots, St Helenian, Lithuanian, Tamil, and Muslim residents (Nasson 1990) — combining marked inequality with dense mutuality; resident writer Richard Rive called it both home and "a ripe, raw and rotten slum." Proclamation 43 (February 1966) declared it for white ownership under the Group Areas framework; the government estimated ~62,000 residents, three-quarters tenants, all but ~1,000 classified "coloured" (Horrell 1967). Clearance, planned for five years, took until 1984 at six times the estimated cost (Hart 1990). Churches, mosques, and schools were left standing amid the demolition; Rive called the result "South Africa's Hiroshima." Redevelopment for white residents collapsed under publicity pressure; the land went to military/police housing and the whites-only Cape Technikon. The Hands Off District Six campaign (from 1986, against BP's proposed "open" redevelopment) held the ground until the August 1997 Land Court ruling returned the area to former residents — itself contested between council-managed redevelopment and the District Six Restitution Front's direct-restitution position.

## Discovery and Excavation History: Archaeology and Memory Work

Little was known of the 19th-century urban fabric beyond municipal surveys until house excavations revealed plans, narrow corridors, and successive tenant modifications (Hall 1994b; Hart and Halkett 1996). The 1996 Tennant Street midden collection ran as an educational project with local schoolchildren, some descended from the dispossessed community (Clift 1996). The District Six Museum (opened 1994) centres on the salvaged street signs — preserved secretly by a white demolition worker and donated as "an act of personal reparation" — and a floor map on which former residents mark their homes. Commemorations Hall documents as participant-photographer: the February 1996 candlelight pilgrimage on Proclamation 43's 30th anniversary; the September 1997 sculpture festival built from demolition debris.

## Significance

For Hall, District Six demonstrates that memory is "historically conditioned, changing colour and shape according to the emergencies of the moment" (Raphael Samuel, quoted as the chapter's frame). The site's cultural afterlife is itself plural and contested — positions Hall records without adjudication: Sue Williamson's debris installations were reviewed both as exploitative commodification and as icon-making; *District Six – the Musical* (Kramer and Petersen, 1987–90, 350,000+ audience) was intended as indictment but received nostalgically; celebration of a "merry" Coon-Carnival District Six could shade into accommodationist "coloured culture" politics (Prah 1997), against the sustained-opposition tradition. La Guma's *A Walk in the Night* (1962) and Rive's *Buckingham Palace, District Six* (1986) supply Hall's literary [[third-space]] readings.

## Key Debates

Restitution vs. council redevelopment (1997, above); the politics of memorializing apartheid-era destruction — compare the whites-only 1987–88 tercentenary spectacles ([[heritage-management]]) staged while District Six's rubble was still fresh.
