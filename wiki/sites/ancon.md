# Ancón–Chillón (Ancón Bay)

The 25 km desert littoral from the mouth of the Río Chillón north past the (now-dry) Bay of Ventanilla to sheltered Ancón Bay — [[moseley-michael|Moseley's]] own primary fieldwork area and the empirical core of the [[moseley-maritime-foundations-1975|Maritime Foundations]] argument. Andean foothills lie 10–20 km inland. As of Moseley 1975 the Ancón Bay flanks carried a resort community; preceramic basal deposits were continually re-exposed by bulldozing and construction — without which, Moseley notes, "the scope of the early maritime settlement would have gone undetected."

## Discovery and Excavation History

Moseley opened small excavations at six sites — Encanto, Pampa, Banco Verde, Camino, Punta Grande, and the Tank Site — supplemented by [[lanning-edward|Edward Lanning's]] earlier survey and work (esp. the Yacht Club site, 1963/1965, not reported in detail) and [[engel-frederic|Frederic Engel's]] work at [[el-paraiso|El Paraíso]] on the Chillón south bank. Excavation was midden-analysis-driven: small stratified cuts assessing dietary and technological change through time.

## Occupation Sequence

Moseley's three-stage local scheme (built on, and partly disputing, Lanning's periodization; see [[andean-chronology]]):

- **Lithic Stage** — hunter-gatherer entry; lomas-oriented foraging "by about 7000 BC" (Lanning); latest phase **Encanto** beginning ~3600 BC. Sites sited at elevations between lomas stands and the sea (Moseley's "compromise settlement pattern"). Lanning located 13 Encanto sites 3–7 km inland plus a quarry. Encanto type-site cut: shells, chipping debris, points, manos/metates, twined sedge mats, small-fish vertebrae (no nets/hooks), minor cervid bone, gourd, 23 squash seeds, lomas seeds; radiocarbon 2770±80 BC (UCLA-97, uncal).
- **Cotton Preceramic Stage** — begins ~2500 BC, defined by cotton artifacts (Engel's horizon marker). Full marine subsistence with no lomas holdovers by the Pampa phase; industrial cultigens (cotton, gourd) and minor food plants (squash, legumes, guava, capsicum). Textile sub-phases by twining technique: **Playa Hermosa** (single-warp) → **Conchas** (adds split-paired) → **Gaviota** (plural-warp). Basal Pampa Cut 2: 2500±110 BC (GX-1134, uncal). By the Gaviota phase, three large sedentary communities ~10 km apart — El Paraíso, Punta Grande, Tank Site.
- **Ceramic Stage** — first pottery ~1750 BC; six periods per Rowe (1960), earliest the Initial Period. Tank Site Cut 1 spans the transition: preceramic Level 4 passes conformably up into ceramic Level 3, pottery essentially the only marker separating physically similar deposits; woven cloth replaces twined; Level 1 dates 1120±95 BC and 1325±95 BC (GX-1233/GX-1234, uncal), pottery linked to the Huaca La Florida style dated ~1750–1650 BC (Patterson & Moseley 1968).

**Dietary trajectory (Tank Site Cut 1):** lucuma, then large tubers, then peanuts and pacae appear up-section; no maize at any level — Moseley holds the sample large enough that maize's absence is meaningful (attributed).

## Significance

The type-region for the maritime-subsistence argument: dense, stratified middens dominated by mollusks, sea fowl, and fish, with cotton fishing tackle (nets, shell hooks, stone line weights, gourd floats) and no evidence of dietary dependence on farming through the whole Preceramic. The site-by-site transition (abandonment of coastal Punta Grande/El Paraíso, rise of inland Huaca La Florida) is Moseley's principal evidence for an Initial Period inland shift of population and corporate authority once irrigation began.

## Key Debates

Recorded as positions (Moseley reports each; unresolved in text):

- **Lomas desiccation:** Lanning (1963, 1967) argued lomas were ~10× more extensive early and that climatic desiccation drove abandonment of foraging; Craig & Psuty (1968, 1971) challenged the evidence at Paracas; Moseley "remain[s] in doubt," calling Lanning's hypothesis plausible but the evidence tenuous.
- **Transhumance and early farming:** Lynch (1971, 1973) proposed seasonal lomas–highland transhumance aided cultigen transfer; Moseley counters it would have created scheduling conflict retarding early farming.
- **Microcyclic constraint:** Parsons (1970) posited seasonal prey variation and red-tide-like blooms as constraints; Moseley disputes both as unsupported by the Ancón–Chillón record.

## Historiography

Rests on Moseley's own excavation reporting (1975) plus Lanning's and Engel's central-coast work; radiocarbon assays are uncalibrated (1975 convention) and Rowe (1966) is cited on the inconsistency of C-14 dates across the Preceramic–Ceramic boundary. Treat the stage dates as weakly anchored.
