## Discovery and Excavation History

Caral, in the Río Supe drainage of coastal Peru, was investigated principally by Ruth Shady Solís. As of [[scarre-human-past-2013|Scarre ed. 2013]], it is the largest fully excavated Preceramic monumental center in Supe and one of roughly 17 early monumental centers concentrated in that small valley. [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]] places Caral within the **Supe Tradition** of Preceramic public platform display — named for the valley with 17 civic-ceremonial centers ranging 10–>70 ha, the largest prepottery monumental concatenation on the continent (Lima museum survey). Related coastal [[aspero|Aspero]] at the Supe mouth has basal dates reported 3055–2558 BC (calibration status unstated in Scarre; Moseley: Huaca de los Sacrificios late phases avg 2857 BC, basal older).

## Occupation Sequence

### Scale and plan (sources differ slightly — report both)

- **Scarre ed. 2013:** Caral covers 66 ha; Pirámide Mayor 18 m high, 160 × 150 m base, fronted by large circular sunken court; smallest surviving mound ~10 m high, ~60 × 45 m. Around 2400 BC (Shady and Leyva 2003), a central authority reconfigured six previously idiosyncratic platform temples to face a single grand nodal plaza — read as advancing socio-political integration (attributed).
- **Moseley 2001:** Caral ~50 ha, 22 km inland at 350 m elevation; main mound ~140 m wide, three-step ends, circular sunken court, sunken stair to atrium with natural lance-like obelisk 1.70 × 0.45 m as sacred huaca; six major mounds each unique in layout, form, orientation, and size (intentionally distinguishing constituencies). Amphitheater complex reverses size relations: 29 m diameter deep court, stepped white/yellow plastered walls, causeway, two rectangular plazas, modest platform; "fire altar" ~4 m diam. circular dais with vents.

### Economy (Moseley reporting Shady)

Shady’s plant suite: achira, sweet potato, squash, beans, pacae, guayaba; industrial cotton and gourd; abundant **marine protein** (anchovies in storage, shellfish) but **no fishing tackle** found. Shady argues carbohydrate–protein **exchange** of cultigens for seafood — if valley-wide, foreshadows Initial Period subsistence specialization. Scarre notes only 15 maize cobs, one sweet potato, one achira tuber in the extensively excavated sample, with plant assemblage dominated by fruit and cotton — Shady and Leyva (2003) interpret dearth of agrarian staples as consistent with part-time/seasonal residence (attributed). Maize is more sporadic regionally (Aspero small cache; Los Gavilanes more common).

### Social organization (Shady via Moseley)

Two residential classes postulated: (1) majority — compacted floors, wooden supports, reed/mat/thatch; (2) minority — plastered floors/walls, masonry + vertical beams for large roofs, immediately adjacent to platforms as privileged managers. Shady postulates class-structured hereditary elites and that 17 lower-Supe complexes required outside labor; Supe as early sacrosanct landscape with multiple supernaturals attracting disparate populations — precedent for pluralistic pantheons and pilgrimage centers. Segmentary lineage/moiety models fit smaller Preceramic sites (e.g. Rio Seco twin platforms); multi-platform Caral/Aspero imply multifaceted constituencies.

Aspero contrast (Moseley): 11 small mounds 1–2 m + 6 major platforms, 15 ha dark midden; Harvard 1970s work; infant burial with 500 beads; Huaca de los Idolos cache ≥13 unbaked clay figurines (11 female).

## Significance

Key case for origins of Andean civilization: large-scale monumental construction, sunken-court ceremonialism (*plaza hundida* tradition), and social differentiation before pottery and before (on recovered evidence) a full staple-agriculture calorie base. Anchors both [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]] debates and Supe-centered alternative models. Inland location on shallow-incised Supe with valley-floor springs supports simple extensive irrigation unlike deeper southern valleys — Moseley’s exception to "few interior Preceramic sites."

## Key Debates

Three positions (no wiki-voice adjudication):

1. **Maritime Hypothesis (Moseley 1975 / 2001)** — marine calories underwrite monuments; cultivation especially industrial; Caral’s seafood without tackle = exchange from shore specialists.
2. **Supe Hypothesis (Shady 2006, as summarized Scarre/Moseley)** — inland farming + managerial elites integrate fishing/farming and monopolize arable monument lands; class structure at Caral.
3. **Multi-stranded / cultural-choice synthesis** (Haas & Creamer 2006; Scarre handbook) — agriculture under-detected or deliberately secondary; Ecuador exchange shows farming awareness.

Additional architectural debates: Williams’s Supe pit-platform evolutionary sequence (detached pits → connected → sunken-court-dominant → low square platforms); *plaza hundida* ideology (emergence from earth / Pacha Mama) as Moseley speculation.

Environmental curtailment of Preceramic monument tradition ~1800 BC (Sandweiss et al. 2009 via Scarre) = contributing/concurrent factors, not sole `caused_by`.

## Historiography

Shady’s work transformed Supe from peripheral to central in world prehistory narratives; Moseley 2001 incorporates Caral into MFAC-compatible exchange framing rather than abandoning maritime foundations. Handbook Scarre ed. 2013 packages the Key Controversy for students. Primary Shady monographs not yet fully ingested — figures and arguments as of Moseley 2001 / Scarre 2013.
