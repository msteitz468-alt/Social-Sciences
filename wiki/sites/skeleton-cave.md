# Skeleton Cave

## Discovery and Excavation History

**Skeleton Cave** (also Skull Cave, Apache Cave) is treated in *[[whittlesey-vanishing-river-1997|Vanishing River]]* Chapter 7 (Alan Ferg and Norm Tessman) as an archival and material-culture case study — **not** a modern single-context excavation. On **28 December 1872**, more than 200 troopers and scouts of the U.S. Fifth Cavalry attacked people sheltered in the cave high above the Salt near Fish Creek. Period accounts report on the order of **~75–80 killed** (men, women, and children), roughly **18–20 captives**, one Pima scout dead, and no soldiers wounded; variant casualty figures exist (official vs Bourke). The army burned stores and left bodies unburied. Later episodes of photography, collecting, and looting (1890s–1990s) dispersed remains and artifacts; a Fort McDowell reburial party is documented in the 1910s–1920s; the site was listed on the National Register of Historic Places (**21 February 1991**) after a 1990 nomination. Carlos Montezuma-as-survivor claims are rejected in the LVAP chapter.

## Occupation Sequence

Load-bearing significance is the **1872 massacre and aftermath**. Ferg and Tessman use the assemblage toward identifying [[yavapai|Yavapai]] material culture and differentiating it from [[western-apache|Western Apache]] — and show how difficult that differentiation is.

## Significance

1. Documentary and material record of colonial-period military violence in central Arizona.
2. Test case for **ethnic identification** from artifacts when ethnohistoric labels are themselves fluid (Nanni-chaddi as probable home chief; Delshay not killed there; discussion of Wah-poo-eta, Nantaje, and mixed-group possibilities).
3. Ethics of remains, publicity, museum holdings (Phoenix Museum of History, Pitt Rivers, and other repositories noted in the chapter), and public interpretation.

Whittlesey notes that Ferg and Tessman ultimately lean on **geographic propinquity** (southeastern Yavapai traditional territory) for ethnic assignment — itself evidence of identification limits.
