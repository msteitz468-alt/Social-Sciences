## Discovery and Excavation History

Rojdi sits on the Bhadar River in Saurashtra, at the geographic center of that region. Possehl (2002) himself excavated the site, which serves as the type-site for a regional variant he coined "Sorath Harappan." Because Possehl is both excavator and the source's author, this is a first-hand excavation account rather than a secondary synthesis of another team's report — a distinct reliability profile from most other sites treated here, where Possehl synthesizes others' fieldwork. The chronology rests on 20 radiocarbon dates, a comparatively dense dating program for an Indus-tradition site.

## Occupation Sequence

Continuous occupation from roughly 2500 to 1700 BCE, divided into three radiocarbon-dated phases: Rojdi A (2500–2200 BCE), Rojdi B (2200–1900 BCE), and Rojdi C (1900–1700 BCE). Calibration status for these radiocarbon dates is not stated in the source. Possehl reports that this dating program substantially revised the prior (pre-1982) assumption that Saurashtra's Harappan-tradition sites were only Posturban or Late in date — the Rojdi sequence pushed documented occupation back roughly 500 years, situating it within the Mature Harappan florescence rather than only after it.

## Significance

Rojdi grew from about 2.5 ha to roughly 7.5 ha at its Rojdi C peak. Distinctively, construction throughout is stone-and-mud, with no baked brick and no wells, bathing platforms, or drains of the kind found at Sindh and Punjab sites such as [[harappa]] and [[mohenjo-daro]] — a marked contrast in the "standardization" package usually associated with Mature Harappan urbanism. Subsistence at Rojdi rests on more than 70 identified plant taxa, dominated by millets and other drought-resistant species rather than the wheat/barley staples typical of the Indus core, with a cattle (zebu)-dominant faunal assemblage.

Rojdi anchors Possehl's own coined regional term "Sorath Harappan," which he states explicitly: "I coined the term Sorath Harappan" — the term should be attributed directly to Possehl, not treated as a pre-existing field category.

The site's most significant interpretive contribution concerns the civilization's decline. Possehl documents that Rojdi expanded — from 2.5 to 7.5 ha — at approximately 1900 BCE, precisely the period during which Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, and other Sindh/Punjab settlements were shrinking or being abandoned. He uses this divergence as an explicit caution against the standard "collapse" narrative for the Indus Civilization, writing that "the so-called eclipse or end of the Indus Civilization might well be an overstatement and in need of radical reexamination." This is Possehl's own revisionist reading, attributed to him, not an established wiki-voice consensus.

## Key Debates

Rojdi's counter-trend expansion at ~1900 BCE is the central evidentiary anchor for the [[indus-transformation-debate]] over whether the civilization's Posturban changes constitute a "collapse," a regional reorganization, or simply a shift in where population and material investment concentrated. Possehl's position — that regional divergence (contraction in Sindh/Punjab alongside expansion in Sorath) undercuts any simple collapse narrative — should be recorded on that debate page as a distinct, named position attributed to Possehl. The site's lack of baked brick and standardized urban infrastructure otherwise associated with the [[harappan-civilization]] also bears on how uneven "standardization" was distributed across the civilization's regional Domains.

## Wright (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]], *[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]* 2010, chs. 6, 11.)*

**Crop regime and African millets.** Weber's (2003) seed-remain studies contrast Harappa, where winter crops (wheat/barley) predominated in both Pre-urban and Urban periods with intensifying millet cultivation, against Rojdi, where drought-resistant summer crops dominated the subsistence base throughout, supplemented by barley (Weber 1991). Near the end of the Urban period and into the Post-urban period, African millets appear at Rojdi — one of the clearest instances Wright cites of a non-indigenous crop package reaching the Indus world, though the exact route of introduction (African vs. Chinese origin, and the diffusion path to peninsular India) remains an open question she does not resolve.

**Continuity through the Post-urban decline.** Wright treats Rojdi as the central counter-case to the standard Sindh/Gujarat decline narrative: while Dholavira was abandoned and Kuntasi (an industrial center) shrank to "a jejune shadow of the once flourishing urbanites" (Dhavalikar 1992:81) by 1700 BCE, Sorath Harappan sites as a group (Possehl 1991, 2002) show continuity, reflecting semi-independence from the Harappan core during the Urban period. Rojdi itself was continuously occupied and rebuilt into the Post-urban period, part of 231 Post-urban sites recorded in the region (Mughal 1992a:217); Shaffer and Lichtenstein (1999:254) report 96% of regional settlements changing occupation status and 93% being newly founded — "considerable settlement mobility and influx of human populations." Rissman's (1985) faunal studies at Rojdi and the associated site of Oriyo Timbo suggest a gradual "normalization" or adaptive process already underway during the Urban period, before the wider Post-urban transition.

**Household-level crop processing.** Post-urban seed assemblages at both Harappa and Rojdi show high densities of chaff and weed by-products, read by Wright (following Weber 1999, 2003:181) as evidence of a shift to household-level, rather than centralized or communal, crop processing — interpreted as a marker of political and social reorganization "rather than an environmental crisis."
