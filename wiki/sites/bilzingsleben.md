# Bilzingsleben

Open-air Middle Pleistocene travertine/shore locality in the Wipper valley, eastern Germany; central case for camp structure, “gatherings,” and the limits of social archaeology in [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999|Gamble 1999]], Ch.4.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Excavated by Dietrich Mania and colleagues; ~600 m² excavated with interpretive focus on a ~355 m² shore-terrace “campsite” sealed by travertine (as of Gamble 1999 summarizing Mania). Hominid 1 occurs in redeposited fan deposits; shore sands are claimed as primary context for Hominid 2. Gamble pairs Bilzingsleben with [[dolni-vestonice|Dolní Věstonice]] as contrast types: Lower Palaeolithic **gathering** (lake, trees, stone and bone anvils) versus Upper Palaeolithic **social occasion** (huts, graves, ovens, middens) (Gamble 1999, attributed).

## Occupation Sequence

**Dating (as of Gamble 1999):** Holsteinian interglacial II horizon; ²³⁰Th-²³⁴U and ESR (Schwarcz et al. 1988; Mania 1995) place Bilzingsleben II between ~350–320 and 414–280 ka BP—confirming Holsteinian complex position but **not resolving OIS 11 vs OIS 9**. Warm oak-mixed forest-steppe with southern exotics is reported. ≥3 hominids (skull fragments/teeth): Vlček (1978) classified adults as *Homo erectus*; others prefer *H. heidelbergensis* (via Gamble).

**Fauna (Mania via Gamble):** identified sample dominated by rhino (27%), beaver (14%), red deer (13%), elephant (12%), bear (11%); almost no carnivores/gnawing. Elephant teeth show a catastrophic age profile dominated by young (<14 yrs, many 2–6); long bones heavily fragmented for bone-tool raw material. Mania interprets rhino as specialized hunting with selected parts brought in (~50% young). Gamble notes neither elephant age profile necessarily equals hunting (vs prime-age hunting profiles later).

## Significance

### Mania’s campsite reconstruction

Mania reconstructs six activity zones including three semi-circular bone/travertine “shelters” (blocks/bones as windbreak weights), fireplaces, workshop, a **central paved area** (~3–4 m² slabs from ~200 m away) with bison skull and quartzite “anvil,” pebble alignment, diverse local lithics (93% flint <10 km; many pieces <1 cm), bone/wood tools, and “engraved” bones with rhythmic marks Mania regards as symbolic (Gamble 1999, attributed). Lithic types include Keilmesser, scrapers, Tayac/Quinson points, denticulates, notches (Weber 1986; Mania 1995 via Gamble).

### Gamble’s critique and re-reading

Gamble treats Mania’s campsite-as-institutions reconstruction as an **extreme and unique** case, not proof that early Palaeolithic social life equalled Upper Palaeolithic camps. He refuses both naive social archaeology (huts/hearths = society) and the conclusion of “no society,” seeking alternatives that do not require those paraphernalia as sole social proof (Gamble 1999, attributed). Stapert ring-and-sector analysis of circular “shelter 2” (184 objects) shows a unimodal peak at 1.5–2 m—open-air drop zone around a focus, not tent-wall bimodality; Gamble, like Stapert at Rheindahlen, suggests a **tree** as economical focus (alder/box wood, beaver, macaque cited as environmental support), with fallen-tree burning possibly explaining charcoal-rich third structure—while noting hearths themselves are not securely established for the period (circularity flagged).

**Speculative social reading (Gamble, explicitly marked):** performances at gatherings; anvils carried in and positioned form ~8 m interaction rings; setting an anvil is the “attaching ritual” commencing structured rhythmic activity; co-presence and manufacture bind [[intimate-effective-extended-networks|intimate/effective networks]] but leave no lasting structure once co-presence ends—complex dramas, not corporate institutions. Offered also for [[boxgrove|Boxgrove]] and other flagships.

### Densities and period signature

Across Cromer/Holstein open locales, primary-context sites typically show low lithic densities. Bilzingsleben (~32.1/m² all areas) is higher than [[boxgrove|Boxgrove]] Area A (~12.3/m²) but Gamble argues it is **not an outlier** within the period’s low-density signature (Table 4.19). Possible stone/bone consolidation of soft ground is one of the few repeated “features” excavators note (with Hoxne, Miesenheim I, Isernia).

## Key Debates

- **Structure vs taphonomy** — living-floor shelters vs open drop zones around natural foci; Villa’s Terra Amata refitting caution (strata-crossing groups) is cited against naive living floors.
- **OIS 11 vs 9** — absolute dates and Holsteinian correlation unresolved (as of Gamble 1999).
- **Hominin taxonomy** — *H. erectus* (Vlček) vs *H. heidelbergensis*.
- **Symbolic engraved bones** — Mania’s symbolic reading vs natural/rhythmic mark alternatives.
- **Hunting intensity** — Mania’s specialized rhino hunting vs Gamble’s caution on age profiles.
- **Gathering vs social occasion** — programmatic contrast with Dolní Věstonice in Gamble’s locale typology.

## Historiography

Bilzingsleben is the textbook “early campsite” in much secondary literature; Gamble 1999 is a sustained demurral that keeps the locale as first-class evidence while relocating social inference from architecture to performance, co-presence, and networks. Artists’ reconstructions are among the “natural-history curtain” devices Gamble critiques when they smuggle modern social occasions into the Middle Pleistocene.
