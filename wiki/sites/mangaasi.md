## Discovery and Excavation History

Mangaasi is the type-site (locus EF-17, with an adjoining Arapus locus) on western Efate Island, Vanuatu, for the post-Lapita ceramic tradition that bears its name. It was originally discovered and excavated by José Garanger in 1972 (Garanger 1972a), and re-investigated from 1996 onward by a joint Australian National University–Vanuatu National Museum project led by Stuart Bedford and Matthew Spriggs (attributed, [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 2017; Bedford 2006b). The site's dual excavation history — an original 1970s campaign followed by modern re-investigation — allows its ceramic seriation to be cross-checked against later fieldwork, though specific radiocarbon lab dates and error ranges are not given in the ingested excerpt, a precision gap flagged here pending fuller sourcing.

## Occupation Sequence

Bedford (2006b:161–68) defines five ceramic phases for post-Lapita Efate, based at Mangaasi and cross-referenced across the region:
- **Arapus (950–850 BCE)**: short-lived, less than a century in duration.
- **Erueti (850–250 BCE)**: early and late subphases; first recognized by Garanger.
- **Mangaasi proper (250 BCE–AD 750)**: early and late subphases, characterized by incised and punctate decoration with lip notching.
- Pottery manufacture on Efate ceases after AD 750, correlating with the appearance of Terebra shell adzes — a discontinuous but widespread artifact type appearing across island Melanesia and Micronesia after this date.

## Significance

Mangaasi gives its name to the broader "Mangaasi ceramic tradition" — the post-Lapita ceramic style dominated by incised, applied-relief, punctate, fingernail-impressed, and end-tool-impressed decoration, which replaced Lapita dentate-stamping. This broader "Mangaasoid" ceramic-transition phenomenon occurs across the Bismarcks–Vanuatu–New Caledonia corridor roughly 550 BCE–AD 250, with a distinct paddle-impressed intermediate stage appearing later in Fiji. Whether these parallel post-Lapita ceramic transitions across such a wide area reflect continued inter-island contact and shared technical innovation, or independent convergent responses by potters to the declining social/exchange value of elaborately decorated pottery as the pan-regional [[lapita-cultural-complex|Lapita]] exchange network fragmented into localized networks, remains debated; Kirch favors the latter economic/social-function explanation but explicitly flags it as unresolved ("the debate will continue," attributed).

Trade ware of Mangaasi-affiliated type is also found well outside Vanuatu: incised pottery nearly identical to Tikopia's "Sinapupu Ware" has been recovered at the Emo site on Vanikoro (Santa Cruz Islands), apparently deriving from northern Vanuatu — evidence of extended exchange links between Vanuatu and the Santa Cruz group (Kirch 1983).

Mangaasi's post-Lapita ceramic sequence is also cited in a broader debate over Fijian ceramic change: Navatu-phase Fijian pottery has been argued by several scholars (Green, Bellwood 1979, Burley 2005/2013) to show close stylistic similarity to Mangaasi ceramics, supporting a hypothesized population movement from Vanuatu into Fiji around AD 1100 — a position contested by Rechtman (1992), who prefers an internalist, non-migrationist account.

The site is also associated with the nearby elite burial ground at Retoka (Roy Mata), excavated by the same original investigator, Garanger, and linked by oral tradition to a distinct later phase of Vanuatu sociopolitical hierarchy.

## Key Debates

The causal explanation for the widespread, near-simultaneous post-Lapita ceramic-style transition (shared innovation/contact vs. independent local response to fragmenting exchange networks) remains explicitly unresolved per Kirch. The related dispute over whether Mangaasi-style ceramics in Fiji reflect an actual population movement from Vanuatu (Green, Bellwood, Burley) or purely internal stylistic change (Rechtman) is also open.
