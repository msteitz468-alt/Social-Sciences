## Discovery and Excavation History

Kalhu — biblical Calah, modern Nimrud — lies 35 km south of Mosul near the Tigris/Upper
Zab confluence. Per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], an earlier town at the site,
founded by Shalmaneser I in the 13th century BCE, had fallen to ruin before
[[assyrians|Ashurnasirpal II]] (883–859 BCE) refounded it as his new royal capital: the
ruin-mound was leveled, an 8 km fortified perimeter wall built, and an artificial and
natural acropolis hill raised with a ziggurat, temples, and royal palace; the canal
"Patti-hegalli" was cut from the Zab to supply water and defense; deportees from
conquered lands were settled at the new city, whose tutelary god was the war-god
Ninurta. The dedication ceremony, recorded on a stele, describes a ten-day banquet for
69,574 guests (879 BCE).

Nimrud was excavated 1845–1851 by Austen Henry Layard, who sent much of the recovered
material to the British Museum; later British archaeologists carried out further
campaigns at the site (unnamed individually in this ingest's source material). Roux
calls Ashurnasirpal's palace "the best preserved of Assyrian royal dwellings," as of his
writing. In 1878, Hormuzd Rassam discovered the bronze gates of Balawat (ancient
Imgur-Enlil) near Nimrud. In 1989, an Iraqi excavation team made a major find: three
unplundered royal tombs beneath the domestic wing of Ashurnasirpal's palace (see below).
As with Nineveh, the earliest excavation campaigns predate modern single-context
recording standards — a reliability consideration when weighing fine stratigraphic
claims from the Layard-era work against the far more rigorously documented 1989 tomb
excavation.

## Occupation Sequence

**Ashurnasirpal II's palace** covered more than three hectares in three sections — an
administrative courtyard block, a ceremonial block with the throne room, and a domestic
wing (harem, stores, ablution rooms). Features included lamassu (winged bull-men) gate
guardians, fresco-decorated mudbrick walls, carved and inscribed orthostats, burnt-brick
floors stamped with the king's name, and a wall air-vent system Roux describes as an
early form of "air conditioning."

The city remained an important royal center under later kings: Sargon II occupied and
modified Ashurnasirpal's palace before building his own new capital at Khorsabad, and
vassal-treaty tablet copies from Esarhaddon's 672 BCE succession arrangements have been
found at Nimrud.

**Later finds** include Shalmaneser III's **Black Obelisk** (a 2 m black alabaster
monument, found by Layard in Ninurta's temple, now in the British Museum), depicting
tribute-bearers including Jehu, King of Israel, shown prostrate — the same Jehu named as
the first biblical figure to appear in a cuneiform inscription; a prayer-attitude statue
of Shalmaneser III; **Fort Shalmaneser**, a large building that also served as the *ekal
masharti* (military muster and storehouse complex), used down to the empire's fall; and
the **bronze gates of Balawat**, bronze strip reliefs depicting Shalmaneser III's
campaigns with explanatory captions. Nimrud is also, per Roux, "the richest site of all"
for Assyrian ivory-work, including the celebrated "Mona Lisa" ivory; most of the ivory
corpus arrived as tribute or booty from western provinces, though some pieces are
judged purely Assyrian in style and manufacture — it remains ambiguous whether these
were made by Syro-Phoenician or Mesopotamian craftsmen. Writing boards bearing
astronomical content were recovered in 1953 from a well at Nimrud, apparently discarded
during the city's sack.

## Significance

**The 1989 royal tombs.** A major archaeological finding, reported by Roux as
essentially contemporary with his writing: an Iraqi excavation team found three
unplundered tombs beneath Ashurnasirpal's palace. One held a single male skeleton with
some 200 gold jewelry pieces. A joint tomb was tentatively identified — Roux flags this
identification explicitly as tentative — as belonging to Taliya (wife of Sargon II) and
Yaba (probably wife of Shalmaneser V), containing roughly 200 gold jewels plus three
gold bowls, an ivory box, a mirror, and alabaster jars. A third tomb, associated with
Ashurnasirpal's wife Mulisu, held an empty sarcophagus, but a further 440 gold items
were found separately in bronze coffins with other skeletons. The combined gold across
the finds is estimated at roughly 57 kg. Further excavation was halted by the Gulf
War — dating the find, and Roux's report of it, to approximately 1989–1992.

Nimrud's significance thus rests on three distinct evidence classes converging: the
architectural/relief record of Ashurnasirpal's and later kings' building programs, the
inscriptional/iconographic record of the Black Obelisk and Balawat gates documenting
9th-century Assyrian foreign relations (including the earliest cuneiform mention of a
named biblical figure), and the exceptionally rich, largely undisturbed material record
of the 1989 royal tombs.

## Historiography

Roux speculates, without confirming, that Aramaean pressure on Assur from the west may
have been part of Ashurnasirpal's motive for relocating the capital to Nimrud — he
flags this explicitly as unproven ("we do not know"). As with Nineveh, Nimrud (and
probably Assur) was destroyed by fire in 612 BCE alongside Nineveh's fall; the wooden
elements of Ashurnasirpal's palace perished in the fire, while stone, ivory, and metal
objects survived — a preservation-bias point relevant to why so much of the surviving
material culture from this period is durable-media (stone, ivory, metal) rather than
organic.

**Collection coverage note**: strong for the 9th-century palace and later royal
monuments (Black Obelisk, Balawat gates) and for the 1989 tomb find; weak for a
systematic stratigraphic account of the full excavation history, which this single
secondary source does not provide in detail.

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] uses the ancient
name **"Kalhu"** for this site throughout rather than "Nimrud" (the modern toponym used
on this page) — recorded as an `aliases` entry above so the two names resolve to the
same page rather than reading as a duplicate entity. Consistent with the account above,
Van De Mieroop confirms Kalhu's role as the imperial capital founded/refounded under
[[assyrians|Ashurnasirpal II]] (r. 883–859), and notes it remained an important
administrative center even after the Sargonid capital shifted to Dur-Sharrukin under
Sargon II and then to [[nineveh|Nineveh]] under Sennacherib: Kalhu is named, alongside
Nineveh, as one of the two major recipients of the roughly 2,300 surviving Assyrian
state letters, and — like other Assyrian cities — was administered by a city mayor
(hazannu), a role attested for it into the empire's later, Nineveh-centered period.
