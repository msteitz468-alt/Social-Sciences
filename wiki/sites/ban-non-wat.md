## Discovery and Excavation History

Ban Non Wat is a multi-period moated settlement and cemetery in the upper Mun Valley of Northeast Thailand. [[higham-charles]] and Rachanie Thosarat began major excavations in **2002** — the same year *Early Cultures of Mainland Southeast Asia* appeared — so the volume can report only first-season snapshots rather than a finished site sequence. Higham lists Ban Non Wat among principal Bronze Age sites on distribution maps (map item 40) and includes a 2002 caption for a partially revealed burial, but does not yet offer a monographic occupation narrative comparable to [[ban-lum-khao]], [[ban-chiang]], or [[noen-u-loke]].

The site was chosen within the same Mun Valley research design that targeted social ranking, technology, subsistence, and the nature of banks and moats across the Iron Age (see [[noen-u-loke]] for the fuller 2002-era Iron Age exposure). Later monographs from the Higham–Thosarat programme supersede the 2002 snapshot; this page records what [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]] could already say.

## Occupation Sequence

### Neolithic (as of Higham 2002)

Higham reports the 2002 excavation of a Neolithic settlement/cemetery component. The most striking feature is an adult **seated in a huge lidded pot** about **80 cm** in diameter, associated with a small cup and a bivalve shell; the pot shoulder is finely incised with burnished and red-painted bands. Higham states that **no similar burial had been found in Southeast Asia**. Three further graves oriented north–south contained fine red-paint and impression vessels. Radiocarbon dates for these burials “may well lie in the first half of the second millennium BC” (as of Higham 2002) — consistent with his broader Khorat Neolithic envelope of roughly **2300–1500 BCE** (cf. [[ban-chiang]], [[non-nok-tha]], [[ban-lum-khao]] first phase ~1500 BCE).

### Bronze Age

(as of Higham 2002) Ban Non Wat appears in the Bronze Age site distribution apparatus and in the “People of the Bronze Age” section via **Burial 20**, partially revealed in 2002: a young teenager with **over 30 pots**. No full Bronze Age mortuary seriation or wealth analysis is published in the 2002 synthesis — unlike the 111-grave sample at [[ban-lum-khao]] or the 166-grave cemetery at [[nong-nor]]. Higham’s late Bronze Age Khorat model (autonomous villages, descent-group status competition, valuables exchange for copper, tin, stone, and coastal shell) is the interpretive frame into which Ban Non Wat is expected to fit once fully published, not a site-specific demonstration in this volume.

### Iron Age

The site is a large moated mound in the Mun distribution that Moore (1986) and others had already mapped as part of a size hierarchy far exceeding Bronze Age village areas (as of Higham 2002). Higham’s 2002 Iron Age discussion develops the ranking-and-moat problem primarily through [[noen-u-loke]] (126 graves, five mortuary phases); Ban Non Wat is geographically and culturally adjacent but not yet the analytical workhorse in this book. Early Iron Age pottery at [[noen-u-loke]] MP2 is described as still very similar to latest Bronze Age [[ban-lum-khao]] — the regional ceramic continuum into which Ban Non Wat’s later phases will later be placed.

## Significance

Even as a 2002 partial report, Ban Non Wat matters because:

1. **Unique Neolithic mortuary form.** The large painted/incised jar burial expands the known range of early Khorat mortuary practice beyond the extended inhumations familiar from Ban Chiang and Non Nok Tha.
2. **Stratigraphic promise.** A single moated site spanning Neolithic through Iron Age (once fully excavated) can test continuity vs rupture across the bronze and iron transitions central to the [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]] and [[southeast-asian-state-formation]].
3. **Programme keystone.** Together with [[ban-lum-khao]] and [[noen-u-loke]], it anchors Higham’s upper Mun laboratory for ranking, exchange, and hydraulic landscape change.

Treat all sequence claims beyond the jar burial and Burial 20 caption as **provisional placeholders** pending post-2002 monographs.

## Key Debates

- Linked to [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]] for the regional dating framework into which Ban Non Wat’s radiocarbon will feed.
- Mun Valley moat/bank function (water control, defence, labour organisation) — argued at regional scale in Higham 2002 mainly from [[noen-u-loke]] and survey; Ban Non Wat becomes central in later literature not yet ingested here.
- Neolithic intrusive-farmer vs local continuity models (Higham’s broader language-farming discussion) — the jar burial is a cultural data point, not a population or language equation (four-way non-identity).

## Historiography

Ban Non Wat’s historiography in this vault necessarily splits: **(A)** the 2002 “work in progress” snapshot in [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]]; **(B)** the subsequent multi-volume excavation reports (not yet ingested) that made the site one of mainland Southeast Asia’s most important prehistoric sequences. Do not back-project later findings into this page without a new source ingest. Excavation quality for the 2002 seasons is that of a modern large-area programme continuous with Higham–Thosarat methods at Ban Lum Khao and Noen U-Loke (fine recovery, mortuary exposure priority). Collection coverage: weak on Ban Non Wat specifically until later monographs are filed; strong on the comparative Mun context via Ban Lum Khao and Noen U-Loke as treated in Higham 2002.
