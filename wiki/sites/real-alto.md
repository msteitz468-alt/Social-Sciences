## Discovery and Excavation History

Real Alto is an early Valdivia-culture site covering approximately 12.4 ha. Excavation and synthesis draws on Jonathan Damp's 1984 fieldwork at Real Alto and the related site of Loma Alta, and a later synthesis by J. Scott Raymond (1994), building on earlier work by Lathrap et al. (1975), Marcos (1978, 1988), and Zeidler (1984). Early Valdivia phases (Phases I and II, following Hill's 1975 chronology) are dated circa 3300–2300 B.C.E. (Damp 1984:573); Moore (2005) does not state the dating basis or calibration detail for this Valdivia chronology in the passage excerpted, so the method is flagged as unstated.

## Occupation Sequence

Early Valdivia dwellings at Real Alto and Loma Alta were small, elliptical-plan structures. The best-preserved house at Real Alto (Structure 2-77) measured 4.5 × 3.2 m; Structure 4 at Loma Alta measured 3.1 × 2.3 m (Damp 1984:578, 580). Middle and Late Valdivia houses, also elliptical, were much larger — roughly 12 × 8 m — and are thought to have housed extended families (Lathrap et al. 1975).

Damp (1984:577, 583) argues the early village was spatially planned in a horseshoe-shaped arrangement of dwellings around an open space, a pattern directly observed at Loma Alta and inferred at Real Alto, where Damp interprets the early layout as having "established the geometrical foundation for the subsequent construction" (Damp 1984:577). Damp (1984:582) suggests a population of "150 to 200 village inhabitants seems reasonable," though he notes this estimate is uncertain given unclear house-contemporaneity.

After roughly 3000 BCE (Valdivia III), Real Alto developed from a residential village into a regional ceremonial center: a plaza surrounded by dwellings developed over time into a ring of mounds (Lathrap et al. 1975; Marcos 1988; Raymond 1994). Two mound-top structures had nondomestic functions: the "Charnel House," containing multiple burials, and the "Fiesta House," containing numerous ceramic drinking vessels — the latter analogized by Marcos (1988:40) to lowland South American men's houses.

Raymond's (1994:32, 39–44) model holds that in the earliest Valdivia phase, small oval dwellings were themselves loci of ritual, comparable to burial practice documented at the earlier Las Vegas culture site (Stothert 1985, 1988); by Valdivia II, ritual activity had formalized into the Fiesta House/Charnel House complex. The plaza, Raymond argues, was initially "undifferentiated space" enabling egalitarian communal rite, but as population grew and multifamily residence groups formed, access to the plaza became progressively restricted. In later Valdivia phases the resident population moved to nearby hamlets, leaving Real Alto largely empty as a ceremonial center (Raymond 1994:32; Zeidler 1984).

Small figurines recovered across all Valdivia phases — in burials, residences, and middens — are argued by Raymond (1994:41, following Stahl 1986) to be evidence of shamanism, made and used in ritual then discarded; Moore reads this as a continuity in shamanic practice even as settlement and architectural forms changed markedly, complicating a simple binary between "shamanic" religious authority and monumental architecture.

Per Moore's comparative Table 3.3, Real Alto's ritual practice is classified as burials, feasting, and burned offerings; permanence as episodic; ubiquity as subregional; visibility as personal/public-distant. Moore notes Real Alto lacked emphasis on processional architectural design, and its mound-top structures (not the mounds themselves) are grouped among "relatively impermanent, widely distributed, and intimate ceremonial spaces," alongside La Emerenciana, CA-09-27, and Gavilanes.

Moore (2005) also includes Real Alto in a Valdivia soundscape reconstruction (his Figure 2.21, redrawn from Marcos 1988), part of the same egalitarian-pattern interpretive set as Acha-2, [[asana]], and [[paloma]], extending the soundscape argument beyond the Archaic into the chronologically overlapping Early Formative.

## Significance

Real Alto anchors Moore's (2005) argument that Formative ceremonial architecture is not a uniform proxy for centralized political authority. Its trajectory — from an undifferentiated communal plaza to progressively restricted mound-top ritual space, alongside continuous shamanic figurine use — is offered by Moore as a case where architectural formalization and scale increased without a corresponding shift away from shamanic religious authority, complicating the [[formative-stage]] link between monumentality and canonist or state authority (see [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]]).

## Key Debates

Real Alto does not itself carry a named dispute between excavators, but it is central evidence in the wider debate (see [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]]) over whether large-scale Formative ceremonial architecture implies centralized political authority (Sheila and Thomas Pozorski) or reflects cumulative construction by nonhierarchical, religiously motivated communities (Richard Burger and Lucy Salazar-Burger). Moore uses Real Alto's shamanic-figurine continuity to argue against treating architectural formalization alone as a marker of canonist or state authority.
