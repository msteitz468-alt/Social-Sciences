# Jiahu

## Discovery and Excavation History

Jiahu is a Neolithic settlement in the Huai River drainage of Wuyang County, Henan, excavated by the Henan Provincial Institute of Cultural Relics and Archaeology across multiple campaigns (as of [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen 2012]]). It is classified as a [[peiligang-culture|Peiligang-culture]] site — an etic, material-culture-defined archaeological construct — and is one of the largest and best-documented sites of that culture, alongside Tanghu. Liu & Chen do not equate the Peiligang construct with a single ethnic or linguistic population; the culture is defined by shared pottery, tool, and settlement traits, not shared identity (four-way non-identity rule).

## Occupation Sequence

Jiahu's occupation spans roughly 9000–7700 cal BP (as of Liu & Chen 2012). Across this span the site yielded a dense and unusually well-preserved subsistence, ritual, and craft record, summarized below by evidence class.

### Plant use and early cultivation

Rice remains at Jiahu date to ca. 9000 cal BP, associated with the **earliest known rice-harvesting tools in north China**: 45 polished stone sickles, comprising 16% of the site's polished stone implement assemblage — though sickle function has not yet been confirmed by use-wear analysis (as of Liu & Chen 2012; see [[rice-domestication]]). Soybean seeds (N = 581) account for 14% of total recovered seed remains, uniform in size (3.28 mm ± 0.47 length; 2.33 mm ± 0.35 width) and smaller than modern wild soybean from Anhui — interpreted by Zhao Z. & Zhang (2009) as anthropogenically collected **wild** soybean rather than a domesticate. Starch-grain and use-wear analyses at related Peiligang-culture sites (Egou, Shigu) show grinding stones and sickles were used on a broad-spectrum plant diet including acorns, beans, tubers, and millet, not on rice alone.

### Animal use and early domestication

Three Jiahu pig specimens show distorted tooth alignment and an M3 length range (36.39–46.66 mm) that overlaps wild and domestic forms. Roughly 27% of meat consumed at the site was pork, and about 80% of pigs were killed before age 3; pig bones are found in association with human burials, linear enamel hypoplasia (LEH) frequency is higher than in wild populations, and stable-isotope data show most pig diets closely matched the local human diet — indicating some degree of domestication — with a minority of pigs isotopically distinct and probably wild (as of Liu & Chen 2012, summarizing Luo 2007; see [[animal-domestication]]). This mixed wild/domestic/feral pattern is characteristic of the domestication-as-continuum framing developed on [[domestication]].

Complete dog skeletons were intentionally buried in cemeteries and near houses at Jiahu (ca. 7000 BC); hunting nonetheless remained an important subsistence component alongside ritual dog burial, indicating dogs at this stage served both ritual and practical roles rather than one exclusively.

### Ritual, craft, and proto-writing

Jiahu yielded elaborate burials containing turtle shells used as rattles and flutes made from red-crowned crane ulnae; the flutes are described in the literature as **the earliest known playable multinote musical instruments** ("the flutes rank as the earliest playable multinote instruments ever found," as of Liu & Chen 2012 citing Li, Xueqin et al. 2003). Some of the turtle shells bear **incised signs** that anticipate — but do not constitute — the later Shang oracle-bone inscriptions: Liu & Chen report that "several signs anticipate later inscriptions on oracle bones in the late Shang dynasty, although these symbols do not represent a true writing system" (as of Liu & Chen 2012). These marks should be read as a documented precursor phenomenon, not as writing — the same caution the wiki applies to comparable proto-sign traditions (Dawenkou dakouzun marks, Liangzhu pictographs) elsewhere in the Chinese Neolithic sequence.

Chemical residue analysis of Jiahu pottery vessels indicates rice was used to brew **fermented beverages**, potentially linked to ceremonies confirming social hierarchies (McGovern et al. 2004), alongside independent evidence of incipient social differentiation at the site (Henansheng Wenwu Kaogu 1999a). Liu & Chen (citing Hayden 2011) suggest Jiahu rice may possibly have functioned as a luxury or feasting good tied to early status negotiation, but caution — because the initial phase of rice domestication remains archaeologically unclear — that this ritual association may not reflect the original motivation for rice domestication itself (attributed, not wiki voice).

## Significance

Jiahu anchors several evidentiary threads at once: the earliest north-China rice-harvesting toolkit, one of the earliest secure musical-instrument traditions worldwide, an incised-sign tradition read as a precursor to (not an instance of) Chinese writing, and among the earliest domesticated-pig and ritual-dog-burial evidence in China. Liu & Chen characterize the site's overall economy as **broad-spectrum with low-level food production and emergent prestige differentiation** rather than a mature agricultural or clearly stratified society — an egalitarian-with-emergent-prestige pattern consistent with the site's mixed wild/domestic faunal and floral record.

## Key Debates

- Whether Jiahu's incised turtle-shell signs constitute genuine proto-writing or are unrelated symbolic marks is unresolved; Liu & Chen explicitly deny they form "a true writing system" (as of Liu & Chen 2012), consistent with the wiki's broader caution around Dawenkou and Liangzhu proto-sign traditions.
- Whether Jiahu pottery-residue evidence for fermented rice beverages reflects a feasting/status-competition dynamic (Hayden's model) or simply ordinary subsistence use is part of the wider live dispute over domestication's social vs. ecological drivers recorded on [[domestication]].
- Whether the Jiahu pig assemblage represents true local domestication or predominantly managed-wild populations remains open, given the overlap between wild and domestic M3 tooth-length ranges.
