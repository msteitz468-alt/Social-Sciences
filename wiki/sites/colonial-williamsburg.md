# Colonial Williamsburg

Restored and reconstructed colonial capital at **Williamsburg, Virginia**, operated as a major **outdoor history museum** by the Colonial Williamsburg Foundation. In the vault it is both a historic settlement/landscape and — for [[critical-archaeology]] — a **public ideology site**. [[leone-mark-p|Mark P. Leone]] uses Williamsburg as foil and case for exclusionary narratives, the commercial irrelevance of archaeology to the museum product, gallery analysis (DeWitt Wallace), and the problem of why excavate for outdoor history (*[[leone-critical-historical-archaeology-2010|Critical Historical Archaeology]]* Ch. 1, 10).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Rockefeller-funded restoration from the interwar period onward; professional historical archaeology under **Ivor Noël Hume** became a standard-bearing tradition for colonial site archaeology in North America. Leone's late-1970s encounter (including a 11 Sept 1978 Raleigh Tavern bakery reenactment read as race/class structure) produced anger at what he took as arrogance of "unmediated" lessons from the past and the sense that archaeology was commercially irrelevant to the authentic-looking set. His method when records were closed: structural/psychoanalytic reading of exhibits (Lévi-Strauss + Freud).

## Occupation Sequence

Colonial and early national capital/town → decline relative to Richmond → 20th-c. restoration as themed historic city → continuous tourism and research archaeology; major decorative-arts gallery (DeWitt Wallace) as later institutional layer.

## Significance

For [[historical-archaeology]]'s institutional history: flagship of reconstructive colonial archaeology. For critical HA (Leone):

- **Ch. 1 (1981 essay):** purpose of popular American history; exclusion and beauty; hypothesis that exhibits can justify modern race relations; Mormon comparison on unmediated access to past.
- **Ch. 10 (1994):** DeWitt Wallace Gallery analyzed via oppositions and Foucaultian discipline — capitalism's twin products of segmented production and categories of mental instability; protest of how mental illness is presented in the museum landscape.

Compare **Shakertown at Pleasant Hill** (Leone Ch. 2): smaller outdoor-history analog where beauty is preserved while Shaker purposes are "castrated" by artifact fetish and dual ideology (hide history + impose capitalist values).

## Key Debates

Patriot heritage vs. critical inclusion (enslaved people, class, gender); reconstruction authenticity; archaeology as service to museum narrative vs. independent critique. Linked: [[public-archaeology]], [[heritage]], [[leone-mark-p]], [[critical-archaeology]].

### Hall (2000) on the site's self-presentation and Carter's Grove

[[hall-martin|Martin Hall]], in *[[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Archaeology and the Modern World]]* (2000), reads Colonial Williamsburg as a case of contradictory self-presentation. The restoration began in 1926 as a collaboration between the local parish priest and John D. Rockefeller Jr; Hall, following Handler and Gable (1997), situates the project within a broader American reaction against communism and a promotion of "traditional values." The Foundation's own website (as accessed in 2000) framed Rockefeller's funding as teaching "the patriotism, high purpose, and unselfish devotion of our forefathers to the common good," with a mission of "responsible leadership, a sense of public service, self government, and individual liberty" — while, on the same site, explicitly acknowledging slavery as "a defining characteristic of 18th-century Virginia society" that "tinctured all aspects of life" and describing "unyielding racial prejudice" among white Virginians. Hall argues these two strands sit in unresolved tension: the same colonial gentry is celebrated as a model of patriotism and high purpose while the site concedes the racial order that gentry upheld. [ATTRIBUTED — Hall 2000, following Handler and Gable 1997]

At the reconstructed **Carter's Grove** slave quarter, African American interpreters perform enslaved daily life on site. Hall quotes the Foundation's 2000 web account ("To Live Like a Slave," Curtia James) describing the enslaved as "theoretical people" because "evidence about them is so scant, any attempt to fully recreate their lives would be mere conjecture." Hall argues this framing misrepresents the actual evidentiary record: the Carter's Grove reconstruction itself drew on archaeological evidence from storage and root-cellar pits, which Chesapeake historical archaeology (Kelso 1984, 1986; McKee 1988) has treated as a rich source on enslaved daily life, and a substantial body of further evidence (Franklin 1995) contradicts any picture of enslaved African Americans as an undifferentiated, evidence-poor population. Hall cites Theresa Singleton's (1995) review of the archaeology of slavery in North America as showing how privileging memory and oral tradition over available archaeological evidence works to "bracket off" an "African American experience" as separate from a supposed historical mainstream. [POSITION — Hall/Singleton vs. Colonial Williamsburg's official self-presentation; not adjudicated in wiki voice]

Hall (following Handler and Gable 1997 and Singleton 1995) further characterizes the spatial and representational separation of Carter's Grove (slavery) from the main Historic Area (mainstream colonial life) as constituting "an ethnic archaeology of the Other." As a contrast case, he cites the website's account of "Sampling 18th-Century Fare at Shield's Tavern" (Theobald 2000), which presents reconstructed colonial dining as fully "historically accurate" on "an interdisciplinary approach to the study of food," with none of the epistemic hedging used for enslaved life — an asymmetry Hall reads (via Gable et al. 1993) as treating white mainstream history as confidently factual while treating Black history as near-fiction. [ATTRIBUTED — Hall 2000]
