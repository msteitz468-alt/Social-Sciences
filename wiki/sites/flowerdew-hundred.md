# Flowerdew Hundred

James River plantation landscape in Virginia — major Chesapeake anchor in [[deetz-james|James Deetz]]'s later career and in the 1996 expansion of *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]*. Deetz's separate monograph *Flowerdew Hundred: The Archaeology of a Virginia Plantation 1619–1864* (1993) is **not yet ingested**; this page densifies from ISTF 1996 in-text uses and notes.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Deetz cites **1971–1978** excavations by the College of William and Mary under **Norman Barka** (field directors including Leverett Gregory, Charles Hodges, Andrew Edwards). Yeardley-period settlement layout compared to Ulster and to Noël Hume's **Martin's Hundred**. Industrial features (bloomeries) discussed via Ann Markell dissertation (1990) as Deetz notes.

## Occupation Sequence

*(As Deetz frames via pipestem histograms and historical episodes.)*

- **Group 1:** early settlement; **[[earthfast-construction|earthfast]]** building dominant.
- **Group 2:** industrial activity and greater architectural permanence (post-tobacco-boom restructuring as Deetz narrates Navigation Acts / labor history).
- **Group 3:** later plantation landscape including **[[colono-ware]]** presence.
- **1625 muster:** seven "negroes" at Flowerdew — one "young child" of an anonymous "negro woman" possibly among first Black children born on English soil in the New World (Deetz).
- **1830s slave cabin** on brick piers: wooden floor raised ~1 ft; Deetz reads healthier damp control **and** planter **surveillance** (hidey-holes unusable covertly under open piers).

## Significance

- Textbook earthfast + tobacco-capital investment logic (opening 1695 vignette: posts without footings free capital for tobacco).
- Long plantation sequence for English *and* African American archaeology.
- Comparative node for Deetz's continuum of slave housing (vs Kingsmill root cellars/porches; SC wattle/clay; free **[[parting-ways|Parting Ways]]** / [[shotgun-house]]).

## Key Debates

Impermanent architecture thesis (Carson et al.); ethnicity markers (root cellars, Colono); social control vs benevolence readings of pier foundations.
