# Avebury

Late Neolithic henge and stone-circle complex on the north Wiltshire Downs, England — one
of five chalkland central-southern mega-henge clusters in *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain
Begins]]* (with Mount Pleasant, Knowlton, Durrington Walls, and Marden) and among Europe’s
largest prehistoric earthworks (attributed, Cunliffe 2013). Not a single finished temple: a
**palimpsest** of henge, sarsens, avenues, and neighbouring monuments in a landscape already
marked by ancestral long barrows and a causewayed enclosure.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Cunliffe reports that **John Aubrey** treated Avebury/Stonehenge-class stone circles —
also known in Ireland and Scotland beyond Roman, Phoenician, or Danish reach — as temples
of the Britons’ Druids (attributed historiography). **William Stukeley** produced detailed
1719–24 plans of Avebury and [[stonehenge]] and an early landscape perception of the site;
after ordination he turned to Druidic fantasy in *Stonehenge* (1740) and *Avebury* (1743)
(attributed, Cunliffe 2013). Cunliffe notes that the great enclosing bank and ditch survive
remarkably intact; a number of standing stones visible today were re-erected in the 1930s;
the village spreading into the monument dates from the late Saxon period (attributed
descriptive frame, 2013). Full modern excavation campaign lists are not densified in this
source — treat Cunliffe as synthesis, not site monograph.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed chronology — Cunliffe 2013; date precision approximate / sparse for many
components.)*

**Henge first, stones later.** First phase of the henge around **3000 BC**: circular
enclosure defined by ditch and bank with **four entrances**. Earthwork reconstructed a
century or two later. Massive **sarsen** stones set up around the inner lip of the ditch
probably between **2400 and 2200 BC** — raising the possibility that stone circles are often
a later phenomenon following henge inception around the thirty-first century BC (attributed).
Southern British phase-1 henges (Flagstones near Dorchester, Stonehenge phase 1, Avebury
phase 1) cluster ~3000 BC; earliest known henges currently Orkney (end fourth millennium);
origins left open for lack of reliable dates (attributed).

**Landscape palimpsest.** Cunliffe lists components of the Avebury sacred landscape:

- the great henge itself;
- the **Sanctuary** on Overton Hill (massive timber building surrounded by a henge
  enclosure);
- **West Kennet** enclosures;
- **Silbury Hill** (construction placed in the **twenty-fourth century BC** in his Beaker-
  period discussion);
- the **Longstones** enclosure;
- standing-stone **avenues** linking Longstones–Avebury and Avebury–Sanctuary;
- ancestral **long barrows**, including West Kennet long barrow;
- the earlier **Windmill Hill** causewayed enclosure overlooking the region.

Preferred henge locations often develop where clusters of earlier monuments — causewayed
enclosures, cursuses, and long barrows — already existed (attributed general rule, Avebury
as exemplar).

**Beaker-period works.** Beaker pottery at traditional monuments need not mean more than
continued practice, but Cunliffe cites new or enhanced works of the period: West Kennet Farm
and Mount Pleasant palisaded enclosures (c. twenty-third–twenty-second centuries), Silbury
Hill (twenty-fourth century), and Avebury’s great stone circle and stone avenues (best dated
to the Beaker period in his account) as possible constructive interest by new elites or
ideologies (attributed theoretical reading). Do not equate Beaker pots with a single
incoming people ([[bell-beaker-culture]]; four-way non-identity).

## Significance

Avebury is Cunliffe’s type-example of a **preferred ritual landscape** on the Wessex
chalk: the mega-henge is one element in a far more complex third-millennium ceremonial
geography, functionally comparable (as a class) to the Durrington Walls–Woodhenge–
[[stonehenge]] cluster even though the two complexes are distinct places (attributed).
Together with Stonehenge it anchors his middle/late Neolithic (c.3800–2500 BC) narrative of
intensified indigenous development and free flow of ideas after formative Neolithization,
before Beaker-related pan-European mobility ~2500 BC (attributed Ch.6 framing).

Henge form (ditch inside bank) revives causewayed-enclosure geometry and makes little sense
as defence; Cunliffe suggests the ditch may separate secular from religious space while the
outer bank provides a place for spectators (attributed interpretation). Circularity,
vertical settings of timber or stone, and solar-calendar axes are shared henge themes;
homogeneity of henge practice across Britain and Ireland in the third millennium implies
shared religious practice without equating communities as one ethnicity (attributed).

**Grooved Ware** association with henges (pan-British/Irish ceramic fashion ~1000 years from
thirty-second/thirty-first century) applies to this landscape class; elite-only use remains
unproven (attributed caution).

## Key Debates

- Henge origins: Orkney-earliest dates vs open question (Cunliffe leaves open; as of 2013).
- Whether stone circles regularly post-date henge earthworks (possibility raised from
  Avebury/Stonehenge sequences).
- Degree of Beaker-period elite agency in sarsen avenues vs continuity of indigenous
  monumental practice ([[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]], Beaker chapter of same).
- Antiquarian Druid reading (Aubrey/Stukeley) vs archaeological multi-phase Neolithic
  construction.

## Historiography

Knowledge of Avebury in this vault’s Cunliffe layer is **synthesis-grade**: landscape
components, relative sequence (henge → rebuild → sarsens/avenues), and placement among
Wessex mega-henge clusters, not a full excavation history or room-by-room finds catalogue.
Stukeley’s plans remain foundational for antiquarian landscape perception; 1930s re-
erections affect what visitors see. Dating of individual components is uneven; Cunliffe
flags sparse reliable henge dates nationally. Theoretical lens: Atlantic/insular
connectivity and ritual-landscape palimpsest, not *ex oriente lux* diffusion. Compare
[[stonehenge]] (Renfrew calibration argument; Cunliffe eight-stage model) and Silbury labour-
scale figures carried on that page from Renfrew 1973.

## Bradley 1998 — insider/outsider and horizon (attributed)

*[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998]]* (Ch. 8) uses Avebury as the exemplar **large henge**:

- Sited in an enormous chalk basin ringed by hills; skyline carries many Early Bronze Age barrows seemingly placed relative to the already-established enclosure; Windmill Hill and Silbury summit visible from the bank (Woodward & Woodward via Bradley).
- **Insider** cut off from the horizon by the massive earthwork — a world in itself; only views out via narrow entrances.
- **Outsider** on the bank sees landscape relations and skyline barrows (circuit needed to see all).
- Contrast with [[causewayed-enclosure|Windmill Hill]] (built across contours, facing one direction) vs Avebury’s continuous-horizon design.
- Possible composite timber–stone structure at the Sanctuary (Pollard 1992 via Bradley).
