# Danebury

Hampshire Iron Age [[hillfort]] on the eastern Hampshire chalkland — one of the best-excavated early/developed hillforts of central southern Britain and a type case in [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]]’s long research programme, synthesized for a general audience in *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* (2013). All interpretive claims below are **attributed to Cunliffe 2013** unless marked as bare descriptive finding from that synthesis.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Cunliffe’s multi-season excavation of Danebury made the site a national reference for Iron Age hillfort interiors (streets, houses, storage, faunal and human-bone assemblages). *Britain Begins* uses those results as exemplars rather than republishing full site reports; excavation quality for the modern programme is high relative to pre-war British hillfort digs. Full specialist volumes and radiocarbon schedules are outside the 2013 synthesis’s resolution — date ranges on this page follow Cunliffe’s narrative chronology, not a re-tabulation of every lab date.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed narrative — Cunliffe 2013.)*

1. **Early hillfort horizon (broadly sixth–fifth centuries BC).** Cunliffe generalizes that most southern British hillforts were constructed in this period, with defensive-proportion rampart and ditch and usually two opposed gates; early faces often timber- or stone-revetted and long maintained. Danebury belongs with Maiden Castle (Dorset) as a site showing **streets, houses, storage, and domestic activity** — permanent occupation rather than empty enclosure.

2. **Regional dominance / polity aggregation.** On the eastern Hampshire chalk, early Danebury **emerged as the dominant place** while **four surrounding early hillforts were abandoned** — Cunliffe’s clearest British example of later aggregation of territories into fewer enhanced central places (parallel: Maiden Castle vs its Dorset neighbours). Why particular sites won out (veneration, leadership, coercion) cannot be decided on available evidence (position).

3. **Late fourth-century BC interior fire.** A devastating fire affected the interior; cause need not be enemy action, though regional evidence of violence exists.

4. **Early third-century BC redefence.** After the first fire, Danebury was **strongly redefended** and remained dominant for centuries. Broader central-southern pattern in this period: glacis ramparts, reduced/blocked gates, hornworks — read by Cunliffe as response to social tension, not mere display (see [[hillfort]]).

5. **First-century BC crisis and abandonment.** The gate was **totally destroyed** in the first century BC; the site was then largely abandoned. Sling-stones in the entrance forecourt and slash/thrust marks on human bones support deadly conflict at the entrance zone. Endemic hostility, boasting, and occasional skirmishes/raids — not standing armies — are Cunliffe’s preferred warfare model.

6. **Bury Hill contrast (regional politics).** Around the beginning of the first century BC, long-abandoned **Bury Hill** (~6 km away) was reoccupied with new ditch and inner/outer rampart. Fauna there: horses >¼ of bones, plus exceptional horse gear and chariot fittings — interpreted as war-chariot building/training and an “excitable” elite stance — while Danebury remained focused on **sheep and corn**. Cunliffe finds it tempting to see Bury Hill as a new polity inside former Danebury territory that may even have destroyed Danebury (explicitly speculative, attributed).

## Significance

- **Type case for “developed” southern British hillforts** with permanent internal occupation versus empty or seasonal enclosures.
- **Polity aggregation model:** one fort’s rise and neighbours’ abandonment as a micro-regional political process.
- **Violence and stress:** fires, trauma, sling-stone stocks, and later multivallate/glacis architecture feed Cunliffe’s rejection of a purely peaceful Iron Age “rural idyll.”
- **Central place in the hillfort zone** of his three-zone Britain (eastern open/enclosed farms; western defended homesteads; central hillfort belt) — see [[hillfort]].
- Anchors links from site-scale archaeology to concepts of territory, [[prestige-goods-economy|elite economy]], and later SE British reordering under Gallo-Belgic and Roman contact ([[oppida]], [[roman-britain]] where treated).

## Key Debates

- **Defence vs display vs community centre.** Cunliffe’s multi-function reading (cohesion, territory, seasonal assembly, refuge, possible long-term residence) vs purely military or purely symbolic models — filed on [[hillfort]].
- **Cause of fires and final destruction.** Enemy action vs accident vs internal conflict; Bury Hill rivalry is a hypothesis, not a demonstrated campaign history.
- **How representative is Danebury?** Exceptionally well dug southern chalkland site; cannot stand for Ireland’s multivallate LBA forts, northern brochs, or eastern open settlements without argument.
- **Invasion vs continuity.** Danebury’s sequence is indigenous Iron Age social process in Cunliffe’s frame, not a chapter of successive ethnic replacements ([[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]]).

## Identity Cautions

Danebury is an excavated **place**, not a people-name, language, or gene pool. Occupants are not labelled “Celts” in wiki voice from the earthworks alone; tribal names in the region (later Belgae / Roman *civitas* geography) are classical/post-conquest frames and must not be projected wholesale onto sixth–second-century BC layers without evidence.
