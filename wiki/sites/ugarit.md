# Ugarit (Ras Shamra)

## Discovery and Excavation History

Chance tomb find at Minet el-Beida (1929) led French excavators to the port and to Ras Shamra inland — capital of the kingdom of **Ugarit**. Long-running French then Franco-Syrian excavations recovered a major LBA commercial city destroyed early in the twelfth century BCE. Archive quality and continuous work make Ugarit a cornerstone for reconstructing Eastern Mediterranean diplomacy and trade; early excavation standards (1930s storage jars without modern residue analysis) limit some economic inferences.

## Occupation Sequence

LBA peak as international entrepôt; Egyptian connections earlier, Hittite vassalage after Suppiluliuma (~mid-14th c. BCE). Last kings include Ammurapi (first decade of the twelfth century). Merchant and official houses (Yabninu, Rapanu, Urtenu — 500+ tablets in Urtenu’s archive) document contacts with Cyprus (Alashiya; king Kushmeshusha named), Egypt (including Merneptah gifts), Hatti, Assyria, Carchemish, and Levantine ports; “Hiyawa-men” letters add Aegean references. Violent destruction under Ammurapi c. **1190–1185 BCE** (Cline synthesis); city not reoccupied for ~650 years. Marguerite Yon: enemy attack with street fighting and arrowheads, not the final earthquake. Schaeffer’s earthquake damage is now placed ~1250 BCE with restoration before the end. The famous “kiln letter” was **not** found in a kiln and cannot date the final destruction (revision Cline reports).

## Significance

- Primary textual window on the [[late-bronze-age-international-system]] (multilingual tablets; trade and diplomacy to the last moment).
- Key case for [[late-bronze-age-collapse]]: late letters on enemy ships (Carchemish advice to wall cities; Eshuwara of Cyprus blaming Ugarit’s own ships; private letter that the city is sacked).
- Alphabetic Ugaritic corpus foundational for West Semitic studies.
- Singer/Monroe (via Cline): overdependence on international trade as vulnerability; sudden end vs gradual trade-cut debate.
