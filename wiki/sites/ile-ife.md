# Ile-Ife

Yoruba spiritual/ceremonial creation centre and early second-millennium **urban** capital in south-western Nigeria. In [[connah-african-civilizations-2016|Connah, *African Civilizations* (2016)]] Ife is the forest-zone case that most clearly joins **high art**, **formal architecture**, and **urban ranking**—alongside [[benin-city|Benin City]] and in contrast to regalia-without-city [[igbo-ukwu|Igbo-Ukwu]]. All substantive claims below are attributed to Connah 2016 and the specialists he cites, until independent sources densify.

**Identity caution:** Archaeological locus ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetic population. Link with `associated_with` unless identity is argued. “Yoruba” as city-state culture is an etic/ethnohistoric package; early Ife archaeology should not be collapsed uncritically into later ethnolinguistic or ritual titles (e.g. Oni of Ife) without argument—though Connah uses those institutions as comparative ideology for ruler-as-chief-priest functions.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Connah synthesizes mid–late twentieth-century urban archaeology at modern Ife rather than a single discovery narrative:

- **Willett** (and collaborators) on art, chronology, and copper-alloy technology;
- **Eyo** and **Garlake** on pavements, urban layout, and occupation;
- glass studies (Freestone; Lankton et al.; Babalola) on melting and likely **primary glass manufacture**.

Art objects (terracottas, copper-based alloy castings, stone sculptures) entered world art history earlier than full urban archaeology; Connah’s interest is what the art implies for **stratification and patronage**, not connoisseurship alone. Written and oral sources for the forest zone cover only about half the time-depth available for the West African savanna; origins of cities and states are pushed into or before the first half of the second millennium AD largely by archaeology (Connah 2016).

Stable lead isotope analysis (Willett & Sayre via Connah) linking metal used at Ife (and early Benin) to sources in **France** is cited as evidence of the geographical extent of trading networks—not as foreign foundation of the city.

## Occupation Sequence

**Late first millennium AD onward.** Radiocarbon chronology suggests occupation commenced during the **late first millennium AD** (Willett; Eyo; Garlake via Connah).

**Pavements and formal architecture (~twelfth–fifteenth centuries).** Edge-laid potsherd and stone pavements mainly date approximately **twelfth to fifteenth century**. As at Benin, such pavements suggest formal architecture. Garlake excavated **fourteenth-century altars** built into the edge of potsherd pavements—material traces of ritual power in an urban setting.

**Art floruit.** Combined radiocarbon and thermoluminescence dates have been used to suggest the most important period of Ife art in the **late fourteenth to early fifteenth century** (**dating flag**: combined methods). By the second quarter of the second millennium, Ife metalsmiths produced copper-base alloy castings of technical excellence and artistic refinement; lost-wax skill already demonstrated earlier at Igbo-Ukwu (Craddock & Picton via Connah). Art representing personages of importance, at scale, and in **probably imported** copper alloys, implies emerging social stratification and patronage (Willett; Willett & Sayre via Connah).

**Urban form.** A series of **concentric city walls** suggests phased growth around the palace of the ruler (many walls poorly dated; some only last few centuries—Ozanne via Connah). Garlake argued ancient Ife was at least as large as the nineteenth-century walled town and compact enough to rank as **urban**, with a thriving technological base **including likely primary glass manufacture** from primary materials (late eleventh–fourteenth centuries at a factory site; Freestone; Lankton et al.; Babalola via Connah). Terracottas, hard-stone sculpture, potsherd pavements, and glass work exemplify the diversity of forest technology Connah stresses for the zone.

**Regional urban context.** Larger Yoruba settlements are treated as individual **city-states** constituting a city-state culture (Peel via Connah); Bascom’s claim that Yoruba are “undoubtedly the most urban of all African peoples” is quoted with a warning that many studies wrongly assume the most impressive urbanism postdates 1800. Old Oyo is a related case: substantial settlement >1 km² as early as the twelfth century (and **perhaps as early as the eighth**—dating flag), apparently predating its earthwork complex (Soper & Darling; Calvocoressi & David; Agbaje-Williams via Connah).

**Ideology and trade (forest package).** Connah places Ife among forest centres where late first / early second millennium growing stratification based on control of trade resources and agricultural surplus led toward centralized authority, especially **divine kingship**; Oni-as-chief-priest functions and yam-harvest ceremonies are ethnohistoric analogues for ideology legitimizing authority. Ife heads are discussed (Herbert via Connah) as possible funeral/altar objects—“actual containers of power.” Luxury imports (copper-base alloys, cowries, beads, etc.) fed status display; regional ecozonal trade likely predated Arab trans-Saharan takeoff (Connah theoretical claim for the forest as a whole).

## Significance

Ife anchors **art + urbanism** together: not a free-floating art style, and not bare walls without craft specialization. Connah’s forest chapter uses Ife (with Benin, Begho, Bono Manso) as evidence of **emergent city-states** by early second millennium—nucleation, craft specialization, and Mabogunje’s limiting conditions (food surplus, power-holding groups, traders) being met in some forest areas. Indigenous multifactor origins are preferred over external-stimulus stories; trade is one internal factor among productive land, iron technology, surplus, and spiritual-temporal centralization (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]*, Ch. 7 conclusion position).

Paired with [[benin-city|Benin City]], Ife shows potsherd pavements and copper-alloy art as shared formal vocabulary of forest capitals; paired with [[igbo-ukwu|Igbo-Ukwu]], it shows that regalia skill can travel with **or without** urban nucleation.

## Key Debates

- **Art chronology.** Late fourteenth–early fifteenth century floruit depends on combined radiocarbon and TL—dating flag; occupation begins earlier (late first millennium).

- **Urban ranking and wall dates.** Garlake’s urban claim vs poorly dated concentric walls (some late)—size and compactness arguments carry weight even where wall phases are insecure.

- **Primary glass manufacture.** Melting evidence clear; primary production from raw materials “now seems likely” (Connah synthesizing Freestone/Lankton/Babalola)—still a specialist debate at the edges.

- **Metal sources and network extent.** French lead-isotope signatures for Ife (and early Benin) metals document long-distance networks without licensing foreign-origin city myths (parallel caution to Great Zimbabwe historiography).

- **External stimulus vs indigenous multifactor origins.** Connah rejects savanna-to-forest lag-via-trade/migration models as “now thought to be unacceptable”; European seaborne trade shaped later development but did not originate forest states/cities (position).

- **Divine kingship and ideology.** Ruler-as-chief-priest and altar/power-object readings are attributed interpretive packages—material supports ritual centrality, not a complete political constitution from archaeology alone.

All interpretive claims date-stamped **(as of Connah 2016)**; post-2016 Ife excavations and compositional studies are not incorporated here.

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed — Ogundiran in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]] Ch. 59; Monroe Ch. 48. **as of 2013**.)*

### Classical period (AD 1000–1500)

**Ile-Ife** oldest known rainforest urban centre; Classical period **AD 1000–1500**: concentric walls (inner ~7 km, outer ~15 km), potsherd pavements, ceramic-disc wall decoration. Peak 14th/15th c. est. **70,000–105,000** people. **Divine kingship ideology** most significant institutional development; elite mortuary with glass/carnelian beads, terracotta/granite/copper-alloy sculpture, occasional human sacrifice; life-size naturalistic heads. Primary **glass bead production** at Olokun grove; lost-wax brass/bronze casting partly elite-controlled. Copper alloys assumed from Sahara via Niger Bend. Forest products (ivory, pepper, kola) exchanged for Saharan/Sudanese salt, cloth, copper; possible gold supplier near Itagunmodi to Mali Empire. Model of divine kingship radiated regionally (**Benin to Sabe**).

**Trade entrepôt role secondary to political/ideological nucleation from agricultural villages** (Ogundiran). Three rainforest urbanization processes: (1) millennium-long population increase and agricultural land quest; (2) towns on major trade routes; (3) convergence of sociopolitical expansion and external economic opportunities. Fragile soils, forest-clearing energy, and malaria delayed population concentrations until late 1st millennium AD.

Community-building vs authority-building town goals; divine kingship + town as political centre simultaneous AD 800–1000 in Yoruba–Edo, most elaborate at Ile-Ife from 11th c. Hierarchies permeable even with kingship (royalty + lineage chieftains). Farming basis; craft specialization from later 1st millennium AD underwrote itinerant trade/markets.

*Source: [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]].*
