# Gorttoose

Rural **townland** / tenant community on the Mahon (later Pakenham-Mahon) estate near Strokestown, County Roscommon, Ireland — Orser's European case in *[[orser-historical-archaeology-modern-world-1996|A Historical Archaeology of the Modern World]]* (1996), paired with [[palmares|Palmares]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Orser's Irish research supported by Luke Dodd (Strokestown Famine Museum), Kevin Whelan, Terry Barry, and others. Landscape- and document-forward; Orser stated the book is not a dual site report. Write claims in the past tense of documentation and of Orser's 1990s research moment.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Orser 1996 and sources he cites.)*

- **Before settlement:** Townland (*baile*) on the Strokestown Park estate; boggy / "under woods" in 1727 estate records. Variants Gurthuse / Gurtose. Mid-19th c. size ~102.4 ha / 253 acres (Griffith 1857 via Orser). Head-tenant partners (late 18th c.): Bryan Murray, Daniel Maguire, Walter Holmes, James Carlos.
- **Tenant occupation ~1780–1847:** Nested landscapes — market town; Palladian/Ascendancy **demesne** (expansion over earlier Gaelic bawn space); peasant **townlands** including Gorttoose. Colonial deep background: Elizabethan Anglicization of Connacht/Roscommon Gaelic lordships (O'Conor Don, O'Conor Roe, McDermott) as Orser's relevant colonial layer for this land.
- **Estate capitalism and "improvement":** 1837 Ordnance Survey road-journey vs crow-flies; rundale/clachan vs improving linear settlement (Evans vs Whelan as Orser stages it); conacre and potato-centered rent (Devon Commission materials).
- **Famine crisis 1847:** Eviction/assisted emigration during the Great Famine; Orser reports large house-loss at Gorttoose in landscape tabulations (order-of-magnitude collapse — treat figures as his reported synthesis). Pre-Famine conflict: timber marking and wool-pilfering searches (1840–41, Pakenham-Mahon Papers).
- **Aftermath:** Landlords' spatial net more permanent than erasable peasantry housing; present landscape can erase tenant visibility — bridge to archaeology of the poor (Ch. 7). Field entry via Farrell O'Gara (13 June 1994).

## The Haunts at Gorttoose (Ch. 4)

Orser titled the chapter "The Haunts Confer at Gorttoose" and used three documentary epigraphs (grievous situation; music prohibited / Irish language in chains; land let for grass potatoes) to frame:

| Haunt | Local expression (Orser's reading) |
|---|---|
| Colonialism | Long English/British domination of Ireland; Cromwellian and later plantation/estate structures; Mahon family as landlord agents of colonial property order |
| Eurocentrism / Anglocentrism | Language and cultural suppression; surname and naming politics (O'Gara / O'Garra examples); who counts as historical subject |
| Capitalism | Estate rent, conacre, cattle grass vs potato subsistence, eviction as property logic |
| Modernity | Improvement ideology; OS cartography; demesne aesthetics; Famine-era state and landlord techniques |

Personal entry: Orser recounted meeting elderly farmer Farrell O'Gara (13 June 1994) as a living link into local memory.

## Significance

Shows the **[[four-haunts]]** operating **inside Europe** — counters the assumption that historical archaeology's "colonial" cases are only extra-European. Supports "reattach Europe" (Ch. 8): Europe is dig object, not only colonizer homeland. Tenant **arts of resistance** (Scott via Orser) under structural landlord power; Mahon's murder as extreme edge of conflict, not the typical material signature.

## Key Debates

- How far "colonialism" applies inside United Kingdom historical geography.
- Rundale romanticism vs improvement narratives in Irish landscape archaeology.
- Famine archaeology's political stakes (sesquicentenary memory; museum contexts at Strokestown).

## Identity Cautions

Gorttoose is a **locality/community under estate tenure**, not a tribe or archaeological culture. Documentation dates govern tense. Landlord papers privilege elite voice; material landscape and oral memory partially offset but do not cancel archive bias.
