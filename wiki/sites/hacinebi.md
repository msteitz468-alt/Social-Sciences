# Hacınebi Tepe

A 3.3-hectare mound on a bluff over the Euphrates in the Taurus piedmont of southeastern
Turkey, ~3.5 km north of Birecik, excavated by [[stein-gil|Gil Stein]] in six seasons
(1992–1997). Hacınebi is the empirical linchpin of the [[uruk-world-system-debate]]: because
it preserves a **pre-contact local phase** and an overlying **contact phase** with an
intrusive [[uruk-culture|Uruk]] quarter, it allows both a diachronic (before/after) and a
synchronic (Uruk quarter vs. local quarter) test of whether the [[uruk-expansion|Uruk
expansion]] transformed and dominated its Anatolian periphery. Stein's answer — no core
domination, symmetric coexistence for centuries — is the case for his
[[distance-parity|distance-parity]] and [[trade-diaspora|trade-diaspora]] models. Analyzed
as a research act on [[stein-hacinebi-study]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Stein directed eighteen trenches over six seasons (1992–1997), exposing ~1,400 m² and
reaching sterile soil in three areas — A (northeast), B (southeast), and C (the west spur);
cultural deposits reach ~9 m deep at the mound's east end. Excavation quality is high by
the standards relevant to this debate: single-context recovery with a full battery of
specialist analyses — [[radiocarbon-dating|calibrated radiocarbon]], archaeobotany,
zooarchaeology with age/sex profiling, lithic analysis, geochemical bitumen sourcing, and
instrumental neutron-activation analysis (INAA) of sealing clays. Because the site's
interpreter is also its excavator and the theorist advancing the distance-parity critique,
the interpretation and the primary data share an author — a standard reliability
consideration for excavation monographs, though the reported material (ceramics, fauna,
INAA, radiocarbon) is independent of the theoretical claim.

## Occupation Sequence

- **Precontact phase A** (~4100–3800 cal BCE) and **phase B1** (~3800–3700 cal BCE):
  purely local southeast Anatolian Late Chalcolithic occupation, chaff-tempered ceramics
  contemporary with the Amuq F assemblage. Already a complex, hierarchically organized
  society *before any Uruk contact*: a monumental niched-and-buttressed stone enclosure wall
  (3 m wide, preserved >3.3 m high), stone-built storerooms tied to metallurgy and
  administration, indigenous **stamp seals** (including an elaborate red-siltstone seal with
  deer, vultures, and a mace-carrying figure), imported prestige goods (chlorite from ~300
  km east, cowrie shell, copper from the Ergani source, silver from the Amanus — the earliest
  silver known from the site and among the earliest in Anatolia), and on-site copper
  metallurgy (smelting-pit furnaces, molds, crucibles, slag), characterized as "specialized
  but not centralized."
- **Contact phase B2** (~3700–3300 cal BCE): an intrusive Uruk community appears,
  spatially segregated in the northeast, coexisting with the continuing local population.

The mound was later reused for an Early Bronze Age I cemetery (~3100–2700 BCE), an
Achaemenid/Hellenistic occupation (~500–100 BCE), and a small Roman farmstead.

## The Contact Phase: Two Coexisting Communities

The contact-phase evidence is the heart of the site's significance. In its northeast corner
Hacınebi hosted a genuine Uruk enclave — full Uruk ceramic forms (beveled-rim bowls make up
>90% of Uruk-context vessels), Mesopotamian ceramic wall cones, an Uruk-style copper pin,
grooved stone weights, high-fired clay sickles, and increased volumes of bitumen chemically
sourced (like Habuba Kabira's) to the Hit/Deh Luran region of the alluvium. Crucially, the
two populations kept **two parallel administrative systems**: the Uruk quarter used
Mesopotamian **cylinder seals**, jar sealings, a token-filled bulla, and a clay tablet,
while the local population continued its indigenous **stamp seals**. INAA of the sealing
clays (Blackman 1998) showed the Uruk-style sealings to be chemically distinct from the
Anatolian ones and split into a local-clay subgroup (used on site) and a non-local subgroup
matching Susa-area clays — read as "two contemporaneous groups of people at the site, each
using their own record-keeping system," the Uruk quarter maintaining live ties to its
homeland. The two communities also **ate differently**: faunal samples from Uruk contexts
(n≈3,949) and local contexts (n≈1,031) show markedly different species preferences and
butchery cut-mark patterns, and body-part distributions show each group raised and
butchered its own animals — no provisioning of one by the other. Ceramic functional
profiles differ too (Uruk contexts >90% bowls; local contexts a mix of bowls, jars, and
casseroles). There is **no** fortification, weaponry, or destruction horizon in phase B2.

## Significance

Tested against Stein's paired [[distance-parity|correlate sets]], Hacınebi shows the
distance-parity signature and not the world-system one: no shift to intensified
export-oriented surplus production, no introduced irrigation or wool/wine/olive
specialization, no Mesopotamian monopoly over the copper it came to tap, continuity in
lithic technology across the contact horizon, and no sudden jump in local complexity. Stein
concludes the Uruk colony was a small minority that "dealt with its host community as an
equal, rather than as a colonial master," an autonomous [[trade-diaspora|trade diaspora]]
that survived "only at the sufferance of the local rulers," probably through marriage or
exchange alliances, for at least 300 and perhaps 500 years — established to plug into the
pre-existing Anatolian copper-exchange network and extend it south. On this basis Stein
"reject[s] the world-system model's assumptions of core dominance, asymmetric exchange, and
trade as the prime mover."

## Key Debates

Hacınebi is [[uruk-world-system-debate|Position 2]]'s flagship evidence against
[[algaze-guillermo|Algaze's]] asymmetric [[world-systems-analysis|world-system]] reading of
the [[uruk-expansion]]. Algaze's rejoinder (2005) locates parity at the *far edge* of a
system that remains asymmetric *in aggregate* — power "decays with distance" — so Hacınebi's
symmetry is, on his account, exactly what his modified model predicts for a site 1,300 km
upstream, not a refutation of it. The genuinely unresolved question is whether aggregate
asymmetry is demonstrated or merely inferred from the core's own growth. Stein's own
network gradient (coercive at the large near-home Syrian colonies like
[[habuba-kabira|Habuba Kabira]], autonomous at Hacınebi and Godin) concedes the site-to-site
variation on which that dispute turns.
