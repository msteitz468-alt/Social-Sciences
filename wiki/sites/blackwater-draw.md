# Blackwater Draw

Type locality for the [[clovis-culture|Clovis]] archaeological construct (near Clovis, New Mexico). Densified from [[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|Meltzer 2009]] (discovery narrative Ch. 3/8; type-site setting and fauna Ch. 8). Related Folsom breakthrough at [[folsom-site]].

**Identity caution:** Clovis is an etic archaeological culture defined by fluted points and associated toolkit—not a people, language, or genetic population.

**Date hygiene:** Meltzer 2009 ages are **radiocarbon years BP (uncalibrated)**.

## Discovery and Excavation History

*(Attributed — Meltzer 2009.)* After [[folsom-site|Folsom]], the search strategy—arroyos, ancient lakebeds, extinct-megafauna bone reports—led to Clovis. A gravel crew mining an old pond near Clovis, NM hit huge bones. **Edgar B. Howard** (University of Pennsylvania museum), after prior summer finds of bone scraps and Folsom-like fluted points in wind-scoured blowouts, caught a westbound train **12 November 1932**; four days later he telegraphed an extensive bone deposit (mostly bison, also horse and mammoth, some hearth evidence) and announced in *Science* humans “at a very remote period,” staking claim to the site.

**Spring 1933** excavation aimed at showing fluted points with mammoth for International Geological Congress field trips; by late July Howard had points “scattered around like a dog buries bones.” On **3 August**, American and European scientists (including Sir Arthur Smith Woodward) saw spear points—fluted but larger and less refined than Folsom—amid mammoth skeletons; Merriam, Stock, Woodward, and Vanstraelen agreed association of artifacts with extinct elephant and bison.

**Naming rights.** Howard’s *Science* statement claimed Clovis artifacts and “matted masses of bones of mammoth” showed America inhabited ~15,000 years ago. Two weeks later Jesse Figgins announced Dent, Colorado mammoth bones with two large fluted points—too late for naming rights. “We speak of Clovis points and the Clovis culture—not Dent points and the Dent culture” (Meltzer).

Same summer **W. A. Johnston** (Canadian Geological Survey) first suggested a Wisconsin-age Siberia–Alaska land bridge and Mackenzie-valley route after ice retreat; Howard and Ernst Antevs soon matched open corridor + dry Bering Strait to estimated Clovis age (pre-radiocarbon scaffolding for Clovis-first).

Twentieth-century **gravel mining** ravaged deposits; salvage incomplete. Springs that once fed the pond attracted plants, animals, and people until modern irrigation.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Meltzer 2009.)*

1. **Clovis component.** Initial occupation of the spring-fed pond into Blackwater Draw ~**11,300 years ago** (as Meltzer states for the type locality). Fauna included frogs, turtles, snakes, mammoth, bison, horse, camel, peccary, deer, antelope, sabertooth, bear, dire wolf—not all food. Bison among most abundant Clovis-age deposits: seven found; ≥1 speared (point against scapula). Nearly a dozen mammoths excavated by archaeologists; more likely lost to machinery; small animals under-recovered.
2. **Butchery pattern.** Meltzer: **no compelling evidence humans killed all/most mammoths** at the type site—only that they died there. ≥3 with projectile points and butchery tools; died at different times, pond margins or center; nearly complete articulated skeletons—carved where fell, only partially (flesh from upper skeleton; skulls smashed for brains; remainder left). Pattern of partially butchered mammoth in marsh/pond/stream recurs (Lange-Ferguson SD; Lehner AZ). Frison: mired very large animals hard to butcher; healthy adults not easily mired; skilled hunters may isolate juveniles, wound, wait elsewhere.
3. **Folsom above Clovis.** Stratigraphic succession used throughout Plains Paleoindian archaeology: Clovis deposits below Folsom (see [[folsom-site]] chronology ~10,900–10,200 ¹⁴C BP after Clovis).
4. **Later occupations** continue the local Paleoindian/Archaic sequence.

## Significance

- Anchors **Clovis** as a named culture and the relative ordering **Clovis → Folsom**.
- Central to Clovis-first models, [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]], and [[pleistocene-overkill-debate]] (mammoth associations as icon of big-game hunting).
- Demonstrates that type-site “kill” localities can be **waterhole death assemblages with partial butchery**, not pure specialized slaughter camps—important for overkill evidence standards (Grayson & Meltzer positions via Meltzer).

## Historiography

Clovis naming is a **priority-of-publication** story (Howard vs Dent/Figgins) nested in the post-Folsom rush for Ice Age sites. Search bias toward kill sites with extinct megafauna shaped the big-game stereotype of [[paleoindian]] lifeways that Meltzer later dismantles with camp, plant, and small-game evidence from other regions. Gravel-mining damage is a reliability fact: incomplete salvage limits what the type site can still resolve.
