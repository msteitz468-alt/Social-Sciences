# Zhokhov

Mid-Holocene High Arctic settlement on what is now Zhokhov Island in the East Siberian Sea (Russia)—about 1,000 km inside the Arctic Circle. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is the earliest known evidence of human presence in the High Arctic and a unique polar-bear hunting economy with early dog traction.

**Identity caution:** Do not equate Zhokhov occupants with Dyuktai culture (lithic tradition already abandoned by this time in Mithen’s account), with First Americans, or with later Arctic ethnic groups. `associated_with` colonization narratives only.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: a 1989 expedition of the Leningrad Institute and the Arctic & Antarctic Research Institute found the settlement; **Vladimir Pitul'ko** excavated permafrost-preserved dwellings. **Aleksey Kasparov** analysed polar-bear butchery. Organic preservation is exceptional relative to Holocene forest Siberian lithic scatters.

Seasonal Lubbock reconstructions (spring arrival, autumn den hunts, winter departure) are narrative, grounded in faunal seasonality and site logistics—not eyewitness prehistory.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Pitul'ko / Kasparov via Mithen 2003, ch.41.)*

1. **Context:** By 15,000 BCE Dyuktai-related people at Berelekh; by 6400 BCE High Arctic settlement at Zhokhov in Mithen’s colonization sketch. By Zhokhov’s time, bifacial Dyuktai and wedge cores had been abandoned across forested Siberia for conical/prismatic cores, blades, and ground axes.
2. **Economy:** fauna include reindeer, geese, swans, occasional seal, but **primary food was polar bear**—unique among known settlements; contradicts the assumption that High Arctic colonization required specialized sea-mammal hunting.
3. **Butchery (Kasparov):** field discard of lower legs/feet; carcass jointed; heads opened for canines and brains; bone sizes suggest mainly females hunted while seeking winter den sites.
4. **Transport:** shaped wooden sledge runner (~1 m, polish/striations, bevelled left-side use) plus dog bones intermediate fox–wolf size and concentrated dog faeces (reindeer hair/bone) indicating tethered dogs—Pitul'ko guesses dog traction, pushing Arctic dog-sled technology back several millennia.
5. **Seasonality (reconstructed):** arrival in spring with reindeer and nesting birds; late summer/autumn polar-bear hunts as females den; departure south before polar night—until sea breaches make Zhokhov an island.
6. **Clothing:** no direct remains; Mithen uses Arctic ethnographic analogy (reindeer-skin outerwear, bird-skin under-jackets, polar-bear-sole boots, sinew thread) as necessary for the hunting regime—**analogy flagged**.

## Significance

- Earliest High Arctic settlement evidence as of Mithen 2003; extends human colonization narratives into polar deserts.
- Unique polar-bear staple economy revises models of Arctic adaptation pathways.
- Early dog traction / sledge evidence of major technological interest.
- Showcase of permafrost preservation versus the thin Holocene forest archaeological record of Siberia.

## Key Debates

- Certainty of dog *traction* vs dogs as camp animals with sledges used otherwise (Pitul'ko’s inference).
- How seasonal vs multi-season the occupation was.
- Relation of Zhokhov people to earlier Dyuktai and to Beringian/American colonizations (chronological association only in Mithen—not identity).
- Ethnographic clothing analogies as necessity arguments vs direct evidence.
