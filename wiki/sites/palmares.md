# Palmares

Seventeenth-century **quilombo** / maroon polity (multi-settlement complex) in the hills of Alagoas–Pernambuco, northeastern Brazil. Principal New World case in [[orser-charles-e|Orser]]'s *[[orser-historical-archaeology-modern-world-1996|A Historical Archaeology of the Modern World]]* (1996). Colonial Portuguese name **Palmares** (from palm trees); endonym **Angola Janga** ("Little Angola") — Orser used the colonialist name because it is "the name most recognized today."

## Discovery and Excavation History

Orser's 1990s collaboration with Pedro Paulo Funari and Brazilian institutions (including Afro-Brazilian studies and local culture authorities at União dos Palmares). As of the 1996 book, Palmares is a **program illustration** grounded in chronicles, Dutch-period descriptions, and survey — Orser stated the book is not an extended excavation report. Later fieldwork and Brazilian scholarship may supersede 1996 specifics; date-stamp claims to Orser 1996 and his cited sources.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed findings as Orser reports them from secondary/documentary sources.)*

- **Formation ~1605:** Fugitives from coastal *fazendas* of the sugar captaincy of Pernambuco; early founding narrative of ~40 "Negroes of Guine" (Rocha Pita via Orser) — "Guine" as Portuguese pan-West-African label. First military raid recorded 1612.
- **Flourishing 17th c.:** Multiethnic population — African-descended people (including central African Bantu connections; *kilombo* homeland nets), Tupi-linked indigenous peoples, dissatisfied Portuguese *moradores*, possibly Dutch-period rebels. Dutch descriptions (Lintz 1640; Reijmbach 1645 via Barlaeus): Great/Lesser Palmares population estimates in thousands; palisades; forges; council house; crops and livestock; king **Ganga Zumba** ("Great Lord"). Leadership later associated with **Zumbi**; internal tension between accommodation and armed resistance.
- **Destruction 1694:** Capital taken night of 5–6 February 1694 after roughly two decades of annual assaults; Portuguese-funded campaign for "extinction of the Negroes of Palmares" (Ennes materials via Orser). Orser stressed Palmarista power was real (Serra da Barriga defense) but "ultimately short lived" as a lasting polity — while the spirit remained strong in modern Brazilian memory.

## Significance for Global Historical Archaeology

Orser used Palmares to argue:

1. **Against isolation.** Maroon communities sought remoteness (hard for archaeologists to find — Nichols's empty-handed Great Dismal Swamp search), yet Palmares's **outside links** (guns, trade, intelligence, indigenous allies and co-residents, African homeland nets) enabled nearly a century of survival.
2. **[[mutualism-historical-archaeology|Mutualist]] implications.** Guns matter as relationships, not as traits; macro + micro scales; reassembly of world connectedness (Wolf).
3. **[[four-haunts|Four haunts]]** in a non-European, subaltern modern-world context — colonialism and capitalism structuring the sugar frontier; Eurocentrism in naming and archive; modernity of Atlantic commercial world.
4. **Multiscalar dialectics (Ch. 8).** Physical structure (forest/mountain shelter, palms, clays) → sociohistorical nets to Africa and Europe → contradictions (internal Zumbi crisis; external colonial cracks) → Dutch attack as mutualist problem, not one-sided colonial narrative.
5. **Subaltern speech (Ch. 7).** Elite-first documentary entry (governors, mercenary captains); archaeology and critical reading recover names (Zumbi, Aqualtune, Ganga Zumba); relations as "shouting matches."

**Pipes (Ch. 5):** Orser discussed a small set of clay smoking pipes from Palmares survey contexts and compared them with Barbados, Santo Domingo, Cuba, and West African (Ghana) materials — reading palm-tree motifs and distribution as network and resistance evidence while flagging **surface-find and small-n caveats**. Not wiki-voice ethnic equations from pipes alone.

## Key Debates

- African cultural continuity vs. creolized multiethnic frontier culture.
- How archaeology relates to nationalist and Black-consciousness commemorations (Orser notes 1995 Zumbi tricentenary politics of research).
- Scale and quality of archaeological vs. documentary knowledge (1996 vs later work).

## Identity Cautions

"Palmares" names a **polity/complex**, not a single village or single ethnic group. Material culture ≠ "African survival" without argument. Endonym Angola Janga vs. colonial ethnonyms ("Guine," "Negroes of Palmares") are different naming regimes — record both. Four-way non-identity: assemblage ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetics.
