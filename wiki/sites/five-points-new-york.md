## Discovery and Excavation History

The Courthouse Block excavation in lower Manhattan uncovered eighteen tenement foundations beneath a parking lot, along with truncated backyard privies, wells, and cisterns beneath basement floors — fifty such features across fourteen historic properties, yielding "nearly a million artefacts," the possessions of immigrant tenants who had no formal garbage collection ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]], citing O'Keeffe & Yamin, ch. 6). The site is now occupied by a courthouse in Foley Square, New York City's judicial district.

Sequential occupation of the block was documented archaeologically and historically: the area, originally outside the city proper, is shown on 18th-century maps as containing a large pond bordered by a "negroes' burying ground" on one side and an industrial area (tanneries, breweries, rope walks polluting the pond) on the other. It became working-class housing as the city's population grew in the 19th century. Early residents included free Blacks and newly arrived immigrants — day labourers, tanners, bakers, brewers, carpenters, and tailors, many German. One documented early resident, Tobias Hoffman, a baker on Pearl Street in the 1790s, served meals on floral Chinese porcelain, drank from etched goblets, and smoked a German-style porcelain pipe — evidence, per the excavators, that some residents maintained "Old World elegance" despite crowded conditions ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

## Occupation Sequence

Eastern European Jewish immigrants settled subdivided houses on one bounding street by the 1830s, working as second-hand clothing dealers, tailors, and shoemakers in what became New York's first garment district. Irish immigrants fleeing the potato famine filled four- and five-storey tenements on the other side of the block in the 1840s. Peter Hall (1998) termed this juxtaposition of distinct worlds on different sides of one urban block a "cultural crucible" (as cited in [[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

Comparative artefact analysis found differences between Irish and Jewish tenement households: Irish households used Staffordshire teawares (including Willow ware) identical to those used in Ireland, favored a diet with pork despite cheaper fish being available, drank more wine than beer at home, relied heavily on patent medicines (reflecting manual-labour injuries), and avoided tobacco pipes bearing patriotic or Nativist symbols such as stars and eagles — Nativist symbolism being associated with anti-Irish prejudice — choosing the plainest, cheapest fluted pipes instead. Jewish households, mainly tailors and second-hand clothing dealers, consumed more fish than meat, favored lamb over pork or beef, owned fewer and different dishes, preferred wine to beer but drank less overall, and used significantly less medicine than their Irish neighbours. German and Polish (non-Irish, non-Jewish) populations on the block owned Nativist-symbol pipes ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

Backyard space on the block shrank over time as tenements were added first to lot fronts, then to backs — sometimes leaving only about 20 by 20 feet (roughly 6 metres square) of open space, sometimes none at all. This spatial change was documented via 19th-century insurance maps, which the excavators note are "the urban archaeologist's basic tool" in the United States but do not record informal plumbing or interior life ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

Rebecca Yamin's (2002) study of children's toys at the site juxtaposed technical ceramic classifications — "frozen charlotte" dolls, hand-painted and moulded porcelain, spatter-painted whiteware — with quoted voices of working-class parents and 19th-century critics on child labour, and used comparative bar-graph analysis of marbles across three sites, including a middle-class Brooklyn comparison ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]).

## Significance

Mary Beaudry and colleagues (Beaudry et al. 1991: 284) argued that urban archaeological narratives from the site should be told "from the inside out," contrasting the archaeological record with outside, mythologized perceptions of neighbourhoods like Five Points ([[hicks-beaudry-companion-historical-archaeology-2006|Hicks & Beaudry 2006]]). The excavators (O'Keeffe & Yamin) explicitly contest Dolores Hayden's (1994: 466) claim that urban landscapes rarely preserve "the spatial history of ordinary working people and everyday lives" above ground: while this may hold above ground, the chapter argues it is "certainly untrue" of subsurface archaeological remains, which do reveal such history.

## Key Debates

Alan Mayne (1993) coined the term "imagined slum" for the way outside observers misunderstood and mythologized urban neighbourhoods such as Five Points, framed by the chapter as the "Five Points myth" (also discussed by Fitts 2000; Yamin 1997, 1998c; Cook 1998b). This "imagined slum" framing stands in tension with the "inside out" evidentiary approach of Beaudry et al. (1991), and with Mayne's own comparative work with Tim Murray on urban-slum landscapes elsewhere (Mayne and Murray 2001), including the comparable "opportunistic" urban-lot excavation programme at "Little Lon," Melbourne, Australia (Mayne and Lawrence 1998).
