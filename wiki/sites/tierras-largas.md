# Tierras Largas

## Discovery and Excavation History

Small, shallow Early Formative hamlet near **Atzompa**, Valley of Oaxaca (low piedmont spur overlooking Atoyac floodplain ~5 km N of Oaxaca City). **Marcus C. Winter** excavated it (1969) as a probability-sample horizontal exposure — presented in *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* (study [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]]) as among the first serious attempts to map Formative village layout via random quadrats rather than mound-centered trenches. Flannery called it “perhaps the first serious attempt to apply the principles of probability sampling to extensive horizontal exposure of Formative deposits,” producing the first maps that approximate Formative village layout. Surveys 1966/1968: Early/Middle Formative ≤2 ha, generally under 50 cm above bedrock — ideal for extensive horizontal excavation of a “Formative hamlet” (Parsons/Blanton definitions). 1968 mapped; 1969 sampling on a featureless plowed field bisected by a dirt road; few mounds suspected to postdate periods of interest.

### From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — Winter’s 3.7% sample design

All design claims attributed to Winter (and Flannery’s framing) in Flannery ed. 1976 Ch. 3.

- **Universe:** based primarily on surface Early Formative **San José phase** (1150–850 B.C.) sherds (not other phases); extended 10–30 m beyond gradual sherd decrease on E/NE/SW; other edges by contour changes (piedmont spur, later terrace, slope). Excavation later found earlier **Tierras Largas phase** (1400–1150 B.C.). Omitted: road and scraped road-fill area, visible mounds (separate problem; most presumably postdate San José), Area A prior test.
- **Sample fraction:** **3.7%** random sample of squares — Flannery’s R.M.A./S.G.S. parable: yielded six Early Formative houses each with 3–5 storage pits, burials, household inventory in situ; R.M.A. dismissed them as “Gross,” preferred 5 m deep mounds; site chosen “precisely because it's only 30 cm to bedrock” despite nearby 7 m and 17 m deep sites.
- **Retrospective preference (Winter):** might have been useful to draw a **stratified systematic unaligned** sample to guard against clustering of sample units and large unsampled areas.
- **Population-range framing used to justify sample:** if ~5 people per house, 8–40 houses imply village population 40–200 — taken as reasonable for a small Formative village.

## Occupation Sequence

Early Formative residential features (houses, pits, burials) cut into bedrock; shallow deposit (~30 cm to bedrock in the programmatic description), making wide horizontal exposure feasible.

### From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — ~8–12 households, stable hamlet

**Layout (Winter):** sampling showed that during any one period the village comprised a series of **8–12 [[household-cluster]]s**, separated by courtyards or open work areas **20–40 m** across; each cluster ≥300 m² with at least one house plus pits, burials, ovens, drainage ditches, features, activity areas. Early Formative spacing of clusters ~20–40 m; two Late San José clusters closer (possible extended household); Rosario phase spacing ~35 m (~1.5× San José spacing). Only 3–10% of surface area within excavated clusters yielded primary deposits. Spatial layout change: initial Early Tierras Largas occupation possibly two discrete groups (north edge + southwest); later single unit over 2 ha; end of Early Formative concentration in north ~1+ ha (TL phase occupation area ~2× San José phase); Middle Formative spread ~3 ha. Flannery: late Tierras Largas and late San José phase maps suggest roughly rectangular open areas 30–40 m across at 1250 B.C., ~20 m at 900 B.C. (possible courtyard groups — pattern may be read into data). By Rosario phase, possible high-status residence at site center (prepared earth floor, posthole concentration, three large ovens, artifacts) — could add 5–10 to ~50 population estimate; commoner households dispersed around center.

**Table 8.1 best estimates (Winter):**

| Early Tierras Largas | 1400–1300 | 1.58 | 10 |
| Late Tierras Largas | 1300–1150 | 2.24 | 10 |
| Early San José | 1150–1000 | 0.95 | 5 |
| Late San José | 1000–850 | 1.59 | 8 |
| Early Guadalupe | 850–750 | 3.0 | 9–10 |
| Late Guadalupe | 750–650 | 3.0 | 9–10 |
| Rosario | 650–500 | 3.0 | 9 |

At 5 persons/household → ~50 people first half of Early Formative; post–Tierras Largas phase drop to ~half then recovery to ~9–10 households through Middle Formative. **Essentially no growth in households** across seven subphases despite hectare fluctuations — Winter’s central contrast with [[san-jose-mogote]] (grew more than 20×). Pattern holds for many Valley of Oaxaca EF–MF hamlets (e.g. Fábrica San José ~10–12 households throughout Middle Formative; nearly a dozen more hamlets stayed 1–3 ha). Winter: most hamlets, once at 8–12 households, remained stable — **modal size** for Early–Middle Formative hamlets (1–3 ha on survey). Late MF changes (high-status residence; larger spacing without more households) **cannot** be attributed to population pressure at Tierras Largas itself — intercommunity / valley-wide processes.

**Momentary population caution (Marcus citing Winter):** Winter originally ~12–22 San José phase houses (Flannery 1972:44); ceramic subphases Early/Late San José cut momentary estimates roughly in half (~5 Early, ~8 Late).

## Significance

Method type-site for within-community sampling and [[household-cluster]] recovery; contrast case to deep stratified "telephone booth" sites preferred by traditional Formative excavation. Load-bearing stable-hamlet case in Winter’s growth chapter and Flannery’s multi-scalar program; shell-working and household-level obsidian variation (Winter & Pires-Ferreira) test reciprocity vs pooling against [[san-jose-mogote]].

### Household economy and exchange (Flannery ed. 1976)

Flannery & Winter: universal household activities present; Feature 184 greater than 300 small chert flakes + broken biface (pressure-retouch specialist); Feature 140 deer bone cache for handles/rings; shell ornament production nearly every extensively excavated 1150–850 B.C. house; macaw wing-bone feather prep in 2–3 pits (not other valley villages). Exemplar Late Tierras Largas Cluster (~1250 B.C.): partial ~4×6 m whitewashed wattle-and-daub house; 8 bell-shaped pits avg 1.5 m³; burials adult male greater than 40, female 20–30, female prone; macaw feathers, Gulf Coast turtle-shell drum — agriculture, hunting, food prep + ceremonial participation. Winter & Pires-Ferreira: 107 of 249 chipped-stone pieces from 8 EF/MF clusters; average ~15% obsidian vs ~85% local cherts; no wide inter-household amount differences; Early Formative differential source use by household (individual negotiation); Middle Formative more uniform sources (tentative pooling at hamlets with resident elite). Pacific marine shell import before 1300 B.C.; even distribution; no Atlantic shells then. Etla linear system: Late San José intersite distances ~5 km including Tierras Largas; mid-line land partition ±645 ha alluvium; capacity far above 8 households (Flannery Table 6.1).

## Key Debates

Representativeness of a small shallow hamlet for "the" Early Formative village; sampling fraction and reconstruction of full community plan (Winter / Flannery ed. 1976). Whether Tierras Largas is a separate community or outlying barrio of a larger village (Flannery open question deferred to regional chapters). Whether Rosario “elite” center residence is securely high-status. Winter’s modal-size thesis vs growth–climax–decline models applied indiscriminately to all Formative sites.

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996)

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

**Phase name and egalitarian prestige (Tierras Largas phase, 1400–1150 BC).** The ceramic phase takes its name from this hamlet type-site; red-painted bands, stripes, and chevrons make the pottery “instantly recognizable on survey.” Marcus & Flannery reconstruct the type-site as a hamlet of **5–10 households** (~4×6 m whitewashed wattle-and-daub houses; family of ~4–5) engaged in agriculture, food preparation, hunting/trapping, and sewing. Prestige imports appear without hereditary rank: Household LTL-1 storage pits held *Ara militaris* wing bones and a *Dermatemys mawit* turtle-shell drum imported from tropical lowlands; late Tierras Largas phase communities imported a “steady stream” of Pacific mother-of-pearl, *Spondylus*, and estuary snails. Valley-wide, ≥19 permanent Tierras Largas settlements were nearly all hamlets <3 ha; [[san-jose-mogote|San José Mogote]] was uniquely large (~5× second-largest) but, they argue, still failed to overcome nearby hamlets’ autonomy — labor and materials remained local. Obsidian reached even smallest hamlets at the household level (autonomous-village procurement pattern).

**San José dualism and Guadalupe feasting.** In the San José phase Earth/Sky vessel dualism, small-village Tierras Largas had almost exclusively **Earth (Earthquake)** vessels (vs Sky/Lightning at Abasolo/Tomaltepec). Guadalupe phase: ~3 ha, 9–10 households (~45–50 persons); Feature 99 held remains of ≥5 dogs (~50 kg meat) as rare large-scale dog feasting; multiple burials pair adult males (presumed household heads) with women and/or children.

**Rosario satellite.** In the Rosario phase Marcus & Flannery place Tierras Largas as a **third-order satellite** in the San José Mogote hierarchy (~50 persons in 9–10 households). It is among communities that lose population with San José Mogote at the Rosario → Monte Albán I transition (synoikism-related depopulation of Etla centers).
