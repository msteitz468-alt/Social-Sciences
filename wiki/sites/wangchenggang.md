# Wangchenggang

## Discovery and Excavation History

Wangchenggang, in Dengfeng, Henan, was occupied from the early Neolithic into the Bronze Age; its principal significance is as a Longshan-period fortified center in a multicentered, competitive regional settlement system in the upper Ying River Valley (Liu & Chen 2012). Two small connected rammed-earth enclosures (~1 ha each) were built ca. 2200 BC; a much larger enclosure (35 ha) followed ca. 2100–2050 BC. The site was first excavated in 1977, when the discovery of the small internal enclosures suggesting a segregated ruling elite led to it being long regarded as connected to the Xia dynasty's origins (Henansheng Wenwu Yanjiusuo 1992; Beijing and Henanshang 2007). Subsequent discovery of the large enclosure prompted a revised interpretation (see Key Debates).

## Occupation Sequence

Wangchenggang served as the settlement center for 22 unwalled sites in the upper Ying River Valley during the Longshan period (ca. 2200–1835 BC). Excavation has revealed many ash pits, some containing human sacrifices, and evidence of stone-tool craft production (drills, blanks) for spades, axes, knives, and sickles (Beijing and Henanshang 2007; Henansheng Wenwu Yanjiusuo 1992). No evidence of elaborate elite burials has been found at Wangchenggang, in contrast to the contemporary site of [[taosi]] in the Linfen Basin.

## Significance

Wangchenggang is a key data point for the Longshan-period Central Plain's multicentered, competitively organized settlement landscape — rammed-earth-fortified centers vying regionally rather than a single dominant polity. Liu and Chen (2012) situate it within a broader pattern (alongside [[taosi]] and other Longshan centers) of hierarchically organized societies with concentrated elite political, ritual, and economic power, prestige-goods exchange, and intergroup warfare — a chiefdom-type/proto-state framing they treat as distinct from a settled "state" classification (see [[chiefdom]], [[state-formation]]).

## Key Debates

- **Yu the Great / Xia-capital attribution (position).** Some scholars identify Wangchenggang with Yangcheng, the capital said to have been founded by Yu the Great, legendary founder of the Xia dynasty. The 1977 discovery of small internal enclosures suggesting a segregated ruling elite supported this reading for a time. The subsequent discovery of the large 35 ha enclosure prompted a revised interpretation attributing the small enclosures to Gun (Yu's legendary father) and the large walls to Yu himself (Henansheng Wenwu Yanjiusuo 1992; Beijing and Henanshang 2007). Liu and Chen (2012) note this revised attribution "fails to explain a gap of some 100 years between the two constructions at Wangchenggang" — an attributed critique, not a resolution of the underlying identification question, which remains a live position tied to the broader [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]] (not owned by this page; link only).
- **Absence of elaborate elite burials vs. Taosi.** Liu and Chen suggest the contrast with Taosi's rich mortuary record "may be related to different leadership strategies used by the Longshan elite" rather than to differential excavation coverage — an attributed interpretive claim, not a settled finding.
