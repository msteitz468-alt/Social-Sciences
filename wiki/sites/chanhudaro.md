# Chanhu-daro

Smaller Mature [[harappan-civilization]] settlement in Sindh (Pakistan), best known as a craft and trade node rather than a first-tier city. In [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Kenoyer 1998]], it is the only site with a **confirmed long-carnelian-bead workshop** carefully excavated with the full manufacturing sequence and tools.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The site’s principal modern documentation rests on excavations directed by [[mackay-ernest|Ernest J. H. Mackay]] (Chanhu-Daro reports cited throughout Kenoyer 1998). Relative to the long multi-season programs at [[harappa]] and [[mohenjo-daro]], the published record is more compact but unusually rich for **chaîne opératoire** craft evidence: bead drills, blanks, grinding stones, unfinished beads, and related debris were recovered and later reanalyzed experimentally (Kenoyer, Vidale, and collaborators as of Kenoyer 1998).

Excavation quality: Mackay’s work predates modern single-context standards but remains the load-bearing primary record for Indus long-carnelian manufacture; later interpretations (including Kenoyer’s) rest on that assemblage plus experimental drilling studies.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]] 1998, synthesizing Mackay and Localization Era work.)*

- **Harappan Phase (Integration Era):** Settlement functioning as a major craft/trading center within the southern Indus network. Heavier cubical [[standardized-weights]] occur here (as at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa) — Kenoyer notes such large weights are confined to major trading centers rather than ordinary rural sites. Miniature inscribed tablets are rare (one or two examples), consistent with production concentrated at the largest cities.
- **Craft production:** Confirmed workshop for long biconical carnelian beads: nodules (Gujarat source area per Kenoyer), heating, sawing, chipping, grinding, multi-stage perforation with specialized “Ernestite” stone drills (named after Mackay), and polishing. Catalogue material also includes wild-animal seals, cubical and barrel weights, bleached carnelian designs, and bronze cart models shared in style with Harappa.
- **Localization / Jhukar Phase:** Per Kenoyer Ch. 9, Jhukar Phase occupation continues at Chanhu-daro (with Jhukar, Mohenjo-daro, and Amri) after the Integration Era’s political-economic package fragments. Major differences include pottery designs, loss of [[indus-script]] and Harappan animal seals, and rise of circular geometric seals, alongside technological continuity in many crafts.

## Significance

- **Type case for specialized craft towns** in the Indus system: not among the five largest urban centers ([[mohenjo-daro]], [[harappa]], [[ganweriwala]], [[rakhigarhi]], [[dholavira]] per Kenoyer’s size list), yet indispensable for reconstructing elite ornament production and long-distance exchange of finished carnelian.
- Long carnelian belts like the [[allahdino]] hoard (~36 beads; Kenoyer estimates >480 person-days for one worker) are argued to have taken multi-year workshop production; similar belts and beads reached Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Allahdino, and, via trade, Mesopotamian contexts (Ur royal cemetery; Meluhhan connection in Kenoyer’s reading).
- **Ernestite drills** and bead-grinding equipment from the site are primary evidence for a technology that, Kenoyer argues, was later cut off in the Punjab Phase — forcing tubular-drill reuse on small beads at Harappa Periods 4–5.

## Key Debates

- Functional identity of smaller “factory towns” vs residential settlements in the [[indus-polity-debate]] — craft control without palace archives.
- Continuity vs rupture into the Jhukar Phase at southern sites ([[indus-transformation-debate]] / deurbanization literature).
- Whether long-carnelian production was uniquely concentrated at Chanhu-daro or whether other workshops remain unexcavated (Kenoyer: only confirmed workshop *so far*).

## Kenoyer 1998

[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998]] and [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark]] treat Chanhu-daro as the anchor for bead technology (Ch. 8; catalogue Ernestite drills and manufacturing stages), a secondary tablet findspot, a heavy-weight trading center, a source of local-style unicorn figurines and cart models (Ch. 5), and a southern Localization/Jhukar continuity site (Ch. 9). ## Historiography

The site’s reputation is inseparable from Mackay’s mid-20th-century campaigns and from later experimental archaeology that turned those finds into measurable production rates. As with most Indus small towns, absolute chronologies remain less tightly radiocarbon-anchored in Kenoyer 1998 than the Harappa HARP sequence; periodization follows the broader Indus Valley Tradition frame (Integration → Localization).

## Wright (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]], *[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]* 2010, chs. 7, 9, 11.)*

**"Populated chiefly by artisans."** Mackay (1943:38), the original excavator, found production debris and craft variety so extensive at Chanhu-daro that he described the settlement as "populated chiefly by artisans" — a characterization Wright repeats as evidence for craft-town status rather than a first-tier administrative center. Production was spatially segregated: seal manufacture concentrated in the northern quarter, while beads, weights, and metalworking were concentrated in the southern portion (Mackay 1943). One sizable structure (locus #459, 17'8" × 14'8", with substantial walls and a bathing platform) is of otherwise unclear function.

**Bead and carnelian production.** Carnelian arrived at Chanhu-daro unmodified, "without any preliminary stage of reduction" (Vidale 2000:102), contrasting with the prepared chert cores and blades shipped in from the Rohri Hills. Four bead types are documented (Vidale 2000): a simple disk bead; an elongated/long-barrel bead, the most technically demanding, traded as far as Mesopotamia and possibly requiring specialized knowledge held only by Indus artisans; a white-to-red agate "eye" bead exploiting the stone's layered structure; and a bleached/etched bead produced through a combined reductive-and-pyrotechnological chemical-and-refiring process. Based on this production and possible export to the Royal Cemetery of Ur (Pollock 1999), Kenoyer (1997a) and Vidale (2000) "hypothesized" — explicitly marked by Wright as a hypothesis, not a settled finding — centralized bead production at Chanhu-daro "controlled by state institutions or important merchants connected with political elites" (Vidale 2000:58).

**Hoards.** Chanhu-daro yielded four large hoards plus several smaller ones (compare twelve at Mohenjo-daro, seven at Harappa, three at Lothal, one at Allahdino); one Chanhu-daro axe bears an inscription. Rissman (1988) and Kenoyer (1998) read such caches as strongly associated with artisans or merchants rather than elite consumers.

**Jhukar-phase decline.** As the Post-urban period set in, Chanhu-daro's settlement pattern became "haphazard and less focused"; square stamp seals were replaced by circular seals bearing new motifs, terracotta female figurines and cubical weights became rare, and the Indus script disappeared except for incised pottery marks — possibly signaling the end of a formal administrative system (Mughal 1992a; H. Miller 2005, reanalyzing the Chanhu-daro Jhukar assemblage). This documented change is more gradual than Mackay's original view of a sharp cultural break at the Jhukar horizon. The site was abandoned after this phase and not reoccupied for centuries.
