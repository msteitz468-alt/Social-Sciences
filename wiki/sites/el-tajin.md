# El Tajín

Classic-to-Epiclassic center in northern Veracruz, notable for its concentration of ball courts and elaborate architectural relief programs. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], El Tajín has 17 ball courts; the site's modern name derives from a Totonac belief in twelve old thunderstorm-lord spirits called Tajín, equated with the Rain God.

## Discovery and Excavation History

El Tajín was first occupied in the Classic period as a minor village. Work by Arturo Pascual Soto at nearby outlier sites — Morgadal Grande (site of the earliest known ball court with carved relief benches) and Cerro Grande (a Classic stela fragment) — shows early Teotihuacan-influenced decoration prefiguring the later Tajín style. Systematic conquest and rebuilding of these outlying sites by El Tajín began in the seventh or early eighth century AD.

## Occupation Sequence

Epiclassic El Tajín's nucleus covers roughly 146 acres (60 hectares), comprising a lower zone of pyramids and ball courts and an upper zone of elaborate palaces. The **Pyramid of the Niches** (~60 ft / 18 m high, four-sided, six-tiered, faced with carved stone and originally covered in red stucco, with blue used on some structures) is the site's central emblematic structure; its niche-and-flying-cornice motif spread through northern Veracruz as Tajín's influence extended. The **Building of the Columns**, the largest palace complex, has carved column drums depicting a procession of victorious warriors presenting captives to a ruler named "13 Rabbit" (a 260-day-count calendrical name), with a disemboweled victim shown before him. The **South Ball Court** relief panels (six in total) depict a ritual sequence — alliance-making, war preparation, the decapitation sacrifice of a ball player (read as the losing team's captain), and a skeletal figure rising from a jar of water — interpreted (attributed) by Coe & Koontz as symbolizing the ball court's "skull place" as a spring of agricultural fecundity, per later Aztec belief. The site's destruction by fire is "traditionally by 1200, but perhaps by 1000" per recent evidence of post-Tajín squatter occupation.

## Significance

El Tajín is the principal Classic Veracruz center in Coe & Koontz's account, illustrating the ballgame's ritual-sacrificial elaboration and the northward spread of a distinctive niche-and-cornice architectural style; see [[concepts/mesoamerican-ballgame]].

## Key Debates

- Dating of the site's final destruction (c. 1000 vs. c. 1200) is unresolved pending further evidence of post-abandonment squatter occupation.
