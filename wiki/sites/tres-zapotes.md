## Discovery and Excavation History

Tres Zapotes, about 100 miles (160 km) northwest of [[sites/la-venta]], comprises roughly fifty earthen mounds stretched along two miles (3.2 km) of stream bank. Matthew Stirling excavated the site in the 1930s–40s, discovering it along with San Lorenzo and La Venta; more recent excavation has been directed by Christopher Pool (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Occupation Sequence

Early occupation at Tres Zapotes was contemporary with La Venta, with the site reaching its zenith in the Late Preclassic — after the Gulf Coast Olmec heartland's classic phase — and continuing into the Epi-Olmec period. Two colossal heads at the site date to the earlier Olmec horizon. Pool suggests, in attributed voice, that the site's relatively flat architectural hierarchy indicates control by several powerful lineages of roughly equal rank rather than a single paramount ruler (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Significance

Tres Zapotes is best known for Stela C, which bears a Long Count date read as (7).16.6.16.18. Stirling reconstructed the stela's missing coefficient as "seven," proposing a reading of 3 September 32 BCE (Gregorian); this reconstruction was vindicated in 1969 when a farmer discovered the stela's missing top fragment, confirming the coefficient (Coe & Koontz 2013). At the time of the source's writing, this made Stela C one of the earliest securely read Long Count dates from the Olmec heartland — though Coe & Koontz note it is not the very earliest known: a reused slab at Chiapa de Corzo carries a Long Count date of (7.16).3.2.13, equivalent to 8 December 36 BCE, roughly four years earlier. The Tuxtla Statuette, an Epi-Olmec jade duck-billed winged figure, carries a later Long Count date of 8.6.2.4.17 (14 March CE 162).

Coe & Koontz offer, as attributed speculation, the suggestion that "it is not unlikely that Olmec literati invented the Long Count and perhaps also developed certain astronomical observations with which the Maya are usually credited" — a claim contested by the comparably early priority of Izapan-tradition Long Count dating at El Baúl (see [[sites/izapa]]).

## Key Debates

Tres Zapotes' role in the earliest Long Count dating feeds discussion in [[concepts/long-count]] and, indirectly, the [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]] over whether the Gulf Coast Olmec or the Izapan tradition developed Mesoamerican calendrical writing first.
