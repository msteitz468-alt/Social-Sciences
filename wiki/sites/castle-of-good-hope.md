# Castle of Good Hope

A pentagonal stone fortress at **Cape Town, South Africa**, built by the Dutch East India Company (VOC) from 1666. It replaced the earlier (1652) wood-and-sod fort and became the seat of Dutch, then British, then apartheid-era South African military authority at the Cape.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The Castle has not been treated in the ingested source ([[hall-archaeology-modern-world-2000|Hall 2000]]) as an excavated site in the conventional sense; its analysis rests on documentary and architectural-plan evidence. [[hall-martin|Martin Hall]] and colleagues (Hall et al. 1990a, cited by Hall 2000) analyze the fortification's plan and its relationship to the surrounding town grid. Excavation history proper is [[unknown]] from the material ingested here.

## Occupation Sequence

The foundation stone was laid in 1666 under Commander **Zacharias Wagenaer**, following a standard Dutch military fortification plan. The new stone fortress was sited roughly 500 paces north of the original 1652 wood-and-sod fort — a relocation that, per Hall et al. (1990a), threw off the street grid laid out by surveyor **Pieter Potter**, whose plan had been oriented to the earlier fort. The Castle subsequently passed through Dutch VOC, British colonial, and (in the 20th century) apartheid-era South African Defence Force (SADF) headquarters use. [ATTRIBUTED — Hall 2000, citing Hall et al. 1990a]

## Significance

The 1666 foundation was marked by a commemorative poem celebrating the Dutch conquest of the Cape — Hall reads this as evidence of the fortification's ideological register from its inception, not merely a defensive work. **Krotoa** (a Khoikhoi woman central to early Cape contact history; see [[khoikhoi]]) was, per Hall (2000), the first person buried at the Castle. In 1995–96 the photographer/artist Lien Botha staged the exhibition **"Krotoa's Room"** at the Castle, inspired by Karen Press's narrative poem *Krotoa's Story* (1990); the installation layered archival images of colonial material culture (gables, furniture, porcelain, silverware) against images of oppression (slavery, Robben Island's prison) viewed through circular apertures, with Botha stating the aim was "to open up history to other perspectives and meanings" (Botha 1995). [ATTRIBUTED — Hall 2000]

## Key Debates

The Castle's layered use — VOC fortress, British garrison, apartheid-era SADF headquarters, and site of a 1990s postcolonial memory-art intervention (Krotoa's Room) — makes it a recurring case in debates over how colonial-era monumental architecture is reinterpreted after apartheid; see [[heritage-management]] and [[district-six]] for related South African heritage-politics cases documented in the same source.
