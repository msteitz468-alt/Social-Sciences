# Tlatilco

A large Middle Preclassic village or small town (about 160 acres / 65 hectares) on the former
lakeshore of the Valley of Mexico, settled by about 1300 BCE (Coe & Koontz 2013). Tlatilco is
known almost entirely through burials rather than architecture, and its material culture is a
central body of evidence for the reach of Olmec stylistic influence into the highlands.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Tlatilco was discovered in 1936 by brickworkers digging clay, not by archaeologists; only a
small fraction of the site was ever excavated under controlled conditions, and reportedly many
more burials were destroyed by brickworkers and collectors than were scientifically recorded
(Coe & Koontz 2013). This origin as an accidental, salvage-mode discovery is a reliability
caveat on the completeness of the burial sample: the 340 controlled excavations likely
undersample the site's true range of variation.

## Occupation Sequence

Settled around 1300 BCE, Tlatilco's controlled excavations recovered 340 burials, all extended
skeletons with lavish grave offerings, especially figurines — a burial-furniture pattern the
authors note "only rarely" appears elsewhere in Preclassic Mexico (Coe & Koontz 2013). Two
figurine traditions are described: large, hollow, red-painted figures, and small, solid, finely
modeled figures depicting nude or scantily dressed women and men in scenes of dancing,
ballplaying, acrobatics, and domestic or marital life; a further class of dualistic or
monstrous imagery (two-headed figures, split-skull/idiot masks) is interpreted by Coe & Koontz
as an early expression of the life/death dualism later central to Mexican religion — an
attributed interpretive claim, not a settled finding (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Significance

Tlatilco is a principal highland Valley-of-Mexico witness to Olmec-Preclassic interregional
contact. Its pottery and figurines show pronounced Olmec stylistic influence, feeding directly
into the mother-culture/emulation debate over how that influence was transmitted.

## Key Debates

**[POSITION] Olmec influence: migration vs. emulation.** Miguel Covarrubias argued the Olmec
stylistic influence at Tlatilco reflected an actual influx of Olmec aristocrats from the Gulf
lowlands. Coe & Koontz (2013) instead suggest it is equally plausible that the Tlatilco elite
adopted Olmec religious and symbolic prestige items without any population movement — describing
the process as being "under heavy influence from 'missionaries'" rather than migrants. Both
positions are recorded here without adjudication; this is directly relevant to
[[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate|the Olmec mother-culture debate]] if that page exists in
the vault (not confirmed at time of writing — flag for follow-up linking).

**[POSITION] Long-range Preclassic diffusion to South America.** Tlatilco's material culture
(stirrup-spout jars, rocker-stamping, long-necked bottles, split-face dualism, roller seals)
closely resembles that of the Chavín culture of Peru (c. 900–200 BCE). Older editions of Coe's
synthesis favored a Mexico-to-Peru diffusion hypothesis (echoing Spinden); the current edition
(2013) considers this "unlikely," favoring either independent evolution of Peruvian civilization
or diffusion to both areas from Pacific coastal Ecuador (which had settled village life with
ceramics and maize by 3000 BCE). Circumstantial supporting evidence cited: Capacha, Colima
(Isabel Kelly excavation; grave goods c. 450 BCE with Ecuadorian-style stirrup spouts) and El
Opeño, Michoacán (elaborate tomb, similar ceramics, radiocarbon c. 1300 BCE) (Coe & Koontz
2013). This is a live scholarly position on long-range Preclassic diffusion, recorded here as a
position rather than a finding.

## Basin of Mexico Survey Context (Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979)

The regional survey reported in [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]] (study page [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]) situates Tlatilco within Early Horizon (1500–1150 BCE) Basin settlement: in survey Phase One (1500–1300 BCE) Tlatilco was one small village among nine Early Horizon sites; by Phase Two (1300–1150 BCE) it had expanded to a large village, the single largest Early Horizon settlement documented anywhere in the Basin. The authors caution that Tlatilco's location under modern Mexico City left it effectively unsurveyable by their project, so Basin-wide Early Horizon population trends may understate growth actually concentrated there.

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979), citing Piña Chán (1958) and Tolstoy (1975), noted burial evidence from Tlatilco's Phase Two component "suggests some kind of ranking structure," though the site's overall settlement pattern (no known civic-ceremonial or elite architecture) does not otherwise indicate elaborate political organization — the authors read this as consistent with an emergent-rank rather than fully stratified society. A comparative site-rank ordering proposed elsewhere in the same survey (drawing on burial-lavishness data separate from the core Basin sample) places nucleated Tlatilco (estimated population ~1,500) above the smaller villages of El Arbolillo (~700–900) and the hamlet-scale Zacatenco, citing Sahlins's (1958) observations on rank and status variation in Polynesian chiefdoms as a cross-cultural comparandum for interpreting the mortuary gradient — an explicit ethnographic analogy, not a direct evidential claim. Tlatilco's famously rich burial assemblage (jade ornaments, figurines, stone implements; some interments showing women as central figures accompanied by sacrificed men and children) is contrasted in this survey against the comparatively modest burial sample from the excavated site of [[loma-torremote]], though the authors caution that Loma Torremote's ranked social distinctions may simply be expressed through non-mortuary evidence not captured by a grave-goods comparison alone.
