# Sans-Souci (Milot)

Palace complex at **Milot**, northern Haiti, built under King **Henry Christophe** in the 1810s and ruined after his death (1820). Densified from [[trouillot-michel-rolph|Trouillot]]'s *[[trouillot-silencing-the-past-1995|Silencing the Past]]* Ch.2 (“The Three Faces of Sans Souci”) — a historical-anthropological reading of **layered silences**, not a full architectural or excavation monograph.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Trouillot’s account is **experiential and historiographic**: walking the ruins; listening to peasant guides; reading Haitian and European historians. He notes written records from before the 1842 earthquake that corroborate guides’ sense of former glory. This is not modern single-context archaeology; excavation quality for scientific architecture is largely unknown on this page. Standing walls are his primary [[historicity-1-and-2|historicity 1]] anchors.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Trouillot 1995.)* After the [[haitian-revolution|Haitian Revolution]], Christophe’s kingdom monuments (Sans-Souci palace below; **Citadelle Laferrière** above as fort and burial place) claim Black honor and statehood. Guides narrate glory and blood price. After Christophe’s fall, ruin; later national/touristic memory. “Society of King Christophe’s Friends” and similar groups as alchemists of memory (Trouillot’s phrase).

## Significance — Three faces of Sans Souci

Trouillot’s analytic triple (attributed):

| Face | Referent | Silencing dynamic |
|---|---|---|
| 1. The man | Colonel **Jean-Baptiste Sans Souci** — Bossale/Congo leader in the **war within the war** (guerrilla success vs French and Creole hierarchy; refused Dessalines chain of command; assassinated) | Body never “missing” but no portrait; facts available yet underused until late; war-within-the-war incomplete in historiography |
| 2. The palace | Milot ruins — Christophe’s royal seat | Material wreck vs Potsdam’s knowability; glory narrative can erase the man |
| 3. Potsdam | Frederick the Great’s Sanssouci | Non-Haitian readers often tie the name only to Germany; Haitian silence may treat Potsdam as a fact that does not matter |

**Christophe killed Sans Souci twice** (Trouillot): literally, and symbolically by building/naming the palace (Trouillot reads possible transformative ritual at the killing site; Dahoman foundation analogy). European writers (Pamphile de Lacroix; later Cole, etc.) fail to connect or actively overwrite the three faces.

**Silences of resistance:** For Ardouin and others, the war-within-the-war distracts from the main epic of 1791–1804 against slavery and colonialism that “the white world did its best to forget.” Silencing Colonel and Potsdam can converge as defense against superior Western silence on the revolution.

## Key Debates

- Naming politics of the three faces; class/color and Bossale vs Creole leadership memory.
- Relation to [[unthinkable-haitian-revolution-debate]] and [[historical-silences]] (Ch.2 as strategy of repositioning available evidence).

## Historiography

Evidence class: textual/archival + standing architecture + oral guides. Trouillot models how guild rules and previous understandings limit narrative even when sources exist. Identity caution: the palace is not “the people”; the Colonel is not the palace; Potsdam analogy is a naming claim, not architectural identity.
