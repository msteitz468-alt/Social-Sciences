## Discovery and Excavation History

Blombos Cave, on the south coast of South Africa, has produced the oldest secure dates for well-made bone artifacts, in layers radiocarbon-dated to more than 40,000 years ago (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]). Klein flags this as a fast-moving dating result subject to revision by later work beyond his 2nd-edition (1999) synthesis.

## Occupation Sequence

The bone-tool layers at Blombos comprise points and other formal bone objects, associated with standardized bifacial stone points of the "Still Bay" type. Klein treats this combination — formal bone technology plus standardized bifacial stone points — as part of the southern African record bearing on the emergence of behaviorally modern technology.

## Significance

Blombos Cave anchors the case that some elements of what Klein calls the "Upper Paleolithic Revolution" — standardized, diverse tool types and routine formal bone-artifact production — appear in the southern African Middle Stone Age record, feeding directly into the debate over whether behavioral modernity emerged gradually or abruptly (see [[upper-paleolithic-revolution]]).

## Key Debates

Blombos is cited within the broader, unresolved question of whether the appearance of formal bone tools and standardized bifacial points in the MSA at sites like Blombos supports a gradual accumulation of modern behaviors in Africa, against Klein's preferred reading of an abrupt behavioral "metamorphosis" around 50 ka. See [[modern-human-origins-debate]].

Discussed in [[klein-human-career-1999]] ([[klein-richard]]) alongside [[klasies-river]] and [[enkapune-ya-muto]] as part of the southern and eastern African evidence for early symbolic and technological behavior.

## Stringer — *Lone Survivors* (2012)

*Source: [[stringer-lone-survivors-2012]], [[stringer-chris]]; as of Stringer 2012.*

**Best-dated African MSA symbolic site.** [[stringer-chris|Stringer]] treats Blombos as the single most impressive site for early symbolism: a relatively small sandstone-cliff cave on South Africa’s southern coast, discovered by Chris Henshilwood on family land and excavated over ~20 years. Clear stratigraphy plus **four dating methods**, including luminescence on quartz grains and heated stone tools, make it one of the best-dated MSA sites in Africa (as of Stringer 2012).

**Finds he emphasizes.**

- **Still Bay** thin lanceolate bifacial spear points; earliest application of **pressure flaking** ~55 ka before its best-known Solutrean European manifestation.
- **~20 engraved red ocher** fragments with purposeful wavy/fan/mesh patterns; some ~75 ka, some extending back to ~100 ka; engraved lines originally vivid bloodred (experimental recoloring). Stringer treats these as **generally accepted as symbolic**.
- **Hundreds of *Nassarius* (tick-shell) beads** with piercing and suspension wear; some colour-modified by hematite rubbing or heating — possibly strung in multi-colour patterns.

**Regional frame.** Grouped with [[klasies-river]] and Pinnacle Point as southern coastal MSA sites that may have kept the “torch of modernity” after northern African/Levantine deterioration post–~120 ka Sahara greening (give or take Toba interruption). Some researchers hint the site’s richness indicates a **sacred** locale; Still Bay florescence is brief, followed by Howiesons Poort rise and fall. Red pigment use in Africa is cited beyond 250 ka (Kapthurin, Olorgesailie) with stronger symbolic expression from ~120 ka, of which Blombos is the flagship. Feeds McBrearty & Brooks-style gradual African assembly of modern behaviour against [[klein-richard|Klein]]’s ~50 ka neural-switch model (see [[behavioral-modernity]], [[modern-human-origins-debate]]).

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*Attributed — Marlize Lombard (Ch. 26), Lyn Wadley (Ch. 25), Christian Tryon (Ch. 8), Lawrence Barham (Ch. 24) in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]; **as of handbook ~2012**.*

Lombard affirms a MSA **bone-tool industry** at Blombos ~**72–80 kya** (awls, polished points, one tanged) — overturning earlier dismissals of MSA bone as late aberrations or mixing. The region’s oldest known beads are *Nassarius kraussianus* from Blombos ~**72 kya** (deliberate manufacture, use-wear, clustered groups); fully syntactical language is argued as indispensable for transmitting bead meanings (Henshilwood et al.; d’Errico et al. via Lombard). Engraved ochre nodules (~72–100 kya) unsettled models placing “art” after ~40 kya; at least eight pieces have complex geometric patterns in predetermined areas — part of a geometric engraving tradition to ≥100 kya (Pinnacle Point, Klein Kliphuis, Klasies bone, Diepkloof eggshell). Still Bay bifacial points at Blombos are the type exemplar in Tryon’s inventory figure; pressure flaking for final shaping of Still Bay points >70 ka is cited (Mourre et al. 2010 via Tryon).

Wadley synthesizes Blombos engraved ochre >70 kya as debated early symbolic expression alongside North and South African MSA shell beads. Barham frames Blombos within the Henshilwood & Marean (2003) symbolic-*sapiens* definition of modernity and the counter-arguments that symbolic packages 120–60 kya are episodic/demographic rather than a species-essential package. Basell (Ch. 27) notes eastern Africa has yet to produce shell beads or engraved ochre comparable in antiquity to Blombos, Taforalt, and other South/North African sites (**as of handbook ~2012**).

### Tattersall — *Masters of the Planet* (2012)

*Source: [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012]], [[tattersall-ian]]. **As of Tattersall 2012.***

**Best MSA personal adornment.** At Blombos (southern African coast), numerous perforated *Nassarius* shells worn in a way strongly implying stringing as beads; MSA context ~**76 ka**; general acceptance as personal adornment makes it probable some African MSA populations began body decoration after ~100 ka (attributed). Personal ornamentation among historically documented peoples usually carries deep symbolic significance (identity as group/class/profession/age-cohort) — ethnographic analogy flagged as interpretive frame, not direct evidence (Tattersall).

**Earliest confidently symbolic objects (his judgment).** Ochre plaques with deliberately smoothed surfaces bearing engraved cross-hatched patterns, same MSA levels as beads (~**77 ka** for the illustrated piece); two pieces several vertical inches apart suggest consistent meaning over time, not idle doodling; a third ochre ~250 miles away may show a simplified version of the same motif — “the earliest objects that we can confidently interpret as symbolic” / “earliest evidently symbolic object” (**thesis** on confidence, Tattersall 2012).

**Bone tools and pressure flaking.** Same deposits: bone tools, probably originally hafted, of a kind “conspicuously missing from the Neanderthals’ contemporary tool kits in Europe.” Latest Blombos report he cites: ~**75 ka** fire-hardened silcrete tools with **pressure-flaked** edges — technique otherwise known only after ~20 ka in Europe among late Cro-Magnons — making reported ~90 ka bone harpoons at Katanda more believable; evidence piles up that “something really important was astir” in middle/later MSA Africa (attributed).

**Place in his lag thesis.** Blombos and Pinnacle Point give reason to reject a Cro-Magnon-lineage-only recent neural gene for creativity; symbolic sensibility was already initially astir earlier; earliest possessors need not discover all dimensions at once. Southern drought after 80–60 ka makes Blombos/Pinnacle Point only **moderately** likely direct antecedents of later northern expressions — more a quickening capitalizing on predisposition born with physical *H. sapiens* (**thesis**). Cognitively modern humans who took over the world very likely lived in an eastern African population **after** Blombos times (molecular + archaeological synthesis as of 2012). See [[behavioral-modernity]], [[homo-sapiens]], [[neanderthals]].
