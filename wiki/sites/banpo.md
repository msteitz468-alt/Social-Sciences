# Banpo (Pan-p'o)

## Discovery and Excavation History

Pan-p'o-ts'un, near Sian (Xi'an) in Shaanxi, is the most fully excavated [[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]]
village in [[chang-kwang-chih|Chang]]'s [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|1963]] account —
a settlement on a river terrace ~800 m east of the River Ch'an (a Weishui tributary), ~9 m above
the riverbed, measuring roughly 200 m (N–S) by 100 m (W–E), with 3 m of cultural deposit
representing at least four successive occupations. Its layout became Chang's model for the
Yangshao village pattern generally.

## Occupation Sequence

Chang described a village divided into functional zones: a **dwelling area in the west**, a
**pottery-making quarter to the east** with at least six kilns (one still holding unfired pots),
and a **cemetery to the north** with over 130 extended, supine adult burials (mostly single),
while infants and children were urn-buried between the houses. A long, wide ditch ran along the
northeastern edge of the dwelling area, possibly separating it from the cemetery and kiln zones
and perhaps serving as an impermanent wall foundation. Chang generalized this
dwelling / kiln / cemetery subdivision to other extensively excavated Yangshao villages (Pao-chi,
Hua Hsien, Lin-shan-chai, Miao-ti-kou).

## Significance

- **House forms.** The commonest houses were ~3.5 m across — square, rectangular, or round, with
  plastered floors, semisubterranean or at ground level, wattle-and-daub walls on wood posts;
  about 40 were found. A later, very large rectangular ground house (>20 m long, 12.5 m wide),
  internally partitioned, at the center of the village plaza was interpreted as a communal house,
  surrounded by small houses facing the center — a layout Chang read as a self-sufficient
  lineage-based "little community."
- **Painted pottery and symbolism.** Fish motifs are especially abundant on Weishui-valley
  (Pan-p'o-region) pottery. A painted bowl depicts, on its inner surface, a tattooed human face
  wearing a fish-shaped headdress — interpreted (attributed) as possibly a priest and connected
  to a fertility rite.

Banpo anchors the Yangshao stage as, in Chang's framing, a stage of "primary village-farming
efficiency" — settled but, he argued, in a shifting/repetitive slash-and-burn pattern rather than
the large permanent sedentism earlier scholarship assumed.

## Key Debates

- **Settlement permanence.** Chang used Pan-p'o-region survey data to argue Yangshao villages were
  shifting and repetitive rather than large sedentary communities — an attributed reinterpretation
  of the earlier consensus.
- **Chronology.** Dated relatively in 1963; later radiocarbon placed the Yangshao occupation in
  roughly the 5th millennium BCE. Banpo is now a museum site with its own extensive post-1963
  literature.

## Liu & Chen (2012) note

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu, Li & Xingcan Chen, The Archaeology
of China]], 2012, ch. 6.)* Banpo is grouped by Liu & Chen among the extensively excavated
early-Yangshao (Banpo-phase) sites of the Wei River region, alongside Jiangzhai, Beishouling,
and Dadiwan; several Wei-valley sites of this phase measure 5–6 ha. Banpo-phase pottery types
(pen basins, *bo* bowls, pointed-bottom *jiandiping* vessels, *hu*/*guan* jars, *weng* urns) are
illustrated in the source; pottery marks found at Banpo and Jiangzhai have "exact meanings...
still a matter of speculation" — flagged explicitly: do not treat these marks as deciphered or
as evidence of writing.
