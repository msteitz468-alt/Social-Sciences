# Maltese Megalithic Temples

The free-standing stone temple-complexes of the Maltese islands (Malta and Gozo), treated by
[[renfrew-before-civilization-1973|Renfrew's *Before Civilization*]] (1973) as **the world's
oldest free-standing stone monuments** and a central case of the autonomy of European
prehistory. Distinct from burial [[megalith|megaliths]]: these are ceremonial temples, not
tombs, and Renfrew insists they be discussed separately from the Atlantic tomb tradition.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The temples were long known; systematic interpretation rests on **J. D. Evans**'s survey and
sequence and on **David Trump**'s excavations at **Skorba**, which found clay figurines and a
"shrine" room reaching back to ~4000 BCE, read by Renfrew as ancestral to temple-period cult
(attributed, Renfrew 1973).

## Occupation Sequence

First farming settlement in Malta dates to ~5000 BCE (calendar years): a simple agrarian
economy (wheat, barley, sheep, goats, cattle), stone and bone tools, imported Sicilian
greenstone axes and obsidian. Pottery runs continuously from the first settlers to the temple
builders — "the material equipment of the temple builders was not so very different from that
of the first settlers" (attributed, Renfrew 1973). Temple building was under way before 3000
BCE, perhaps several centuries earlier, and the Tarxien phase ended by ~2200 BCE, when a
seemingly different culture replaced it.

Principal temples (findings, as described by Renfrew): **Ġgantija** (Gozo) — twin
non-interconnecting courtyard temples, a coralline-limestone façade up to 8 m high;
**Mnajdra**; **Tarxien** and **Ħaġar Qim** (interconnecting, more complex — Ħaġar Qim uses
dressed softer limestone, "the earliest use of dressed stone in the world"); the **Ħal
Saflieni hypogeum** (rock-cut charnel chambers reportedly holding the bones of ~7,000
individuals). Tarxien yielded relief spiral carvings and a colossal seated "fat lady" statue
~2 m in seated height — "the earliest colossal statue in the world."

## Significance

**The dating argument.** Before radiocarbon, the temples were dated by comparing the Tarxien
spiral motifs to Mycenaean/Cretan spirals (Aegean 1800–1500 BCE); J. D. Evans (1967) still
argued Aegean contact was needed. Renfrew held that the calibrated dates — the Tarxien phase
ending before 2200 BCE — "rule out the derivation of any of the decorative motifs … from the
middle or late bronze age Aegean" (attributed). He endorsed Evans's earlier (1959) judgment
that the temples were "something indigenous … no question of their having been introduced,"
crediting the new chronology as the decisive addition.

**Social interpretation.** Renfrew modelled the temple society as a **[[chiefdom]]** rather
than egalitarian villages or a state, mapping the temples into ~six spatial clusters, each
commanding an estimated arable territory (Figure 33), and estimating the island's population
between ~500 (at Hole & Flannery's Iranian density of 2.3 persons/km² of arable) and ~11,000
(at 50 persons/km²), with each territory supporting up to ~2,000 people (attributed; Renfrew
explicitly hedges these as extrapolations). He flagged the evidentiary limit plainly: "We do
not even have any direct evidence in the archaeological record for the existence of chiefs."

## Key Debates

- [[diffusion-collapse-european-prehistory-debate]] — indigenous origin vs. Aegean derivation
  (the Trump–Evans dating dispute, resolved by Renfrew via calibration).
- Renfrew's chiefdom reading and the Easter Island analogy (which he warns "could easily be
  pushed too far," given asymmetric preservation).

## Historiography

The reinterpretation rests on [[radiocarbon-dating|radiocarbon]] calibration via
[[dendrochronology|tree-ring]] chronology and on Renfrew's [[processual-archaeology|"new
prehistory"]] toolkit (settlement-territory analysis, population-density modelling,
ethnographic analogy flagged as analogy, not evidence). Excavation quality is mixed —
early clearance work vs. Trump's controlled Skorba stratigraphy.
