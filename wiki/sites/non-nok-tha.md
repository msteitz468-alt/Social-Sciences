## Overview

Non Nok Tha is a low mound on sandy-loam soil near the confluence of two streams close to the Phu Wiang monadnock, Northeast Thailand. It was located during a 1963–4 survey of the Nam Phong dam area led by Wilhelm Solheim, with the site itself found by Chester Gorman, and it became, alongside [[ban-chiang]], one of the two excavations that opened the debate over the antiquity of Southeast Asian bronze-working. The site's excavated burials and its associated bronze-casting debris are the primary basis for the Non Nok Tha and Phu Wiang phases of Donn Bayard's regional periodization. All claims below derive from Higham (1989), *The Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia*.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Test excavations in 1965 found inhumation burials accompanied by pottery and bronze fragments. Full-scale excavation followed in 1966 (Bayard, working under Solheim, with Parker) and again in 1968 (Bayard alone), together opening 340 m² of the roughly 1.1 ha mound — at the time the largest area excavation undertaken in Northeast Thailand. Average deposit depth was only 1.4 m, and the site functioned mainly as a cemetery, with some postholes suggesting associated houses. Excavation proceeded by cultural layer rather than arbitrary spits.

Excavation quality at Non Nok Tha is a documented reliability concern. (ATTRIBUTED — Higham 1989) No shelter was built over the excavation, so sun and rain degraded the soil-colour breaks that would otherwise have marked stratigraphic boundaries and grave edges; the hard soil also could not be screened, unlike the finer recovery regime later used at [[ban-chiang]] and [[ban-na-di]]. Graves were shallow and frequently intercut, and Higham (1989) reports that the resulting radiocarbon sequence "has been the centre of debate over the past two decades," with dates liable to be contradictory or confusing. This combination of conditions makes Non Nok Tha's stratigraphic and chronological record markedly less reliable than the later, sheltered and finely screened excavations at Ban Chiang and Ban Na Di.

## Occupation Sequence

Bayard (1971, 1984a) subdivided the sequence into three successive periods, later renamed the Phu Wiang, Non Nok Tha, and Don Sawan phases (Phu Wiang with three sub-phases, Non Nok Tha with eight). Only one bronze item — a socketed axe of 17% tin bronze, found in position over the chest of an adult male in burial 90 — belongs to the Phu Wiang phase; the Non Nok Tha phase shows a marked increase in bronze artefacts, including crucibles, casting spillage, and moulds, indicating local casting rather than import of finished goods. Six sets of double sandstone moulds for axes and six crucibles (complete and fragmentary) were recovered, alongside 28 bracelets and 9 socketed axe heads in total; axes were cast in sandstone moulds and subsequently cold-hammered and annealed, while decorated bracelets were likely lost-wax cast.

(ATTRIBUTED, per Higham 1989, synthesizing the post-1970s revised chronology) After eliminating dates judged clearly erroneous and cross-referencing related sites, the bronze-bearing levels at Non Nok Tha probably fall within 2000–1000 BCE, with initial bronze more narrowly estimated at c. 2000–1500 BCE and pre-metal (Phu Wiang) occupation reaching back into the 3rd or late 4th millennium BCE. The Phu Wiang 3 phase (pre-bronze) likely ended c. 2000–1500 BCE. Non Nok Tha was probably abandoned before local iron came into use in the region — no red-on-buff painted ware, characteristic of later Iron Age assemblages at [[ban-chiang]] and [[ban-na-di]], has been found there.

Of 87 well-provenanced burials — the largest Northeast Thailand mortuary sample at the time — only 5 included shell beads and 2 shell bracelets, indicating exotic goods were rare even where present. Extended inhumation was the standard rite, with graves 40–90 cm deep containing pottery, small polished stone quadrangular adzes, bronze artefacts, shell ornaments, and animal bone (pig, dog, cow). Some burials show apparent deliberate mortuary ritual: burial 8 (a child of the Phu Wiang phase 1) was interred with pig limbs and jaw and adult dog remains, and burial 14 (a child) was covered in smashed pottery sherds, shell-disc beads, and apparent remnants of a pigskin shroud.

## Significance

Non Nok Tha anchors the early phase of the Southeast Asian bronze-dating controversy. Bronze and stone bivalve moulds were already known from O Pie Can and Samrong Sen (Cambodia), but their chronology and the question of indigenous versus imported origin were unresolved before Non Nok Tha's stratigraphic excavation provided the first controlled sequence. (ATTRIBUTED) Bayard (1972a) and Solheim (1968) initially proposed a far earlier start to Southeast Asian metallurgy than the field's prevailing view — that metallurgy arrived late via China — had allowed, "raising the possibility of a far earlier start to metallurgy in Thailand than had hitherto been imagined."

The site's burial-wealth patterning is a key data point for the emergence of social ranking without centralization (see [[chiefdom]]). (ATTRIBUTED) Bayard (1984a) inferred initial social ranking from differential grave wealth beginning in the Phu Wiang phase and intensifying into the early Non Nok Tha phase: six deep burials with mound offerings clustered in the northwest quadrant, contrasted with shallower graves of disarticulated bone and with mound-only deposits containing no human remains. A further analysis of 87 of 217 total burials/mortuary contexts using 54 variables identified two distinct, largely non-overlapping forms of footed jar during the Non Nok Tha phase, interpreted by Bayard as marking two distinct, unequally ranked affiliative kin groups (not correlated with age or sex) — but no graves showed markedly higher "energy expenditure" of the kind that would indicate a dominant, centralized elite.

Faunal evidence documents multiple early domesticate lines: pig (45 individuals) and cattle (36) dominate the grave assemblage, with short, broad adult pig skulls and small cattle bones (relative to wild stock) indicating [[domestication]]; a complete dog skeleton in a Phu Wiang phase 1 burial confirms an early domestic dog. No rice grains were recovered; evidence for [[rice-domestication]] instead comes from pottery temper analysis, where rice-chaff-tempered sherds rose from roughly 1.5% (Phu Wiang 1) to 5.7% (Phu Wiang 1–2) to 13.5% in later levels. Botanists disagreed on the domestication status implied: Chang (1976) placed the material between wild and weed rice, while Vishnu-Mittre (1975) judged it indeterminate — contrasting with the flotation-recovered grains at [[ban-na-di]], which Chang and Loresto (1984) judged cultivated.

Skeletal analysis (Pietrusewsky 1974, 1982, 1984) of 188 complete and partial skeletons found high mortality among 2–6-year-olds and young/middle-aged adults in the earlier sample, with no individual reaching old age and an average death age in the early 20s; the later sample (Non Nok Tha phases 2–7) shows fewer child deaths and greater life expectancy. Over half of adults exhibited porotic hyperostosis, possibly linked to nutritional deficiency or a haemoglobin disorder associated with malaria resistance. Adult stature ranged 1.62–1.78 m for men and 1.42–1.57 m for women. Cranial and non-metric traits link the Non Nok Tha sample to "Neolithic" Viet Nam specimens (Mansuy and Colani collections) and to Ban Chiang crania (Pietrusewsky 1982, 1984).

## Key Debates

Non Nok Tha's radiocarbon sequence sits at the centre of the [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]]. Early chronology estimates (Solheim 1968; Bayard 1972a) attempted to date the bronze-bearing levels when Non Nok Tha was the sole known bronze-bearing site in mainland Southeast Asia, and were later revised substantially downward as further excavations (notably [[ban-chiang]] and [[ban-na-di]]) provided better-controlled comparanda. (ATTRIBUTED, as of Higham 1989) Higham's revised synthesis places initial bronze at Non Nok Tha at c. 2000–1500 BCE, reconciling the site with the Ban Chiang and Ban Na Di sequences and representing a marked downward revision from the "extremely early" metallurgy claims current in the 1960s–70s. The dating difficulties are compounded by the site's excavation conditions (no shelter, unscreened hard soil, shallow and intercut graves), which Higham treats as a methodological caution rather than a resolved question.

## Historiography

Non Nok Tha was excavated under a culture-historical/salvage framework — a joint University of Hawaii–Thai Fine Arts Department program directed by Solheim, undertaken ahead of the Nam Phong dam project — predating the more explicitly processual, multi-specialist approach later applied at Ban Chiang (1974–5) and Ban Na Di (from 1980). Its dating rests on radiocarbon determinations whose calibration status is not stated explicitly in Higham (1989); per this wiki's Chronological Uncertainty Protocol, all B.C./A.D. dates drawn from this source should be treated as of unstated, likely-uncalibrated status until confirmed elsewhere. The site's initial 1960s–70s framing as evidence for "extremely early" indigenous Southeast Asian bronze-working was progressively revised downward as excavation methods and comparative chronological control improved at subsequent sites; this revision is documented in detail on [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]].
