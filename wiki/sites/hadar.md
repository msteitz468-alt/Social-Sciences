## Discovery and Excavation History

Hadar is a fossil locality in the Afar Depression, Ethiopia, worked in the 1970s by [[johanson-donald|Donald Johanson]] (Cleveland Museum of Natural History) and colleagues. Per [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]], Johanson's team recovered a partial skeleton nicknamed "Lucy" and a separate multi-individual hominid group ("the Afar group"). The source presents the Afar group as the earliest clear fossil evidence of a group of hominids living together; the cause of death of the group is stated as unknown, with a catastrophic flood explicitly ruled out as a cause by the authors (attributed).

Excavation quality: fieldwork at Hadar was still active and the finds only recently described at the time [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] went to press (1977) — the book predates Johanson's 1978 formal naming of Australopithecus afarensis, so its taxonomic assignments for Hadar material are provisional and should be read as reflecting 1977-vintage uncertainty, not the field's later consensus.

## Occupation Sequence

The source dates the Afar hominid group's fossils to roughly three million years old and reports Mary Leakey's team recovering, from long-neglected deposits a few miles from Olduvai Gorge (not Hadar itself), primitive Homo fossils of nearly four million years — cited by the authors as comparably early evidence elsewhere in the region rather than as part of the Hadar sequence proper. Relative dating at Hadar follows the same volcanic-ash-bracketing method used at Olduvai, Omo, and Koobi Fora (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977).

## Significance

Hadar's Afar group is treated in [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] as important early evidence for hominids living in a social group, feeding the authors' broader thesis (see [[food-sharing-hypothesis]]) about early cooperative behavior. "Lucy" is presented, notably, with taxonomic ambiguity in the source's own text and captions.

## Key Debates

**Internal taxonomic inconsistency (flagged, not a scholarly debate, but a source-reliability note).** [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] contains an internal tension: one caption describes "Lucy" as "possibly a female member of a late form of Ramapithecus," while elsewhere the Afar group to which Lucy belongs is called a group of "genus Homo." This reflects the book's provisional, pre-1978 taxonomy for Hadar material (Australopithecus afarensis was not yet named), rather than an extraction error; readers should treat any species-level identification of Hadar fossils in this source as unsettled at time of writing. See also [[ramapithecus]] for the broader supersession of that taxon's status as a hominid ancestor.

### Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein reports the Hadar Formation (Awash Valley, Afar, Ethiopia) in four members (Basal, Sidi
Hakoma, Denen Dora, Kada Hadar), with high-precision single-crystal laser-fusion 40Ar/39Ar dates
bracketing the [[australopithecus|*A. afarensis*]] fossils between **3.4 and 2.9 Ma**. Over 320
*A. afarensis* specimens are known; site AL 333 (the "First Family," Denen Dora Member) yielded
200+ specimens from at least nine adults and four juveniles, and "Lucy" (AL 288-1) is ~40% of one
skeleton. Klein reads the large size range as favoring a **single, highly dimorphic species**
over a two-species split. A [[homo-habilis|*Homo*]] maxilla near the top of the Kada Hadar Member
dates to ~2.3 Ma — among the oldest secure *Homo* — and the nearby Kada Gona artifacts (2.6–2.5 Ma,
40Ar/39Ar + paleomagnetism) are among the oldest known stone tools ([[oldowan-industry]]). See
[[potassium-argon-dating]].

### Tattersall — *Masters of the Planet* (2012)

*(Attributed — [[tattersall-masters-of-the-planet-2012]], [[tattersall-ian]]; as of Tattersall 2012.)*

**Lucy and paleoenvironment.** Lucy discovered at Hadar, northeastern Ethiopia, 1974 — *Australopithecus afarensis*’s most famous representative; ~40% skeleton of a tiny individual usually considered female by size; lived ~**3.18 Ma**. Hadar sediments ~**2.9–3.4 Ma** in the ancestral Awash valley; climatic fluctuation (drier/wetter, cooler/warmer) but overall **grassy woodlands with denser river forest** — “trees never far away”; Lucy’s body form reflects this wooded mosaic (not open Serengeti savanna). Year before Lucy (1973): knee joint with clear **carrying angle** — then earliest known bipedal evidence by several hundred thousand years.

**Anatomy densification.** Lucy: little more than ~3 ft tall, perhaps ~60 lb; males up to a foot taller and heavier. Pelvis short top-to-bottom (not tall narrow ape ilia), iliac blades broader than modern humans with dramatic lateral flare, long femoral neck. Short legs (~bonobo proportions) advantage climbing not striding; feet relatively long, toes somewhat curved; Hadar hand bones shorter than apes but curved fingers + strong flexors — already lose specialized ape grasping, **not** for toolmaking (tools later). Cranial fragments + two ~3 Ma Hadar crania: small braincase, projecting face, postcanine megadonty. AL 333: ~240 fossils of 17 individuals (9 adults, 3 adolescents, 5 juveniles); fine-grained river sediments; hypothesis of single social-group flash-flood catastrophe ~3.18–3.22 Ma; majority opinion single highly dimorphic *A. afarensis*.

**Species unity and tools context.** Holotype of *A. afarensis* deliberately chosen from [[laetoli]] (not Hadar) to emphasize sample unity; some scientists saw >1 species at Hadar alone — uneasy truce. Nearby Awash/Dikika context: ~3.4 Ma cut-marked bones and later Gona tools ~2.6 Ma (see [[oldowan-industry]]). Dating note: most early year-dates are on volcanic rocks interleaved with sediments, not on fossils themselves.

See [[australopithecus]], [[bipedalism]], [[laetoli]], [[johanson-donald]].
