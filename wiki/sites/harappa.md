## Discovery and Excavation History

Harappa lies on the Ravi River in West Punjab, at a point where the river's entrenchment broadens into arable land, roughly 600 km northwest of [[mohenjo-daro]] (as reported by Possehl 2002). It is the type-site that gave the Harappan/Indus Civilization its conventional name. According to Possehl, Harappa has seen 36 excavation seasons — more than any other Indus-age site — reflecting both its early prominence in the discipline's history and a sustained joint American-Pakistani research program renewed continuously since 1986. Earlier excavation phases were associated with [[wheeler-mortimer]], whose terminology (notably "citadel" for the walled high mound) Possehl treats with explicit skepticism, calling it "another archaic thought about these cities" rather than a demonstrated functional fact.

Excavation quality varies considerably across the site's long history: the modern joint-team seasons since 1986 represent controlled, research-driven fieldwork, while earlier work (as at many Indus sites) predates modern stratigraphic and recording standards. Possehl does not, in this chapter, detail the excavation methods of the earliest campaigns; this reliability gradient should be read into any claim sourced to pre-1986 work.

## Significance

At its Mature Harappan extent, Harappa's mounded and artifact-scatter area covers approximately 100 ha, though Possehl notes that deposits buried under alluvium suggest the true extent could reach 200 ha — the exact size is unknown. Applying an assumed settlement density of roughly 200 people/ha, Possehl derives a population estimate of approximately 20,000; this figure is explicitly a derived estimate resting on a density assumption, not a directly counted fact. Harappa is one of the nine sites of ≥50 ha (Table 3.1) that anchor Possehl's civilization-scale settlement analysis, alongside [[mohenjo-daro]], [[rakhigarhi]], [[ganweriwala]], [[dholavira]], and several Sindh sites without individual pages here.

Key excavated features include Mound A-B, termed the "citadel" by Wheeler — a walled high area on the site's west side that remains, per Possehl, poorly understood in function; and Mound F, a large parallel-wall structure with a central corridor, begun in late Period 3A/early 3B and completed in late 3B, conventionally labeled a "granary." Possehl explicitly flags that this identification is unconfirmed by any grain or storage-vessel evidence recovered from the structure — the "granary" label is an attributed interpretation, not an established fact. South of this structure, circular platforms interpreted as threshing floors are better evidenced: excavated wooden mortars there preserved grain husks. Mounds E and ET may represent an outer city wall.

Two cemeteries document mortuary variability across the occupation sequence: R-37, the largest known Mature Harappan cemetery, with extended supine burials including wooden coffins; and Cemetery H, dated to Period 5 (Posturban), containing pot burials and fractional/dismembered interments — a marked departure in burial practice associated with the civilization's transformation.

## Key Debates

Wheeler's "citadel" terminology for Mound A-B is itself a live point of interpretive caution: Possehl treats the label as importing unwarranted state/military assumptions rather than a demonstrated function, feeding directly into the [[indus-polity-debate]] over whether the Indus Civilization possessed centralized, state-like authority structures or a more heterarchical/corporate organization. The unconfirmed "granary" identification at Mound F is a parallel case of a conventional functional label outrunning the evidence.

Harappa's long occupation sequence — Ravi Aspect through Cemetery H — also bears on the [[indus-transformation-debate]] over the nature of the civilization's Posturban decline, since the site's own Period 5 mortuary and material changes are among the primary local evidence for that transformation.

## Kenoyer 1998 and the Harappa Archaeological Research Project (HARP)

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|J. Mark Kenoyer]], *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* 1998; HARP co-direction with [[meadow-richard|Richard Meadow]] and Pakistani collaborators.)*

### HARP / modern excavation frame

Kenoyer densifies the post-1986 joint American–Pakistani program: **U.C. Berkeley excavations 1986–1990** under [[dales-george-f|George F. Dales]], then the **Harappa Archaeological Research Project (HARP), 1993–1997** (operations listed on Kenoyer’s site-plan caption: Op. 1 R-37; Op. 2 Mound AB; Op. 3 Mound E; Op. 4 Mound AB SW corner; Op. 5 low western mound; Op. 6 low area between 2 and 3; Op. 7 Mound F; Op. 8 low area north of 3; Op. 9 corings around site). HARP supplies primary floor/street contexts missing from pre-1940s digs: seal wear (boss-intact house floors vs street finds), Period 2 Early script, tablet chronology late Period 3B–3C, weight concentrations at gateways, cart tracks (~1.6 m wheel span with Wheeler 1946), latrines in nearly every excavated house, and craft-neighborhood mapping.

### Urban formation and size

Recent excavations indicate the earliest **city-scale** formation may predate full Integration: **Kot Diji Phase / Period 2 (2800–2600 BCE)**, when the settlement grew to ~**25 ha** as a trade node linking Baluchistan/Afghanistan west to the southern coast (carnelian, chert, copper, shell into workshops). Within a few hundred years Harappa expanded to **over 150 ha** (Kenoyer’s major-city tier, with Mohenjo-daro >200 ha, Dholavira 100 ha, Ganweriwala and Rakhigarhi ~80 ha). Only a small proportion of the site has been excavated despite major campaigns.

### Multi-mound walls, gates, and trade control

Walled sectors spread around a **central depression** Kenoyer reads as a possible large tank/reservoir. Each major mound had massive mud-brick walls with brick gateways and bastions:

- **Mound E** (E–W; among the oldest sectors, over early village levels): mud-brick wall >9 m wide (~11 m with bastions), southern gateway opening **2.8 m** (one ox cart), baked-brick gate walls ~1 m thick, estimated height 3–4 m; interior open space as market or tax checkpoint; shell/agate workshops E/N, copperworking W. ~30 m south: Harappan-phase extramural houses/drains/bathing platforms (possible caravansarai for arrivals after gates closed).
- **Mound E–ET gateway** (~2.6 m opening; pylons/rooms ~25 × 15 m) controlling suburb workshops; second possible rest stop ~50 m south. **Four sequential drains** exit here; one preserved corbeled arch drain 1.6 m high × 60 cm wide × 6.5 m under a street. Baked-brick size sequence at the gateway: earliest 7 × 14 × 28 cm → final rebuild 5 × 12 × 24 cm.
- **Mound AB:** wall up to **14 m** wide at base, exterior baked brick, tapering to >11 m above plain; three small western gateways (Wheeler); larger northern gate/ramp to **Mound F** suburb (houses, craft debris, circular platforms, twelve-section large building read as palace or storehouse; mound enclosed by mud-brick wall).

Mounds founded at different times but share painted ceramics, figurines, seals, ornaments, and inter-sector goods movement; **why separate walls persisted and how political unity worked remains unclear** (feeds [[indus-polity-debate]]).

Kenoyer **rejects military-defense** as primary wall function at Harappa: no battle damage; gateways lack frontal-defense planning (no sharp interior turns of later historical cities); unlike [[dholavira]], no moat or double/triple wall system — gates/walls more **symbolic and commercial** (control of trade).

### Wells, sanitation, street aggradation

Perhaps as few as **~30 wells** citywide (only 8 found as of 1998) vs Jansen’s **700+** estimate for [[mohenjo-daro]] — possibly more winter rain and/or river proximity at Harappa; central depression as shared tank. Private wells likely householder-built, not state works. Current excavations find **latrines** (sump-pot commodes, often with jota-like water jars) in almost every house — early excavators neglected them. Periods of drain failure caused street-level rise, doorway raising, room fill, and multi-century mound buildup.

### “Granary,” circular platforms, and public architecture

**Mound F structure** (Vats): mud-brick foundation >50 × 40 m; two rows of six rooms along ~7 m central passage; sleeper walls/air spaces; wooden superstructure on columns — conventional “granary.” Kenoyer: little secure link between nearby **circular brick platforms** and that building (platforms inside smaller buildings across many phases); husking/mortar readings (Wheeler) and Roman granary analogies are weak; traditional NW South Asian grain storage uses mud-plastered bins on raised platforms. **Scholarly consensus** (as Kenoyer summarizes): little evidence for massive state granaries at Harappa or Mohenjo-daro — treat as large public buildings of uncertain precise function.

### Writing, weights, crafts, cemeteries

- **Early Indus script** on Period 2 pottery; miniature tablets concentrated at Harappa (many Mohenjo-daro tablets may originate here); weights concentrated inside gateways (taxation reading).
- Crafts: potters, faience, stoneware bangles, copper/bronze, gold/silver, steatite/agate beads, shell, ivory (Mound ET multi-craft association).
- **Cemetery R-37 / Harappan-phase cemetery:** deep rectangular (sometimes mud-brick-lined) graves; pottery under coffins; rosewood/cedar wood IDs (Wheeler 1946); Kenoyer: possible single kin/clan burial ground with later graves cutting earlier ones; shell bangles on left arms of middle-aged women, width decreasing ~2600→2000 BCE (less heavy labor?); only gold ornaments known from a Harappan burial in burial 196a (Dales & Kenoyer).
- **[[cemetery-h]] / Punjab Phase (Periods 4–5):** gradual transition; early Cemetery H burials like Harappan coffins; later adult cremation and child urn burial; new painted motifs with partial Indus symbol continuity; eastern expansion, western/coastal trade breakdown; **1996 bead pot c. 1730 BCE** with earliest Indus glass bead and post-Ernestite tubular small-bead drilling.
