# Parting Ways

Free African American settlement near Plymouth, Massachusetts (period name **New Guinea**), excavated under [[deetz-james|James Deetz]]'s direction beginning **1975** and interpreted as the central case of Ch. 7 in *[[deetz-in-small-things-forgotten-1996|In Small Things Forgotten]]* (1996). Associated founders: **Cato Howe**, **Prince Goodwin**, **Plato Turner**, and **Quamany** — Revolutionary-era Black veterans/community figures as Deetz reconstructs from military, pension, and probate fragments.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Town of Plymouth granted (12 March 1792) a strip of poor sheep-pasture land (~twenty rods by ~1.5 miles; ~94 acres provisionally to Howe) to persons who would clear it in three years. No clear freehold title; land remained town property after 1824 sale authorization failed. Maps of 1840, 1857, and 1879 still show Black-associated houses. Bicentennial Black-history committee landscaped the cemetery and secured memorial designation; archaeology began **1975**. Deetz argues sparse documents force methods closer to prehistoric archaeology. Last known inhabitant **James Burr** (Plato Turner's grandson) — house photographed 1895; informant: burned **1908**.

## Occupation Sequence

- **1792–1820s:** four households clear land, build, live with town permission; Howe dies 1824 (probate $61.82½); extreme poverty in 1820 pension inventory ($27 personal property).
- **Mid–late 19th c.:** cartographic continuity (Quam's house, J. Turner, Burr).
- **Excavated architecture:** Turner-Burr house in two stages ~30 years apart — first **12-ft-square** room on mounded platform (creamware/pearlware → turn of 19th c.); later contiguous 12-ft cellar room; overall two 12-ft modules. Second cellar 12 ft square with high-quality ceramics whose mean ceramic date **lags** known occupation (secondhand acquisition; poverty signal). Mud-wall-and-post 12×9 ft structure. West Indian red sugar/tamarind jars on cellar floor.
- **Settlement:** dwellings clustered near center of 94 acres vs scattered Yankee pattern.
- **Fauna:** no sawn bone; majority cow's feet — does not match contemporary Anglo sites.
- **1978 paved area (~25×45 ft)** across road: ~7,000 intentionally broken ceramics/glass, TPQ 1840s; Deetz/Vlach parallel to African American/West African grave and ritual decoration (not ordinary trash). Traditional "four slaves" slate cemetery questioned as possible smallpox burials (marble had replaced slate by death dates of residents).

## Significance

Deetz's boundary case for the **[[georgian-worldview]]** model: Parting Ways "does not reflect the changes we have suggested for Anglo-American culture change" and thereby shows those patterns apply only within one tradition. Materials were largely Anglo-American, but **rules of combination** (plan module 12 ft vs Anglo 16 ft; refuse; settlement; foodways) may have been African American — **[[creolization]]** / creole-language analogy. Free status (post-1783 Massachusetts trajectory) allowed clearer material expression than under plantation slavery (continuum developed in Ch. 8). Dick Gregory on visiting the dig: "Now we will get a piece of the ink."

## Key Debates

- Strength of Yoruba/shotgun analogies for Turner-Burr plan.
- Cemetery identity and ritual paved-area interpretation.
- How far one small free community generalizes.
- Ceramics-as-status fallacy (finewares on $8/month pensioners).

## Historiography

Primary vault narrative: Deetz 1996 Ch. 7. Notes credit Crosby & Emerson (under Marley Brown III) for paved-area work. Later heritage and restudy literature not yet ingested.
