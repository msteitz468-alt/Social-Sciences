# Chichen Itza

## Discovery and Excavation History

Northern lowland mega-site; Carnegie Institution and Mexican projects; ongoing dating debates. [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] separate Terminal Classic Puuc-related “Old Chichen” from later Toltec-Maya city and from 13th-c. Itza narratives.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]]; chronologies contested.)*

- **Terminal Classic Old Chichen:** Nunnery, Ak'ab Tz'ib, Casa Colorada, lintel temples; texts mainly second half of 9th c. celebrate dedications/bloodletting/new fire more than southern-style king lists. K'ahk'upakal K'awiil as possible *k'uhul ajaw* c. AD 870–890. Caracol as Venus/sun/moon observatory (Aveni et al.).
- **“Toltec invasion” horizon:** Traditional chronicle link to Topiltzin/K'uk'ulkan c. AD 987; warrior orders, chacmools, Tzompantli, large ball court. C&H present **invasion vs trade/cult/emulation** as open. Volta–Braswell and related work revise Castillo (~950s) and ball-court (~1000) timings and challenge simple Old/New Chichen overlap models (as cited).
- **Sacred Cenote:** pilgrimage and sacrifice; osteology complicates “virgin only” myths; peak activity late.
- **Later:** Itza-related reoccupation narratives 13th c.; Mayapan rise; drought coincidence for end of Toltec Chichen.

## Significance

Pivot of Terminal Classic–Early Postclassic northern politics; key case for Mexican–Maya interaction; archaeoastronomy (Caracol); cenote cult.

## Key Debates

- Toltec invasion historicity ([[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] adjacent).
- Chronology of Castillo/ballcourt vs Puuc buildings.
- Putun/Itza ethnic labels vs archaeology.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Research history and revised chronology

Carnegie Institution excavations (Morley 1924–40) restored the Great Ball Court, El Castillo, Temple of the Warriors, and Caracol; Morley also outfitted mapping expeditions to Coba and Calakmul. Carnegie assigned “florescent” (Terminal Classic/Puuc) then “modified florescent” (Postclassic with non-Maya traits) linked to Cehpech then Sotuta ceramics. Sharer & Traxler report more recent research finds **substantial overlap** of pottery traditions and architectural styles: Chichen rose in the Late Classic with ascendancy in the Terminal Classic; dominance lasted later than other Classic cities until ca. AD 1050–1100. Founding ca. AD 750–800 (pottery, dates, investigations); name “opening of the wells of the Itza”; also **Uucyabnal**; mythical Tollan associations. Earliest Maya date at the site AD 867 in their summary. Terminal Classic polities survived as late as ca. AD 1100 at Chichen while Postclassic onset is placed ca. AD 900 for most of the Maya area.

### Rise, Itza identity, and military expansion

At peak, authors call Chichen likely the largest/most powerful Maya city and most cosmopolitan capital — precursor to smaller Postclassic polities blending regional Mesoamerican cultures. Colonial Yucatecan chronicles: new peoples “Itza” from ca. AD 800 — “who speak our language brokenly.” Origins unclear; some evidence Peten lakes, other evidence west-coast Yucatan/Chakanputun Campeche → Chontal-related; significant Ch’olan component in Chichen inscriptions supports Chontal link. Expansion filled central Yucatan no-man’s-land between [[coba]] and Puuc/northern plains rivals. Early texts: 869 Casa Colorada fire rituals by three lords overseen by Ek Balam’s king; **K’ak’upakal** as *yajaw* of an unidentified king then master of his own destiny. By ~AD 900 sustained campaign east and west incorporating most of the northern lowlands — authors’ “largest/most populous state in Maya history.” Yaxuna conquered; Coba declined (surrounded/probably attacked) and was abandoned by ca. 1200, cut off from western links. Puuc demise positions include ecological failure and/or conflict with growing Chichen (authors: both may be correct).

### Economy, multepal politics, and K’uk’ulkan cult

Long-term dominance attributed less to conquest alone than to commodity/trade control, flexible politics more successful than traditional divine kingship, and **K’uk’ulkan** ideology facilitating pan-Mesoamerican commerce. Controlled own coastal ports (Emal; Isla Cerritos seawall harbor with Sotuta pottery including volcanic-ash-tempered slateware). Mainstay: Chontalpa–Yucatan–Gulf of Honduras coastal route; bulk oceangoing canoes; highland obsidian/jade via Motagua then Caribbean coast exchanged for Yucatan cotton, cacao, salt. Art/architecture does **not** advertise individual divine kings (unlike Coba, Dzibilchaltun, Uxmal); emphasizes state economic/military/religious power. Few traditional palaces (Monjas early); open colonnaded galleries interpreted as *popol naah* council houses — decentralized power-sharing (*multepal*) of elite lords (military, mercantile, religious offices). Cult of K’uk’ulkan / Quetzalcoatl as pan-Mesoamerican state religion (feathered-serpent columns); also Chaak, Ehecatl. Authors stress idolatry and human sacrifice predated any Mexican “introduction” (Preclassic/Classic precedents).

### Monumental complex and Sacred Cenote

Site core ≥5 km² dense architecture; Old Chichen Puuc/Chenes southern buildings; northern platform: El Castillo (nine terraces, radial stairs, buried temple with red jaguar throne); Great Ball Court largest in Mesoamerica (166×68 m overall; alley 146×36 m; thirteen ball courts at site); Lower/Upper Temple of Jaguars; *tzompantli*; Temple of Warriors (possible prototype for Tula Pyramid B; earlier Temple of Chacmool with turquoise mosaic cache); Court of Thousand Columns; El Mercado. Vertical ball-court walls with stone rings (northern lowland form). Greatest number of early metal artifacts dredged from the Sacred Cenote — gold/copper disks (repoussé battle scenes), jewelry, bells (often trade imports); carbonized cloth fragments; scores of copal cakes (turquoise blue). Popular virgin-sacrifice cenote belief unsupported in their Ch. 13 discussion. Northern lowland vertical-wall ball courts at Uxmal and Chichen contrasted with Preclassic sloping-wall forms.

### Downfall and succession by Mayapan

Major construction ended by ca. AD 1050; by 1100 lost capital status and most population. Historical sources describe conquest; Temple of the Warriors shows sculptures thrown down and cached offerings anciently looted — but violence may postdate prior political breakdown. Authors favor multi-factor collapse (overpopulation, environmental degradation, droughts, leadership failure, warfare) analogous to southern Classic/Puuc demises. [[mayapan]] (~100 km west) as new Yucatan capital; chronicles of Hunak Keel conquering Chichen with Mexican mercenaries may account for destruction evidence; transition mechanism remains unclear archaeologically. Postclassic highland houses claimed Tollan descent; Popol Vuh journey to Nacxit may point to Chichen (Yucatan chronicles name Nacxit-Xuchit as Chichen founder). Peten lakes Kan Ek’ rulers claimed Itza descent after Chichen’s fall. Spanish second entrada: younger Montejo founded Ciudad Real at Chichen among the Cupules (1531–35), then abandoned under siege.
