# Chichen Itza

## Discovery and Excavation History

Northern lowland mega-site; Carnegie Institution and Mexican projects; ongoing dating debates. [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] separate Terminal Classic Puuc-related “Old Chichen” from later Toltec-Maya city and from 13th-c. Itza narratives.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]]; chronologies contested.)*

- **Terminal Classic Old Chichen:** Nunnery, Ak'ab Tz'ib, Casa Colorada, lintel temples; texts mainly second half of 9th c. celebrate dedications/bloodletting/new fire more than southern-style king lists. K'ahk'upakal K'awiil as possible *k'uhul ajaw* c. AD 870–890. Caracol as Venus/sun/moon observatory (Aveni et al.).
- **“Toltec invasion” horizon:** Traditional chronicle link to Topiltzin/K'uk'ulkan c. AD 987; warrior orders, chacmools, Tzompantli, large ball court. C&H present **invasion vs trade/cult/emulation** as open. Volta–Braswell and related work revise Castillo (~950s) and ball-court (~1000) timings and challenge simple Old/New Chichen overlap models (as cited).
- **Sacred Cenote:** pilgrimage and sacrifice; osteology complicates “virgin only” myths; peak activity late.
- **Later:** Itza-related reoccupation narratives 13th c.; Mayapan rise; drought coincidence for end of Toltec Chichen.

## Significance

Pivot of Terminal Classic–Early Postclassic northern politics; key case for Mexican–Maya interaction; archaeoastronomy (Caracol); cenote cult.

## Key Debates

- Toltec invasion historicity ([[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] adjacent).
- Chronology of Castillo/ballcourt vs Puuc buildings.
- Putun/Itza ethnic labels vs archaeology.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Research history and revised chronology

Carnegie Institution excavations (Morley 1924–40) restored the Great Ball Court, El Castillo, Temple of the Warriors, and Caracol; Morley also outfitted mapping expeditions to Coba and Calakmul. Carnegie assigned “florescent” (Terminal Classic/Puuc) then “modified florescent” (Postclassic with non-Maya traits) linked to Cehpech then Sotuta ceramics. Sharer & Traxler report more recent research finds **substantial overlap** of pottery traditions and architectural styles: Chichen rose in the Late Classic with ascendancy in the Terminal Classic; dominance lasted later than other Classic cities until ca. AD 1050–1100. Founding ca. AD 750–800 (pottery, dates, investigations); name “opening of the wells of the Itza”; also **Uucyabnal**; mythical Tollan associations. Earliest Maya date at the site AD 867 in their summary. Terminal Classic polities survived as late as ca. AD 1100 at Chichen while Postclassic onset is placed ca. AD 900 for most of the Maya area.

### Rise, Itza identity, and military expansion

At peak, authors call Chichen likely the largest/most powerful Maya city and most cosmopolitan capital — precursor to smaller Postclassic polities blending regional Mesoamerican cultures. Colonial Yucatecan chronicles: new peoples “Itza” from ca. AD 800 — “who speak our language brokenly.” Origins unclear; some evidence Peten lakes, other evidence west-coast Yucatan/Chakanputun Campeche → Chontal-related; significant Ch’olan component in Chichen inscriptions supports Chontal link. Expansion filled central Yucatan no-man’s-land between [[coba]] and Puuc/northern plains rivals. Early texts: 869 Casa Colorada fire rituals by three lords overseen by Ek Balam’s king; **K’ak’upakal** as *yajaw* of an unidentified king then master of his own destiny. By ~AD 900 sustained campaign east and west incorporating most of the northern lowlands — authors’ “largest/most populous state in Maya history.” Yaxuna conquered; Coba declined (surrounded/probably attacked) and was abandoned by ca. 1200, cut off from western links. Puuc demise positions include ecological failure and/or conflict with growing Chichen (authors: both may be correct).

### Economy, multepal politics, and K’uk’ulkan cult

Long-term dominance attributed less to conquest alone than to commodity/trade control, flexible politics more successful than traditional divine kingship, and **K’uk’ulkan** ideology facilitating pan-Mesoamerican commerce. Controlled own coastal ports (Emal; Isla Cerritos seawall harbor with Sotuta pottery including volcanic-ash-tempered slateware). Mainstay: Chontalpa–Yucatan–Gulf of Honduras coastal route; bulk oceangoing canoes; highland obsidian/jade via Motagua then Caribbean coast exchanged for Yucatan cotton, cacao, salt. Art/architecture does **not** advertise individual divine kings (unlike Coba, Dzibilchaltun, Uxmal); emphasizes state economic/military/religious power. Few traditional palaces (Monjas early); open colonnaded galleries interpreted as *popol naah* council houses — decentralized power-sharing (*multepal*) of elite lords (military, mercantile, religious offices). Cult of K’uk’ulkan / Quetzalcoatl as pan-Mesoamerican state religion (feathered-serpent columns); also Chaak, Ehecatl. Authors stress idolatry and human sacrifice predated any Mexican “introduction” (Preclassic/Classic precedents).

### Monumental complex and Sacred Cenote

Site core ≥5 km² dense architecture; Old Chichen Puuc/Chenes southern buildings; northern platform: El Castillo (nine terraces, radial stairs, buried temple with red jaguar throne); Great Ball Court largest in Mesoamerica (166×68 m overall; alley 146×36 m; thirteen ball courts at site); Lower/Upper Temple of Jaguars; *tzompantli*; Temple of Warriors (possible prototype for Tula Pyramid B; earlier Temple of Chacmool with turquoise mosaic cache); Court of Thousand Columns; El Mercado. Vertical ball-court walls with stone rings (northern lowland form). Greatest number of early metal artifacts dredged from the Sacred Cenote — gold/copper disks (repoussé battle scenes), jewelry, bells (often trade imports); carbonized cloth fragments; scores of copal cakes (turquoise blue). Popular virgin-sacrifice cenote belief unsupported in their Ch. 13 discussion. Northern lowland vertical-wall ball courts at Uxmal and Chichen contrasted with Preclassic sloping-wall forms.

### Downfall and succession by Mayapan

Major construction ended by ca. AD 1050; by 1100 lost capital status and most population. Historical sources describe conquest; Temple of the Warriors shows sculptures thrown down and cached offerings anciently looted — but violence may postdate prior political breakdown. Authors favor multi-factor collapse (overpopulation, environmental degradation, droughts, leadership failure, warfare) analogous to southern Classic/Puuc demises. [[mayapan]] (~100 km west) as new Yucatan capital; chronicles of Hunak Keel conquering Chichen with Mexican mercenaries may account for destruction evidence; transition mechanism remains unclear archaeologically. Postclassic highland houses claimed Tollan descent; Popol Vuh journey to Nacxit may point to Chichen (Yucatan chronicles name Nacxit-Xuchit as Chichen founder). Peten lakes Kan Ek’ rulers claimed Itza descent after Chichen’s fall. Spanish second entrada: younger Montejo founded Ciudad Real at Chichen among the Cupules (1531–35), then abandoned under siege.

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel, *A Forest of Kings*, 1990]], Ch. 9 “Kingdom and Empire at Chichén Itzá,” Ch. 10 collapse/legacy framing, and notes. **Strong interpretive FLAG.** “Empire,” *multepal*/joint rule, and single Maya–“Toltec” culture reconstructions are Schele & Freidel **1990 POSITION/THEORY** — later contested; do not promote to wiki voice. Compare Coe & Houston 2015 and Sharer & Traxler 2006 densifications above for revised chronology and open invasion-vs-emulation debate.)*

### Reject sequential Maya/Toltec occupations; cosmopolitan unified city

Schele & Freidel summarize the traditional archaeological model: two major occupations—an earlier “Maya” community with Puuc-style temples/palaces and hieroglyphic lintels, and a later “Toltec”/foreign community of Mexican conquerors and Maya allies. **POSITION (1990):** Chichén was always a **single city** occupied by an increasingly cosmopolitan nobility who manipulated diverse Maya and Mexican political expressions in public art to reinforce authority—not two sequential ethnic occupations. Grounding as they present it: (1) “Toltec” pottery at least partly contemporary with Puuc/“Maya” Chichén pottery; (2) unitary settlement organization with a network of stone roads linking principal groups; (3) “Toltec” northern artistic style still utilizing Maya hieroglyphic texts. Northern “Toltec” patrons “were not illiterate foreigners. They were true Maya citizens” (echoing Proskouriakoff 1970 on “Toltec” art co-occurring with Maya texts). Notes: Kubler (1975)—Tula shows only a fraction of Chichén’s program; Chichén likely dominant partner; Mexicanisms via merchant-warrior collaboration but Maya civilization fundamental; authors use “Toltec” in quotation marks. Patio Quad / Mercado elite residences traditionally attributed to illiterate foreigners; Maya-style glyphs on the Temple of the Hieroglyphic Jambs support simultaneous styles within one culture.

### Rise, Yaxuna war, and regional triumph (as of 1990)

Opening setup: coastal canoe peoples labeled Itza; Isla Cerritos port; capital at Chichén (“Well of the Itza”) ~20 km north of contested Yaxuna. Coba’s ~100 km *sacbe* to Yaxuna as territorial monument (~4,000 km² claim). After years of fighting Chichén won Yaxuna fields; war reliefs cast down; Puuc cities and Dzibilchaltún collapsed as political capitals; Uxmal final occupation shows Chichén pottery; Coba slow decline in public construction. War earnest by mid-ninth century; major temple dedications ~860–880 (notes). Written history dates cluster within the second katun of the tenth baktun; earliest clear date **2 July 867** (Watering Trough Lintel); authors re-date High Priest’s Grave from traditional 998 to **10.0.12.8.0 (20 June 842)** as earliest city date (POSITION; contested). High Priest’s Temple cast as architectural prototype of the four-sided Castillo; earlier dating would tie “Toltec” northern center to “Maya” southern center.

### Texts, *multepal*, and absence of a single king

**FINDING contrast:** southern inscriptions commemorate major life-events of kings; Chichén texts focus on **dedicatory rituals by groups of lords**—dates, names, relationships among participants, not births/accessions/wars/deaths as personal history. Texts scattered city-wide in traditionally royal locations (lintels, jambs, freestanding piers, interior friezes). Examples: Temple of the Four Lintels dedication **13 July 881** (fire-drilling, “fire” titles, bird-clawing-heart and rising-serpent sacrifice images); Casa Colorada frieze multi-lord rituals **869–870** (fish-in-hand bloodletting, fire-drilling); Structure 6E1 column—titled participants with darts, severed head, decapitation axes, heart-extraction knives.

**POSITION (core 1990 *multepal* thesis):** kinship reconstruction from mother-of/child-of-mother and *yitah* (“sibling”) ties yields two–three generations linked to a major group of five men as siblings/brothers (**Kakupacal**, Kin-Cimi, Ah-Muluc-Tok, Wacaw, Double-Jawbone, etc.); as of their reading, no clear evidence one individual was superior; all carry *ahau* and *yahau kak* (“lord of fire”); no certain single king; no clear father–son relationships; native chronicles that Chichén was ruled by brothers match the texts. Spanish-period term *multepal* (joint/confederate government) applied to Mayapan; authors are **convinced** Chichén textual evidence points to an earlier precedent-setting *multepal*—“probably did not originate at Mayapan… but in Chichén Itza itself.” Emblem Glyph “divine Chichén Itza lord” used widely by sibling-set leaders (even oldest female as ancestress)—hierarchical ambiguity supports confederate rule in their reading. Precedents for power-sharing (Tikal Smoking-Frog/Curl-Snout; Copan Yax-Pac’s brothers; Bird-Jaguar’s *cahalob*) exist, but dissolution of kingship into a council of nobles was still “fundamentally new and revolutionary.” Cocom pedigree: Casa Colorada and Akab Tzib associate notables with Hun-Pik-Tok, “Divine Cocom.”

### Warrior imagery, empty throne, and incorporation strategy

Public art celebrates **groups and processions**, not the individual king. **Temple of the Warriors / Northwest Colonnade:** frozen procession of 221-plus striding men—majority warriors with spearthrowers, some ax-blade clubs or Tlaloc-associated defensive sticks; veterans with amputated limbs; individual portraits; unarmed sorcerers/priests; one old matriarch; center columns before Chac Mool stairway show **bound captives**—victory celebration. Captives richly dressed like highest-ranking victors (vs southern stripped/humiliated prisoners)—**POSITION:** “the Itza preferred to absorb their enemies rather than destroy them.” Upper Temple of the Warriors columns stand in place of the traditional carved portrait of the victorious king; throne upheld by small warriors but personal identity of sitter not recorded. Temple of the Chac Mool murals: multiple lords on jaguar pillows with god masks—**not a single preeminent personage**; empty throne depicts “the historical idea of a central public persona… not a real individual”; government pictured as assembly/*multepal*. **Captain Sun Disk** and **Captain Serpent** read as statuses, not unique individuals (counters personalist Kukulcan-hero readings).

Upper Temple of the Jaguars murals: Tlaloc spearthrower warfare inside vanquished communities; women fleeing homes; *hom* (“tearing down of vaults and buildings”) as at Tikal/Caracol. Sacrifice: hearts torn out by bird-costumed warriors under feathered serpent; arrows; decapitation especially with ballgame and fire ritual; Great Ballcourt “evidently constructed as a monument to the successful completion of the Itza’s wars of conquest”; skull-rack platform commemorates volume of sacrifice. *(Dramatized victory-procession vignette is NARRATIVE DEVICE.)*

### Institutional theory and afterlife of the model

**THEORY/POSITION package (1990):** several generations built architecture for war-and-trade success while forging a conquest state; abandoned southern narrative portraits with inscribed texts in favor of assemblies of portraits on colonnades; redefinition of *ahau* reaching into lineal male-descent essence. Success lay in redefining defeat as alliance/incorporation rather than dynastic blood feud—template later guiding Culhua-Mexica empire. Brotherhood reasserted; dynasty dismantled; lords celebrated as brothers and *ahauob* “as their ancestors were at the beginning of time.” One idea not exported: divine kingship and its hieroglyphic literature—to expand beyond southern limitations they “terminated the office of king and the principle of dynasty.” Abstract kingship vested in objects (sun-disk ancestor; mirror set into throne—“two-day occupant of the mat”); **Kukulcan**/Feathered Serpent as symbol of divinity of the state, not a named person. Reject “vigorous Mexican foreigners”; leaders were Maya *ahauob* using four-hundred-year-old Tlaloc-Venus warfare precepts. At height “ruled supreme in the Maya lowlands” (empire language = POSITION); extent beyond lowlands “still an open question.” Chichén at height a capital without public royal history—literacy relegated to books of *chilanob* (prophets, not kings). Postclassic: template for cyclic centralization; Mayapan under Cocom lords claiming Chichén descent; last independent Itza king Can-Ek at Tayasal (to 1697).
