# Great Zimbabwe

Type-site of the **zimbabwe tradition** on the high granite Zimbabwe Plateau (modern Zimbabwe), treated in [[connah-african-civilizations-2016|Connah, *African Civilizations* (2016)]] as southern Africa’s classic case of urbanism and state formation in Zambezia (Limpopo–Zambezi zone). All substantive claims below are attributed to Connah 2016 and the specialists he cites, until independent sources densify.

**Identity caution:** Archaeological locus ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetic population. Link with `associated_with` unless identity is argued. Pottery continuity from the twelfth century is used by Huffman & Vogel (via Connah) to argue “Shona identity” for the builders; oral tradition is judged sufficient to link the site to ancestors of present Shona peoples—but peak prosperity ~600 years before modern recording and nineteenth-century disruptions weaken chronological depth of those traditions (Connah 2016, attributed).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Connah presents Great Zimbabwe as Africa’s classic case of colonial **denial of African cultural heritage**: alien-origin myths (Karl Mauch’s 1871 Solomon/Sheba association) survived into the 1960s–70s as a political issue because they became “psychologically essential” for some European settlers (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]*, position).

On excavation historiography, Connah (citing Garlake) contrasts early diggers with later scientific work:

- **Bent** and **Hall** excavated determined to prove alien builders and ignored contrary indications.
- **Randall-MacIver** and **Caton-Thompson** treated the evidence objectively and demonstrated **indigenous identity**. For those who understood the archaeology, Connah holds there was never serious doubt about African origins.

Research bias long focused on stone ruins: after Randall-MacIver, nearly all published work at zimbabwe-tradition sites concentrated inside or immediately outside stone-walled enclosures—Morais & Sinclair’s phrase that at Great Zimbabwe “over 90 percent of archaeological effort has been focused upon 2 percent of the population” (elites over commoners) until reorientation including Huffman’s early-1970s commoner-housing work (Connah 2016 summarizing Sinclair; Morais & Sinclair 1980; Huffman).

Randall-MacIver’s insight that huts are the “flesh and bone” and stone walls only the “skin,” and Caton-Thompson’s courtyard-wall reading of wall jumbles (Maund Ruin), are endorsed as correctives to stone-centric tourism views (Connah 2016, attributed).

Portuguese documentation (sixteenth–seventeenth centuries) knew Mwene Mutapa gold country better than peak Great Zimbabwe; de Barros’s 1552 secondhand description is judged the nearest documentary account of the site after its heyday. Looting of gold objects from ruin sites 1895–1900 (estimated >2,000 ounces / ~62,200 g) destroyed elite grave-goods evidence across the tradition (Connah citing Swan; Mapungubwe gold objects as a surviving reminder).

## Occupation Sequence

**Pre-stone and main building.** The site was occupied before the earliest stone walls. Stone building dates mainly **AD 1275–1450**, with a late period **1450–1550** (Huffman & Vogel 1991 via Connah). Architectural studies show indigenous evolution from poorly coursed (**P**) to regularly coursed (**Q**) masonry; radiocarbon later showed **R** (uncoursed loosely piled) walling was contemporary with P and Q, not a separate late devolution (Robinson et al. 1961; Huffman & Vogel via Connah). Natural granite exfoliation into thin parallel-sided slabs enabled the unusually regular coursed appearance of many walls; fires could also induce artificial exfoliation.

**Architecture and settlement hierarchy.** The Great Enclosure (Elliptical Building) has maximum diameter ~89 m; outer wall ~244 m long, up to ~5 m thick and ~10 m high (~5,151 m³ stonework)—Garlake’s “largest single prehistoric structure in sub-Saharan Africa”; solid conical tower ~5.5 m diameter and >9 m high. Much stonework was originally plastered with **daga** up to ~2 m; daga building is treated as coequal with stone. Archaeologists agree (via Connah) that zimbabwe-tradition stone structures mark elite dwelling-place status and conspicuous labour consumption rather than primary defence.

Cultural/chronological relations among zimbabwe-tradition sites suggest a graded series from capital (Great Zimbabwe) through regional elite centres (e.g. Chipadze’s Ruin, Nhunguza, Ruanga, Zvongombe) to unwalled rural peasant sites (Chivowa Hill, Montevideo Ranch)—implying a formally organized state in the second quarter of the second millennium AD, though the emergence process remains a major research problem (Connah 2016, theoretical claim). About **150** ruins in Great Zimbabwe or later Khami style survive on the plateau; perhaps another **fifty** destroyed since the 1890s; related sites also in Botswana, Limpopo Province (South Africa), and Mozambique. Manyikeni (Manekweni) on the Mozambique coastal plain is a thirteenth–fifteenth-century outlier (~150–200 people). Northern plateau sites (e.g. Zvongombe) appear imposed on unrelated Musengezi/Harare traditions, suggesting expansion from a southern core (Pwiti via Connah).

**Urban scale and social differentiation.** Recognition of extensive densely packed huts revised Garlake’s 1973 estimate (1,000–2,500 adults) upward to as high as ~**18,000** total population (Huffman via Connah). Ordinary townspeople lived at about three times elite density and, at Manyikeni, ate less well (cattle-dominated enclosure diets vs sheep/goat/game on the periphery). Enclosure pottery suites show limited cooking/serving vessels and near absence of grindstones—food prepared outside then brought in (Garlake via Connah). Hill Ruin midden fauna: cattle overwhelmingly dominant; preferential killing of immature animals, especially in elite/hill contexts vs commoner valley housing (Brain; Thorp via Connah).

**Economy and trade.** Mixed farming: sorghum, finger millet, pearl millet, beans, peas (carbonized seeds at Great Zimbabwe and Leopard’s Kopje); livestock as famine buffer and cattle as negotiable wealth/power (linked to the “Bantu Cattle Pattern” from Toutswe through Mapungubwe and zimbabwe sites). Gold mining: Summers listed 1,267 ancient workings; Phimister estimated 7–9 million ounces late tenth–nineteenth centuries, peaking twelfth–early fifteenth concurrent with major building; Swan found lower-status mining populations vs smelting/artefacts at elite sites. Swan (and early–mid first-millennium marine shells/beads) suggest gold mining began in the first millennium AD, contra Huffman’s eleventh-century-or-later origin for mining as direct cause of Great Zimbabwe (Connah 2016, finding). Crafts: iron, copper, gold, tin; spinning, soapstone, ivory/bone, figurines—functional specialization by the second quarter of the second millennium.

The **Renders Ruin** hoard documents fourteenth-century Indian Ocean connections (Persian glazed bowls, Chinese celadon/stoneware, Near Eastern glass, coral, tens of thousands of glass beads, brass wire, cowries)—better read as royal repository than a visiting Arab trader’s stock (Connah via Garlake; Huffman). A **Kilwa** coin (probably early fourteenth century) is the only one from a scientific context. Fourteenth-century building boom coincides with East African coastal expansion especially at [[kilwa-kisiwani|Kilwa]]; both decline in the fifteenth century—Garlake’s “close economic connection”; Sutton links mid–late fourteenth-century coastal depression to falling gold prices after the Black Death. Internal African trade (copper, iron, salt, Congo/Zambia-type iron gongs, Ingombe Ilede-style copper ingots) is argued to pre-date and underpin coastal gold trade (Connah 2016, theoretical claim).

**Decline and successors.** Great Zimbabwe style sites ceased to be important by the end of the fifteenth century; the capital declined sharply as an urban centre by mid-century but likely continued inhabited to the nineteenth century at reduced importance and population (post-1450 archaeology needs more attention—Pikirayi via Connah). Documentary/oral sources make the northern **Mwene Mutapa** kingdom the immediate successor (Baranda: abundant Near/Far Eastern and European glazed ceramics and Indian glass beads with indigenous graphite pottery, but no sophisticated stone building). In the south-west, **Khami**-style revetment platforms (Khami, Danangombe, Naletale) develop; van Waarden’s Butua State (1425–1830) is described as the largest and most powerful southern African state for four centuries until Ndebele invasions (via Connah).

**Chronology flag vs Mapungubwe.** Although zimbabwe-tradition sites have been thought successors to [[mapungubwe|Mapungubwe]], Chirikure et al. (2013a, 2013b) reexamination suggests **chronological overlap** of Great Zimbabwe and Mapungubwe—not a clean sequential replacement only (Connah 2016, dating flag).

## Significance

Connah argues the only “mystery” is why it took so long to recognize Great Zimbabwe’s **urban character**; by the 1970s Huffman called it southern Africa’s first town. The real research problem is explaining settlement growth, centralization of authority, and why by the late nineteenth century only ruins remained in a rural landscape (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]*, theoretical claim).

**Productive-land / multifactor synthesis (Connah’s Ch. 9 conclusion).** Successful mixed farming in a healthy tsetse-free environment raised population; limited good soils and fluctuating tsetse made some areas more productive, enabling controllers of productive land to dominate expanding populations (Beach’s “Great Crescent of population”; Sinclair on land pressure). Mining, crafts, and interregional raw-material trade offered escape from land limits and more negotiable wealth than land or cattle alone. East Coast gold trade greatly enhanced elite power without “causing” the rise of Great Zimbabwe—trade facilitated rather than originated urbanization and state formation; cause was deeper (productive land, cattle, internal networks). Decline reversed these factors (gold price/deposit depletion plus environmental overexploitation of firewood, wildlife, soils, pasture—analogous to Connah’s reading of [[aksum|Aksum]]; Sinclair’s ~200-year capital periodicity Bambandyanalo–Mapungubwe / Great Zimbabwe / Khami). Garlake’s cattle-transhumance model is noted as questioned (Thorp) but cattle’s major economic role affirmed. Religion (Mwari, mhondoro cults, Eastern Enclosure monoliths/birds) may reinforce elite authority; Connah finds no archaeological evidence that religion was the prime mover of first stratification, and rejects a migrant religious-minority origin hypothesis.

Granite architecture is quoted (Garlake via Connah) as developing from an indigenous tradition into “an architecture that is unparalleled elsewhere in Africa or beyond.” Sites are called *zimbabwe* from Shona usage; folk etymologies include *dzimba dza mabwe* (“houses of stone”) / *dzimba woye* (“venerated houses”); linguists note *dzimbahwe* as court, home, or grave of a chief—largest site conventionally “Great Zimbabwe.”

Four-way non-identity stressed in the claims extract: Great Zimbabwe **≠** foreign/alien builders; **≠** solely a gold-trade colony; **≠** empty stone “mystery”; **≠** Mapungubwe (related but distinct capital phase).

## Key Debates

- **Indigenous builders vs colonial foreign-origin myths (historiography debate; position recording).** Attribution of Great Zimbabwe to outside influence (Mauch/Solomon–Sheba lineage; settler political use into the 1960s–70s) is recorded as the classic colonial denial of African cultural heritage. Connah’s position, and that of archaeologists who treated the evidence objectively (Randall-MacIver, Caton-Thompson; Garlake synthesis), is that **indigenous African builders were never in serious archaeological doubt**. This page records both the colonial myth-system and the archaeological demonstration that rebutted it—not as open empirical doubt about builders, but as a **historiography and politics of archaeology** debate.

- **Successive rulers vs contemporaneous spatial zones.** Chirikure & Pikirayi (2008) argue parts of the site represent successive rulers each founding a new residence/power centre (Shona practice), not contemporary activities by different social groups; Huffman (2010a) objected; they defended (Pikirayi & Chirikure 2011). Connah reports the exchange without resolving it.

- **Huffman's Venda ethnohistoric spatial model.** Projecting Venda-related ethnohistory from Danangombe back through Khami to Great Zimbabwe and Mapungubwe is judged “stimulating” but “imposed on the physical evidence rather than generated from it,” drawing criticism (Beach et al. 1997; Beach 1998 via Connah)—a caution on ethnoarchaeological over-reach.

- **Trade vs productive land / cattle as prime mover.** Trade hypothesis (surplus wealth of East African gold trade) is archaeologically testable; Connah’s synthesis: trade facilitated elite power but did not originate hierarchy. Garlake’s specialist pastoralism model competes with mining/trade-first accounts; both sit under Connah’s productive-land framing.

- **Religion as initiator vs reinforcer.** One school elevates religion as major initiator of cohesion/stratification; Connah rejects Garlake’s “most important single factor” claim as archaeologically unsupported while allowing ideology as elite reinforcer.

- **Mapungubwe succession vs chronological overlap.** Traditional successor model challenged by Chirikure et al. 2013 dating reexamination—flag against simple sequential replacement (see [[mapungubwe]]).

- **Oral tradition depth.** Valuable for Shona ancestry and political/religious centre status; weak for peak-period chronological detail (~600 years depth; Mauch 1871 found residents who knew little of the site’s history).

- **Gold-mining start date.** Huffman (mining from eleventh century earliest, Great Zimbabwe as consequence) vs Swan/first-millennium shells-and-beads evidence for earlier mining (Connah 2016).
