# Tell Brak

A very large multi-period tell in the upper Khabur basin of northeastern Syria (ancient
**Nagar**), central to the [[uruk-world-system-debate]] because its deep stratigraphy
records both an independently complex **indigenous** Late Chalcolithic center *and* its
apparent replacement by intrusive [[uruk-culture|Uruk]] material in the Late Uruk period.
Whether that replacement means colonization or emulation is the interpretive crux Algaze
and his critics contest.

## Discovery and Excavation History

First excavated by **Max Mallowan** (1937–38), who exposed the "Eye Temple"; systematically
re-investigated from 1976 by **David and Joan Oates** and successors, with **Geoff
Emberling** directing the pivotal **Area TW** exposure (1990s–2000s). The long,
multi-period stratigraphy — a reliability caution against the horizontally-exposed
single-period clarity of [[habuba-kabira|Habuba]] — is what makes Brak's sequence
evidentially rich but interpretively harder.

## Occupation Sequence (Area TW, attributed — Oates; Emberling 2002)

- **Levels 20–14:** continuous, entirely **indigenous** Late Chalcolithic sequence, no
  Uruk contact. Ubaid-period lower levels attest importance long predating Uruk contact.
- **Level 13:** first contact — isolated Middle Uruk seal impressions and ceramics within
  an otherwise indigenous assemblage.
- **Levels 11–12:** **wholly southern** Mesopotamian material — a Habuba-süd-style
  tripartite house, riemchen bricks, complex tokens — described (J. Oates; H. Wright) as
  "wholly intrusive."

Mallowan's **Eye Temple** sequence shows a parallel discontinuity: three earlier
northern-style temple phases, then a fourth built to southern Late Uruk standards.

## Significance and the Replacement Question

Emberling (2002) concludes the indigenous settlement **contracted** in the Late Uruk
period and reads the wholly-southern Levels 11–12, superimposed on a long local sequence,
as tentative evidence of **colonization / population replacement** by Uruk settlers —
either local elites emulating Uruk temple-building or intruders building it themselves,
the latter representing (his words) "an extraordinary ideological coercion of the local
inhabitants." Emberling and Algaze judge the replacement reading more likely, but Algaze
frames it as open ("only future research will allow us to discern"). **Four-way
non-identity flag:** inferring a *population* replacement from an *artifact* replacement is
an argued interpretation (record as `associated_with`), not an established identity —
material-culture change need not equal migration.

Brak's precontact scale matters for the [[uruk-expansion|expansion]] argument: new surveys
(Matthews 2000; Wright & Rupley 2001) place its maximum size in the Northern Middle Uruk
(2nd quarter of the 4th millennium BCE). Joan Oates (2002) estimated 110–160 ha
(satellite-inclusion dependent); Algaze calls this "problematic," since survey found much
of the intervening area to be clay-extraction pits rather than settlement — but agrees
Brak was a "very substantial" center, minimally >65 ha. Its precocious scale feeds the
argument (Frangipane; Oates) that the northern periphery was already highly complex before
Uruk contact — a pillar of the case against Algaze's original asymmetry reading (see
[[uruk-world-system-debate]]).

## Key Debates

- **Colonization vs. emulation** at Brak (Emberling; Algaze) — see above.
- **Prior peripheral complexity** — Brak as evidence the north was no passive backdrop.
- After the Uruk collapse, Brak grew (min. 43 ha in the later Early Bronze Age) as one of
  several powerful indigenous kingdoms of the post-Uruk periphery (with Ebla, Tell Chuera,
  Tell Mozan, Tell Leilan, Tell Taya), later an Akkadian administrative center — the
  `informal-empire` "strengthening" fork realized (see [[informal-empire]]).

## Matthews densification (2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]], who co-directed the final publication of three years' excavation at Tell Brak — a project in which five years elapsed between fieldwork completion and the final volume, illustrating the long lag common to major excavation reports.)* Matthews notes Brak is known from Mari, Ebla, and Tell Beydar texts as the regional power "Nagar" in the later third millennium, yet has yielded little on-site textual material itself — an evidential gap he attributes to non-survival, recycling, or deposits not yet excavated, rather than to Nagar/Brak's identification being in doubt. He also cites Brak, alongside Tell Taya and Tell Asmar, as a find-spot for later-third-millennium hoards across the Akkadian imperial periphery (R. Matthews 1994): the HS3 hoard at Brak was buried under a subsequently abandoned building, with wind-blown deposits indicating a settlement interruption consistent with — though not securely datable to — Akkadian-collapse disruption (R. Matthews 1994: 301).
