# Kruger National Park

Kruger National Park is a ~2-million-hectare protected area and parastatal in northeastern
South Africa, bordered by Zimbabwe to the north and Mozambique to the east, managed by
**South African National Parks (SANParks)**. It is the central case of [[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|Lynn Meskell's *The Nature of Heritage* (2012)]], which treats the park as a
"socio-nature" where nature conservation has consistently overridden the management of a rich
archaeological and cultural past. All interpretive claims below are attributed to Meskell 2012 and
the specialists she cites unless noted.

**Identity caution:** Kruger is a survey-region containing 1,000+ loci across many periods.
Archaeological culture ≠ language ≠ genetic population ≠ self-conscious people; ethnonymic labels
(San, Venda, Shangaan/Tsonga) attached to sites are `associated_with` links, not identities, unless
argued.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The park originated as the **Sabie Game Reserve** (proclaimed 1898), was consolidated and
proclaimed **Kruger National Park in 1926**, and was named for Boer president Paul Kruger as a
symbol of Afrikaner nationalism. Historian **Jane Carruthers** has shown (attributed, Carruthers
1994) that Kruger himself was *not* the driving force behind the reserve — Volksraad members
Richard Loveday and J.L. van Wijk forced the government's hand in 1895, and Kruger's memoirs
suggest he regarded game chiefly "as biltong"; Meskell presents the nationalist attribution of
"foresight" to Kruger (NPB official R.J. Labuschagne) as apartheid-era mythology contested by
Carruthers's archival account.

The 1926 lobbying explicitly invoked the American **"Yellowstone model"** of Native expulsion
(Ramutsindela 2004, attributed). The park's administrative center **Skukuza** takes its Tsonga/Shangaan
name — "he who sweeps/strips clean" — from a derisive nickname given to the first warden **James
Stevenson-Hamilton**, referencing his forced evictions of indigenous inhabitants.

Archaeology in the park has been institutionally thin. **University of Pretoria** held exclusive
apartheid-era excavation rights over Kruger and [[mapungubwe|Mapungubwe]], and Meskell reports
(attributed to an anonymous senior interviewee, and Bonner & Carruthers 2003) that 1970s–80s
excavations were mobilized to support the state's ethnic-homeland ("Bantustan") program and were
"highly militarized." As of Meskell's fieldwork **SANParks employed no full-time archaeologist**
across its 22 national parks, leaving heritage without institutional oversight.

## Occupation Sequence

Meskell reports the park-area human record running from **Early Stone Age** tools at Pafuri,
Stolsnek, and Makhadzi (human presence 1.5+ million years BP, Verhoef 1986), through **Middle
Stone Age** finds (c. 250,000–40,000 BP) and **Later Stone Age** material (from c. 20,000 BP:
lithics, ostrich-eggshell beads, rock art), into **Iron Age** settlement. Hundreds of **San
rock-art** sites are recorded and hundreds more presumed undiscovered; linguistic analysis
(Barnard 1992) associates the makers with a Southern San language, and Lewis-Williams inferred the
art is likely millennia old. Later **geometric rock art** is associated with Khoekhoe herders,
with proto-Khoekhoe/San interaction argued for the Limpopo basin from the start of the first
millennium AD (Eastwood & Smith 2005, attributed).

The most prominent Iron Age site is **[[thulamela]]** (c. 1440–1640), a stone-walled hilltop
settlement of the broader Zimbabwe Culture tradition; other Iron Age industrial sites include
Masorini, Makhahane, Shilowa, and Masorini's metalworking furnaces. Ina Plug's 1980s faunal
analysis of 22 sites (170,000 bones) found modern animal distributions "artificial" (fencing,
boreholes), differing from prehistoric patterns. Palaeoenvironmental work (Gillson & Ekblom 2009)
shows climatic swings and significant human impact (maize cultivation, fire) after 1800 —
evidence, Meskell argues, against the notion of a pristine, unpeopled wilderness.

## Forced Removals and the Militarized Park

Meskell's archival and ethnographic work documents systematic dispossession. Stevenson-Hamilton
enlarged the reserve and evicted roughly **2,500 Africans** (Pollard, Shackleton & Carruthers
2003); a 1902 Native Affairs report recorded 20 kraals and 200 people under Swazi chief Mambatine,
contradicting official historiography's "isolated black families." From 1905 resident Africans paid
**squatters' rents** (a revenue source); the **Vagrancy Act (1879)** and the *trekpas* system
regulated and expelled them; "squatters" were still recorded near camps into the 1950s.
Stevenson-Hamilton's 1937 memoir records the near-complete "elimination of all native stock,"
including ~3,000 cattle destroyed (none found infected); a 1939 campaign killed ~14,000 animals in
the Crocodile River area alone (Bunn 2006). Meskell's interviewees (e.g. Mr Khoza, b. 1920s;
"Alpheus"; "Freddie"; "Philleos") recount evictions, cattle-killing on false disease pretexts, and
denial of compensation; evicted elders still refer to the whole park bitterly as "Skukuza."

Kruger also operated a **de facto juridical apparatus** (Proclamation 731 of 1933 made the Warden
an ex officio Assistant Native Commissioner) and, in the 1970s–90s, hosted apartheid **SADF/Third
Force** operations — funding wars in Mozambique/Angola via ivory, rhino-horn and gem smuggling
(Ellis 1994 and others); anthropologist **David Webster** was murdered by an SADF death squad in
1989 after exposing such smuggling. Skukuza-archive documents detail commando deployments, informant
recruitment against border communities, and 1988 orders to "capture or kill."

## Significance

Meskell's central thesis: **"biodiversity trumps culture."** Kruger prepared a UNESCO World
Heritage nomination on natural "outstanding universal value" alone, with no cultural history; it is
not even a biodiversity hotspot (more biodiversity is claimed for adjacent impoverished Bushbuck
Ridge, Magome & Murombedzi 2003), yet biodiversity discourse — Meskell reads it through Escobar's
"invention" of biodiversity and Agrawal's **environmentality** — functions as a racially "neutral,"
cosmopolitan idiom that bypasses the contested politics of cultural heritage and aligns the new
nation with First-World modernity and international funding (Gates, Packard, Pew, Ford, Rockefeller
foundations). Archaeology, by contrast, sits under the **"People and Conservation"** division
(formerly "Social Ecology"), aligned with development/education rather than science and thus
structurally marginalized. Meskell terms the recurring pattern — from removing people to literally
burning-clearing the Masorini site — a mechanism of **[[past-mastering]]** ("Skukuza" = stripping
bare). Nelson Mandela, at Kruger's 1998 centenary, acknowledged that black rural people had
sacrificed their land for the park and were long denied access to "their own natural heritage."

**Environmental-citizenship framing.** Meskell reads SANParks programs ("Kids in Kruger," "Keep
Kruger Clean," a 2006 cleanup with planted "fake" garbage) as Foucauldian production of disciplined
"environmental citizens." She contrasts Kruger unfavorably with **New South Wales, Australia**,
where cultural-heritage management is integrated into the park service (~14 archaeologists, ~80
mostly-Aboriginal heritage staff, a Statement of Reconciliation, the living-significance
[[authenticity|Burra Charter]] frame, and post-*Mabo* land rights) — a comparative model, attributed
to Byrne, Brayshaw & Ireland 2001.

## Key Debates

- **Peopling chronology (institution vs. evidence).** SANParks' public materials (as of 2010) state
  Bantu people entered the region "about 800 years ago, gradually displacing the San." Meskell
  identifies this as myth: archaeological and faunal evidence (Mitchell 2002a; Blombos/Spoegrivier
  sheep bones dated to the first century AD) implies pastoralist and farming presence closer to two
  millennia ago, and Iron Age industrial sites contradict claims of low, "harmonious" population
  density. Recorded as Meskell's critique of an official narrative rather than a scholar-vs-scholar
  dispute; see [[khoe-san]] for the four-way non-identity cautions on this material.
- **Land claims.** As of c. 2002 roughly half the park (~1 million ha; ~25,000 potential-beneficiary
  households) was under active land claim; the [[makuleke|Makuleke]] settlement (1998) is the
  landmark co-management case.
- **Commercialization.** McKinsey-led restructuring ("Operation Prevail," early 2000s) pushed private
  lodge concessions and wildlife sales to fund land acquisition and treated heritage as potential
  merchandise revenue rather than science — products Meskell reports were never produced.

## Historiography

Meskell's account rests on **archaeological ethnography** (participant observation, 100+ videoed
interviews, Skukuza-archive research) and is openly reflexive about positionality — she reports her
own exclusion from a project meeting after raising evidence of a peopled precolonial past. Excavation
quality across the park is uneven: the 1970s Masorini reconstruction was outdated and partly
destroyed by ranger "controlled burning" (2004), and Thulamela was excavated by a civil engineer to
sub-international standards (see [[thulamela]]). The culture-historical and apartheid-nationalist lens
shaped much of the earlier interpretation; Carruthers's revisionist history and post-1994 critical
scholarship (Meskell, Ramutsindela, Bunn) reframe the park as a site of dispossession. Source:
[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012]].
