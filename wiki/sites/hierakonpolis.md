# Hierakonpolis (Nekhen)

Predynastic–Early Dynastic Upper Egyptian centre (Greek “City of the Falcon”; ancient Nekhen) where [[lehner-mark|Mark Lehner]] locates the earliest association of king, mound, and Horus—foundational to his origin narrative for the Old Kingdom [[pyramid-complex|pyramid]] in *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* (1997).

## Discovery and Excavation History

J.E. Quibell excavated the town temple enclosure in 1897–8; F.W. Green continued the following year, exposing features from late predynastic (c. 3200 BCE) through New Kingdom (c. 1425 BCE). Their work recovered the Main Deposit cache—including the Narmer Palette—that still anchors narratives of early kingship and unification. Michael Hoffman later discovered protodynastic rulers’ tombs in Wadi Abu Suffian, roughly contemporary with the Nekhen temple mound. Lehner’s 1997 treatment is a short but load-bearing origin chapter (pp. 72–74), not a full settlement monograph; subsequent Hierakonpolis Expedition results post-date or sit outside his synthesis and are not incorporated here.

## Occupation Sequence

### Town and temple mound

Near the beginning of the 1st dynasty (c. 2900 BCE), settlements scattered across the low desert and up Wadi Abu Suffian coalesced into the walled town of Nekhen. In one corner, a mudbrick enclosure holds Egypt’s oldest known temple mound—where, Lehner argues, basic concepts of divine kingship appear to have originated.

The circular mound, retained by a revetment of coarse sandstone blocks in horizontal courses on Layer 2, measures ~49.26 m (162 ft) across, courses stepped at 45°; original height unknown (top probably cut down for later buildings). Layer 3 (early 1st dynasty) around the mound held abundant charcoal, fire-reddened earth, and protodynastic/1st-dynasty sherds—traces of conflagration suggesting reed-matting and wood structures of the traditional type later imitated in stone by Djoser at [[saqqara]]. Lehner presents the mound as a “high place” for a chapel in the form of the Per Wer (“Great House”), the national shrine of southern Egypt; a temporary ruler’s shrine might have been set up on ritual occasions.

### Main Deposit and Narmer

Immediately outside the mound to the northeast, the Main Deposit included flint knives, decorated stone maceheads, faience figurines, slate palettes, and a concentrated heap of ivory figurines and wands—almost all protodynastic and Archaic in style. Green thought the cache lay upon or within Layer 3.

The **Narmer Palette** is the most striking object: monument of early kingship and a pictorial statement of forcible union of southern with northern Egypt. Narmer’s name is written with catfish (*nar*) and chisel (*mer*) in the *serekh*; he precedes Hor-Aha, probably first king of the 1st dynasty. Findspot clues (slotted limestone pavement; limestone door jamb with basalt pivot socket as bound prisoner) suggest near-original position by the Archaic temple entrance—toward the south end of the east side, matching later temple entrances and, Lehner notes, the entrance location of Djoser’s complex.

The great limestone **Narmer macehead** shows the king seated atop a stairway at a ceremony some place at Buto (Delta counterpart of Nekhen); Lehner finds it tempting to see the stair as a stylized Nekhen-like mound, though no stairway evidence was found at the temple mound itself.

### Later sanctuary

Later Egyptians built a sanctuary centered on the mound, possibly in Middle Kingdom times, with five chambers—recalling the five niches standard in pyramid temples from Khafre onward. In the central chamber a copper-plate falcon with gold head and plumes was ritually buried upright on a standard, making the mound a virtual tomb of Horus of Nekhen.

### Desert tombs and provincial pyramid

For generations before the 1st dynasty, local rulers placed tombs far into the desert near cliff passages; Hoffman’s Wadi Abu Suffian tombs are roughly contemporary with the temple mound. Nearby el-Kula is one of seven provincial small step pyramids Lehner catalogues (with Sinki near [[abydos]], Seila, Elephantine, etc.)—possibly a Huni-period programme of living-sovereignty markers rather than tombs.

### Afterlife of the name

Userkaf’s 5th-dynasty sun temple at [[abusir]]/Abu Ghurob was named Nekhen-Re (“Stronghold of Re”), after Hierakonpolis—explicitly tying solar royal architecture back to the falcon city.

## Significance

- **Primeval mound theology.** Lehner links protodynastic grave mounds and the Nekhen temple mound to the fertile first land of creation; priests “planted” the king’s body so he would rise like seedlings after the Nile flood. Pyramid Texts Atum-as-mound and the later Rite of the Mound of Jemme are read as elaborations of the same complex; the Old Kingdom pyramid is the “mound of transformation,” the pharaoh keeper of [[maat|Maat]].
- **Architectural genealogy.** Shared entrance positions and mound-in-enclosure geometry with Abydos valley enclosures and Djoser’s complex underwrite Lehner’s evolutionary chain Hierakonpolis → Abydos → Saqqara mastabas → Step Pyramid.
- **Icon of unification.** Narmer Palette remains the emblematic visual of early dual kingship—presented by Lehner as Main Deposit star object, not as unproblematic historical reportage of a single battle.

## Key Debates

- **Nekhen as southern “capital”** (Lehner’s cautious “perhaps”) vs one of several competing Upper Egyptian centres.
- **Narmer macehead ceremony locus:** Buto vs Nekhen-mound stylization (tempting analogy, no stair found).
- **Main Deposit formation process** (single cache vs multi-phase accumulation)—beyond Lehner’s summary.
- **How directly the temple mound “prefigures” pyramids:** strong interpretive claim in Lehner’s origin chapter; independent architectural and ritual evidence must carry weight, not only homology of mound + enclosure + east-south entrance.

## Historiography

Hierakonpolis entered pyramid studies through Quibell–Green’s spectacular Main Deposit and through mid-20th-century evolutionary models of kingship ideology. Lehner’s 1997 chapter freezes a particular synthesis: mound → Per Wer → Djoser petrification of reed architecture → pyramid as cosmic mound. That chain is attributed theoretical architecture, not a field consensus statement. Hoffman’s desert tombs and later expedition work thicken the settlement picture beyond what *The Complete Pyramids* can hold. Collection coverage here is Lehner-only; full Hierakonpolis Expedition publications are outstanding.
