# Lagash

Lagash (its urban core often called Girsu; [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] uses "Lagash" and "Girsu" for the same city-state complex, centered on Tell Telloh) was one of the largest Sumerian city-states, with a territory of roughly 2,880 km² and 30,000–35,000 people. Before the First World War, French excavations at Girsu supplied most of what was then known of Early Dynastic Sumer, yielding art and inscriptions sufficient to reconstruct a ruler-list for Lagash spanning roughly 2500–2000 BCE. Roux notes Lagash's rulers do not appear on the Sumerian King List, likely because they were never considered to have ruled all of Sumer.

## Discovery and Excavation History

French excavations at Tell Telloh, conducted before the First World War, recovered the bulk of the site's Early Dynastic inscriptional and artistic record. Gudea's diorite statues (see below) survive mostly via illicit excavation and looting rather than controlled recovery, per Roux — a reliability caution on their find-context.

## Occupation Sequence

**Early Dynastic rulers.** Ur-Nanshe (grandfather of Eannatum) built temples, dug canals, and imported wood from Dilmun; he is portrayed on a wall-plaque with his wife, seven sons, and three officials. Relations with [[mari]] appear to have been friendly — a hoard possibly gifted by Mesannepadda of Ur was found at Mari, apparently contemporaneous with Ur-Nanshe's reign.

Eannatum, *ensi* of Lagash (c. 2455–2425 BCE per Roux), was a temple-builder and canal-digger whose military campaigns, per his own inscriptions, "purged" Elamite bands and claimed conquest of Elam "in its entirety" — a claim Roux flags as likely exaggeration. He is credited with overthrowing Ur and Uruk and adding "the kingship of Kish" to Lagash's holdings. His best-documented conflict was the Lagash–Umma war over the Gu-edin border field: Umma's *ensi* allegedly "raided and devoured" Gu-edin and tore out the boundary stele earlier set by Mesalim of Kish; Eannatum defeated Umma's forces (his inscription boasts of having "heaped up piles of their bodies in the plain"), imposed a peace treaty restoring the boundary and levying tribute in barley on Umma, and commemorated the victory on the fragmentary Stele of the Vultures. The inscriptions present the war explicitly as a victory of the god Ningirsu over Umma's god Shara — a theological framing Roux notes rather than endorses. Later in Eannatum's reign, a coalition of Kish and Mari (led by Zuzu/Unzi, King of Akshak) fought him; he claimed victory, but his "small empire" effectively ended around this point.

Entemena, *ensi* of Lagash and Eannatum's nephew, renewed the war with Umma a generation later. His inscriptions, on two clay cylinders, recount the prior history and claim he "slew the Ummaite forces up into Umma itself," after which he dug a permanent boundary ditch and concluded a "brotherhood pact" with Lugal-kinishe-dudu of Uruk (who had by then united Uruk and Ur). His reign ended in peace and prosperity, per Roux. By roughly 2400 BCE, per Entemena's own inscription on a silver vase dedicated to Ningirsu, the office of high priest (*sanga*, held by one Dudu) was already separate from the office of *ensi* — evidence of the broader Early Dynastic trend toward separating Palace from Temple authority.

Following Entemena, priests of Ningirsu seized the throne of Lagash for roughly two decades, expanding their personal property at the temple's expense, until overthrown by Uruinimgina (formerly read Urukagina), the city's last *ensi*, who instituted well-known social and tax reforms — reducing abusive taxation on burials and weddings, revoking corrupt officials, and restoring land and property "encroached upon" back to Ningirsu's temple estate, framed in his own inscription as "freeing the citizens of Lagash from usury, monopoly, hunger, theft and assault" (see [[temple-economy]]). Roux notes these reforms had no lasting effect: Lagash fell regardless, during Uruinimgina's eight-year reign, to Lugalzagesi, *ensi* of Umma, who marched against and destroyed Girsu, ending this dynasty. A scribal lamentation survives describing the sack — looting of the Antasurra temple and the E-engur temple of Nanshe, "shed blood in the temple" — and cursing Lugalzagesi; Roux notes drily that "the curse had no immediate effect."

**Gudea and the Gutian-period revival (c. 2141–2122 BCE).** Gudea, *ensi* of Lagash/Girsu, ruled during the tail of the Gutian period, contemporary with the last Gutian kings, with apparent effective local independence. He rebuilt or built at least 15 temples in Lagash; his major project was the E-ninnu, temple of the city-god Ningirsu, documented on two large clay cylinders and on statue inscriptions — the fullest surviving Sumerian first-person account of a temple-foundation ritual. The narrative includes a dream-vision in which a giant male figure, identified by the goddess Nanshe ("interpreter of dreams") as Ningirsu, and a female figure identified as Nisaba (goddess of writing and science), instruct Gudea to build the temple; it describes purification rites (fire, pure clay) and the import of construction materials from afar — cedar from the Amanus ("Cedar mountains," via a path allegedly never before entered), stone from unnamed quarries, copper from "the Copper mountain of Kimash," silver, and red stone from Meluhha, with craftsmen brought from Elam, Susa, Magan, and Meluhha.

Roux notes that only one military campaign is mentioned across all of Gudea's inscriptions (against Anshan, east of Elam), and infers from this that the temple's exotic materials were acquired through trade rather than conquest — evidence, in his reading, of "the almost unbelievable prosperity of a Sumerian city-state" after a century of Akkadian rule and roughly fifty years of Gutian occupation. This inference is attributed to Roux's own economic reasoning, not a directly stated fact.

Roughly 17 statues of Gudea survive, mostly recovered via illicit excavation rather than controlled recovery, carved from hard polished black diorite imported from Magan (evidence class: material). Roux praises their "simplicity of line...economy of detail...sensitivity of expression," ranking them among world sculpture's masterpieces; their iconography shows a seated young man with a calm expression, hands clasped, and a temple-plan or foot-rule across the knees. Roux reads this portrait type as exemplifying "the perfect Sumerian ruler, pious, just, cultured...faithful to the old traditions...pacific" — a ruler-ideal he flags as "soon to disappear" from the historical record. This aesthetic and ideological reading is Roux's own interpretive judgment, not a neutral description.

## Significance

Lagash's Early Dynastic inscriptional record — particularly the Eannatum/Entemena border-war sequence with Umma — is the best-documented case of inter-city-state conflict from this period, and its temple-economy documentation (the Baba-temple archive at Girsu) shaped, and later distorted, older historiographic models of the Sumerian "temple-city" (see [[temple-economy]]). Gudea's E-ninnu inscriptions are the fullest surviving first-person Sumerian temple-foundation narrative, and his statuary is a benchmark of Sumerian portrait sculpture.

## Key Debates

The Baba-temple archive's outsized influence on the older "all land is temple property" model of the Sumerian economy is a documented historiographic correction — see [[temple-economy]] for full treatment; Roux states modern reappraisal of Presargonic tablets shows a more mixed economy of temple, palace, and private land coexisting, rather than a uniform temple-dominated system.

## Historiography

Reliance on French pre-WWI excavation for Early Dynastic Lagash, and on looted/illicitly-excavated statuary for the Gudea period, both bear on the reliability of specific find-contexts even where the inscriptions themselves are well preserved. Royal inscriptions throughout (Eannatum's, Entemena's, Uruinimgina's, Gudea's) are self-presentational sources and should be read as such — victory claims, reform claims, and piety claims all serve the commissioning ruler's legitimation.

## The Bau/Baba Temple Archive and the Temple-State Model (Postgate 1994)

[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate (1994)]] identifies Lagash/Girsu as **the
single best-documented Early Dynastic–Akkadian temple economy**, via the Bau (Baba)
temple archive recovered at Girsu/Tello — roughly 1,600 tablets, looted and now
scattered across collections worldwide, with the main scholarly studies dating to the
1920s. Postgate treats this archive as the **historically unrepresentative evidentiary
basis** of the older "temple-city" model of the Sumerian economy: until the 1950s,
cuneiform scholarship characterized early Mesopotamian city government as a theocracy in
which (per V. Gordon Childe's extreme formulation) the temple owned all the land and
employed the entire population. Postgate states plainly that "this extreme view is now
discredited. We cannot any longer maintain that because the temple collected
commodities and distributed them to its dependants the entire economy operated through
'redistribution,' or that the priests controlled all agricultural production and
commercial activity" — while cautioning against overcorrection, since the temple
remained a central symbol of communal identity and a major concentrator of wealth and
services. Because Girsu's Bau archive happens to be exceptionally large and well
published relative to other Early Dynastic sites, it disproportionately shaped — and
distorted — modern reconstructions of "the" Sumerian economy generally; see
[[mesopotamian-temple-state-debate]] and [[temple-economy]] for the full historiographic
treatment of this correction (a position Postgate shares with Roux's independent
critique of the same archive-driven model, recorded above under Historiography).

Postgate also cites the Bau temple's own personnel lists as indirect evidence for
military mobilization practice: disappearances from the Bau Temple staff roster under
Uruinimgina's reign are read as war-caused deaths or incapacitation from the fighting
against Umma, supporting a reading of Early Dynastic Lagash's "armies" as drawn from
the same conscripted civilian labor population rather than a distinct professional
soldiery — the generic Sumerian word for "troops" applied indifferently to either
context. He records Uruinimgina's reform texts (which Roux treats in full above) as the
earliest attestation of the royal-protective ideology later echoed in the Ur III law
code and the Code of Hammurapi ("the strong should not wrong the weak"): Uruinimgina is
the first known king to declare the ruler's duty to protect orphan and widow, and his
reforms curb what he frames as exorbitant priestly burial fees — consistent with, and
attributed alongside, Roux's fuller account of the same reform program above. Postgate
separately notes a lament for the sacking of Lagash's temples (after the fall of
Uruinimgina) recording Umma's ruler looting and burning the Bagara, Dugru, Abzu'eg, and
Gatumdug temples and the Eanna shrine of Inanna at Lagash, and burning Ningirsu's barley
fields — framed in the text itself as a "sin" against Ningirsu, a directly attributed
primary-source political and religious framing of intercity conflict, not a wiki-voice
historical judgment. Under the Ur III offerings-to-Ekur system, Lagash's contribution
burden was set at two months (versus one or half a month for other provinces), "in
proportion to their wealth" — evidence of the province's continued economic importance
after its political independence ended. (As of
[[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)

## Potts (*Mesopotamian Civilization*, 1997) — Additions

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]] unless noted.)*

### Eanatum and Entemena's canal works and weirs

Eanatum of Lagash built a canal called Lumagimdu ("Sweet as Luma") for Ningirsu,
together with its reservoir (Cooper 1986, cited by Potts); his nephew Entemena later
restored it, using 648,000 fired bricks and 1,840 *gur* (roughly 264,960 l) of bitumen
(Cooper 1986:66). A structure excavated by the French at Tello is thought by some
scholars to be a weir built by Pirigme (2117-2115 BCE), son of Ur-Ningirsu,
commemorated in a brick inscription (Barrelet 1965, cited by Potts) — an attribution
Potts records as a scholarly position rather than settled fact. See
[[mesopotamia-geography]] for the wider Sumerian/Akkadian water-management
terminology and canal-digging record this feeds.

### Heimpel's challenge: the Tigris as Lagash's main water source

Because Tigris water was historically hard to lift for irrigation before mechanical
lifting devices existed, most scholars have regarded southern Mesopotamia as a "land
of one river" (the Euphrates) rather than "land of the twin rivers." W. Heimpel (1987,
1990) challenges this consensus, showing that in the late third millennium BCE the
inhabitants of **Lagash considered the Tigris their main water source** — a position
directly bearing on Thorkild Jacobsen's landmark 1960 reconstruction of the region's
ancient watercourses, which Heimpel separately argues misidentified the channel
flowing past Girsu, Umma, and Adab as an Euphrates branch when it was actually the
Tigris (position; see [[mesopotamia-geography]] for the fuller "land of one river"
debate).

### Al-Hiba pottery production areas

Seven distinct pottery-production areas have been identified at Al-Hiba (ancient
Lagash), among the denser concentrations of kiln/workshop evidence documented for any
Mesopotamian site (Potts 1997, citing site publications; cf. the roughly 500 kilns
reported at Umm al-Hafriyat near [[nippur]] and at least ten Ubaid-period kilns at
Tell Abada in the Hamrin, per Jasim 1989). Potts also draws on an ethnohistoric
account from al-Hiba (Ochsenschlager 1992:52) describing traditional bitumen-caulking
practice for boat repair — bitumen reused from old boats, heated to liquid, spread
with wooden rollers (*sobay*) in 1-3 cm layers, then covered with wet mud for
strength, with boats stripped and repaired annually — as a plausible ethnographic
analogy (flagged as such, not evidence) for ancient Lagash-territory bitumen use (see
[[mesopotamian-trade]] and [[mesopotamia-geography]] for the wider bitumen-resource
treatment).

### Pre-Sargonic ancestor-cult offering lists

Pre-Sargonic Lagash offering-lists, spanning the reign of Urukagina (who deposed
Lugalanda), document a royal ancestor cult: offerings to the dead Lugalanda; a state
funeral for his widow Baragnamtara involving over 600 lamentation-priests/mourners;
and ongoing support for Lugalanda's two surviving daughters. Statues of Ur-Nanshe
(founder of the First Dynasty of Lagash; eight small, one life-size), Baragnamtara,
Shagshag (Urukagina's wife), and Entemena and his wife stood in various temples
(Nanshe, Baba, Nin-MAR-KI at Guabba respectively) and received ongoing food, clothing,
and jewellery offerings after death — evidence, per Potts, that many surviving
anthropomorphic statues from Mesopotamian excavations more plausibly represent
dedicants than deities. Individual veneration could persist for centuries: the priest
Dudu's personal cult (established under Entemena) continued into the Ur III period
despite Urukagina having earlier cancelled his monthly food offerings, and a hymn to
Ur-Nanshe was composed in the Old Babylonian period, half a millennium after his death
(finding, [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]; see
[[mesopotamian-religion]] for the wider cult-statue and ancestor-veneration
treatment).
