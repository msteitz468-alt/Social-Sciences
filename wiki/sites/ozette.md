# Ozette

## Discovery and Excavation History

Ozette, on the Pacific coast south of Cape Flattery, Washington, is a **Makah whaling village**
and one of the most important wet sites in North American archaeology. As
[[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan (1991)]] recounts, on a late-spring night about **AD
1750 a massive mudslide buried the village**, smashing and sealing at least four houses. The
waterlogged, anaerobic mud preserved fragile organics — barkcloth, hide, netting, basketry,
carved wood — that normally vanish from the record, giving "a remarkably complete picture of
Late Period culture before European contact." It lay at the back of a protected beach ideal for
hunting whales, fur seals, and dolphins.

## Occupation Sequence

A long-occupied [[northwest-coast-cultural-tradition|Northwest Coast]] settlement whose buried
houses date to the mid-18th century; the Makah continued to live in the area into historic
times. The catastrophic burial makes Ozette a **Pompeii-like** snapshot rather than a long
stratified sequence.

## Significance

- **Wet-site preservation.** Hundreds of fish hooks (cod, halibut) and whaling gear, plus
  carved wooden objects — decorated boxes, clubs, a whale-fin effigy inlaid with more than 700
  sea-otter teeth (probably used in whale-hunting ceremonies), and a thunderbird-and-wolf house
  panel — survive because of the mud. Fagan calls it "one of the most important archaeological
  sites in North America."
- **Rank and ethnographic analogy.** Ozette anchors Croes's argument that basketry hat forms
  encode rank: among the historic Makah at Ozette, commoners wore flat-topped hats and
  higher-ranking territory-owners knob-topped ones — a distinction Croes projects back onto
  Middle Period Hoko River material (an explicit, flagged ethnographic analogy).

## Key Debates

- **Emergence of Northwest Coast complexity** — Ozette and nearby Hoko River are the rare
  stratified, organically preserved sites used to test the management-first vs. ownership-first
  models (see [[northwest-coast-cultural-tradition]]).
- **Repatriation and descendant control.** Ozette was excavated in close partnership with the
  Makah Tribe, whose museum curates the collection — an early model of collaborative
  archaeology (context postdating Fagan's 1991 text).
