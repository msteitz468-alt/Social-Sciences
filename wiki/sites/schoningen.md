# Schöningen

Open-cast / peat-deposit locality in eastern Germany yielding wooden spears and associated fauna; key organic-technology case in [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999|Gamble 1999]] (citing Thieme 1997; Thieme and Maier 1995).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Peat deposits preserve fauna and wooden artifacts recovered in the 1990s campaigns reported by Thieme and colleagues (as of Gamble 1999). Parallel organic finds Gamble places in the same discussion include the Clacton spear point (Oakley et al. 1977) and wood pieces at Torralba and [[bilzingsleben|Bilzingsleben]]; the later Lehringen yew spear is treated with Schöningen as short *chaînes*/façonnage in the Middle Palaeolithic organic-tech summary (Ch.5).

## Occupation Sequence

Middle Pleistocene peat context (as of Gamble 1999 literature). Fine absolute ages and species of makers are not densified in the 1999 synthesis beyond the general first-European-societies / post-500 ka frame; later dating refinements are outside this source layer.

## Significance

### The spears (as of Gamble 1999 / Thieme)

- Longer **throwing spears** ~2.5 m; three made from felled spruce with tips worked from hard basal wood.
- Site 13: complete short **thrusting spear**, 78 cm, pointed at both ends.
- Site 12: probable **hafting splice**.

Gamble links the expensive-tissue / social-intellect package to predictions of higher-quality animal foods and carnivore-scale ranging: “The expensive tissue hypothesis therefore predicts finds such as the Schöningen spears,” supported by raw-material transfer means (Europe furthest stones often ~28 km vs Africa ~14.6 km) and generic skills (ovates from FLK to [[boxgrove|Boxgrove]]) (Gamble 1999, attributed).

### Curation and *chaîne*

Gamble notes the Schöningen spears appear **lost or discarded while still serviceable**, paralleling many stone bifaces, and asks whether technology was carried only infrequently and over short distances—counter to Binford’s curation-as-energy-investment notion (Binford 1973, 1977 via Gamble). In the later Middle Palaeolithic organic-technology survey he groups Schöningen with Lehringen as short *chaînes*/façonnage rather than long curated organic systems (Gamble 1999, attributed).

Source: [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999]].

## Key Debates

- **Hunting vs other formation pathways** — spears as planned hunting technology challenge purely opportunistic scavenger models; Gamble cites them as predicted by high-quality meat / larger-range models without equating every wooden point to a drive hunt.
- **Curation** — serviceable discard vs Binford energy-investment curation.
- **Age and makers** — Middle Pleistocene assignment as of 1999 literature; species of makers not fixed on this page from Gamble alone.
- **Comparanda** — Clacton, Lehringen, Bilzingsleben wood: same class of rare organic preservation, not a single industry.

## Historiography

Thieme’s 1990s publications made Schöningen a European type-site for wooden weapons. Gamble 1999 deploys it inside a social-technology argument (generic skills, short-distance carry, effective networks) rather than as a pure “modern hunters already” narrative. All claims **(as of Gamble 1999)**.
