## Discovery and Excavation History

Kalibangan lies on the Sarasvati (Ghaggar-Hakra) near its confluence with the Drishadvati. Per Possehl (2002), the Archaeological Survey of India excavated the site over nine seasons, from 1960/61 to 1968/69, under B.B. Lal and B.K. Thapar. A sustained nine-season, ASI-directed campaign represents a comparatively well-resourced excavation program by the standards of Indus-site fieldwork, though this chapter does not detail specific stratigraphic recording methods.

## Occupation Sequence

Kalibangan is a two-period site: an earlier Sothi-Siswal (Early Harappan) occupation, followed by Mature Harappan. Calibration status for the underlying radiocarbon chronology is not stated in the source.

## Significance

The settlement follows a double-mound layout — a High Mound (KLB-1) and a Lower Town (KLB-2) — paralleling the plan seen at [[mohenjo-daro]]. The Lower Town preserves a gridiron street plan. Kalibangan is best known for its so-called "fire altars," ritual structures paralleled at [[rakhigarhi]]; the functional/ritual identification of these features is an attributed interpretation, following the excavators' original reading, rather than an uncontested fact.

A notable and unusually direct piece of subsistence evidence is a preserved plowed field from Period I (the Sothi-Siswal occupation), oriented to the cardinal directions — Possehl notes this pattern parallels modern Rajasthani agricultural practice, an ethnographic-analogy observation that should be flagged as analogy rather than direct continuity evidence.

## Key Debates

The "fire altar" identification at Kalibangan, echoed at Rakhigarhi, bears on debates over the presence of formalized ritual/religious architecture in the Indus Civilization — directly relevant to the [[indus-polity-debate]], since Possehl's central argument for a heterarchical, non-state Indus polity rests partly on the claimed absence of monumental religious architecture; if the "fire altars" are read as evidence of formal cult structures, that reading would need reconciling with Possehl's broader thesis. Kalibangan's Sothi-Siswal-to-Mature-Harappan sequence also reinforces the "continuity beneath discontinuity" pattern documented at [[rakhigarhi]] and [[kot-diji]].
