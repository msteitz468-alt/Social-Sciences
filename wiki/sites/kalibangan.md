## Discovery and Excavation History

Kalibangan lies on the Sarasvati (Ghaggar-Hakra) near its confluence with the Drishadvati. Per Possehl (2002), the Archaeological Survey of India excavated the site over nine seasons, from 1960/61 to 1968/69, under B.B. Lal and B.K. Thapar. A sustained nine-season, ASI-directed campaign represents a comparatively well-resourced excavation program by the standards of Indus-site fieldwork, though this chapter does not detail specific stratigraphic recording methods.

## Occupation Sequence

Kalibangan is a two-period site: an earlier Sothi-Siswal (Early Harappan) occupation, followed by Mature Harappan. Calibration status for the underlying radiocarbon chronology is not stated in the source.

## Significance

The settlement follows a double-mound layout — a High Mound (KLB-1) and a Lower Town (KLB-2) — paralleling the plan seen at [[mohenjo-daro]]. The Lower Town preserves a gridiron street plan. Kalibangan is best known for its so-called "fire altars," ritual structures paralleled at [[rakhigarhi]]; the functional/ritual identification of these features is an attributed interpretation, following the excavators' original reading, rather than an uncontested fact.

A notable and unusually direct piece of subsistence evidence is a preserved plowed field from Period I (the Sothi-Siswal occupation), oriented to the cardinal directions — Possehl notes this pattern parallels modern Rajasthani agricultural practice, an ethnographic-analogy observation that should be flagged as analogy rather than direct continuity evidence.

## Key Debates

The "fire altar" identification at Kalibangan, echoed at Rakhigarhi, bears on debates over the presence of formalized ritual/religious architecture in the Indus Civilization — directly relevant to the [[indus-polity-debate]], since Possehl's central argument for a heterarchical, non-state Indus polity rests partly on the claimed absence of monumental religious architecture; if the "fire altars" are read as evidence of formal cult structures, that reading would need reconciling with Possehl's broader thesis. Kalibangan's Sothi-Siswal-to-Mature-Harappan sequence also reinforces the "continuity beneath discontinuity" pattern documented at [[rakhigarhi]] and [[kot-diji]].

## Wright (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]], *[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]* 2010, chs. 5, 9.)*

**Settlement plan.** Wright reports a raised western sector with a walled/towered entrance and a north-south-oriented residential lower town (Possehl 2002:241), part of a denser Ghaggar Plain settlement cluster (with [[rakhigarhi]] and Banawali) numbering 218 sites — denser than contemporary Cholistan. Bisht (1987:142) is quoted describing Kalibangan and Banawali as the best-documented Ghaggar-region Pre-urban sites, with materials "identical in respect of ceramics, architecture, and antiquities." Plowed-field remnants documented at Pre-urban levels are cited by Wright alongside cattle-bone pathologies elsewhere in the Indus (Raymond and Bridget Allchin 1997:169) as evidence for traction plow cultivation.

**Cemetery (Sharma 1999) — three burial types.** A.K. Sharma's (1999) excavation identified three contemporaneous burial types in segregated cemetery sectors: Type 1, supine burial in a rectangular or oval pit, sometimes brick-lined, with pottery grave goods, similar to Harappa's R37 cemetery; Type 2, pot burials in circular pits with no skeletal remains (symbolic); and Type 3, a large pot plus grave goods in a rectangular or oval pit, likewise with no skeletal remains (symbolic). Sharma interprets Type 1 groupings as reflecting family- or kin-based cemetery organization. Grave goods include pottery, shell ornaments (a bangle fragment with a female burial, an earring with a male), carnelian/steatite/agate/gold beads, an agate gamesman or amulet, and a copper mirror. The most elaborately furnished individual was an older male interred in a mudbrick chamber with 70 pottery vessels (Sharma 1999:87); most interred individuals were "middle-aged" (35–55 years).

**Cross-site comparison.** Wright reads the homogeneity of Type-1 grave-good and burial practice at Kalibangan and at Harappa's R37 cemetery as evidence of broadly shared ideologies about death and the afterlife across Indus cities; she proposes, explicitly speculatively ("conceivably"), that the two singled-out older males with disproportionate grave goods — one at Harappa R37, one at Kalibangan — may represent lineage heads. She concludes, more broadly, that differential mortuary treatment across the Indus civilization is comparatively rare and muted relative to the status differentiation visible in domestic architecture, statuary, and hoards. Kennedy's (2000) physical-anthropology team did not examine Kalibangan directly, but limited evidence suggested "regional phenotypic variability" there, implying the Harappans were not a single biologically homogeneous population — consistent with the four-way non-identity principle (culture, language, genes, and self-identified people remain analytically distinct). A cylinder seal found at Kalibangan replicates motifs known from Indus square seals, paralleling similar finds at Mohenjo-daro and suggesting local rather than imported manufacture (Mackay 1938:344ff.).
