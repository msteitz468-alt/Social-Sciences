# Ollantaytambo

Planned royal estate and inhabited valley town of the [[inca-empire]] on the middle Urubamba, ~40 km downriver from the Pisac sector and within the Sacred Valley estate belt between Pisac and [[machu-picchu]]. As of [[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]], Rowe suggested Pachakuti founded estates such as Pisac and Ollantaytambo to commemorate military victories; the site is treated as a peer of [[machu-picchu]] among spectacular Pachakuti-generation manors, not as an isolated “lost” redoubt.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The living town preserves a large share of the Inca street grid, so research has combined architectural survey, ethnohistory, and landscape engineering analysis rather than pure “discovery.” As of D'Altroy 2002, Protzen’s 1993 study is a principal modern reference for plan, quarries, and multi-phase construction; earlier travelers and Cuzco-region archaeology (including UNESCO-era Cuzco mapping) advanced knowledge of Urubamba estates more generally. Stereotomy experiments at Kachiqata and unfinished wall stones ready to mount at Ollantaytambo figure in Protzen/Lee discussions of pounding-based ashlar technique (as of D'Altroy 2002).

## Occupation Sequence

As of D'Altroy 2002 (after Protzen), the site is a planned residential settlement with palaces, religious and defensive structures, storehouses, roads and bridges, terraces, and waterworks, set on an alluvial fan at the Patakancha–Urubamba confluence and protected by forts and high terraces with entry via bridges and narrow gates. A trapezoidal street grid still organizes the modern town; blocks of enclosed *kancha* held up to six one-room buildings on a patio, with street canals for water (and possibly effluent). Western terraces cascade toward the Fortaleza and Temple of the Sun spur. Engineers diverted Urubamba flow bank-to-bank and channelized the Patakancha through the site; eleven expansive terraces blend with the piedmont. Construction was multi-phase: Hollowell and Protzen identify at least three major imperial phases, still active at Spanish conquest, with ≥7 stone types each in distinct styles; early structures were dismantled and stones reworked—so estate design was not inviolate even for the founding emperor’s *panaqa*, though both Pachakuti and Thupa Inka Yupanki are said to have ordered important buildings. In 1536 steep walls helped repel a Spanish expedition against Manco Inka; Incas flooded the valley against attackers—P. Pizarro’s only real campaign defeat (as of D'Altroy 2002). Saqsawaman and Ollantaytambo may have been only partly designed as fortifications but served that purpose in colonial wars.

## Significance

Ollantaytambo is a type case of heartland royal-estate urbanism: continuous *kancha* plan, landscape hydrology as both productive and military infrastructure, and unfinished fine masonry that documents Inca building process. Relative to [[cuzco]] and provincial “New Cuzcos” such as [[huanuco-pampa]], it shows how private imperial manors concentrated labor and water control in the Urubamba corridor supporting the aristocracy of the [[inca-empire]].

## Key Debates

- **Temple masonry and Tiwanaku.** Six pink rhyolite vertical ashlars at the Temple are unique; some archaeologists (Gasparini and Margolies; Hollowell), citing reported Qolla masons (Sarmiento) and step-fret/T-sockets, argue Inca cut-stone style as a direct outgrowth of Tiwanaku; others (Protzen) doubt a clear link, citing stylistic differences and a multi-century break after Tiwanaku’s demise (as of D'Altroy 2002). Architectural influence is not an ethnic or genetic equation of masons with Tiwanaku culture.
- **Inviolate *panaqa* estate vs. multi-phase imperial redesign.** Archaeological phases challenge a static model of exclusive *panaqa* control.
- **Fortress vs. estate.** Degree to which defensive works were primary design vs. dual-purpose estate landscape later used in 1536.
