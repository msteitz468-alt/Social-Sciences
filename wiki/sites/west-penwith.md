# West Penwith

Westernmost Cornish granite peninsula, treated by [[tilley-christopher]] in *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010|Interpreting Landscapes]]* (2010, Ch. 9) as a near-island landscape of **supernatural places** — tors, solution basins, quoits, circles.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Long antiquarian tradition (Borlase and others as Tilley cites). Tilley's contribution is phenomenological re-walking relating dolmens to tors, processional axes to Carn Galver/Kenidjack, and Bronze Age cairns among rocks and basins.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed, Tilley 2010.)* Neolithic **mimesis**: dolmens look like tors (not the reverse). Bronze Age **appropriation**: cairns among tors and basins; promontory works read as symbolic rock demarcation more than pure defense. No certain Neolithic/BA hilltop enclosures of Bodmin type in Tilley's account.

## Significance

Load-bearing interpretive claims (attributed): solution basins as ancestral/supernatural water places; Men-An-Tol as inverted basin/solar metaphor; northern moors vs southern hills contrast; fire–water–sun metaphorical complex. Anchors [[geological-identity]] for granite-to-sea settings.

## Key Debates

Tilley replies to Bradley's Irish-Sea portal-dolmen problem with local-meaning argument (attributed). Identity cautions: archaeological monuments ≠ continuous ethnolinguistic "people."

## Historiography

Antiquarian sacred-rock readings (Borlase) are historicized; modern survey + Tilley 2010 interpretive layer.
