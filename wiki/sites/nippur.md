# Nippur

Major city of the Babylonian alluvium (modern Nuffar), religious center of Enlil and a recurrent anchor in [[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]]'s central Euphrates survey (*[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]*, 1981). Settlement and watercourse maps for the Nippur–Adab region are among the book's densest case materials.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Long excavated by University of Pennsylvania and successors; this page currently holds **survey-context** densification from Adams 1981, not a full excavation synthesis.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Adams 1981 unless noted.)*

- Multi-period religious-urban center (Enlil) on the central floodplain; intensive survey densest for the Nippur–Adab region.
- Early–Middle Uruk: major channel succession north and east of Nippur; northern enclave settlement area ~360 ha (~36,000 persons at Adams's density constant) after near-empty Late Ubaid — rapid growth pulse.
- Rank-size / hierarchy: northern early Uruk often "flattop"/convex (imperfect integration) vs southern Uruk primacy; later periods show Nippur among volatile major centers under contending dynasties.
- Sasanian third configuration: major artificial arteries including Shatt al-Nil north of Nippur as type large canal.
- Neo-Babylonian–later: Murashu archive and Nippur region as Adams's window on agricultural/fiscal management.

## Significance

- Geographic and religious core of northern Sumer / Babylonia in many periodizations.
- Survey type-area for Adams's watercourse-succession and [[settlement-hierarchy]] arguments.

## Key Debates

- Role of Nippur as religious vs political capital across periods (specialist literature).
- Reconstruction of Early–Middle Uruk channels north/east of Nippur (Adams 1981 vs later geoarchaeology).
- How far Sasanian main-canal control (e.g. Shatt al-Nil) extended into secondary networks ([[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]]; Adams loose-coupling position).

## Historiography

Survey claims date-stamped to Adams 1981; absolute chronologies and channel reconstructions revised by later landscape archaeology.

### Roux (*Ancient Iraq*, 1992, attributed)

[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] adds a religious-political dimension absent from
the settlement-survey layer above. Nippur, per Roux, **never gained or claimed
political ascendancy** over other Mesopotamian city-states and does not appear among
the "dynasties" recorded by the [[sumerian-king-list]]; instead it functioned as
**Sumer's religious capital**, seat of the chief god [[mesopotamian-religion|Enlil]] —
Roux's own simile: "the Rome or Mecca of the Sumerians." Rulers across the Early
Dynastic and later periods competed to send valuable gifts to Enlil's shrine and to
restore or rebuild its temples; by the end of the 3rd millennium BCE, "chosen by Enlil
in Nippur" had become a standard element of royal titulary (e.g. Mesannepadda and
Meskiagnunna of Ur's First Dynasty rebuilt Nippur's Tummal temple, originally built by
Mebaragesi of [[kish]]). A **linguistic frontier** between Sumerian-speakers to the
south and Semitic-speakers to the north fell, per Roux, "somewhere between Kish and
Nippur" — see [[sumerians]].

Roux raises, without resolving, **three competing explanations** for
Nippur's special political-religious status, presented here as positions rather than
settled fact: (a) simple religious fervor; (b) a survival of a "primitive democracy"
tradition — a place where city-state delegates once elected a common war-leader
against invasion threats (see the Jacobsen primitive-democracy thesis on
[[city-state]]); (c) genuine political influence wielded by Nippur's priesthood,
analogous to the priests of Heliopolis in Egypt. Roux states plainly: "these have no
definite answer."
