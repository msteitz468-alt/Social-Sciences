## Discovery and Excavation History

*(Body through Historiography grounded in Higham 1989, *[[higham-archaeology-mainland-southeast-asia-1989|The Archaeology of Mainland Southeast Asia]]*; see **Higham 2002 update** for AMS-era densification from *[[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002|Early Cultures of Mainland Southeast Asia]]*.)*

Ban Na Di lies roughly 20 km from [[ban-chiang]] near Lake Kumphawapi. Charles Higham
had surveyed in the wider Roi Et Province/Phu Wiang area from 1969, and excavations at
Ban Na Di itself, directed by Higham with Amphan Kijngam, began in November 1980
(Higham and Kijngam 1984). The site was partially screened at 1 mm mesh — finer than
the 10 mm screen used at [[ban-chiang]] and the largely unscreened excavation at
[[non-nok-tha]] (whose hard soil precluded screening) — improving recovery of small
finds and microfauna relative to those comparator excavations. Charcoal samples came
from well-provenanced, undisturbed contexts (sand-lens-sealed pit fills, bronze-working
furnace fill, hearths, and a posthole base), yielding what Higham (1989) describes as a
"more acceptable pattern" of radiocarbon dates than either Non Nok Tha or Ban Chiang,
though four dates from a sealed level-7 pit were anomalously later than expected. Of the
sites compared in this respect, Higham judges Ban Na Di the most reliably dated, owing
to its well-provenanced charcoal.

## Occupation Sequence

Stratigraphic level 8 correlates most likely with Ban Chiang Early Period V (dated
1600–900 BCE per White's 1986 revision), and Ban Na Di's mortuary phase 1a is equated
chronologically with Ban Chiang Middle Period VI (Higham and Kijngam 1984, attributed).
Higham (1989) reconciles the Ban Chiang and Ban Na Di bronze evidence to place initial
regional bronze-working at roughly 2000–1500 BCE.

At Ban Na Di, metal (copper and tin) was probably imported as ingots — there is no
local smelting evidence and the ore itself does not appear to have been exchanged
(Maddin and Weng 1984). Two mould types are identified: double sandstone moulds
(pouring spout plus conduits) for axes and arrowheads, possibly requiring a
lead-casting intermediate step or mould pre-heating to prevent thermal-shock cracking;
and clay lost-wax moulds for bracelets, in one of which Pilditch (1984) recovered
surviving insect-wax residue, likely from post-casting polishing rather than the pour
itself. General Period B artefacts run around 10% tin. Bronze artefacts are dominated
by bracelets and arrowheads; only about one in ten burials was accompanied by bronze.
Arrowheads were annealed (hardened by heat and hammering), and broken stone bracelets
were repaired using bronze wire threaded through drilled holes — one bracelet repaired
twice. Bronze-bracelet corrosion preserved cloth-weave impressions (Pilditch 1984):
burial 36 held a silk shroud or garment, while other burials showed only coarse
matting.

The level 6–7 cemetery's latest two burials included iron artefacts (a spearhead,
circlets, a knife). Level 5 then shows a radical change: a dedicated bronze-working
area appears, with crucibles, furnaces, and clay moulds for bells and bracelets;
crucibles were now tempered with rice chaff rather than grog. Vincent (1984) records
that late-phase Ban Na Di pottery was made from a new clay source with a different
temper — rice-and-clay balls, lightly fired, then crushed and mixed with raw clay.
White (1986) dated the phase equivalent to Middle Period VII at Ban Chiang/levels 6–7
at Ban Na Di to roughly 800–400 BCE and associated it with early iron use, while Higham
and Kijngam (1984) preferred a somewhat later range of 700–300 BCE for the equivalent
Ban Na Di context — a minor attributed dating disagreement between the two teams. Both
the Ban Chiang and Ban Na Di excavations reveal a major cultural dislocation around
300 BCE. A comparable transition — basal-layer pottery matching Ban Na Di levels 4–5,
without level 6–7-type material — is reported at Ban Muang Phruk, roughly 10 km
southwest of Ban Na Di, near a salt-extraction mound of the first centuries CE that
parallels the Bo Phan Khan technique.

Rice at Ban Na Di is represented by flotation-recovered grains, judged cultivated by
Chang and Loresto (1984) — a specific finding, though set against a broader regional
picture in which rice-domestication status is contested and inconsistent across
General Periods A–B sites and specialists (e.g., ambiguous chaff-temper-only evidence
at Non Nok Tha, per Chang 1976 and Vishnu-Mittre 1975). No calibration status is
specified for the relevant radiocarbon dates in the available material, so they should
be treated as possibly uncalibrated unless stated otherwise.

Skeletal evidence (Houghton and Wiriyaromp 1984) from the level 6–7 cemetery shows
high infant/young-child mortality, low adolescent mortality, adult males dying younger
than females on average, a maximum age of roughly 40–45, and a life expectancy at
birth of 22.8 years, attributed to pre-modern infection susceptibility rather than
necessarily poor nutrition. Notably, the sample shows no evidence of squatting facets,
in contrast to the [[ban-chiang]] and [[non-nok-tha]] samples; Higham raises the
tentative, attributed possibility that Ban Na Di and Ban Chiang — though only about
20 km apart — may reflect different population habits or origins, not simply different
material culture. Nordin's score (cortical bone thickness of the second metacarpal)
indicates nutrition as robust as that of Finnish farmers or modern Ohio inhabitants,
with few Harris lines (a retarded-growth indicator); one female of roughly 17 years had
already borne a child, indicating menarche around age 15 and, inferentially, good
nutrition.

## Significance

The comparison of the two excavated burial areas, A and B, revealed a marked wealth
disparity load-bearing for the site's importance to social-organization debates: 97%
of shell beads, all five stone bracelets, all clay figurines and trochus-shell
armlets, and 25 of 30 total bronze artefacts (versus 5 in Area A) came from Area B
graves, and only Area B graves contained iron weapons or jewelry. Stratigraphy rules
out a simple early/late explanation for this pattern. Higham (1989, attributed),
drawing on Dalton (1977) on "primitive valuables" and reciprocal exchange in
ethnographically documented societies **as an explicit interpretive analogy, not
direct evidence**, interprets this disparity as evidence that one kin-affiliated
group at Ban Na Di held higher rank and preferential access to prestige valuables.

Both major regional cemetery analyses available to Higham — Non Nok Tha (Bayard 1984a)
and Ban Na Di (Higham and Kijngam 1984) — support the conclusion (attributed) that
domestic communities during General Periods A–B contained internally ranked
affiliative kin groups, evidenced by differential grave-good wealth, but show no
evidence of a dominant regional center or markedly higher "energy-expenditure" elite
burials: emergent social ranking without political centralization. Higham further
notes (attributed) that mortuary evidence from [[khok-phanom-di]], [[non-nok-tha]],
and Ban Na Di together suggests communities had for millennia organized burial groups
around ancestry-based rank, a pattern he treats as predating the later General Period
C/D transition. Higham and Kijngam (1984, attributed) interpret the level 6/7→5
transition at Ban Na Di, together with the parallel Middle-to-Late Period transition
at [[ban-chiang]], as reflecting the expansion of groups from the fringes of emergent
Chi valley [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]].

## Key Debates

Rice-domestication status is contested across the General Period A–B sites: Ban Na
Di's flotation-recovered grains were judged cultivated (Chang and Loresto 1984), in
contrast to the ambiguous, chaff-temper-only evidence at Non Nok Tha and the explicitly
unresolved wild/domestic status of rice at [[khok-phanom-di]] — the wider comparison
is itself a live, unresolved question in Higham (1989), not a settled finding. Dating
disagreement between White (1986) and Higham and Kijngam (1984) over the level 6–7/
early-iron phase (800–400 BCE vs. 700–300 BCE) is a minor, explicitly attributed
dating position rather than a resolved chronology.

## Historiography

Higham's (1989) treatment of Ban Na Di is processual in orientation, applying Dalton's
(1977) ethnographically derived model of "primitive valuables" and reciprocal exchange
as an attributed interpretive lens for the Area A/Area B wealth disparity, rather than
asserting rank or kin affiliation as directly evidenced fact. Excavation quality is
comparatively strong: well-provenanced, sealed charcoal contexts and partial 1 mm
screening (versus 10 mm at [[ban-chiang]] and no screening at [[non-nok-tha]]) make
Ban Na Di's radiocarbon sequence, in Higham's assessment, the most reliable among the
three comparator sites, though not without its own anomalies (the four late level-7
dates). Ban Na Di's data is used throughout the source to reconcile and narrow the
regional bronze-age and iron-age chronology (initial bronze c. 2000–1500 BCE) against
the competing White (1986) revision of the Ban Chiang sequence.

## Higham 2002 update

*(Attributed — [[higham-charles|Higham]], *[[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002|Early Cultures of Mainland Southeast Asia]]* 2002; distinguish from the 1989 synthesis above.)*

### Setting, methods, and basal chronology

(ATTRIBUTED) Ban Na Di, 23 km SW of [[ban-chiang]] near a Lake Kumphawapi tributary confluence on low-terrace wet-rice soils, has five prehistoric levels to 4 m. Basal occupation includes freshwater molluscs, mammal/fish bone, domestic cattle, dogs, and probably pigs; **1 mm mesh screening** recovered microfauna and carbonised rice, showing heavy fishing emphasis. Basal layer has crucible fragments with metal scoria and bronze artefacts; **in situ charcoal dates the basal layer 1300–900 BC** (as of Higham 2002) — later than the ~1600 BCE EP V correlation still reported in the 1989-based sections above, and consistent with the AMS-downward revision of regional bronze at Ban Chiang and [[non-nok-tha]] (see [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]]). The BA cemetery comprises three phases, probably c. 900–400 BC (calibration complex). Thin discrete **flood sand lenses** allowed excavators to conclude material beneath was not disturbed by later grave digging — strengthening association of hearth/furnace/pit charcoal with depositional events.

### Local casting restated

(ATTRIBUTED) Clay-lined furnace ringed by bronze detritus and crucibles for heating copper and tin to melting point before casting; stone mould fragments confirm on-site casting; metal adhering to crucibles indicated copper–tin mixture; two latest graves of the mortuary phase included a few iron offerings — the same local-casting-from-exchanged-metal picture as 1989, now dated to the later BA horizon.

### Mortuary ranking refined (not a separate elite stratum)

(ATTRIBUTED) Burials in two areas 30 m apart, laid in rows over ~2 m depth; men heads south, women opposite. Phase 1A little bronze (cast wire repairing stone bangles); phase 1c more goods including 19 bracelets with one woman, cowries, coastal-origin shell; final phase still rare bronzes, some early Iron Age pottery matching Ban Chiang MP VII. One excavated area consistently richer (exotic ornaments, clay figurines of cattle/deer/humans/elephants, most bronzes) across all three phases, with multidimensional scaling isolating a child, two men and a woman as unusually well endowed — yet Higham judges differences **insufficient to mask underlying similarity**: the richer group is still of the same social stratum (**ranking without centralization / not a separate elite stratum**). This is a sharper formulation than the 1989 "ranked affiliative kin groups" language and is load-bearing for the [[chiefdom]] concept page: Bronze Age Ban Na Di does not meet Peebles–Kus centralised-chiefdom criteria.

### Bioarchaeology densification (Domett 1999 via Higham 2002)

(ATTRIBUTED) Ban Na Di is one of the large BA skeletal samples analysed by Domett (1999). Infant mortality (~19% under one year) is far lower than coastal [[khok-phanom-di]] early phases (>50%) but higher than [[non-nok-tha]] (5.5%, possibly under-represented). Female childbirth mortality peak; barely one in ten lived beyond ~40; Nordin’s score indicates good nutrition; most Ban Na Di women showed pregnancy evidence. Dental enamel hypoplasia lower than Khok Phanom Di adults. Screening method remains decisive: faunal spectra from sites without fine mesh must be treated cautiously — Ban Na Di is Higham’s positive demonstration case.

### Peripheral Iron Age position

(ATTRIBUTED) Higham (2002), discussing Lao Pako, notes that Ban Chiang and Ban Na Di may be read as **peripheral** relative to the major Iron Age changes documented in the Chi and Mun Valley moated centres (Ban Chiang Hian, [[noen-u-loke]], Non Muang Kao) — consistent with the 1989 reading of the level 6/7→5 transition as expansion from fringes of emergent Chi valley chiefdoms, now with denser Mun Valley comparanda.
