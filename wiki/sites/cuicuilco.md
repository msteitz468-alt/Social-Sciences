# Cuicuilco

Formative-period urban center in the southwestern Basin of Mexico, [[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]]'s only peer and rival before Teotihuacan's ascendancy — and one of Mesoamerican archaeology's great mutilated datasets, buried first by the Xitle lava flows (the Pedregal) and then by Mexico City.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Byron Cummings exposed the great circular platform in the 1920s (Cummings 1933); Heizer and Bennyhoff re-excavated in 1957–58; the most useful data come from 1967 INAH salvage excavations under Florencia Muller ahead of Olympic construction, after mechanical removal of lava overburden — much of it still unanalyzed and unpublished when [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979)]] wrote. The site could never be systematically surface-surveyed: the lava sheet and the city's growth had destroyed or sealed it (the Basin survey classified it as an "excavated site" of indeterminate density), and Sanders, Parsons & Santley record that continued urban expansion through the 1960s–70s obliterated all but the major ceremonial structures. Excavation quality is correspondingly uneven; the settlement reconstruction rests heavily on Muller's trench transect and personal communications.

## Occupation Sequence

As reported in Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979): ceremonial construction associated with Late Middle Formative ceramics suggests Cuicuilco was probably already the largest, most complex site in the Basin in the Middle Formative (First Intermediate Phase Two, when they infer a possible fourth, dominant organizational tier in the southern Basin with Cuicuilco atop several regional centers). At its Phase Three peak (300–100 BCE) they estimate a settlement of at least 400 ha and a minimum ~20,000 inhabitants, with temple platforms to 80 m diameter and 20 m height — public monumentality "without parallel in the entire Basin," equalled or exceeded only by Teotihuacan. Muller's excavations show continuous Terminal Formative occupation across a 1.5-km transect: walled house-compounds (residence + patio) along streets on a grid oriented to the pyramid complex, subsidiary pyramids among the housing, total extent perhaps 4–5 km² — a configuration the excavators and authors noted resembles Teotihuacan's urban plan.

The destruction chronology was revised repeatedly: Heizer & Bennyhoff found Phase Four ceramics (refuting the older view that a lava flow ended occupation at the close of Phase Two), and INAH work extended occupation to Phase Five, with geological studies indicating **two** Xitle flows — one near the end of Phase Three that reduced the site's agricultural base, and a later one that buried it; Muller (per Sanders, Parsons & Santley) placed the final burial as late as the mid-Teotihuacan period. Sanders, Parsons & Santley argue the first eruption `contributed_to` (not single-handedly caused) Teotihuacan's supremacy by curtailing Cuicuilco's agricultural base while its rival grew.

## Significance

Anchors the Basin's first documented case of political bipolarity: Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) read Phase Three settlement gaps and hilltop fortifications as buffer zones between hostile polities and hypothesize "high-level confrontation between Cuicuilco and Teotihuacan," with Teotihuacan's victory — aided by volcanism and its irrigable alluvium — opening the way to its Phase Four explosive nucleation (see [[state-formation]], [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]], [[basin-of-mexico-chronology]]).

## Key Debates

The rivalry-and-confrontation reading is the authors' hypothesis, explicitly hedged in the source ("an attractive hypothesis for us"); the site's burial makes decisive testing unlikely — Sanders, Parsons & Santley themselves rated future research prospects poor.
