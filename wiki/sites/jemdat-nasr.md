# Jemdat Nasr

Jemdat Nasr is the **type site**, located between Baghdad and Babylon, that lends its
name to the archaeological period (c. 3150–2900 BCE) bridging the [[uruk-culture|Uruk
period]] and the Early Dynastic period. Its distinctive painted pottery — "Jemdat Nasr
ware": large, thick jars bearing geometric and naturalistic designs in black and red
paint on buff clay — was discovered at the site in **1925**.

## Discovery and Excavation History

This source (Roux 1992) gives the ware's 1925 discovery date but does not detail a
full excavation history or excavator for the site itself; the ware's diagnostic weight
is, notably, downplayed by Roux rather than emphasized (see below).

## Occupation Sequence

[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]] argues there is **no fundamental cultural break**
between the Uruk and Jemdat Nasr periods — architecture, seal iconography, and ceramics
are continuous, with only "simple variations in style and quality." Jemdat Nasr ware
itself is found in only small quantities at other Mesopotamian sites, and Roux
explicitly **downplays its significance as a diagnostic marker**, suggesting it may
represent "nothing more than a transient local fashion" rather than a distinct cultural
horizon, and noting it may be of Iranian inspiration. The Jemdat Nasr period's main
original contribution, per Roux, lies instead in sculpture — relief and in-the-round
work on stone vases, mural plaques, stamp seals, and ex-voto statuettes, including a
basalt hunting stele from [[uruk|Warka]] regarded as an ancestor of later Assyrian
royal hunting-scene iconography.

Two unrivaled Uruk-period masterpieces belonging to this horizon — the alabaster "Warka
Vase" and the "Warka Mask" ("Lady of Uruk") — are documented on [[uruk]] rather than
here, both having been recovered at that site.

## Significance

Contemporary tablets from Jemdat Nasr, [[ur]], and Tell 'Uqair are, per Roux,
"practically certain" to be written in **Sumerian**, licensing the label "Sumerian
civilization" for this horizon as a whole — one of the source's key evidentiary anchors
for dating the presence of Sumerian speech this early (see [[sumerians]] and the
[[sumerian-problem-debate]]). Distributionally, Uruk-culture traces are widespread
across northern Mesopotamia, but Jemdat-Nasr-culture traces proper are confined to a
handful of "Sumerian colony" sites in the north (Tell Brak in the Khabur basin, Grai
Resh in the Sinjar area, Tell Asmar and Khafaje in the Diyala valley) — an asymmetry
Roux treats as evidence that the deeper Uruk cultural package spread more readily than
this later, narrower stylistic phase.

## Key Debates

- **Diagnostic status of the ware itself:** whether "Jemdat Nasr" ware marks a genuine
  cultural-period boundary or merely, as Roux suggests, a "transient local fashion"
  superimposed on essential Uruk-period continuity.
- Whether the Jemdat Nasr horizon's Sumerian-language tablets settle (or merely bear on)
  the "Sumerian problem" of ethnolinguistic origins — see [[sumerian-problem-debate]].
