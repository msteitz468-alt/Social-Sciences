# Abusir

Fifth-dynasty pyramid field on desert slopes northwest of Abusir lake—a northerly extension of the [[saqqara|Saqqara]] necropolis. Arabic *Abusir* < Greek Busiris < Egyptian Per Wsir (“Place of Osiris”). Primary synthesis: [[lehner-mark|Mark Lehner]], *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* (1997).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Ludwig Borchardt’s 1902–8 excavation of Sahure’s complex revealed a “great wealth of relief” and established Abusir as the type site for the decorated 5th-dynasty [[pyramid-complex]]. Schaefer and Borchardt (1898–1901) dug Niuserre’s sun temple “Delight of Re”; Herbert Ricke (1955–7) reconstructed Userkaf’s Nekhen-Re sun temple in four continuous major phases. Czech Institute work under Miroslav Verner (from the 1970s, intensified on Raneferef from 1974) reopened the unfinished pyramid, Khentkawes II, and much of the cluster’s mudbrick architecture and papyrus finds. Paule Posener-Kriéger published Neferirkare’s Abusir Papyri—still the key administrative archive for Old Kingdom pyramid-temple economy. Zahi Hawass later reported a Sahure causeway relief of dancing and celebration after setting the capstone (Lehner 1997).

## Occupation Sequence

### Setting and cluster plan

Abusir lake served as a natural harbour for the complexes. Userkaf initiated the royal cemetery by building his sun temple slightly north of the plateau; successors formed a pyramid cluster of four kings’ pyramids plus Khentkawes II, possible queens’ Lepsius XXIV–XXV, and an unfinished pyramid perhaps of Shepseskare. Three pyramids (Sahure, Neferirkare, Raneferef) align on a NE–SW diagonal along their NW corners; Verner suggests the Abusir and Giza diagonals converge toward Heliopolis and the ben-ben. Niuserre broke the Abusir diagonal by inserting his pyramid between Sahure and his father Neferirkare.

### Royal pyramids

**Sahure** — “The Rising of the Ba Spirit”: ~78.75 m square, ~47 m high, slope ~50°11′40″; stepped core of rough western Abusir limestone; satellite at SE corner (thereafter standard to end of OK). Valley temple with lake landing; causeway ~235 m fully relief-decorated (gods leading prisoners—genre lasting ~2,500 years). Mortuary temple plan called the “conceptual beginning” of subsequent Old Kingdom examples: pillared court, five niches, offering chapel, complex drainage; estimated ~10,000 sq. m of fine relief.

**Neferirkare** — larger attempt (~105 m base; if completed true pyramid ~72 m at ~54°); evidence of six-tier step core, girdle, unfinished red granite casing. Mortuary temple finished in haste (inner stone; court/entrance mudbrick with wooden lotus columns). Valley temple foundations taken over by Niuserre, forcing entrance and administration up to the mortuary temple—preserving part of the Abusir Papyri; nine or ten service houses.

**Khentkawes II** — small pyramid south of Neferirkare (~25 m square, ~17 m if complete); mother of Raneferef and Niuserre (not the Giza Khentkawes). Uraeus and papyrus sceptre; Verner reads titles allowing rule “as King of Upper and Lower Egypt”; second temple stage with royal E–W axis and her own satellite. Worshipped ~300 years to end of 6th dynasty; temple yielded further papyri.

**Raneferef** — “Unfinished Pyramid”: base laid ~65 m; only one core step faced at ~78° as a low mastaba—“the Mound” in temple papyri. Unfinished state left construction fill and ritual equipment less robbed. Core design (outer retaining wall + inner trench frame + poor limestone/sand fill) refutes simple onion-accretion models for 5th-dynasty cores. First stone chapel quickly after death (~70 days); Niuserre added sprawling mudbrick complex including hypostyle hall and “Sanctuary of the Knife” abattoir (papyri: up to 130 bulls in a 10-day festival).

**Niuserre** — “The Places of Niuserre Endure”: ~78.9 m square, ~51.68 m high, slope ~51°50′35″; reigned >30 years but built smaller than Neferirkare, constrained by the family cluster. Reused Neferirkare’s valley temple/causeway foundations; bent causeway; L-shaped temple; single-pillar square antechamber; massive corner blocks as precursors of later temple pylons. Completed unfinished monuments of father, mother, and brother.

### Sun temples (Abu Ghurob)

Texts name six 5th-dynasty sun temples (one per king except Djedkare-Isesi and Unas); only Userkaf’s Nekhen-Re (“Stronghold of Re,” after [[hierakonpolis]]) and Niuserre’s “Delight of Re” survive archaeologically. Both resemble a mini pyramid complex (valley temple, causeway, upper temple). Userkaf’s went through four phases (central mound → granite obelisk → limestone rebuilds → mudbrick casing, altar, five benches). Palermo Stone: two oxen and two geese daily under Userkaf. Niuserre: limestone obelisk ~36 m on ~20 m pedestal (combined height ≥ his pyramid); alabaster altar readable as “May Re be satisfied”; huge mudbrick boat model; Sed and seasonal reliefs. Abusir Papyri show Neferirkare’s (unlocated) sun temple delivering bread, beer, and meat by canal twice daily to the pyramid—Lehner’s “sacred filter” for goods sustaining the pyramid economy.

### Archives

Three hieratic pyramid archives: Neferirkare (mostly from Djedkare-Isesi’s reign, when South Saqqara still administered Abusir family cults), plus Raneferef and Khentkawes sets. Categories: priestly duty schedules; equipment inventories; estate/product accounts; architectural inspection records—key to the economic function of pyramids.

## Significance

- **Type site of the 5th-dynasty complex:** smaller pyramids, vastly increased decoration, standardized SE satellite, conceptual mortuary-temple plan.
- **Best OK temple-economy documentation:** Abusir Papyri + sun-temple redistribution.
- **Queen as possible pharaoh:** Khentkawes II’s titles, uraeus, and temple form.
- **Construction method laboratory:** Raneferef’s unfinished core and Neferirkare’s step-to-true conversion.
- **Solar theology materialised:** dual sun temples and six named in texts.

## Key Debates

- **Six sun temples vs two found:** rebuild/rename theory vs simultaneous temples destroyed for stone (Sahure sun-temple block reused in Niuserre’s pyramid temple).
- **Khentkawes II as ruling king** (Verner) vs queen mother only.
- **Shepseskare’s unfinished start** between Sahure and Userkaf’s sun temple vs burial in a large mastaba.
- **Borchardt’s “slaughter court” at Niuserre’s sun temple** doubted by Verner (no knives, bones, tethering stones—contrast Raneferef abattoir).
- **Diagonal–Heliopolis convergence** (Verner) as intentional landscape theology.

## Historiography

Abusir’s modern identity is Borchardt’s relief-rich Sahure plus Verner’s Czech long-term project and Posener-Kriéger’s papyrology. Lehner’s 1997 account is the best single-volume cross-cut of architecture, archives, and sun temples; it is not a substitute for the Czech monographs or the full Abusir Papyri editions. Scale reduction versus Giza is a structural theme Lehner returns to repeatedly—Abusir’s slumped cores “form a line in front of the giant pyramids of Giza in the background.”
