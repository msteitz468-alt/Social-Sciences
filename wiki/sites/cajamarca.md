# Cajamarca

Northern highland node of the [[inca-empire]] on the mountain highway between [[huanuco-pampa]] and Tumipampa/Quito, and the decisive conquest locus of November 1532–July 1533. As of [[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]], it is both a standard provincial installation class and the stage where Spanish seizure of Atawallpa opened the collapse of Tawantinsuyu.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Knowledge of the Inca center is disproportionately ethnohistoric: eyewitness writers (Hernando Pizarro, Pedro Sancho, Miguel de Estete, Francisco de Xérez, Cristóbal de Mena, Juan Ruiz de Arce; Pedro Pizarro’s later memoirs) recorded day-by-day events of the capture, ransom, and execution. Among major mountain-highway centers north of [[cuzco]], only [[huanuco-pampa]] had been excavated intensively as of D'Altroy 2002; Cajamarca’s colonial and modern city constrains architectural recovery relative to abandoned planned centers. Road/*tampu* maps after Hyslop place Cajamarca on the arterial highland route of Chinchaysuyu.

## Occupation Sequence

**Imperial provincial role.** In expansion sagas (composite account as D'Altroy frames Ch. 4), early northern campaigns reached Cajamarca, defeated local lords and coastal Chimú allies, and left a garrison; later campaigns used it as a base toward Ecuador and Chachapoyas. By the early sixteenth century it ranked among major planned installations (with Vilcashuaman, Hatun Xauxa, Pumpu, Huánuco Pampa, Tumipampa, Quito). Craft policy included transplanted specialists—e.g. ~100 north-coast potters (as of D'Altroy 2002). Spanish observers found Atawallpa’s army in orderly tent arrays without fortifications; large forces exceeded what *kallanka* halls could shelter.

**Dynastic war and Spanish contact.** After victory over Waskhar’s faction, Atawallpa waited at Cajamarca for both Spaniards and the defeated Waskhar, having heard of bearded men at Tumbes and dispatched Rumiñawi with a force said to include 20,000. On 15 November 1532, 168 Spaniards entered the plaza; de Soto’s party greeted Atawallpa at the hot springs. Protocol vignettes (as of D'Altroy 2002) place him on a low *duho* among ranked wives and lords, with royal insignia in the square. On 16 November, Spaniards used restricted plaza access and multi-doorway halls to hide men, horses, and artillery; Atawallpa entered with high lords and several thousand personal guard; Valverde’s book episode triggered the attack. Up to ~7,000 Andeans may have been killed in ~two hours with no Spanish deaths; Pizarro captured Atawallpa personally. Spanish estimates of the wider army run ~40–80k+ (source-critical caution); the camp included large Chachapoya contingents destined for resettlement.

**Ransom and execution.** Over ~eight months a ransom filled a room ~6.2 × 4.8 m to ~2.5 m with gold and twice that volume with silver (Xérez), drawn largely from *panaqa* assets. Melted totals cited via Hemming: ~13,240 lbs of ~22.5-carat gold and 26,000 lbs silver. Expeditions to [[cuzco]] and Pachacamac fed the forges. On 26 July 1533 Atawallpa was convicted of treason in a hasty trial and garroted after baptism. Pizarro entered Cuzco unopposed on 15 November 1533—one year to the day after entering Cajamarca.

## Significance

Cajamarca is the hinge between Inca provincial logistics and European conquest narrative: a highway center without bastion architecture that became the theater of hostage politics, ransom extraction, and multi-causal Spanish advantage (as of D'Altroy 2002). It links northern military geography of the [[inca-empire]] to the fall of [[cuzco]].

## Key Debates

- **Army scale.** Oral/*khipu* and Spanish figures vs. skepticism on high-end totals; D'Altroy treats scores of thousands as plausible while cautioning top estimates.
- **Mutual deception.** Spanish ambush architecture vs. attributed Inca plans to capture the strangers—both as reported plans, not moral verdicts.
- **Causes of early Spanish success.** Multi-factor coincidence (timing after civil war, alliances, horses/arms, personalized sovereignty, seasonal soldiery); not disease as primary invasion mechanism in D'Altroy’s early-victory framing.
- **Source bias.** Dynastic-war narratives filtered by survivor politics; eyewitness day-records without full hindsight.
