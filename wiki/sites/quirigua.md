# Quirigua

## Discovery and Excavation History

Motagua site controlling jade/obsidian trade corridor. Peabody and Guatemalan projects. [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] narrate its rebellion against Copan.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- Client or subordinate to Copan until K'ahk' Tiliw’s rebellion from AD 725.
- 4 May AD 738: capture and beheading of Copan’s 18 Rabbit — political inversion celebrated on Quirigua monuments.
- Distinctive zoomorph altars and exceptionally tall stelae.

## Significance

Case study in Classic dynastic warfare and secondary-center agency; Motagua economic corridor.

## Key Debates

- Trade control vs dynastic ideology as drivers of the Copan–Quirigua war.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Founding as Copan/Tikal Motagua subsidiary (426)

Sharer & Traxler report Zoomorph P (Looper & Schele) recounts the same founding events as [[copan]] Altar Q: Quirigua founded in **426** by an *ajaw* nicknamed **Tok Casper** under authority of **K’inich Yax K’uk’ Mo’**. Archaeological and historical evidence suggests [[tikal]] orchestrated the 426 Copan takeover **and** establishment of Quirigua as Copan’s subsidiary controlling the Motagua Valley (jadeite, Ixtepeque obsidian, Caribbean gateway). For the next ~300 years Copan controlled Motagua via Quirigua. Early Classic settlement: Group A hilltop and floodplain (Stela U 478; Monument 26 ca. 493); possible founder crypt under early Acropolis east side (jade-inlaid notched teeth like Hunal); founder may have come from Tikal with the overlord. River-capital location type (with Yaxchilan, Piedras Negras, Lamanai). Pyrite mirrors among prestige goods at Quirigua. Cacao plantations inferred from effigies/residues at Copan, Quirigua, Lubaantun among others.

### Independence after 738 and monumental program

K’ak’ Tiliw Chan Yopaat recorded inauguration under supervision (*u kabi*) of Copan’s Waxaklajuun Ub’aah K’awiil — then in **738** captured and sacrificed the 13th Copan ruler; inscriptions also claim capture of Copan’s **patron gods**. Thereafter Quirigua expanded ~a century from Motagua control; **most Main Group construction post-738**; Altar L references Smoke Imix of Copan. Exemplar of warfare ideology: subordinate lord sacrificed overlord and became *k’uhul ajaw*, yet the **Copan polity continued** — victorious kings allowed or encouraged perpetuation of defeated royal houses. Size ≠ power illustration: small capital defeating larger overlord. Structure density well below most Classic sites; central Late Classic population estimates only 1,200–1,600. Public vs private spatial contrast noted (with Lubaantun). *Hotun* (quarter-*k’atun*) period-ending practice shared with Piedras Negras. Creation text: Stela C (Monument 3) describes primordial three-stone hearth at Orion’s belt. Distance numbers: Stela F records a date calculated ~91,683,930 *tuns* (>90 million years) earlier than 9.16.10.0.0; Stela D secondary series ~411,863,930 *tuns* (>400 million years) before 9.16.15.0.0.

### Terminal Classic fate and Motagua corridor

Copan’s weakening triggered by loss of the thirteenth ruler and Quirigua’s breakaway. Trade-economy models list Quirigua with Palenque, Piedras Negras, Yaxchilan, and Copan as Motagua/Usumacinta riverine sites vulnerable when seaborne Yucatan routes rose. Earthquake position (Motagua Fault): may have affected Quirigua and Copan but inhabitants rebuilt — insufficient alone for Classic demise. Terminal Classic arrivals at Copan and Quirigua appear **after** demise of centralized order — more result than cause. Copper bells/ornaments at Quirigua Terminal Classic or Early Postclassic. Cortés’s 1524–25 march passed near the Quirigua vicinity without it being a major living capital visited.

*associated_with:* overlord then rival [[copan]]; Motagua trade; founding under [[tikal]]/Yax K’uk’ Mo’ authority; size-vs-power and warfare-ideology cases.

## Densification from Martin & Grube 2008

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube 2008]], Quiriguá chapter. Complements Coe & Houston and Sharer & Traxler densifications above.)*

### Motagua client of Copán (426–724)

Martin & Grube place [[quirigua|Quiriguá]] on the Motagua floodplain thoroughfare between highlands and Caribbean: for most of its Classic life a modest site under [[copan|Copán]]’s political thumb, then transformed after an 8th-century break into a ceremonial capital out of proportion to its population, with some of the largest Maya monuments. They frame that emergence as both Late Classic organizational vitality and a symptom of decaying long hegemonies.

Official 8th-century history begins in **426**: a lord “arrives” at the “Foundation House” (Mexican-inspired dynastic ritual locale); from Copán texts this is office-taking by **Yax K'uk' Mo'**; three days later Quiriguá’s first king (**Tok Casper**) is crowned under his supervision — Motagua and highland–Caribbean trade inside a Copán hegemony from inception, with “clear echoes” of the Teotihuacan-inspired New Order. Early centuries are thin: Group A hilltop and valley-floor platform 1C-1; Stela U (Group A; wrap-around style like early Tikal stelae) references ~480 dedications under supervision of an ***ochk'in kaloomte'*** — a title Martin & Grube say was reserved for highest rank and, in this region, only carried by Copán rulers. Mid-6th/early-7th-century flood buried Early Classic surfaces; the later river-port core developed on a lesser complex. **Altar L** (652 / 9.11.0.0.0) names local K'awiil Yopaat and mentions Copán’s 12th king **K'ahk' Uti' Witz' K'awiil** in a *tzak huun* event — evidence Quiriguá remained in Copán’s orbit ~226 years after the founder’s overlordship; crude execution shows local ateliers still junior to Copán skill.

### K'ahk' Tiliw: supervised client, 738 revolt, possible Calakmul support

**K'ahk' Tiliw Chan Yopaat** (“Fire-burning Sky Lightning God”; Cauac Sky) acceded **724**, already mature; one of four investiture references states the ceremony took place **“under the supervision”** of Copán’s **Waxaklajuun Ubaah K'awiil** — still a provincial client, like the first Quiriguá king ~300 years earlier. In **738** (14th full year) he captured and **decapitated** that overlord. How the Copán king was taken is unclear: Martin & Grube note Quiriguá accounts do not use a recognized war term and may imply more duplicitous means. Copán sank into a **17-year** monument silence; K'ahk' Tiliw may have imposed temporary authority on his former master — his “14th in the line” title can look like a claim to succeed the 13th Copán king, and “black”-prefixed Copán titles/god names may mark rebellion as legacy-taking.

How a small site overturned southeastern power is obscure, but Martin & Grube (via Matthew Looper) highlight a **736** visit/event — two years before the revolt — linked by an undeciphered term to a **“holy lord of Chiik Nahb”** (a [[calakmul|Calakmul]] ruler). Given the text’s concern with otherwise unmentioned rebellion details, they treat this as non-coincidental: Quiriguá seems to claim **Calakmul support**, conceivably switching to a more agreeable patron. Independence is judged uniquely successful: full Motagua command funded a makeover; urban population “never exceeded a few thousand” versus crowded Copán. Emulation-and-outdoing of Copán: quadrangle masonry, mosaic Sun God masks, longest Maya monumental plaza (~325 m), layout echoing Copán’s core. Giant stelae after 746: **F (761), D (766), E (771)** among the tallest Maya stones — E rising ~10.6 m / ~60 tons — designed to impress as “superman” presence. Programme is functional (capital worthy of its own hegemony), not mere showmanship; client Xkuy’s king “Sun Jaguar” captured **762**. Stela C (with A, **775**) gives the most detailed account anywhere of Maya Creation (13.0.0.0.0) — hard to miss as charter for Quiriguá’s rebirth. Zoomorph programme begins late in the reign (Zoomorph B, **780**). Died **27 July 785** after ~60 years; Zoomorph G records death/burial in exceptional detail.

### Sky Xul, Jade Sky, last date 810, Yucatecan reoccupation

**Sky Xul** (785–~795/800) spurned stelae for zoomorphs: G (jaguar throne homage to predecessor); O and P (790, **795**) in Ballcourt Plaza — P (~20 tons) the sculptural peak. In **795** he memorialized both K'ahk' Tiliw and the vanquished Waxaklajuun Ubaah K'awiil — victory ideology still live amid regional decline. Successor **Jade Sky** (16th; ~10 years; possible name-variant plurality kept as one ruler for convenience): monuments shrink in size and material quality; Stela I (**800**) still recounts the Copán triumph and names Calakmul; energy shifts to Acropolis buildings 1B-5 and **1B-1**. Structure **1B-1** carries Quiriguá’s **last date: 9.19.0.0.0 (June 810)** on façade and benches — describing Copán counterpart **Yax Pasaj Chan Yopaat**’s K'atun-ending dedication. Martin & Grube read this not as proven joint ceremony but as probable late **rapprochement** of rival elites losing control; if so, too late — Quiriguá deserted within a few years, valley sites in severe decline or abandonment.

After short dereliction, **reoccupation** by a new settler group: substantial Acropolis additions; glossy **plumbate** ware and a reclining **chacmool** point to distant, probably **Yucatecan** origins. No inscriptions; Martin & Grube treat them as a merchant elite exploiting the vacuum for Motagua trade. The river remained a Postclassic artery, later likely controlled from Nito on the Dulce.

*associated_with:* Copán client then invertor; possible Calakmul patronage of 738; Motagua wealth funding giant stelae/zoomorphs; Terminal Classic elite rapprochement (810); Yucatecan merchant reoccupation after sacred-king desertion.

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel, *A Forest of Kings*, 1990]], Ch. 8 (Copan chapter’s Quirigua nodes) and related notes. 1990 nicknames retained with later-standard equivalents. Compare Martin & Grube 2008 densification above for fuller Quirigua-side monumental program and possible Calakmul support of 738.)*

### Motagua client under Copan hegemony

Schele & Freidel cast Quirigua as straddling the rich Motagua trade routes and, under **Smoke-Imix-God K** of Copan, as a subordinate neighbor. On period ending **9.11.0.0.0 (AD 652)** Smoke-Imix celebrated preeminence by erecting **Altar L** at Quirigua. Notes (Stuart 1987a reading as of their apparatus): Altar L rim names Smoke-Imix-God K; interior *ta yuc* “join/unite” ~231 days after the period ending—hegemonic incorporation. Later Quirigua kings claim “Black Copan Ahau” and Yax-Kuk-Mo’ as founder—legacy-taking from the overlord dynasty. For ~three centuries (from founding package through the mid-eighth century) Quirigua lay inside Copan’s southeastern hegemony.

### Cauac-Sky installed by 18-Rabbit, then revolt

Mid-career of Copan’s **18-Rabbit** (Waxaklajuun Ub’aah K’awiil), a new ruler named **Cauac-Sky** (K’ahk’ Tiliw Chan Yopaat) was installed at Quirigua in a “God K-in-hand” event on **2 January AD 725**, recorded as taking place *u cab* “in the land of (in the territory of)” 18-Rabbit of Copan—still a provincial client, like the first Quirigua king ~300 years earlier under Yax-Kuk-Mo’ (as Martin & Grube also stress; S&F share the supervised-accession frame).

Thirteen years later Cauac-Sky **turned on his liege**: attacked, took 18-Rabbit captive in battle, and **sacrificed him at Quirigua on 3 May 738** (9.15.6.14.6 6 Cimi 4 Zec; text “he was sacrificed 18-Rabbit-God K”). Historiography of the reading (notes): Morley noted the date’s importance; Kelley once thought Copan conquered Quirigua; **Proskouriakoff** then **Marcus** correctly identified Cauac-Sky as captor—Quirigua’s independence battle. The “ax” verb with T757 may be battle or *ch’ak* “decapitate” (Grube p.c. 1989 as cited). Ballcourt dedication context may place 18-Rabbit’s capture 113 days after Ballcourt III dedication—Schele & Freidel float that he may have been seized while seeking sacrificial victims for the new ballcourt. “Ironically, 18-Rabbit himself may have been dispatched by exactly this means.”

### Independence without empire over Copan

Schele & Freidel stress the asymmetry of outcome: Quirigua achieved independence and Motagua control in a “spectacular battle,” yet archaeologists found **no evidence Quirigua dominated Copan**—Copan population continued to burgeon, lords pursued architecture, merchants traded with Honduras (“business as usual”). The political disaster was to Copan’s divine-king prestige (seventeen-year monument silence; ~twenty years to recover royal performance), not a territorial conquest of the Copan pocket. Later Quirigua monumental florescence (giant stelae, zoomorphs) is the counterpart of that independence, funded by Motagua command—developed more fully in Martin & Grube densification above.

### Late rapprochement node (810)

Schele & Freidel report Copan’s last major king **Yax-Pac** (Yax Pasaj Chan Yopaat) paid a state visit to old rival Quirigua to perform a scattering rite on **9.19.0.0.0 (28 June 810)**—unusual because kings rarely traveled and scattering was usually performed at the homesite; no known parallel scattering at Copan though he still ruled there. Framed as late Copan–Quirigua elite contact amid regional decline (Martin & Grube read Structure 1B-1’s 810 date as possible rapprochement).
