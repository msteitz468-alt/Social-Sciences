## Discovery and Excavation History

Trinchera (Gran) Dolina at Atapuerca, Spain, is described by Klein as the oldest
unequivocal hominid occupation site in Europe (Klein, [[klein-human-career-1999]]).
The excavators and excavation years are not specified in the source consulted for this
page; see [[unknown]]. A separate locality at the same Atapuerca complex, the Sima de
los Huesos ("Pit of Bones"), has produced a large pre-Neanderthal fossil sample assigned
by Klein to [[homo-heidelbergensis]] and treated as evidence for an indigenous European
evolutionary lineage leading to the [[neanderthals]] — Klein argues Neanderthal
morphology descends from mid-Quaternary Europeans represented at Atapuerca, with
distinctive Neanderthal features possibly owing more to genetic drift than to natural
selection (Klein 1999). Sima de los Huesos itself is treated here as a related but
distinct locality within the Atapuerca complex, not as part of the Gran Dolina sequence.

## Occupation Sequence

[[paleomagnetic-dating]] places Gran Dolina's artifacts and fossils below the
Brunhes/Matuyama polarity boundary (conventionally dated to ~780 ka), which combined
with biostratigraphic correlation places the site at approximately 800 ka (as of Klein
1999) — making it, in Klein's account, the earliest secure evidence of hominin
occupation in Europe.

## Significance

Gran Dolina is significant as a chronological anchor for the peopling of Europe,
predating the well-known Acheulean sites of France, England, and Italy by several
hundred thousand years. The site's stone-tool assemblage (level TD6) notably lacks hand
axes at a time when the [[acheulean-industry]] was otherwise established in Africa,
which Klein flags as part of an unresolved puzzle about the patchy distribution and
eventual disappearance of hand-axe technology. Level TD6 has also yielded cut-marked
hominid bones that have been proposed — tentatively and contested — as possibly the
oldest case of cannibalism on record, alongside comparable but separately dated evidence
at Bodo, Arago, and Ngandong.

## Key Debates

Whether the TD6 cut marks represent cannibalism or another taphonomic process is
unresolved and explicitly flagged by Klein as tentative. The absence of hand axes at
TD6 despite the site's early Acheulean-era date feeds into a broader, unresolved
question about the reasons for regional and temporal patchiness in Acheulean tool
technology. The relationship between the Gran Dolina hominins, the Sima de los Huesos
sample, and [[homo-heidelbergensis]] as a taxon is itself contested — Klein notes the
concept of H. heidelbergensis "remains" disputed, including whether it should
encompass certain East Asian fossils.

## Gamble 1999 layer — early occupation claims (as of 1999)

*Appended from [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999|Clive Gamble, *The Palaeolithic Societies of Europe*]] (1999). Complements the Klein 1999 layer above; does not replace it. All claims date-stamped (as of Gamble 1999).*

### Long vs short chronology

Gamble records two views on first European occupation: a **long chronology** (occupation before the Brunhes/Matuyama boundary; some claims >1.5 Myr BP; long-chronology tables include Vallonnet, Monte Poggiolo, Prezletice, Korolevo, **Atapuerca TD6**, etc.) versus a **short chronology** (earliest undisputed occupation around Cromer IV / OIS 13, ~528–478 ka BP). Gamble supports the short chronology (attributed position). Regardless of preference, he presents (after Roebroeks & van Kolfschoten 1994) a sharp transformation of the European record after ~500 ka BP—from contested small “geofact” pebble collections without human remains to large excavated collections, knapping floors with refits, uncontested industries, and common human remains.

### Palaeomagnetic revision and biostratigraphic tension

Gamble notes that revised palaeomagnetic work (Carbonell et al. 1995; Parés & Pérez-González 1995) nearly **doubled** claimed ages for earliest Atapuerca artifacts and human remains relative to earlier OIS 13 correlations, creating unresolved tension with European vole [[biostratigraphy]]—“special pleading is needed to accommodate the new dates for Atapuerca within the European sequence of vole evolution” (Gamble 1999, attributed). TD6 figures in discussions of the Mimomys–Arvicola transition (“Mimomys extinction” in TD6). Dennell & Roebroeks (1996 via Gamble) modify the colonization picture: older Atapuerca (and possibly Fuente Nueva 3, Orce) dates suggest the northern rim of the Mediterranean may have been occupied earlier than Europe north of the Pyrenees and Alps; Casablanca well-stratified tools ~800 ka BP are cited as comparable to Iberian claims.

### TD6 Aurora and Sima de los Huesos (sample sizes as of Gamble 1999)

Cave gatherings summary: **TD6 Aurora stratum** — 36 hominid fragments associated with technologically simple stone tools. **Sima de los Huesos** (related Atapuerca locality, not the Gran Dolina sequence) — remains of ≥32 individuals from >1,000 specimens, maximum age ~35, few infants; **no artifacts**; catastrophe vs intentional corpse placement still debated (Gamble 1999, attributed). Atapuerca also appears among the rare cave sequences of the 500–300 ka recovery environments (with Arago, Orgnac III, Grotte Vaufrey XII–XI), while most flagship primary-context locales of that window are open lakeside/riverine ([[boxgrove|Boxgrove]], [[bilzingsleben|Bilzingsleben]], etc.).

### Relation to Klein layer

Klein (as already summarized above) treats Gran Dolina as the oldest unequivocal European occupation (~800 ka on palaeomagnetism + biostratigraphy as of Klein 1999). Gamble (1999) places the same site at the centre of a **live dating dispute** between doubled palaeomagnetic ages and vole biostratigraphy, and prefers a short-chronology reading of “undisputed” occupation for Europe north of the Mediterranean rim. Do not merge the two authors into a single wiki-voice date; treat as attributed layers.
