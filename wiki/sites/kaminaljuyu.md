# Kaminaljuyu

## Discovery and Excavation History

Highland capital under modern Guatemala City — severe urban destruction of mounds. Kidder, Jennings, Shook and later projects. [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] treat it as highland counterpart to lowland Preclassic florescence and as Esperanza-period Teotihuacan contact case.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- Middle Preclassic Las Charcas culture; Naranjo ceremonial center nearby; early deity imagery.
- Late Preclassic scale: majority of mounds Preclassic; elite tombs; irrigation; literacy/sculpture.
- Decline before Classic peak of lowlands.
- **Esperanza phase:** Teotihuacan-style talud-tablero tombs and ceramics — older “intrusive Mexican” readings vs isotope evidence for mostly local elites (Wright) — parallel to Tikal debate.

## Significance

Highland Preclassic urbanism; Teotihuacan–Maya interaction laboratory; cautionary case for site destruction under modern cities.

## Key Debates

- Esperanza ethnicity (local vs central Mexican).
- Correlation of highland and lowland Preclassic chronologies.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Research history, scale, and Middle Preclassic capital

Largest Maya highland site, largely destroyed by Guatemala City expansion; Maudslay mapped 200+ mounds (1880s); Gamio 1925 Preclassic deposits; Carnegie Kidder/Ricketson/Shook — Mound E-III-3 Late Preclassic tombs showing Preclassic apogee; first identification of Maya–[[teotihuacan]] Early Classic contact (talud-tablero mounds; tombs of Early Classic rulers with sacrificed retainers). On Valley of Guatemala plateau ~1500 m, around Lake Miraflores, once >5 km². Occupation from Early Preclassic ca. 1200–1000 BC; ~half monumental constructions Middle–Late Preclassic; dramatic growth by ~600 BC supported by first of enormous **irrigation canals** from Lake Miraflores to southern fields (elite-directed corvée); emerged as capital of a large prosperous highland polity. Dominated southern highlands early among successive major Maya centers. Controlled **El Chayal** obsidian quarry (~20 km NE) and highland minerals (jade, mica, hematite, cinnabar).

### Late Preclassic apogee — tombs, texts, southern network

As of Sharer & Traxler 2006: largest and most powerful highland city; capital of the most powerful Late Preclassic highland polity — measured by size, constructions, carved monuments with hieroglyphic texts (e.g. **Stela 10** throne fragment with one of the longest Preclassic Maya texts; mat motif emblematic of Maya kings), external trade, and ruler wealth. **Monument 65** (somewhat earlier) depicts succession of three Preclassic rulers. E-III-3 tombs: Tomb 2 held a tall elite male with jade mask and headdress, three unadorned companions (two children, young adult) face-down; hundreds of pottery vessels, carved jade, stone vessels, incense burners, and stingray-spine bloodletters — blood sacrifice by Preclassic rulers as in later times. Mushroom stones from Late Preclassic tombs (divination/altered consciousness complex). Potbellied monuments at Kaminaljuyu and Pacific sites. Providencia/Miraflores highland ceramic tradition pairs with lowland Chicanel. Strong pottery links to Chalchuapa (likely ally; Ixtepeque obsidian and Pacific trade). Highland–lowland interaction via Verapaz corridor; Tikal North Acropolis Str. 5D-Sub-10 murals related to southern Maya art style at Kaminaljuyu. Authors treat Late Preclassic southern elite culture (writing, zero-date notation, sculpture) as **directly ancestral** to Classic lowland civilization. First Maya states as early as Late Preclassic at [[el-mirador]] and Kaminaljuyu — dual dominant polities (obsidian/jade south; agricultural intensification and Yucatán portage in Mirador Basin).

### Terminal Preclassic decline and Early Classic Teotihuacan partnership

Terminal Preclassic: most profound decline in southern Maya area ca. AD 100–300; cessation of carved stelae with hieroglyphic texts in highlands and Pacific plain. Proposed causes include takeover by expanding western highland populations (ceramic distributions; end of carved monuments) and multi-causal trade/volcanic disruption (Ilopango debate dates vary). Kaminaljuyu declined ca. AD 200–400 (curtailed southern trade; Lake Miraflores dried, irrigation lost); new rulers ended carved texts and royal monuments. Commercial Teotihuacan ties on coast and at [[tikal]] by 200–400, but Kaminaljuyu ties to Central Mexico only after ca. AD 400; thereafter **revival** with jade, quetzal feathers, El Chayal obsidian exported and prestige goods imported; talud-tablero adapted **locally**; green obsidian and stuccoed vessels from Central Mexico — probably mediated by Teotihuacan trade colony at Montana on the Pacific coast. **Isotopes:** Early Classic tomb elites born and raised in Maya highland region, not Central Mexico; sacrificed retainers from more distant lowland Maya regions — serious doubt on Teotihuacan takeover (parallels Tikal local-elite isotope findings). Possible homeland candidate for Siyaj K’ak’ in 378 *entrada* debate (with Teotihuacan). Early Classic Copan Hunal NAA vessels include Kaminaljuyu-region sources. Postclassic: abandoned after ~2,000 years continuous occupation among early Postclassic highland abandonments and shift to defensible hilltops.
