# Huánuco Pampa

Grandest Inca provincial center preserved outside the heartland (as of [[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]]), at ~3,800 m on the highland road north of [[cuzco]]. Guaman Poma listed it among the “New Cuzcos”; Cieza said it had charge of several adjoining provinces. It is the type case of Morris’s “artificial urbanism” and of ethnohistory–archaeology integration for the [[inca-empire]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of D'Altroy 2002, the Huánuco Project (Murra, Thompson, Morris) was the first major project to systematically integrate historical and archaeological research in a regional study, advantaged by the spectacular center and the 1549/1562 visitas; D'Altroy treats it as still the standard for provincial research and returns to it throughout *The Incas*. Among exposed mountain centers on the main highway (Vilcashuaman, Hatun Xauxa, Pumpu, Huánuco Pampa, [[cajamarca]], Tumipampa, Quito), only Huánuco Pampa had been excavated intensively as of that synthesis (Morris and Thompson 1985).

## Occupation Sequence

Built on virgin terrain over ~2 sq km with more than 4,000 buildings. Pathways radiate from an immense plaza (550 × 350 m, ~19 ha) partitionable into 2/4/8/12 parts; Morris (1990) suggests the design may have mirrored administered ethnic relationships, analogous to Cuzco’s ethnic microcosm. An elegant stepped *ushnu* platform (base 32 × 48 m) staged state ceremony; portals align east–west from a cut-stone bath to the platform. Forms include *kancha*, *kallanka*, temples, and sequestered *aqllakuna* sectors—Gasparini and Margolies’s “architecture of power,” ceremonial rather than office-building oriented. The site could house up to ~15,000, but permanent population was likely a fraction; many came weeks at a time for *mit'a* or ceremony. A north-sector compound (single narrow doorway, ~50 buildings) yielded weaving tools and *chicha* jars consistent with permanent *aqllakuna*; royal ashlar residences east were rarely occupied. About 700 storehouses (~30,000 m³) line southern slopes. Provincial Cuzco polychrome formed >98% of ceramics at the center (Morris, as of D'Altroy 2002), mainly for state feasting, storage, and elite contexts. No fortifications—logistical and ceremonial nodes, not bastions (cf. tented armies at [[cajamarca]]). Huánuco administered multiple ethnic groups (Julien 1993); large Chupachu fractions were in [[cuzco]], out-migration, army service, or specialized communities, and late Waskhar claimed the populace as personal estate. Monumental roads near the center contrast with little Inca material in the countryside; only Ichu (home of the highest-ranking ethnic lord) shows substantial Inca-style pottery and architecture.

## Significance

Huánuco Pampa anchors models of Inca provincial rule: temporary populations, storage and hospitality politics, *mit'a* rotation documented in visitas, and the disjunction between a monumental core and thin rural imprint. Methodologically it warns that tabulated labor only indirectly maps to visible remains—only ~1/9 of Chupachu taxpayers did work producing architecture or pottery, while all 400 road/building workers listed were sent to Cuzco despite the elaborate local center (as of D'Altroy 2002). It remains the comparative baseline for other highway centers and for heartland estates such as [[machu-picchu]] and [[ollantaytambo]].

## Key Debates

- **Artificial urbanism.** Morris’s claim that such centers lacked independent craft/market life and collapsed after Inca fall vs. degrees of local embedding.
- **Plaza partitions and dual/multi-ethnic design.** Whether radiating pathways encode administered ethnic relations (Morris) or primarily ceremonial geometry.
- **Storage contents.** Chroniclers emphasize maize and *ch'arki*; Morris’s excavations found mostly local potatoes—culture-of-value vs. practice (as of D'Altroy 2002).
- **Labor lists vs. archaeology.** How to read visita duty tallies against the built landscape.
