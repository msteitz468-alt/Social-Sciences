# Shortugai

## Discovery and Excavation History

An Indus-affiliated settlement near the confluence of the Kokcha and Oxus
rivers in northeastern Afghanistan, roughly 500 km from the nearest other Indus
sites — by far the most distant known Indus outpost. Excavated by
Henri-Paul Francfort's French mission (Francfort et al. 1989). Documented here
from [[wright-ancient-indus-2010|Wright (2010)]]; Wright herself was one of four
archaeologists who surveyed the Kokcha Valley in 1975 seeking third-millennium
Indus-related lapis-trade sites, shortly before Shortugai's discovery.

## Occupation Sequence

About 2.5 hectares. The site sits near the world's largest lapis lazuli source
(Badakhshan, Kokcha Valley). Inhabitants farmed, herded, and produced crafts,
some as full-time specialists; a monumental building held shell bracelets and
beads of unknown function. In its final phase Indus material culture was
replaced by [[bactria-margiana-archaeological-complex|BMAC]] material, around
2200 BCE (as of Wright 2010, following Francfort 1989).

## Significance

Wright argues (attributed) that Shortugai was **founded by Indus people** as a
lapis-exploitation and trade outpost, on four lines of evidence: (1) a
cardinal-oriented gridlike layout matching Indus landscape planning; (2)
locally-made ceramics stylistically and technically similar to Indus wares (not
imported — Francfort et al. 1989:236); (3) a lapis workshop using the
"grooving and splitting" technique typical of Mehrgarh lapis-working; and (4)
proximity to gold/silver/copper/tin sources and overland routes. She reads it
as a "budding off" of individuals from an Indus centre — a frontier resource
outpost, not part of a unified colonising program. Lapis was shipped back as
raw lumps to Lower Indus sites such as Ghazi Shah and [[nausharo|Nausharo]].
The site anchors the [[middle-asian-interaction-sphere|Middle Asian Interaction
Sphere]] and the Indus lapis trade.

## Key Debates

The Indus-founding interpretation is Wright's attributed reading of Francfort's
evidence, not an independently established fact. Its shift to BMAC material in
the early second millennium bears on the [[indus-transformation-debate|decline
and transformation]] discussion.
