## Discovery and Excavation History

Rakhigarhi sits in Hisar District, Haryana, on the right (northern) bank of the Drishadvati River, a tributary of the Sarasvati (Ghaggar-Hakra). Per Possehl (2002), the site was discovered by Suraj Bhan in 1964. It reaches approximately 17 m in height and is divided into five mounds (RGR-1 through RGR-5), with a separate satellite site, RGR-6 ("Arda"), associated with the Sothi-Siswal (Early Harappan) occupation. Possehl's site-size figure (~80 ha) derives from his own site visit rather than a formal survey campaign; the excavation methods and full campaign history are not detailed in this chapter, so excavation-quality assessment beyond Possehl's own observations cannot yet be made here.

## Occupation Sequence

The sequence runs Sothi-Siswal (Early Harappan) into Mature Harappan. Possehl reports that the Early Harappan levels show unusually early use of baked brick — atypical for Early Harappan occupations elsewhere in the civilization's range — with a brick ratio of 1:2:3, distinct from the standard Mature Harappan 4:2:1 (or 1:2:4) ratio seen at sites such as [[dholavira]]. Calibration status for any radiocarbon dates underlying this sequence is not stated in the source.

## Significance

At roughly 80 ha, Rakhigarhi is among Possehl's nine sites of ≥50 ha (Table 3.1), alongside [[harappa]], [[mohenjo-daro]], [[ganweriwala]], and [[dholavira]]. Its Early Harappan levels preserve graffiti marks resembling signs of the later [[indus-script]] — a possible harbinger of the writing system, per Possehl's attributed reading, though not a demonstrated developmental link.

Mature Harappan features include a substantial mud-brick wall on the "acropolis" mound (RGR-2), possibly a fortification, though this functional identification is attributed rather than confirmed; public and household baked-brick drains; "fire altars" that parallel structures at [[kalibangan]]; unicorn seals; and a faience cylinder seal bearing a gavial (Indus crocodile) motif together with script. A cemetery yielded eight new Mature Harappan interments, including brick-lined pits and one wooden coffin, paralleling Cemetery R-37 at [[harappa]]. Possehl flags this cemetery as exceptionally significant given the general scarcity of well-documented Mature Harappan human remains across the civilization.

## Key Debates

The unusually early baked-brick use and script-like graffiti in Rakhigarhi's Early Harappan levels bear on questions of script origins and the pace of the Early-to-Mature Harappan transition — relevant to the "continuity beneath discontinuity" theme Possehl develops for the [[harappan-civilization]] page, where Kalibangan, Rakhigarhi, and [[kot-diji]] all show Early Harappan occupation preceding the Mature Harappan florescence. The "acropolis" wall's possible fortification function, like similar structures elsewhere in the civilization, feeds into the broader [[indus-polity-debate]] over the presence or absence of centralized defensive/administrative architecture.
