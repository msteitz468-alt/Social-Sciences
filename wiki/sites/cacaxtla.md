# Cacaxtla

Epiclassic hilltop palace site on the Puebla-Tlaxcala border famous for Maya-style murals painted for a non-Maya, central Mexican audience. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], the site is described by the early chronicler Diego Muñoz Camargo as a "seat and fortress" of the **Olmeca-Xicallanca**, whose capital was then [[sites/cholula|Cholula]]. "Olmeca" here means "people of the region of rubber" (southern Gulf Coast) — distinct from the archaeological Olmecs of [[cultures/olmec-culture]] — while "Xicallanca" refers to Xicallanco, a Putún (Mayan-speaking, seafaring merchant) trading town in southern Campeche; Sir Eric Thompson called the Putún "the Phoenicians of the New World."

## Discovery and Excavation History

The Cacaxtla murals were discovered by looters in November 1974; the subsequent official excavation revealed a palace complex (7th–8th centuries AD) with pilastered rooms around patios and plazas, flat-roofed (non-Maya) architecture with similarities to the coeval palaces at [[sites/xochicalco]] and [[sites/el-tajin]].

## Occupation Sequence

Murals appear on Building A (the "Building of the Paintings"), the talud of Building B, the subterranean Red Temple, and the Venus Temple. The North and South Murals depict Maya-affiliated dignitaries — a jaguar-costumed figure (North) and an eagle-costumed figure (South) — each holding a Maya "ceremonial bar." Building B's talud depicts a **battle** between victorious jaguar-clad (central Mexican) warriors and defeated bird/eagle-costumed (Maya-profiled, Olmec-heirloom-costumed) warriors, including a graphic wounded, disemboweled figure; art historian Mary Miller believes (attributed) an actual battle, perhaps in southwestern Campeche, underlies the scene, recast in supernatural terms. The Red Temple stairway mural depicts the Maya merchant deity **God L** (given the non-Maya name "4 Dog"), his carrying-frame merchandise pack, a toad-symbol of the Maya 20-day uinal, a cacao tree, and maize plants with Maya Maize God heads bearing distinctive red hair; "Cacaxtla" itself means "Place of the Carrying-frame."

Coe & Koontz describe the murals as "thoroughly Maya in their style" (naturalistic proportions, organic profiles), contrasting with the blocky Teotihuacan-highland style; art historian Diana Magaloni identified a Maya/Gulf Coast vegetable-binder paint technique (as opposed to central Mexican true fresco) used at the site. The accompanying script is a non-Maya hybrid of Teotihuacan and Oaxacan writing systems used mainly for names — "the main audience was not Maya lords, but central Mexican elites." Given limited non-artistic evidence of a "larger Maya presence," Coe & Koontz suggest (attributed) these traits were imported via the intermediate Olmeca-Xicallanca from their Gulf Coast homeland adjacent to the Maya area.

Nearby Xochitecatl, occupied earlier in the Preclassic, was reoccupied in the Epiclassic; its major pyramid is aligned to the Cacaxtla palace, suggesting political control by Cacaxtla elites. A large offering there of female figurines (raised hands, filed teeth, smiling) shares traits with southern Gulf Coast figures (e.g., El Zapotal); archaeologist Mari Carmen Serra identified ritual/political offices associated with these figures.

## Significance

Cacaxtla is a principal case, alongside [[sites/xochicalco]], for the Epiclassic penetration of Maya stylistic and iconographic elements into central Mexico following Teotihuacan's decline.

## Key Debates

- **[POSITION]** Whether the Olmeca-Xicallanca can be securely identified archaeologically in their claimed Gulf Coast/Tabasco homeland is unresolved — Coe & Koontz state "we cannot securely identify their culture in southern Veracruz and Tabasco... their role at Cacaxtla and elsewhere... will remain subject to conjecture."
