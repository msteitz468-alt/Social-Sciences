# Cuicatlán Cañada

## Discovery and Excavation History
The hot, narrow canyon of the Río Grande/Salado linking the Valley of Oaxaca to the Tehuacán Valley, surveyed and excavated by Elsa Redmond and Charles Spencer (1977–1978), including the La Coyotera locality.

## Occupation Sequence
Local Formative villages (Cuicatec-area populations) followed by a marked Period II reorganization.

## Significance
In [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983), Redmond and Spencer argued the Cañada was conquered and administered as a militarized frontier of the [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] II Zapotec state — burned villages, settlement relocation, a tzompantli of local skulls, and fortifications — among the strongest archaeological cases for early Zapotec territorial expansion ([[state-formation]]; cf. the Building J conquest-slab readings on [[zapotec-writing]]).

## Key Debates
Whether Building J's place glyphs name the Cañada's conquered towns is a Marcus hypothesis, attributed not established.

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996)

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996, Chs. 14–15; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]]; drawing on Redmond & Spencer 1977–78 fieldwork.)*

**Setting and Building J claim.** Tropical N–S canyon between Tehuacán and Oaxaca valleys; floor **500–700 m** (vs Oaxaca ~1500–1700 m); cotton, coyol palm (*Acrocomia mexicana*), and tropical fruits requiring irrigation in a rain shadow. Spanish “Cuicatlán” = “Place of Song”; sixteenth-century place glyph (head with feathered song scroll) is similar to a Building J conquest slab with **upside-down head** — read as a conquest claim. Cuicatlán is among four Building J place-names they match to Aztec tribute-document glyphs.

**Quiotepec fortress.** Pre–Monte Albán I pottery follows different canons; by ~100 BC pottery shows close affinity to Monte Albán II. The **Fortress of Quiotepec** at the natural pass sealing the Cañada from Tehuacán: Zapotec-style pottery and noble tombs, defensive walls, large public buildings, plaza controlling a river ford. North of the fort: a **~7 km no-man’s-land** (200 BC–AD 200); first major site beyond has Tehuacán, not Monte Albán, pottery. Marcus & Flannery treat Quiotepec as the **northernmost Monte Albán II expansion** — strategic control of the Oaxaca–Central Mexico route and a block against southward expansion by rivals in Tehuacán, Puebla, Tlaxcala, and the Basin of Mexico (built as much to discourage northern rivals as to hold the Cañada). By Monte Albán IIIa the fortress was **eventually given up** as outer provinces were lost.

**La Coyotera: canals, tribute crops, skull rack.** La Coyotera (700–300 BC) was a 2.5-ha alluvial-terrace village; with Zapotec arrival in Ic/II, it and many communities moved to the piedmont for intensified irrigation (**large aqueducts/canals**). Carbonized plants show higher tropical-crop density — Redmond & Spencer interpret extra land for tropical fruits/nuts possibly as Zapotec tribute demand. Piedmont La Coyotera ~3 ha with public buildings; in front of the largest pyramid, **64 human skulls in rows** interpreted as a collapsed skull rack (Zapotec *yagabetoo*; later Aztec *tzompantli*) to discourage tribute refusal. Marcus & Flannery present the Cañada as their **best fit** between Building J inverted-head claims and excavation (fortress + skull rack), while noting the luck required to recover battle evidence even in forcibly subdued regions.

**Later irony.** In the action-theory epilogue they note Monte Albán’s self-interested development of provincial canal/aqueduct systems later gave subjugated regions skills that strengthened them toward autonomy. Teotihuacan may have prevented Zapotec expansion **north of** the Cañada in the Classic.
