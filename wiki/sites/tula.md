# Tula

Tula (Nahuatl "Tollan," "Place of the Rushes"), in Hidalgo state roughly 50 miles (80
km) northwest of Mexico City, is the archaeologically confirmed capital of the
[[cultures/toltec-culture|Toltec]] state, occupied through the Classic, Epiclassic, and
Post-Classic periods and reaching its monumental florescence in the Tollan phase of the
Early Post-Classic.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Multiple places in Mexico bear the name "Tula" because *Tollan* could mean either
"Place of the Rushes" or, generically, "civilized urban space" — a title applied even to
Teotihuacan and Cholula. This ambiguity produced past scholarly confusion; the
archaeologist George Vaillant mistakenly equated the Toltec capital with Teotihuacan
(Coe & Koontz 2013). In the late 1930s the Mexican ethnohistorian Wigberto Jiménez
Moreno used documentary research to establish that the historical Toltec Tollan was
the Tula located in Hidalgo state; subsequent excavation confirmed the identification.

The first serious archaeological work at the site was conducted in the 1880s by the
French Mesoamericanist Désiré Charnay, who excavated a palace and first noted stylistic
ties between Tula and [[sites/chichen-itza]]. Intensive excavation began in 1940 under
Jorge Acosta of Mexico's National Institute of Anthropology and History (INAH) and
continued roughly twenty years; later work was carried out by Eduardo Matos Moctezuma,
Richard Diehl, Alba Guadalupe Mastache, and Robert Cobean. Diehl's University of
Missouri project documented Toltec domestic architecture in detail.

## Occupation Sequence

Small Preclassic villages preceded a more substantial Classic-period settlement
contemporary with Teotihuacan's apogee, associated with an irrigation system. During
the Epiclassic a new population — possibly the Tolteca-Chichimeca themselves — arrived
from the Bajío region (Guanajuato/Querétaro) bringing Coyotlatelco pottery. By around
AD 900 (per Coe & Koontz, possibly a century earlier) a sizeable artisan community
existed with a civic-religious precinct called Tula Chico; its main obsidian source at
this stage was Ucareo, Michoacán (per Dan Healan).

The Tollan phase, traditionally dated AD 950–1150, marks Tula's major occupation and
the construction of the monumental Tula Grande precinct; Coe & Koontz report that most
absolute dates actually cluster AD 900–1000, suggesting the city's apogee may be
somewhat earlier than traditionally thought (per revised dating of Basin of Mexico
ceramics and Tohil Plumbate trade ware). At its height Tula covered 5.4 sq. miles (16
sq. km) with an estimated population of 30,000–40,000, among the largest Early
Post-Classic Mesoamerican cities. As part of a major reorganization the city's
orientation was shifted from true north to 17° east of north (paralleling Teotihuacan's
orientation), and Tula Chico was abandoned in favor of Tula Grande.

Tula Grande's central plaza includes Pyramid C (the largest structure, unexcavated), an
unexplored ball court, Pyramid B and its annexes, and a second I-shaped ball court
(roughly 120 ft/37 m long) modeled on the Xochicalco prototype. Pyramid B (about 33
ft/10 m tall, five tiers, built in six construction stages) carried in its final form a
colonnaded hall with polychrome warrior bas-reliefs, an entrance flanked by Feathered
Serpent columns, and a temple with four colossal atlantean warrior figures (15 ft/4.6
m) that supported the roof, alongside carved pillars possibly depicting Quetzalcoatl and
Tezcatlipoca. Reclining "chacmool" sculptures bearing receptacles interpreted as vessels
for human hearts were found in the temple precinct, and bas-reliefs around the pyramid
depict warrior-order animals (jaguars, coyotes, eagles eating hearts) and composite
creatures possibly descended from Teotihuacan's Feathered Serpent imagery.

Adjacent to Pyramid B, the Palacio Quemado ("Burnt Palace") comprises spacious
colonnaded halls with sunken courts and frescoed walls, interpreted as meeting or
ceremonial space rather than residence; two separate floor plans resembling Teotihuacan
palaces, found away from the site center, are interpreted as actual ruler residences. A
131-ft (40 m) "Serpent Wall" north of the pyramid depicts a serpent devouring a human
figure shown partially skeletonized. A patio in the Palacio Quemado yielded a shell
breastplate and a turquoise-and-shell *tezcacuitlapilli* back-mirror (over 3,000 pieces
of turquoise and shell) buried with Gulf-of-Mexico and Pacific shells, evidencing
far-flung trade reaching Southwestern turquoise sources.

The most common foreign trade pottery at Tula was Tohil Plumbate ware — one of the few
true glazed pre-Columbian ceramics, produced near the Mexico-Guatemala Pacific coastal
border — with Papagayo Polychrome ware (made from eastern Honduras to northwestern
Costa Rica) also present in storage caches. Richard Diehl estimates that over 40 percent
of Tula's inhabitants were engaged in obsidian core, blade, and point production, on a
scale comparable to Teotihuacan, drawing on the Pachuca green obsidian mines the
Toltecs controlled during the Tollan phase. Local domestic ceramics included Mazapan
red-on-buff ware and moldmade figurines; no metal objects (copper or gold) have yet
been recovered at Tula, though yellow paint on stone ornaments may signal an intended
gold association — Coe & Koontz caution the absence may reflect the lack of excavated
elite tombs rather than a true absence.

Representations of deities are comparatively rare at Tula, which Coe and Koontz
describe as a "secular cast" to the site's iconographic program, possibly reflecting
militarization of Toltec ideology or simply Aztec-era looting; recognizable deities
include Centeotl (Maize Goddess), Xochiquetzal (Goddess of Love), and
Tlahuizcalpantecuhtli (Quetzalcoatl as Morning Star). Tezcatlipoca is notably absent
from the material record despite his narrative "victory" in the Toltec annals (see
[[cultures/toltec-culture]]). A tzompantli (skull-rack altar) base with human skull
fragments was found east of Ball Court II.

Diehl's excavations documented Toltec domestic architecture: flat-roofed house
complexes of up to five houses around courtyards with small altars, Tlaloc-face
braziers, and wheeled animal figurines (possibly toys or household-cult objects, per
Diehl); burials were typically placed under house floors, while sacrificial victims
were discarded in abandoned rooms after apparent cannibalization. The settlement was
organized on a grid of household wards roughly 1,970 ft (600 m) per side. Tula sits on
the agricultural margin of Mesoamerica, with insufficient summer rainfall and winter
frosts; food production depended on small-canal irrigation from check dams.

## Significance

Tula anchors the archaeological identification of the Toltec state described in
post-Conquest Nahuatl annals (see [[cultures/toltec-culture]]) and is one pole of the
long-debated [[debates/toltec-chichen-itza-connection-debate]]. Tula's ceremonial core
shows evidence of sudden, deliberate destruction: halls were burned and the Serpent
Wall toppled. Coe & Koontz attribute this most plausibly to Chichimeca incursions given
Tula's proximity to their frontier, noting that later "Aztec II" pottery reoccupation
does not imply cultural continuity with the destroyers — Xolotl's Chichimeca band is
recorded as having found Tula already in ruins on their way to the Valley of Mexico.
Tula was subsequently looted by the Aztecs, who reused its sculptures, friezes, and
offerings in Tenochtitlan, complicating archaeological reconstruction of the site.

## Key Debates

See [[debates/toltec-chichen-itza-connection-debate]] for the century-long dispute over
whether Tula's material and iconographic parallels with Chich'en Itza reflect conquest,
Putún-Maya intermediation, or a shared cult network without direct political control.
Coe & Koontz consider it "unlikely" that Tula was conquered by Chich'en Itza rather than
the reverse, but treat the wider question as open pending better chronological control
at both sites.
