## Discovery and Excavation History

Nan Madol is a monumental islet complex off the southern side of Temwen Island, in the district of Madolenihmw, Pohnpei, Caroline Islands, Micronesia. The site covers roughly 81 hectares of sheltered reef area comprising 93 artificial stone islets bounded on the west, south, and east by massive seawalls/breakwaters incorporating islets (attributed, [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 2017). German ethnographer Paul Hambruch produced the first accurate map in 1910 (Hambruch 1911, 1936), which remains the basis for modern surveys; Morgan (1988) produced a provisional map incorporating Hambruch's data. Smithsonian Institution archaeologists excavated the Ideht Mound (ceremonial turtle-sacrifice oven debris) in the 1960s. Steve Athens and Bill Ayres synthesized the site's chronology; Joyce Bath investigated the satellite site of Sapwtakai; M.D. McCoy et al. (2016) conducted pXRF geochemical sourcing and high-precision uranium-thorium dating of building-fill coral. The combination of early ethnographic mapping, mid-20th-century excavation, and 21st-century geochemical/isotopic science gives Nan Madol an unusually well-triangulated, if still incompletely excavated, evidentiary record.

## Occupation Sequence

Two named sectors are recognized ethnographically (per Hambruch and later investigators): Madol Pah (west, 34 islets, residential ward with four dominant large square islets) and Madol Powe (east, 58 islets, ritual ward with tombs and priestly residences) — a division Kirch characterizes as a "classic binary structural opposition" (attributed).

Chronology, synthesized by Athens, Ayres, and colleagues (attributed):
- **ca. AD 1**: initial occupation, coeval with human colonization of Pohnpei — ceramic-bearing (CST pottery) stilt-house deposits of probable Lapita-derived type, within the tidal zone, underlying later artificial-islet fill.
- **AD 900–1100**: construction of artificially elevated islets begins, but not yet megalithic.
- **AD 1200–1600**: the true megalithic building phase, correlating with the Saudeleur period recorded in Pohnpei oral tradition (Bath and Athens 1990).
- **AD 1180–1200**: central burial use begins at the Nandauwas mortuary islet, per high-precision uranium-thorium dating of branch coral in building fill (M.D. McCoy et al. 2016) — a distinct, more precise dating class than radiocarbon.
- **After AD 1650**: continued use/construction; the site was abandoned by the time of European contact (mid-19th century), by which time Pohnpeians "greatly fear[ed] and respect[ed]" it (attributed).
- The Ideht Mound (ceremonial turtle-sacrifice oven debris) yielded three radiocarbon ages of cal AD 1180–1430.

The Nandauwas islet, at the southeast corner dominating Madol Powe, is the massive royal mortuary compound of the Saudeleur dynasty: a podium 79 by 63 m, with outer enclosing walls rising 7.6 m above canal level and interior crypts including a main central crypt with columnar basalt lintels, built in a distinctive header-stretcher technique using large columnar (prismatic) basalt slabs; other structures use dry-fitted large basalt boulders. McCoy et al. (2016) found via pXRF geochemical analysis that a significant quantity of the columnar basalt was sourced from the opposite side of the island (the Pwisehn Malek source), interpreting the high transport cost as, in their words, "a good metric for the authority of the island's first chiefs" (McCoy et al. 2016:301, quoted).

Regional satellite architecture in the same "Nan Madol style" (columnar basalt) is known from Sapwtakai (Kiti District), a ridgetop administrative/ritual center investigated by Joyce Bath (1984a, 1984b), radiocarbon dated to roughly AD 1325–1700, contemporary with Nan Madol's major development phase.

## Significance

Pohnpei oral tradition (attributed, not archaeological fact) divides island history into four ages — Age of People, Age of Saudeleur, Age of Nahnmwarki, Age of Foreigners — and holds that Nan Madol was built by two brothers using supernatural power, that a succession of eight Saudeleur rulers held island-wide power from a residence on Pahnkedira islet and were interred at Nandauwas, and that the dynasty ended when the last Saudeleur attempted to capture the god Nan Sapwe; the god fled to the sky-realm Katau, fathered Isokelekel, who returned with 333 warriors, defeated the last Saudeleur, and instituted the modern dual chiefly system (Nahnmwarki/Nahnken). Goodenough (1986:562, per Kirch) cautions against reading "Katau" as a specific real island rather than a mythical sky-realm, and argues that if any direction of architectural influence existed between Nan Madol and [[lelu|Lelu]], it more likely ran from Pohnpei to Kosrae, since Lelu's major construction phase lags Nan Madol's by one to two centuries.

Possible external-contact evidence includes an unusual basalt adz at Nan Madol with closest parallels to Samoan Type I/III adzes (Ayres and Mauricio 1987), and the Pohnpeian word for kava, *sakaw*, likely a Polynesian loanword from *te kava* (Geraghty 1994) — Pohnpei and Kosrae are the only two Micronesian islands where kava is consumed.

Several competing, non-adjudicated sociopolitical models address how Nan Madol's chiefdom-level authority arose: Ayres (1990), drawing on Renfrew's (1982) "peer-polity interaction" theory, frames Nan Madol's development as an evolving chiefdom controlling the Pohnpei polity ca. AD 1000–1500, without settling whether ideological ("religious motivation") or coercive ("commanded labor") mechanisms predominated; Peoples favors the coercive model; Bath and Athens (1990), integrating oral tradition with archaeology, propose a centralized Saudeleur-period four-level hierarchy that later collapsed to a three-level system of independent districts; Ayres and Haun (1985, 1990) emphasize agricultural intensification, especially competitive giant-yam (Dioscorea alata) cultivation, as a significant economic driver.

Contemporaneous context: in the early historic (post-Saudeleur) period, five independent districts (wehi) operated under a diarchy of sacred lord (nahmwarki) and secular lord (nahnken); maximum precontact population is estimated at 20,000–30,000 (Ayres, Haun, and Severance 1979:table 17) — comparable to larger Polynesian [[chiefdom|chiefdoms]] such as the Southern Cooks or the Australs.

## Key Debates

The relative weight of ideological versus coercive mechanisms in Nan Madol's rise as a chiefly/priestly center (Ayres vs. Peoples) remains an open interpretive question, as does the direction and reality of any Pohnpei–Kosrae influence (Goodenough's caution against literalizing the Isokelekel tradition).
