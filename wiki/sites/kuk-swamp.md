## Discovery and Excavation History

Kuk is a wetland/swamp site in the Wahgi Valley near Mount Hagen, New Guinea Highlands (Papua New Guinea), at roughly 1,550 m elevation (attributed, [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 2017). Jim Allen first observed relict drainage stratigraphy exposed by tea-plantation drainage works in 1970; Jack Golson subsequently led long-term excavation and interpretation from the late 1960s, publishing the foundational three-phase sequence (Golson 1977). Later work by Tim Denham, Simon Haberle, Carol Lentfer, and Richard Fullagar added pollen, starch-grain, and phytolith analyses that refined — and in the case of Phase 1, disputed — Golson's original interpretation. As a long-running, multi-technique excavation combining stratigraphic fieldwork with archaeobotanical science, Kuk is a comparatively well-documented site by the standards of tropical wetland archaeology, though its earliest phase remains an unresolved point of interpretation rather than settled fact.

## Occupation Sequence

Three phases are recognized (attributed to Golson 1977, refined by later scholars):

- **Phase 1 (ca. 9,000 years ago)**: gutters, hollows, pits, and stakeholes sealed beneath a grey clay layer. Golson interpreted these features as evidence of simple horticultural activity and associated erosion from human disturbance. Denham (2004:56), in a later reanalysis, disputes this reading and argues the evidence does not support an agricultural-origin claim for the Phase 1 features (see Key Debates below).
- **Phase 2 (6,000–5,500 BP)**: unambiguous swamp manipulation — large channels up to 2 m wide and deep draining the swamp, and circular clay "islands" roughly 1 m in diameter interpreted as raised planting beds, likely for Colocasia taro and possibly Australimusa bananas and Pueraria tubers.
- **Phase 3 (4,000–2,500 BP)**: true reticulate drainage systems, ancestral to the drainage patterns observed ethnographically among 20th-century Highlands cultivators.

Supporting environmental evidence: pollen analysis (Haberle et al. 2012) documents a dramatic vegetation shift beginning about 7,000 years ago — rapid forest loss, increased burning, and the establishment of permanent open grass-sedge swampland, which Haberle et al. (2012:135, quoted) interpret as "establishment of an agricultural landscape in the Upper Wahgi Valley by the mid-Holocene." Starch-grain analysis (Fullagar et al. 2006) recovered Colocasia taro and Dioscorea yam starch on stone tools dating to 10,200 years ago — possibly still wild rather than domesticated forms — with processing patterns by about 7,000 years ago indicating integration into wetland-edge cultivation. Banana (Musa) phytoliths occur throughout the sequence, though deliberate banana planting may not begin until Phase 2 (Denham et al. 2003).

## Significance

Kirch cites Kuk as archaeological support for an independent, pre-Austronesian origin of Near Oceanic/New Guinea Highlands horticulture — taro, banana, sugarcane, and other tree and root crops — one of the world's several independent centers of plant domestication. This is corroborated by ethnobotanical evidence that the wild ancestors of Australimusa bananas, sugarcane, Pueraria, Cordyline, breadfruit, and several aroids are native to the New Guinea/Bismarcks region. Denham (2011, attributed) stresses that these domestication steps preceded the arrival of Austronesian-speaking populations in the region — a load-bearing case for the four-way non-identity principle: the wiki records this development as `associated_with` the region's indigenous population, not as an effect of [[austronesian-expansion|Austronesian expansion]], since the two are independently dated and causally distinct.

## Key Debates

Golson's (1977) reading of Phase 1 as early horticultural evidence is directly disputed by Denham (2004:56), who argues the same features do not support an agricultural-origin claim. This is a live, clearly stated interpretive disagreement over identical evidence (not a mismatch) and remains open; Phase 2 onward (from ~6,000 BP) is uncontested as clear cultivation evidence. It is recorded here as a position pair pending a dedicated `debates/` page (`debates/kuk-phase-1-agricultural-origins-debate`, not yet created) should the wiki expand this line of inquiry.

It also remains an open question, per Kirch, whether Highlands horticultural development proceeded independently of lowland/coastal Near Oceanic developments or via exchange of ideas and genes — Kirch states this explicitly as unresolved.
