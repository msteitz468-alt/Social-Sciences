## Discovery and Excavation History

Koobi Fora — also known in the 1970s literature as East Rudolf or East Turkana — is a fossil-bearing region on the eastern shore of Lake Turkana (formerly Lake Rudolf), Kenya, worked by [[leakey-richard|Richard Leakey]]'s East Turkana Research Group from the late 1960s onward. According to [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] (Leakey & Lewin 1977), the team's camp sat near the Koobi Fora spit, and a hominid-bearing locality a few kilometers to the north — designated "KBS" after its discoverer, geologist Kay Behrensmeyer — became a focal excavation site. At the end of 1972, team member Bernard Ngeneo spotted the first fragments of a hominid cranium eroding from a steep gully; the specimen, catalogued as KNM-ER 1470 ("skull 1470") at the National Museums of Kenya, was reconstructed over six weeks by Meave Leakey and anatomist Bernard Wood (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). In 1975 a further East Turkana team member recovered the most complete Homo erectus cranium then known in Africa, resembling the Peking Man cranium.

Excavation quality: the 1470 discovery and much of the associated fossil recovery at Koobi Fora is reported by the excavating team itself in [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] — a firsthand, but non-independent, account; the book was published while the KBS-tuff dating controversy (below) was still active, so its own dating claims for the site should be read as a position in that dispute rather than a settled result.

## Occupation Sequence

Per [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]], the authors' central thesis for Koobi Fora is that around 2.5 million years ago (as dated in the source; see Key Debates) as many as four distinct hominid types coexisted along the lake margin, differing in stature and, the authors argue, especially in behavior — [[australopithecus]] alongside early [[homo-habilis]]. A femur, tibia, and fibula found by John Harris near the 1470 locality were interpreted as belonging to an advanced hominid with a modern-like striding gait, in contrast to the less habitually bipedal anatomy attributed to australopithecines (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). The 1975 Homo erectus cranium, with an estimated brain size of ~900cc, was dated to about 1.5–1.6 million years, indicating continued hominid presence at the site into the Homo erectus period. Excavations at the KBS locality also recovered stone implements described as "Oldowan-like artifacts," associated with [[homo-habilis]].

## Significance

Koobi Fora / East Turkana is presented in [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] as the key evidentiary basis for the authors' argument that early Homo was contemporary with, rather than descended from, the australopithecines — a position they describe as newly strengthened but not yet universally accepted at time of writing (1977). Skull 1470 anchors the book's claim of very early Homo origins, and the site's stratified volcanic-ash sequence made it a proving ground for potassium-argon and fission-track dating methods applied to human origins (see [[koobi-fora|Koobi Fora]] dating discussion below).

## Key Debates

**The KBS-tuff / skull-1470 dating dispute (open, as of the source; later resolved).** [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] (1977) reports skull 1470 as dated via potassium-argon analysis of the KBS tuff to close to two and a half million years old — in places the text gives a range extending toward "possibly nearer three" million years — a dating the authors present as supporting their contemporaneity thesis against a shorter chronology. This KBS-tuff date was, however, highly contested at the time of the book's writing and was subsequently revised by the discipline: later geochronological work (outside this source) redated the KBS tuff to approximately 1.9 million years, substantially younger than the figure reported here (attributed; standard supersession noted per protocol, not sourced to Leakey & Lewin 1977 itself). The 1977 text should therefore be read as documenting one side of an active, and since-resolved, dating controversy rather than a settled chronology.

### Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein describes the Koobi Fora Formation (East Turkana) as 560+ m of stream-laid deposits in
eight members, dated by K/Ar, 40Ar/39Ar, and [[paleomagnetic-dating|paleomagnetism]] to ~4.34–0.7
Ma, and treats it as the classic case of **faunal cross-checking of radiometric dates**: the
original KBS Tuff estimate (~2.6 Ma, via faunal comparison to the [[omo-river|Omo]]) proved ~700
kyr too old and was corrected to ~1.88 Ma. Early [[homo-habilis|*Homo*]] (KNM-ER 1470, 1813) comes
from the Upper Burgi Member (~2 Ma); the oldest artifacts (KBS Industry, [[oldowan-industry]])
date ~2–1.8 Ma. Microscopic [[use-wear-analysis|use-wear]] on nine flakes showed use on wood,
meat, and grass/reeds; cortex-pattern analysis (57 right : 43 left) implies the knappers were
**predominantly right-handed**, like modern humans and unlike chimpanzees.
