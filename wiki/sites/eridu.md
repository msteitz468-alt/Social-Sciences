# Eridu

Early southern Mesopotamian center (Abu Shahrain), famous for its deep Ubaid temple sequence. Treated in [[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]] as a type early city and covered in detail in Henry T. Wright's appendix survey of the Eridu–Ur margins.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Major mid-20th-century Iraqi excavations (including work associated with Fuad Safar — Adams's volume dedication). Wright's 1966-area survey (published as Heartland appendix) maps the southern margins including Eridu.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Adams 1981 / Wright appendix.)*

- Jacobsen (foreword) and Adams treat early Ubaid/Uruk Eridu (with Uruk) as economic **central places** — pilgrimage and exchange drawing settled and nomadic support.
- [[wright-henry-t|Wright]] appendix: Ubaid two-level hierarchy with Eridu ~**12 ha** (Ur ~10 ha as of Wright); Uruk-period nucleation then Eridu abandonment in the southern margins sequence.
- Catalog entry (Wright): Abu Shahrain / Eridu with Eridu–Hajji Muhammad–Ubaid ceramic series among type collections.
- Southern-margin survey (~1,010 km² / 192 sites) complements Adams's central floodplain intensive zone.

## Significance

- Classic case for early alluvial ceremonial centers preceding full urban checklists.
- Southern margin complement to Adams's central floodplain intensive survey.

## Key Debates

- Whether early Eridu is best read as temple-town, central place, or both (Jacobsen/Adams central-place framing vs excavation-led temple sequence narratives).
- Timing of Eridu abandonment relative to Uruk nucleation on the southern margins (Wright 1981 appendix).
- Gulf shoreline and early settlement hiatus SE of Ur/Lagash (Adams 1981; tentative as of that horizon).

## Historiography

Date-stamp to 1981 synthesis; post-1981 Gulf geoarchaeology and re-excavation revise absolute dates and environmental setting.

### Roux (*Ancient Iraq*, 1992, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]].)* Roux reports Eridu (Abu Shahrain, 19 km SW of Ur) as excavated 1946–49 by Seton Lloyd and Fuad Safar, who uncovered seventeen superimposed temple levels beneath a later ziqqurat built by Amar-Sin of the Third Dynasty of Ur (2046–2038 BCE). The lowest temples (levels XVII–XV) are small one-room buildings with altars and offering tables, associated with "Eridu ware" (elaborate dark-brown geometric pottery related to Choga Mami Transitional ware). The middle temples (XIV–XII) are associated with "Hajji Muhammad ware" (crowded designs, "reserve slip" decoration). The upper temples (XI–VI) carry standard Ubaid ware; temples VI–I belong to the early Uruk period — together forming what Roux calls the Ubaid 1 (Eridu), 2 (Hajji Muhammad), 3, and 4 ceramic-phase sequence (see [[ubaid-culture]]).

Roux reports an extramural cist-grave cemetery at Eridu in which adults and children alike lie supine on potsherd beds in mudbrick-lined graves — a burial pattern he contrasts sharply with Tepe Gawra in the north, where comparable formal burial is rare and most graves are simple pits with flexed on-side burial. Roux reads this contrast as evidence that Ubaid-culture bearers were concentrated in the south (a population interpretation flagged on [[ubaid-culture]] as needing the four-way non-identity caution). He argues architectural continuity across Eridu's full temple sequence, from the mid-sixth millennium BCE into historical times, as evidence for an unusually long-lived continuity of cultic tradition at the site, reinforced by two later Ubaid-period shrines found near the "White Temple" of Anu at [[uruk]].

## Potts (*Mesopotamian Civilization*, 1997) — Additions

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]] unless noted.)*

### Ubaid temple/house tripartite architecture

Ubaid-period temples closely resemble contemporary domestic houses architecturally,
sharing a tripartite core plan (Aurenche 1981), but are externally distinguished by
elaborate niches and buttresses — Potts draws the comparison directly between the Tell
Madhhur house and the Eridu VII temple (Roaf 1984:80-90). This architectural
continuity between house and temple form is part of the wider "Sumerian Problem"
debate over cultural continuity from the Ubaid period into historical Sumerian times
(see [[sumerian-problem-debate]]).

### Oates's temple-continuity argument (pointer)

Joan Oates's 1953/1960 Cambridge dissertation (published in the Woolley memorial
volume) analyzed all available southern Mesopotamian Ubaid material and argued
strongly for material-culture continuity (ceramics, architecture) from the early Ubaid
period into Sumerian historical times, based substantially on the sequence of
superimposed temples at Eridu; she renamed the four southern Ubaid occupation phases
(previously known separately as Eridu, Hajji Muhammad, Ur, and al-'Ubaid phases) as
Ubaid 1-4 on the basis of that Eridu sequence (attributed, J. Oates 1960, cited by
Potts 1997). See [[sumerian-problem-debate]] for the fuller continuity-vs-discontinuity
argument this feeds, and [[ubaid-culture]] for the Ubaid 1-4 phase scheme.

### Sanlaville's sea-level curve and its relevance to Eridu's early setting

P. Sanlaville's sea-level reconstruction for the Arabian Gulf (Sanlaville 1989, cited
by Potts) places sea levels roughly 15-20 m lower than today at the start of the Ubaid
period (c. 5500 BCE), rising to approximately modern levels by the end of the Ubaid
period (c. 3800 BCE), continuing to a maximum of roughly 1-2 m above modern sea level
c. 3500 BCE, then retreating to approximately modern levels by c. 2800 BCE. This
directly contradicts H.J. Nissen's hypothesis (1988a, 1988b) that sea levels up to
2.75 m higher than today made much of lower Mesopotamia — including the Eridu
vicinity — uninhabitable during the Ubaid period until a sea-level recession c. 3500
BCE; Potts, following Oates (1991, 1993), holds that sea level cannot explain any
supposed absence of early settlement near Eridu, since Sanlaville's data show
Ubaid-period sea levels were in fact **lower**, not higher, than today (position; see
[[mesopotamia-geography]] for the fuller progradation/sea-level debate, including the
Lees and Falcon vs. Larsen dispute).

## Matthews densification (2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]].)* Matthews describes the superimposed temple sequence at Eridu (excavated late 1940s) as spanning the whole Ubaid period and beyond — roughly 1,500 years — with each successive rebuild larger and grander, probably dedicated to Enki, on massive mud-brick platforms with a central open space and altar. He notes the continuity of the temple's location, combined with the Sumerian tradition naming Eridu a source of kingship and one of the first post-flood cities, "argue persuasively for cultural and possibly ethnic (Sumerian?) continuity" of south Mesopotamian occupation from an early date — Matthews's own qualifier ("Sumerian?") signals his uncertainty, and this is recorded here as an attributed hypothesis, not an identity claim (material culture, ethnicity, and language are not equated; see the four-way non-identity rule). Susan Pollock (1999: 87–88) reads the Eridu temple assemblages — domestic-type food/textile-processing equipment alongside elaborate vessels and stamp seals — as evidence of supra-domestic ritual activity, while Matthews adds the methodological caution that ritually significant food/textile processing may be archaeologically indistinguishable from ordinary domestic activity despite differing social meaning.

### Dates at Eridu

Site elevation and progradation-rate estimates bearing on Eridu's ancient relationship
to the Gulf shoreline have varied widely across scholars, from Pliny's implied rate of
roughly 120 km/400 years to Rawlinson's (1857) ~58.6 m/yr and Larsen's (1975) revised
30-36 m/yr incorporating the Flandrian Transgression — the latter cautiously accepted
by Adams (1981) and Huot (1989) as lending renewed plausibility to cuneiform sources
linking third-millennium Ur and Eridu to the sea (position; see
[[mesopotamia-geography]] for the full history of this debate, and [[ur]] for the
parallel elevation datum at that site).
