# Nakbe

Middle Preclassic lowland Maya center in the El Mirador Basin of northern Petén, Guatemala, about 13 km southeast of [[el-mirador]]. As of [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]], Nakbe is the first well-documented Middle Preclassic lowland site with monumental masonry buildings, causeways, and carved monuments—a major prototype for later civic-ceremonial cores and a cornerstone of indigenous-lowland (not Olmec-transplant) origin models for Maya civilization.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Sharer & Traxler (2006) present Nakbe through Richard Hansen’s Mirador Basin research program and related surveys rather than a 19th-century explorer narrative. Radiocarbon dates and ceramic assemblages (overwhelmingly Mamom sherds—Sharer & Traxler note Mamom may have originated in the El Mirador Basin) anchor the Middle Preclassic chronology. Hansen and colleagues documented artificial garden plots bounded by masonry walls and filled with rich *bajo* soils laboriously transported from surrounding marshlands. Comparative work at El Mirador, Wakna, Tintal, and other Basin sites with carved monuments collectively revised the origins of lowland Maya civilization. Late Classic Altar 3 at Altar de los Reyes (southern Campeche) and codex-style vessels/texts at Nakbe and Calakmul preserve the name Chatan Winik, which Nikolai Grube suggests may have been the original polity of Nakbe and El Mirador.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]] unless noted.)*

- **Colonization context:** Interior Maya lowlands lagged highland and coastal settled communities; by the end of the Early Preclassic (~1000 BC), coastal and highland-margin populations began colonizing the interior via rivers and other natural routes. Middle Preclassic rapid growth was especially marked in the El Mirador Basin, accelerated by highland and broader Mesoamerican contacts (obsidian, stylistic links) without requiring an Olmec or highland-transplant founding model.
- **Initial settlement ca. 1000–800 BC:** Wattle-and-daub houses, tamped clay floors, stone retaining walls.
- **First monumental masonry ca. 800–600 BC:** Platforms 2–3 m high with rough-hewn stone and clay-lime plaster—earliest well-documented lowland monumental masonry package.
- **Later Middle Preclassic ca. 600–400 BC:** Terraced platforms, expansion of public architecture, causeways, and carved monuments. Middle Preclassic ball court with long sloping side walls (contrasted with later Classic forms and northern vertical-wall courts). Possible earliest E Group assemblage (calendar-related architectural form later widespread: Late Preclassic examples at El Mirador, Caracol, Wakna, Uaxactun, Tikal). East–west site layout emphasizing solar axis—Sharer & Traxler contrast this with Olmec north–south plans and treat orientation as cosmological (east birth/life, west death/underworld).
- **Polity scale and neighbors:** Among the largest early lowland polities; similar monumental Middle Preclassic architecture at El Mirador, Wakna, and Tintal; ≥3 other late Middle Preclassic Basin sites with carved monuments. Close proximity suggests competition, though direct raiding/sacrifice evidence not yet found (unlike the southern area). Middle Preclassic *sacbe* systems at Nakbe map early realm infrastructure later expanded at Late Preclassic El Mirador and Classic Calakmul and Caracol.
- **Succession to El Mirador:** El Mirador’s sacbe network includes a southeast link to Nakbe reflecting succession from the Middle Preclassic neighbor; by the Late Preclassic El Mirador was the far larger capital (east–west axis ~2 km; structures dwarfing later Maya buildings). Whether Nakbe was the absolute first locus of lowland complexity remains unknown—earlier examples may await discovery.
- **Later textual afterlife:** Chatan Winik on Altar de los Reyes Altar 3 (thirteen “thrones”/Emblem Glyphs, possibly a retrospective of oldest dynastic capitals) may encode Nakbe–El Mirador as the first seat of lowland state power (Grube’s suggestion as reported by Sharer & Traxler).

## Significance

Nakbe is load-bearing for Preclassic-first accounts of lowland Maya urbanism: monumental masonry, causeways, ball courts, E Groups, intensive garden agriculture, and east–west cosmogram planning all appear before the Classic period. It undercuts single-cause models (isolation broken only by Olmec diffusion; pure highland transplant) in favor of multi-causal, multi-region Middle–Late Preclassic development with indigenous lowland agency plus southern interaction. Agricultural intensification (transported *bajo* soils) and differential resource access figure in Sharer & Traxler’s developmental model linking population expansion, competition, stratification, and eventual divine kingship ([[kuhul-ajaw]] package fully visible later). As prototype civic-ceremonial core, it is the Middle Preclassic counterpart to Late Preclassic [[el-mirador]] and Classic mega-sites such as [[tikal]] and [[calakmul]].

## Key Debates

- **Olmec “mother culture” vs indigenous lowland innovation:** Sharer & Traxler argue Basin settlement and rapid sociopolitical development were accomplished by local Maya populations (E–W plans vs Gulf Coast N–S), while earliest carved monuments show highland stylistic links and highland-sourced obsidian—interaction without transplant.
- **Primacy:** Whether Nakbe was the first lowland complex center or merely the best-documented; earlier sites may remain undiscovered.
- **Chatan Winik as first lowland state:** Grube’s reading of Altar de los Reyes Altar 3 and codex-style references—retrospective Classic ideology vs recoverable Preclassic political geography.
- **Competition without proven warfare:** Close spacing of monumental Basin centers implies rivalry; absence (as of 2006) of clear raiding/sacrifice evidence in the Basin vs southern-area patterns.
- **E Group origins and calendar:** Earliest E Group possibly at Nakbe; role of E Groups in Maya calendar development is attributed inference, not proven sole origin.

## Historiography

Nakbe’s importance is a late-20th-century archaeological discovery relative to Classic capitals long known to explorers. Hansen’s Mirador Basin program and ceramic/radiocarbon chronology shifted the “origin of Maya civilization” debate from Classic-centric and Olmec-diffusion frames toward Preclassic lowland heartland complexity. As of Sharer & Traxler 2006, claims rest on architecture, ceramics, radiocarbon, and limited sculpture—not a dense dynastic inscriptional record like Classic sites. Garden-plot and causeway evidence is archaeological; Chatan Winik state-primacy is epigraphic conjecture about a much later monument. Identity cautions: Nakbe is an archaeological site and analytical construct of Preclassic urbanism—not silently equated with a language, genetic population, or continuous named people.
