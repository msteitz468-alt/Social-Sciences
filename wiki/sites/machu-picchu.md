# Machu Picchu

Royal estate of the [[inca-empire]] on a cliff-jungle ridge above the Urubamba, treated by [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]] (as of [[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]]) as one of the most prominent Pachakuti-generation rural manors in the heartland Sacred Valley corridor rather than a “lost city” of popular imagination.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Nineteenth-century travelers (Squier, Wiener, Raimondi) mapped roads and Urubamba estates before public attention fixed on the site. As of D'Altroy 2002, Inca archaeology “did not really catch the public’s attention until 1912,” when Hiram Bingham announced Machu Picchu after local farmers guided him there in 1911; Bingham’s early work framed a romantic “lost city” story that long outran the estate interpretation. Subsequent research has refined architecture, hydrology, and burials; D'Altroy’s synthesis draws especially on MacLean on walls and sacred stones and Salazar on multi-locale pottery in burials. Monumental focus in early Cuzco-region work (including Saqsawaman) is part of the same disciplinary history.

## Occupation Sequence

As of D'Altroy 2002, imperial expansion put elegant private reserves for living and dead emperors and aristocrats along an ~100 km Vilcanota/Urubamba stretch between Pisac and Machu Picchu—the “Sacred Valley”—supporting deified stature, *panaqa* wealth, and political-ceremonial activity. Several spectacular sites are identified as Pachakuti royal estates; most prominent among them are Pisac, [[ollantaytambo]], and Machu Picchu. The complex occupies two ridge complexes—main Machu Picchu (“old hill”) and Huayna Picchu (“young hill”)—with western terraces and dense elegant architecture around a main plaza. Residential *kancha*-like compounds are fitted to rugged topography; fourteen fountains and many carved granite outcrops are incorporated into architecture. Bingham and some modern scholars impute religious significance to the stones; MacLean argued that animistic importance of outcrops led architects to situate structures around them. Prominent features include an *intiwatana* (sun gnomon in some readings) at a western temple peak and carved bedrock under the *torreón*. As of D'Altroy 2002 (citing Bauer and Dearborn), the D-shaped Torreón has a window oriented toward the fifteenth-century Pleiades rise (~65°) and may have tracked June solstice sunrise via plumb-line shadow. Walls are better read (MacLean, as of D'Altroy 2002) as sacred/private barriers or terrace supports than as military defenses—unlike partly fort-usable complexes such as [[saqsawaman]] and [[ollantaytambo]]. Burials recovered on Bingham’s expeditions were mostly young women, which fostered a “chosen women” (*aqllakuna*) house interpretation; D'Altroy flags that it is uncertain they represent a cross-section of residents. Burial pottery includes styles from at least four locales (Salazar, as of D'Altroy 2002), including Chimú blackware among other non-local types. Neo-Inca Vilcabamba (~200 km down the Urubamba from [[cuzco]]) is not equated with Machu Picchu in D'Altroy’s account.

## Significance

Within the [[inca-empire]] heartland system centered on [[cuzco]], Machu Picchu exemplifies landscape-engineered royal estate architecture: grandeur from cliff-jungle setting and melding of landforms with stonework more than from masonry alone. It anchors the Urubamba estate belt that concentrated elite wealth under split-inheritance/*panaqa* economics (as of D'Altroy 2002) and supplies classic cases of Inca stereotomy, hydrology, and sacred-stone architecture for comparative study of provincial vs. heartland installations.

## Key Debates

- **Royal estate vs. “lost city.”** Popular discovery narrative vs. archaeological-ethnohistoric identification as a Pachakuti-generation rural manor among peer estates (Pisac, Ollantaytambo).
- **Function of walls and stones.** Defense vs. MacLean’s sacred/private barrier and terrace-support reading; religious significance of carved outcrops and the *intiwatana*.
- **Burial demography.** Whether Bingham’s young-women sample indicates an *aqllakuna* sector or sampling/preservation bias.
- **Astronomical intent.** Torreón and related features as deliberate solstice/Pleiades architecture (Bauer and Dearborn as reported by D'Altroy 2002) vs. more cautious orientation claims.
