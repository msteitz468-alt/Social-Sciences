# Mayapan

## Discovery and Excavation History

Late Postclassic political center of northern Yucatan. Carnegie and later projects (incl. Masson et al. population work as cited). [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] use ethnohistory (Xiu, Kokom, Hunac Ceel) with accuracy hedges.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- After Toltec Chichen’s decline, Itza-related groups and K'uk'ulkan II traditions; Mayapan becomes confederative capital c. 1283 in chronicle time.
- Urban form: dense walled settlement; Masson estimates ~15–17,000 (as cited).
- Economy and ritual continue Postclassic international style connections.
- **Fall:** K'atun 8 Ajaw story of Xiu revolt against Kokom / Hunac Ceel cycle — C&H treat narrative as **hedged** ethnohistory, not precise chronology.
- Aftermath: fragmented sixteen states of Conquest-era Yucatan.

## Significance

Type-site of Late Postclassic Yucatecan political confederation and of chronicle–archaeology tension.

## Key Debates

- Reliability of k'atun chronicles vs ceramics/radiocarbon.
- Degree of “Mexicanization” of Postclassic Maya politics.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Research history and site form

Carnegie Institution excavations 1951–55 (detailed map, defensive wall, central excavations) were the swan song of Carnegie Maya research; results generally confirmed historical chronicles including Landa, with destruction/burning supporting a violent end. 1990s INAH consolidation/excavations refined architectural/developmental history and continued linking the site to Colonial-era chronicles. Densely settled walled city: ~4 km² within walls; wall ~2 m high, possibly palisaded; seven well-defended gateways; ~4,000 buildings may have housed up to ~20,000 people (Masson-scale estimates in C&H are ~15–17,000 — same order); dry-stone property walls enclose ~¼-acre yards with kitchen gardens/beekeeping; denser packing than earlier dispersed Maya cities. Late Postclassic Mayapan second only to central Copan in structure density in their Rice & Culbert summary tables (~986–996 strs/km², defensive-wall concentration). Later Mayapan/Tulum masonry assessed as lower skill than Classic regional styles.

### Chronology (traditional k’atun cycle)

Authors prefer traditional ~20-year *k’atun* (not 24-year Ajaw K’atun) because three Mayapan stelae preserve traditional *k’atun*-ending dates; founding equated with K’atun 8 Ajaw (AD 1185–1204) and abandonment with return of K’atun 8 Ajaw 256 years later; Gaspar Xiu’s 260-year span and 13 Central Plaza floor renovations align with one 13-*k’atun* cycle; Milbrath & Peraza Lope correlated archaeology with Chilam Balam. Postclassic returned to *k’atun* intervals at Mayapan (vs Classic hotun at some sites).

### Political sequence from chronicles + archaeology

Rise as new Yucatan capital succeeding [[chichen-itza]] (~100 km west). Chronicles of intrigue (kidnap of Chichen ruler’s wife by Izamal; **Hunak Keel** conquering Chichen with Mexican mercenaries) may account for destruction evidence; transition mechanism remains unclear archaeologically. Sequential reconstruction: Itza founding/Hunak Keel attack on Chichen (8 Ajaw 1185–1204); Chichen retaliation and first Castillo (4 Ajaw 1224–44); Champoton Itza arrival (2 Ajaw 1244–63); **Cocom** from Chichen (13 Ajaw 1263–83) take rulership with Tabasco **Canul** mercenaries; Cocom rebuild center emulating Chichen (round temples, Castillo, feathered-serpent temples, Round Temple 1283–1362) and revive K’uk’ulkan cult with effigy incensarios. *Multepal* of elite houses: Cocom (most prominent, Chichen origins), Xiu (from Uxmal, allied with Chel), Chel, Canul; ~two dozen colonnaded central buildings as elite residences/admin; realm divided into provinces (*kuchcabal*) whose lords lived in Mayapan supported by *caluac* tribute agents; possible Cocom-east/Xiu-west residential symbolism. Roys outlined similar council form at later Mayapan continuing Chichen’s power-sharing model.

### Economy, fall, and diaspora

Controlled north-coast salt production and the clay-plus-indigo source of Maya blue; merchants traded salt, cotton textiles, honey, pottery, and slaves for western Mexican copper bells and highland obsidian/jade; after ~1375 trade contacts with Mexica Tenochtitlan for blue pigment used on Templo Mayor; Mixteca-Puebla-style murals appear. Mayapan-style deity-effigy incensarios mark state-sponsored cults and occur also in central Peten lakes sites. **Fall:** Xiu-led revolt (Aj Xupan Xiu) against Cocom dated to K’atun 8 Ajaw (1441–61 traditional), near other colonial dates (1420, 1446); Cocom house killed except one son absent in Honduras; burned buildings/destroyed altars support sacking; confederacy fragmented into ~18 northern lowland petty states at Conquest (Sotuta for Cocom; Mani for Tutul Xiu; Tecoh for Chel). 1536 Otzmal massacre of Xiu pilgrims by Nachi Cocom reopened wars that facilitated Spanish conquest. Peten lakes **Kowoj** claimed as latecomers from Mayapan (eastern-gate guardians in Chilam Balam). Tulum possibly founded as Mayapan-allied east-coast trading port; Coba reoccupation with Mayapan/east-coast mural styles. After Mayapan fall, Xiu province of Mani was the most powerful northern polity until Spanish alliance (lord Tutul Xiu baptized Melchor, 1540s).
