# Jericho (Tell es-Sultan)

Tell es-Sultan near the modern town of Jericho in the Jordan Valley — a large multi-period mound at a major spring on the western edge of the Rift. In mid-century world prehistory it is the type-case for early SW Asian sedentism and the farming threshold (attributed, [[clark-world-prehistory-1961|Clark 1961]]).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Major mid-century work by **Kathleen Kenyon** (1952–58) redefined the early sequence; earlier Garstang excavations had established the site's importance. Clark's 1961 outline relies primarily on Kenyon's "Proto-Neolithic" framing (attributed). Later expeditions have revised absolute chronology and interpretations of the tower/walls; this page's depth so far is the Clark 1961 use-case, not a full excavation history.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Clark 1961 ch. 4, after Kenyon.)*

- **Proto-Neolithic / pre-pottery:** large settled community at a perennial spring — exceptional density among early Neothermal hunter-gatherers before full farming.
- Evidence Clark reads as early plant cultivation and a special human–animal relationship "amounting to primitive domestication"; larger population expansion once farming is established.
- **Plastered skulls** (Kenyon; plate II in Clark) and monumental tower/wall features as signs of social intensity (defensive reading is his period interpretation).
- Paired with **Jarmo** (Iraqi piedmont) as the other classic early farming village type-case in Clark's outline.
- Multi-period tell continues through Bronze Age and historic occupations (not detailed in Clark 1961 beyond the early threshold).

Absolute dates, wall/tower function, and the simple “first farmers” narrative have been heavily revised since 1961 — treat Clark's account as a **period position**, not current consensus.

### Braidwood 1959 (*Prehistoric Men*)

*(Attributed — [[braidwood-robert]], *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]* 3rd ed.)* Tell es-Sultan yields a catalogue somewhat resembling [[jarmo|Jarmo]] (including fair depth **without portable pottery**) but with **surprisingly complex architecture** — massive stone fortification walls and formed sun-dried mud brick. Jericho lies in a tropically lush Dead Sea niche ~700 ft below sea level: **geographically within** the hilly-flanks arc but **environmentally not part of** the typical foothill zone. Several radiocarbon determinations fall in a range Braidwood finds reasonable for Jarmo and are more internally consistent than the Jarmo series; meaning of the comparison then unclear. Sequence may proceed from local Natufian into a well-settled community with less gap than Iraq’s Karim Shahir–Jarmo break; **little direct evidence** yet (as of 1959) for the food-production base. Paired with Jarmo as early village type-case; see [[oasis-natural-habitat-debate]], [[natufian-culture]].

## Significance

Type-case in mid-century narratives of the [[neolithic-revolution|Neolithic]] threshold in SW Asia; critical node for [[world-prehistory]] and for debates over sedentism-before-farming. Anchors [[economic-prehistory]]'s global farming chapter. Do not equate the archaeological site with any single people, language, or genetic population without attributed argument.

## Key Debates

Sedentism-before-farming and the status of Kenyon's Proto-Neolithic sequence remain live in later literature (not yet a dedicated `debates/` page). Farming-origin framing intersects [[neolithic-revolution]] and [[ex-oriente-lux]] historiography. No wiki-voice verdict from the 1961 textbook alone.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]]; uncalibrated/legacy textbook chronology as of 2007.)*

W&O place early domestic cereals at Jericho’s lower levels (~10.3–9.3 kya): domestic wheat/barley cultivated though wild ancestors were not local to the arid surrounds—likely brought from Jordan Valley uplands; no domestic animals yet; wild gazelle/goat/cattle/boar hunted; settlement possibly ≥2,000 people. They report a massive stone wall ~9.3 kya (~3 m thick, 4 m high, ~700 m circuit), perhaps for flood control, with moderate trade (asphalt, sulfur, salt, obsidian).

By ~8500 BCE (their synthesis), Jericho’s stone walls/buildings are listed among Neolithic complexity signals directing energies beyond typical foragers; Levantine plastered/painted skulls with shell eyes (ancestor cult? trophies?) and related ‘Ain Ghazal plaster figures mark changing social contexts of village life. By ~8 kya, larger sites including Jericho and [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] are still framed as largely regionally self-sufficient mud-hut communities within a village horizon spanning SW Asia.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003, chs. 7–9; calendar BC; Lubbock scenes = narrative reconstruction.)*

- **Natufian precursor:** Natufian parties camped by ‘Ain es-Sultan (crescent tools); Mithen suggests possible meagre cereal/pea/lentil planting before permanent village (attributed).
- **Founding ~9600 BC:** Holocene rains and Jordan-valley floods renew the spring; “Jericho was founded, and with it people became farmers” (Mithen’s synthesis — not sole Neolithic origin).
- **PPNA architecture/population:** round sunken-floor mud-brick dwellings ~5 m diameter; ~50 dwellings / >500 people at 9600 BC → >70 dwellings / ~1,000 within centuries (counts via Lubbock reconstruction).
- **Kenyon PPNA:** coined Pre-Pottery Neolithic when lifestyle was “Neolithic” without pottery; first village phase ≤~1,000 years after Holocene start — unbundles Childe package (attributed).
- **Burials:** Kenyon 276 burials in ~10% of settlement; Late Natufian traditions (mostly single, few goods); adult skull removal after flesh decay; children/infants ~40% undisturbed (attributed).
- **Wall and tower:** famous stone wall and tower (Kenyon); function disputed (defense vs flood control vs other — Mithen notes definitive answer unlikely).
- **Not sole origin centre:** 1980s Netiv Hagdud/Gilgal reframed West Bank as Neolithic centre; Jericho remains largest known PPNA with unique tower/wall but Jordan valley may be peripheral to northern Fertile Crescent social/ideological developments (attributed).
- **Trade:** disproportionate southern-Turkish-source obsidian marks Jericho a key early trade centre; seed-grain may have moved on same networks (attributed).
- **PPNB:** rectangular plastered houses after ~9000 BC in broader Fertile Crescent sequence including Jericho (attributed).
- **First domesticates list:** among sites with early domesticated grains after Natufian wild-only cereals (with Abu Hureyra, Tell Aswad) (attributed).

Site ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes. Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

## Ucko & Dimbleby 1969 densification

*(Attributed — Hopf, Clutton-Brock, Reed, and related papers in *[[ucko-dimbleby-domestication-exploitation-1969]]* 1969; period chronology.)*

- **Hopf (plant remains):** early farming plant assemblage at Jericho; tough-rachis barley from Pre-Pottery B levels as of Hopf 1969; einkorn noted as northern element in the package discussion.
- **Clutton-Brock (carnivores):** analysis of carnivore remains from the Tell excavations; **no osteologically secure domestic dog** demonstrated in her 1969 treatment (editors allowed sub-specific *Canis* discussion as exception to binomial standardization) — period caution against Zeuner-style early dog claims at Jericho.
- **Reed (regional pattern):** places Jericho in the broader Near Eastern sequence; “perhaps” domestic goats with cultivated barley later in the millennium after Beidha’s goats/barley post-9000 BP (as of Reed 1969); awaits Clutton-Brock re-evaluation of goat status.
- **Historiographic role:** Jericho remains a type early village site in oasis vs hilly-flanks argumentation (Ucko & Dimbleby intro: Jericho often read as oasis response to desiccation — a reading they mark as underdetermined).

Site ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes.

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]* on Sultanian/PPNA Jericho.)*

- **Culture label:** type-site of the [[sultanian-culture|Sultanian]] (Kenyon’s PPNA at Tell es-Sultan); assemblage ~9500–8300 BC; early Natufian below with **stratigraphic lacuna** (no late Natufian/Khiamian on-site in Cauvin’s account); Protoneolithic poorly known (~5 m²).
- **Village:** >2 ha; plano-convex mud-brick round houses, paved floors, ramp/step access.
- **Wall and tower:** unshaped-stone wall ~3 m thick; tower ~10 m base diameter, ~8.5 m preserved, internal staircase — collective construction beyond family domestic sphere. First read as defence; later as monument + possible flood protection; either way implies organised collective labour.
- **Burial:** secondary pit burials and separate skull-group inhumations prefiguring PPNB [[skull-cult|skull cult]].
- **Plastered skulls (PPNB):** textbook modelled skulls in Cauvin’s southern symbolism chapter (with Nahal Hemar masks/bitumen skulls).

## Bellwood *First Farmers* densification (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; synthesis use-case, not a monographic site study.)*

- **Periodization:** Kathleen Kenyon’s 1950s excavations coined **PPNA** (ca. 9500–8500 BC) and **PPNB** (ca. 8500–7000 BC); later research adds a **PPNC** agricultural/environmental-decline phase Kenyon did not name.
- **PPNA:** Round tower and walls as communal/monumental architecture; among late PPNA sites with oldest potentially domesticated cereal remains — near a perennial spring, early cultivation attracted to scarce well-watered parcels.
- **PPNB:** Porched “megaron”-style houses (with Ain Ghazal); mud-brick rectangular shrine with end niche; remarkable plastered/painted portrait skulls among outstanding southern Levant PPNB features.
- **7th millennium:** Among large PPN sites abandoned or shrunk around the pottery Neolithic / 7th millennium (with Bouqras, Beidha, Abu Hureyra).

Site ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes. Source: [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Ch. 4; Sherratt 1997 “older” chronology.)*

- **Late Natufian setting.** Barker (via Sherratt) places alluvial soils of shrinking Jordan lakes (including Jericho) as better for cereals under Younger Dryas aridity but requiring tillage/transplanting/weeding — horticulture trajectory; control of oak/cereal stands at alluvial sites like Jericho; Late Natufian detached skulls prefigure Jericho plastered-skull cult (attributed).
- **PPNA (c.9500–8500 BC as Barker dates).** Early SW Asian farmers aceramic (Kenyon): PPNA then PPNB. Jericho unique monumental wall — defence and/or flood control debated. Netiv Hagdud-type oval semi-subterranean mud/stone/brick huts; many major settlements assumed permanent. First morphologically domestic cereals: **emmer + hulled two-row barley at Jericho** (with Tell Aswad emmer/peas/lentils) while wild seeds/fruits remain staples (attributed).
- **PPNB (c.8500–6500 BC).** Among larger mixed-farming settlements (1,000–2,000 people scale class with Beidha, ‘Ain Ghazal, reoccupied Abu Hureyra, Çatalhöyük, etc.). Sheep/goat herding spreads to Jericho after Zagros/Taurus beginnings; household as primary production unit; skull removal/plastered faces (Jericho, ‘Ain Ghazal, Basta) (attributed).
- **Identity caution.** Monumental PPNA architecture and early domestic cereals are site findings, not equations of Jericho with a people, language, or genetic population. Cross-links: [[natufian-culture]], [[abu-hureyra]], [[animal-domestication]], [[neolithic-revolution]], [[barker-graeme]].

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988]]* 1988, Chs. 1–2.)*

Nissen treated early Jericho as sometimes called a “town” that anticipates later urban aspects and problems but differs decisively: it was **not** the center of a settled countryside or part of a settlement system, but a **river oasis without neighboring dependent settlements**. The perimeter wall, on his reading, pointed not only to protection but also required collective labor of all inhabitants, blocked density relief by expansion (intensifying internal conflict management), and increased differences between settlement inhabitants and outsiders. After a temporal gap, Jericho again resembled ordinary settlements; similar external characteristics only reappear much later when coupled with true centrality. With [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]], he classed it as a spectrum extreme — further development to higher political organization did **not** lead via Jericho but via structured multi-settlement systems. Plastered skulls in prepottery layers allowed him an ancestor-worship inference (ethnographic analogies). Site ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes. Source: [[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988]].
