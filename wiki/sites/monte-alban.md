# Monte Albán

Hilltop capital of [[cultures/zapotec-civilization|Zapotec civilization]] in the Valley of Oaxaca, occupied continuously from its Late Preclassic founding through the Classic period. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], Monte Albán was founded near the close of the Middle Preclassic, c. 500–450 BCE, on hilltop eminences roughly 1,300 ft (400 m) above the valley floor, replacing San José Mogote as the Valley's leading center; the founding is characterized (attributed) as "a rapid, deliberate episode."

## Discovery and Excavation History

Alfonso Caso (see [[thinkers/caso-alfonso]]) conducted the foundational excavations at Monte Albán beginning in the 1930s, establishing the site's phase sequence (Monte Albán I–V) still in use, deciphering the Danzantes and Building J inscriptions, and demonstrating the Zapotec Calendar Round. Later regional settlement survey by Richard Blanton and Stephen Kowalewski extended demographic estimates across the wider Valley of Oaxaca (over 1,000 associated settlements at the site's Classic apogee). Javier Urcid's epigraphic work has revised readings of Zapotec script, noting that most Zapotec monuments were reused or moved, disrupting original inscriptional context, and that many apparent chronological notations are in fact calendrical personal names.

## Occupation Sequence

- **Monte Albán I** (c. 500–150 BCE): founding phase, marked by the Temple of the Danzantes platform in the southwest corner of the main plaza. The Danzantes are roughly 300 known bas-relief figures of nude men with Olmecoid facial features shown in contorted "swimming/dancing" poses, interpreted (attributed) as slain chiefs or captives — distorted limbs, open mouths, and closed eyes read as corpse-indicators; some show sexual mutilation, and one is a severed head. This phase also yields Mexico's first true literary texts, carved on the Danzantes and related slabs in a script with both phonetic and semantic elements; Alfonso Caso deduced the presence of day-glyphs of the 260-day Almanac and month glyphs, establishing the Calendar Round, but found the Long Count "was definitely absent." Monte Albán I pottery is fine gray clay with bridge-spouted vases and hollow tripod-support bowls. Richard Blanton and Stephen Kowalewski estimate the Late Preclassic population at 10,000–20,000; the first palaces appear at this time for administrative needs.
- **Monte Albán II** (terminal Preclassic, c. 150 BCE–AD 150): Building J, a stone-faced arrowhead-shaped structure, bears more than 40 inscribed slabs of conquest records — upside-down defeated-ruler heads, place-glyphs given phonetically/rebus-style, and calendrical notation — correctly interpreted by Alfonso Caso as town-conquest records, and confirmed to be written in the Zapotec language. Astronomer Anthony Aveni and architect Horst Hartung identified an alignment of Building J with the star Capella.
- **Classic period** (Monte Albán III–IV): the Late Preclassic–Classic transition was peaceful; some Monte Albán II burial-cult elements (potstands, painted stucco) arrived from the Maya area, but Maya influence ceased at the start of the Classic proper, replaced by strong influence from [[sites/teotihuacan]] — Coe & Koontz note frescoes at this stage are stylistically "obviously derivative from Teotihuacan," while the Zapotecs maintained a reciprocal enclave (the Oaxaca ward/barrio) at Teotihuacan preserving their own burial customs and scribal traditions. Classic population is estimated at ~25,000 at apogee. A capital-"I"-shaped masonry ball court, replicated at other Valley centers, may (per unnamed researchers cited by Coe & Koontz) have hosted an "official" game used to arbitrate inter-group conflicts. Over 170 subterranean tombs have been found across the site, systematically associated with houses and palaces; the finest have corbeled vaults and ante-chambers with murals. **Tomb 104**, in the site's northern part, is described as "the most spectacular known thus far," containing a niche pottery urn (a figure in Rain-God headdress), a hieroglyph-covered door slab, and murals of a ritual procession, dated to early Monte Albán III-B, c. AD 500. Tomb 5 at Cerro de la Campana (Suchilquitongo, Etla Valley), dating to the early seventh century, was reused for two centuries or more; tomb re-entry and redecoration was common practice in Oaxaca in this period; its entrance carries a monumental name glyph ("10 Alligator") and jamb figures, with an accompanying genealogical stela — a format later central to Epiclassic elite monuments elsewhere.
- **Epiclassic decline**: Monte Albán was largely abandoned before AD 800, with no evidence of conflagration (unlike the roughly contemporary destruction at Teotihuacan); decline was more gradual than Teotihuacan's, and many but not all other Valley regional centers declined simultaneously. The secondary center Cerro de la Campana declined in the same period; the Noriega Stone there (per Javier Urcid) records early rituals and the accession of a ruler named "6 Owl." The Ñuiñe style/script flourished late in the Valley's western lowlands, showing ties to [[sites/xochicalco]], the Guatemalan Pacific coast, and the Gulf Coast; sites in the Río Verde drainage on the Pacific coast erected Monte-Albán-derived monuments with highland connections. Later Mixtecs reused old Zapotec tomb sites as consecrated ground.

## Significance

Monte Albán is the type site of [[cultures/zapotec-civilization]] and the source of the earliest secure writing and Calendar Round dating evidence in the Valley of Oaxaca; its founding is treated by Coe & Koontz as an unusually rapid, deliberate act of political centralization rather than gradual growth.

## Key Debates

- **[POSITION]** Whether Teotihuacan directly controlled Monte Albán at some point during the Classic period: Coe & Koontz note the suggestion exists in the literature but describe supporting evidence as "thin," and record that no major Classic-period population shift occurred at Monte Albán itself.
- **[POSITION]** The Building J ballcourt's possible function in adjudicating inter-polity conflict is speculative (unnamed researchers, per Coe & Koontz), not established.
