# Piedras Negras

Largest [[classic-maya]] city on the Usumacinta, in western Petén, Guatemala, about 40 km downriver from [[yaxchilan]]. Ancient name *Yokib’* (“entrance”)—Sharer & Traxler (2006) connect the name to a sinkhole or to strategic position on the Tabasco route. As of [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]], Piedras Negras and Yaxchilan were the two most powerful Late Classic Usumacinta kingdoms; archaeology is more complete here than at Yaxchilan.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Sharer & Traxler (2006) report that the site’s unbroken sequence of *hotun*-ending monuments AD 608–810 (22 consecutive) provided the foundation for Tatiana Proskouriakoff’s breakthrough demonstration that Maya texts deal with historical events—accession niche motif; birth and death glyphs; reigns within a human lifetime. She analyzed niche stelae groups set every five *tuns*; identified “initial date” (T740 “upended frog” = birth, *siyaj*) 12–31 years before “inaugural date” (T684 “toothache” = accession/*joy*); each group spans ≤60 years (one lifetime); male figure = ruler; females = wives/daughters with *ix*/*ixik* titles. Sylvanus Morley urged the University of Pennsylvania project (J. Alden Mason and Linton Satterthwaite, 1931–39). A BYU / Universidad del Valle project under Stephen Houston and Héctor Escobedo in the 1990s, with settlement research, refined the Early Classic termination and Late Classic refounding model. Charles Golden’s work between Piedras Negras and Yaxchilan defines their boundary via subordinate-site patterning. Feather work is known from Bonampak murals and Piedras Negras Wall Panel 3; pyrite mirrors appear among prestige goods.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]] unless noted.)*

- **Late Preclassic–Early Classic:** Occupation from the Late Preclassic; four Early Classic rulers known retrospectively. By ~550–600, Early Classic West Group/Acropolis structures were demolished with ritual destruction (offerings over burned structures)—around the start of K’inich Yo’nal Ahk I (603–639). Houston and Golden propose he refounded the dynasty after a defeat ended the earlier line, destroying earlier elite architecture as termination ritual.
- **Rulers 2–4: wars, marriage, *sajal* hierarchy:** Ruler 2 accession 639, death 686; wars including Santa Elena 662; Panel 2 recalls Early Classic Turtle Tooth receiving captives under an *ochk’in kaloomte’* (possibly Tikal-related). K’inich Yo’nal Ahk II (Ruler 3) accession 687; married Lady K’atun Ajaw of Namaan (portrayed on three stelae; family portrait Stela 3 with daughter); temporary loss of La Mar; a *sajal* captured by Palenque ~725; capture of a Yaxchilan *sajal* 726; Burial 5 under Str. J-5 with inscribed bloodletters and shell. Organizational hierarchy of *ajawab’* and *sajalob’* administered subdivisions under the king.
- **Rulers 4–7; Yaxchilan entanglement:** Panel 3 (Ruler 7) recalls Ruler 4’s first *k’atun* (749) with Yopaat Balam II of Yaxchilan as visitor; the text suggests Piedras Negras oversaw an ancestor’s Yaxchilan inauguration and supported Yopaat Balam II—a Piedras Negras view of history; no Yaxchilan texts for him survive (expunged if he ruled). Ruler 4 death/burial 757; Burial 13 before Str. O-13 matches *och’ k’ak’* fire ritual (adult male + two juveniles, bones burned/absent). Yo’nal Ahk III (758–ca. 767); Ha’ K’in Xook and Ruler 7 continue the Late Classic sequence. Scaffold elevation of rulers is shown at San Bartolo and Piedras Negras among Classic accession iconography for the [[kuhul-ajaw]].
- **Fall to Yaxchilan:** Dating to 808, Yaxchilan Lintel 10 identifies Ruler 7 as the *b’aak* (captive) of K’inich Tatb’u Skull III of Yaxchilan. Sharer & Traxler judge it likely that Yaxchilan’s victory ended Piedras Negras as a major power. Hotun monuments run through 810. Terminal Classic trade-network realignment models place Usumacinta capitals among those bypassed by coastal routes.

## Significance

Piedras Negras is the type site for the historical decipherment of Maya inscriptions: without its regular *hotun* stelae series, Proskouriakoff’s demonstration that texts record kings’ lives might not have crystallized when it did. Politically it is the northern/downriver pole of the Usumacinta rivalry with Yaxchilan, with a clear archaeological boundary, nested *ajaw*/*sajal* administration, and riverine trade/defense siting. Ritual deposits (Burial 5 bloodletters; Burial 13 fire entrance) and accession iconography feed models of [[kuhul-ajaw]] practice. It also illustrates size ≠ power: larger than Yaxchilan yet finally conquered by it.

## Key Debates

- **Dynastic refounding ca. 603:** Houston & Golden’s model of Early Classic termination and K’inich Yo’nal Ahk I’s refoundation vs continuous dynasty with selective destruction.
- **Piedras Negras claims over Yaxchilan succession:** Panel 3’s support for Yopaat Balam II vs silence/erasure at Yaxchilan—whose version of Mid-8th-century politics is reliable.
- **Boundary maintenance:** Golden’s settlement-pattern boundary between the two kingdoms as a rare archaeological demonstration of Classic polity borders.
- **Trade realignment and collapse:** Parallel riverine-decline scenarios for Usumacinta sites (Palenque, Piedras Negras, Yaxchilan, Quirigua, Copan) as inland routes lost ground to Yucatan coastal commerce.
- **Scaffold/accession iconography** and *hotun* vs *k’atun* period-ending frequency as local ritual variation within the Classic calendrical system.

## Historiography

Evidence quality is unusually high for both epigraphy (continuous *hotun* monuments) and 20th–21st-century excavation (Penn 1930s; Houston–Escobedo 1990s). Proskouriakoff’s Rulers 1–6 sequence remains the backbone of the historical skeleton Sharer & Traxler report; later work by Houston, Golden, and others revises Early Classic political rupture and inter-polity boundaries. Feather-work and mirror finds place Piedras Negras in prestige-good networks from the Middle Preclassic tradition of reflective authority symbols. Capture of Ruler 7 in 808 is known from the victor’s monument at Yaxchilan—an asymmetric documentary situation typical of Classic war narratives.
