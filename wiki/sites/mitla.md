# Mitla

Mitla, roughly 25 miles (40 km) southwest of Oaxaca City, was a new Zapotec center that
emerged in the early Post-Classic period. Its Nahuatl name derives from *Mictlan* ("Land
of the Dead"), while the Zapotec name was *Lyobaá* ("Place of Rest") (Coe & Koontz
2013).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Coe & Koontz (2013) do not detail a specific excavation history for Mitla in the
material extracted from this source; the site's principal documented account is
ethnohistorical rather than archaeological (see Historiography below).

## Occupation Sequence

Construction is thought to date to the Monte Albán V period, corresponding to the
Toltec and Aztec eras; the site was still occupied when the Spanish arrived. Mitla
comprises five architectural groups: three Post-Classic palaces and two reused
Classic-period temple precincts, with a fortified stronghold on a nearby hill guarding
the Post-Classic complex. The Group of the Columns features long masonry halls with
geometric step-and-fret stone mosaic veneer set in red stucco.

## Significance

Mitla is among the best-known examples of Post-Classic Zapotec monumental architecture
and is the subject of one of the fullest early-Colonial ethnohistorical descriptions of
a Mesoamerican priestly center. Father Burgoa's 17th-century account, as relayed by
Coe & Koontz, describes Mitla as the residence of the Zapotec High Priest (*Vuijatao*,
"Great Seer"), with separate precincts for the High Priest, secondary priests, the
Zapotec king's court, and royal officials and military. Burgoa reported continuous
human sacrifice to the High Priest and the gods, and a legendary (unlocated) underground
catacomb housing royal, noble, and hero burials — giving the site its "Land of the Dead"
name. This account should be treated as a single early-Colonial ethnohistorical source,
not verified by archaeological excavation.

## Key Debates

None recorded from this source; the Burgoa account's reliability as a description of
pre-Conquest practice versus early-Colonial elaboration is an open source-reliability
question not further adjudicated by Coe & Koontz.
