# La Tène

## Discovery and Excavation History

Lakeside site on the northwest corner of **Lake Neuchâtel**, Switzerland; **eponymous type-site** of the [[la-tene-culture]].

Cunliffe (1997): discovered **1857** by **Hansli Kopp** (wooden piles; about 40 iron weapons at first recovery). Exploration continued sporadically until **1917**. Site interpretations have varied: pile dwelling, fortified [[oppida|oppidum]], bridge overwhelmed in catastrophe, or **platform for ritual depositions**. Assemblage rich in weapons (swords, spears, shields), personal items (fibulae, belt clasps, razors), bronze vessels, iron ingots, and vehicle parts. Widely known in the 1860s via Ferdinand Keller presenting it as a Celtic-period lake village; material differed from [[hallstatt]] and resembled northern French cemetery finds.

The 1871 Bologna congress recognition that Champagne graves, La Tène, and Marzabotto burials matched fixed La Tène-type finds as the material culture of historic Celts (Mortillet, Desor) — a foundational culture=ethnonym equation that later method must keep under critical control ([[celticity-debate]]).

## Occupation Sequence

Primarily known for **votive/deposit assemblages** of the La Tène Iron Age rather than as a classic stratified town sequence. Cunliffe’s preferred functional reading of the principal structure: a **timber pier** running into the lake as a platform from which votive offerings were thrown into the water. Counts he reports: **269 spearheads, 166 swords, 29 shields**, hundreds of brooches, plus yokes, cauldrons, ingots, knives, and other items. Depositions accumulated over time (seasonal or event-driven), not as a single act.

Parallels Cunliffe cites for watery/bridge deposition: Fiskerton (Witham); Flag Fen platform/bridge deposits (longer Bronze–Iron Age span); Llyn Cerrig Bach (Anglesey) late first century BCE / early first century CE war gear. Deliberate bending of swords before deposition is part of the ritual pattern Cunliffe invokes when reading classical remarks on “bent” Celtic swords (e.g. Polybius at Telamon).

## Significance

- **Names** the La Tène culture and the art-historical styles central to debates over [[celtic-art]] and [[celticity-debate]].
- Anchors the **later European Iron Age** half of Hildebrand’s bipartition and Reinecke’s A–D system — the material horizon classically tied to Celtic migrations and historical Gauls/Galatians without equating assemblage to people.
- Premier case for **ritual watery deposition** of warrior gear in temperate Europe; tradition of deep chronological depth (Cunliffe: gods’ need for war gear in watery places from the sixteenth century BCE into Roman conquest zones) and not the sole preserve of “Celtic” regions (e.g. Hjortspring).
- Weapon typology from the site (including notched spearheads matching Diodorus) is load-bearing for cross-class reading of classical ethnography against material culture.

## Key Debates

- **Deposit interpretation:** warfare dump, sanctuary/votive programme, catastrophic bridge collapse, or combinations — Cunliffe privileges the votive pier model while recording alternatives.
- **Relationship of type-site assemblage to pan-European style:** the site labels a vast cultural continuum; local Swiss deposition is not the geographic origin of all La Tène forms (Early Style centres on Marne–Moselle–Bohemia).
- **Culture = Celts equation** founded partly on this site’s comparison with Champagne and Marzabotto — still debated under [[celticity-debate]]; La Tène material is not coterminous with Celtic language or classical ethnonym (Cunliffe).
