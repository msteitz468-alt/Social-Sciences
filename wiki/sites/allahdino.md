# Allahdino

Small Harappan-phase settlement near Karachi (Pakistan), excavated by [[fairservis-walter|Walter Fairservis]] in 1975–76. In [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Kenoyer 1998]] it is a type case for **rural elite wealth** and for the reach of [[standardized-weights]] and long-carnelian ornament systems beyond the great cities.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Fairservis’s short campaign recovered the famous jar hoard and a suite of cubical chert weights later illustrated in Kenoyer’s synthesis (Fig. 5.28). Relative to multi-decade work at [[harappa]] or [[mohenjo-daro]], the sample is small; reliability rests on published hoard context and weight metrology rather than a fully published city plan.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Kenoyer 1998 citing Fairservis.)*

- Small settlement within the Mature Harappan interaction sphere — not a major urban center.
- **Weights:** Cubical chert weights conforming to the pan-Indus binary/decimal system, used by Kenoyer to show that even smaller excavated settlements participated in the standard metrology (heavier weights concentrated at trading centers such as [[chanhudaro]], Mohenjo-daro, and Harappa).
- **Water:** Cited among sites where wells served small settlements or gardens (Kenoyer Ch. 8 agricultural water control).
- **Hoard (key find):** One of only five major jewelry hoards known from the entire Indus region (as of Kenoyer 1998). Contents include: folded belt/necklace of **36 long carnelian beads** with bronze spacers (Kenoyer: each bead many days’ work; belt ~480 person-days for one worker); multistrand silver bead necklaces with moon-shaped clasp; silver wire coils/rings (14–16 spirals); agate beads; crumpled gold fillets and gold in recycling stages (paper-thin hollow beads folded triangular; melted/hammered lumps). Catalogue Cat. nos. 48–51 (Dept. of Archaeology, Karachi).

## Significance

- Demonstrates that **hereditary high-value ornaments** and standard weights reached small sites, not only citadel elites of the largest cities.
- Links rural consumption of long carnelian to the confirmed manufacturing chain at [[chanhudaro]] and to Mesopotamian finds of similar beads (Ur).
- Gold-recycling debris in the jar shows active metal reworking even outside major urban goldsmith quarters.

## Key Debates

### Goldsmith stock vs family wealth

Kenoyer **contrasts** big-city hoards (read as goldsmith stock and partly processed material) with Allahdino: a small settlement is “unlikely” to have supported a jeweler; more plausible is hereditary ornaments of a woman or family hidden for safekeeping. Failure to reclaim the hoard (as with the Early Harappan Kunal silver hoard) suggests “the fate of such wealthy people was not always predictable” (Kenoyer 1998, attributed).

This interpretation is theoretical — alternative loss/ritual-deposition models are not ruled out by the published evidence alone.

## Historiography

Allahdino’s fame is almost entirely the Fairservis hoard plus Kenoyer’s synthesis. The site remains thin for settlement-scale social reconstruction; use it for craft/trade and wealth-storage arguments, not as a demographic type-site.
