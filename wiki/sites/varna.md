# Varna

## Discovery and Excavation History

Colin Renfrew’s "Varna and the emergence of wealth in prehistoric Europe" (*[[appadurai-social-life-of-things-1986|The Social Life of Things]]*, 1986) treats the **Varna I** Chalcolithic cemetery (Bulgaria; earlier, less rich Varna II nearby). Gold gravegoods discovered about a decade before the essay. Excavation and cataloguing are those of Bulgarian excavators Renfrew cites — excavation quality is a reliability fact of that campaign, not reassessed here.

## Occupation Sequence

Chalcolithic cemetery use. Renfrew stresses Balkan copper metallurgy long preceded Aegean florescence; at Varna, gold takes pride of place for display (“chrysolithic” as much as chalcolithic); copper moves from decorative (Varna II) toward useful forms (chisels, shaft-hole axes) at Varna I, with stone axes still in poorer graves and ceremonial gold-sheathed stone in elite contexts *(Renfrew)*.

## Significance

For SLOT / [[regimes-of-value]]: archaeological case of **prestige, wealth, and ranking** without ethnographic price talk. Renfrew’s theses *(attributed)*:

- High value of gold at Varna must be **argued**, not assumed.
- Early metallurgy is **social before productive** — prestige-before-utility for copper/gold.
- Metal prestige goods **actively form ranking** rather than only reflecting it; ranking and new commodities for expressing status emerge together in a previously more egalitarian-looking Balkan Chalcolithic.
- After Appadurai’s suggestion, Marxian value triad needs **“prime value”** (ascribed intrinsic value) for prestige materials.
- **Wealth** as ownership of valuables, not only generosity-obligation models.
- Completed [[commoditization]] of metal when it circulates as raw-material **ingots**; contrast singular prestige objects *(with Kopytoff)*.
- Predicts gold source ≤ ~50 km from Varna (local polity), while shell (*Spondylus*) shows wider exchange networks.

## Key Debates

Whether Varna indicates a **chiefdom** (Renfrew raises as possibility with caution); inherited vs achieved status (rich children’s graves); sex of high-status burials; later archaeometallurgical and aDNA work may revise 1986 chronology and social models — **date-stamp: as of Renfrew 1986**.

## Identity Cautions

Archaeological cemetery ≠ named people, language, or genetic population. No silent equation across the four-way non-identity boundary.

### Cunliffe (2001) — brief pointer

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]], *[[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Facing the Ocean]]* 2001.)* In passing, Cunliffe notes the Varna cemetery (Bulgarian Black Sea coast, c.4000 BC) produced roughly 6 kg of gold and large quantities of copper — including cast shaft-hole axes/adzes functioning as prestige symbols rather than tools — deposited with elite dead in a display of conspicuous consumption, and suggests south-east European copper production developed a separate momentum from Anatolia/the Near East by the fifth millennium.

### Sherratt (1982)

*(Attributed — [[sherratt-andrew]], *[[sherratt-economy-society-prehistoric-europe-1997]]*, Ch.10. )* Varna (with Tibava) appears among cases of later Neolithic/Copper Age **inequality** in livestock-and-prestige exchange systems on the SE European circuit — not a full cemetery reanalysis. Status as of Sherratt 1982 synthesis; compare Renfrew 1986 layer above.

## Gimbutas densification — *Civilization of the Goddess* (1991)

*(All claims in this section attributed to [[gimbutas-marija]], *[[gimbutas-civilization-of-the-goddess-1991|The Civilization of the Goddess]]* 1991. FLAG source: communal temple metal ownership vs private wealth; Kurgan contact stimulating private ownership; peaceful metallurgy “not for weapons of war”; Wave 1 catastrophic replacement — all **theses**. Site/cemetery ≠ named ethnos, language, or genetic population. Links: [[old-europe]], [[kurgan-hypothesis]], [[old-europe-goddess-civilization-debate]], [[cucuteni-trypillia-culture]], [[vinca-culture]].)*

### Material inventory and trading-center role (as of Gimbutas 1991)

Gimbutas reports unprecedented wealth of copper, gold, Aegean *Spondylus*/dentalium, marble, and gold- and graphite-decorated vases. Inventory figures she cites: around **3,000** gold objects and **more than fifty** copper tools (hammer-axes, flat axes, chisels, awls, needles); beads, bracelets, rings, perforated convex plates, bull figurines, solid gold axes with gold-tube shafts, diadems, religious symbols; **>20,000** *Spondylus* and dentalium shells imported from the Aegean (presumed exchange for metal tools to Cyclades/Aegean coast); marble conical bowls/rhytons likely from Cyclades; transparent obsidian from Tokay (NE Hungary); graphite cone from Stara Zagora area; mid-5th mill. B.C. (c. **45th–44th centuries** for some graves). Gold-painted pottery in Grave 1 — presumed powdered gold post-firing. Outside Varna: gold bracelets and ring pendants in Lower Danube; some hammered-sheet gold pendants as far as southern Peloponnese.

She suggests Varna “may have functioned as a center where different cultures came to exchange goods from their respective regions in a form of free market” (**thesis**). Massive Varna gold treated as specialist (temple workshop) products in her craft-specialization discussion; Hotnica temple (N Bulgaria) parallel for serial bracelets and abstract Goddess plate images. **Metallurgy thesis:** copper and gold manufactured tools, ornaments, jewelry, and ritual equipment, “**not for weapons of war**” — load-bearing peaceful-Old-Europe claim; contested classification of shaft-hole axes etc. as non-weapons. Aligns with Renfrew’s prestige-before-utility frame above on **display value of gold**, without adopting Renfrew’s ranking-formation model wholesale.

### Social reading: Black Sea exception (thesis)

Only marked social-structure change along Black Sea coast Bulgaria after **4500 B.C.** in her Ch. 9 synthesis: Varna, Durankulak, Devnja cemeteries show adult-male dominance more overt; researchers see “incipient patriarchal tendencies.” She attributes the change to rapidly rising **trade with Dnieper-Volga steppe populations** wedging west of the Black Sea — **not invaders** at this stage (finds local manufacture).

**Grave 43:** male 40–50 with extraordinary gold (3 necklaces, 3 massive arm rings per arm, 2 earrings per ear, 6 hair rings, discs, plates, beads; gold total **1,011** objects; 28 find categories) plus copper/flint/stone weapons and tools; two other very rich male graves in a **190-grave** cemetery; female and symbolic (**cenotaph**) graves also very rich, but male graves “unsurpassed” in gold amassed. Devnja: three adult males with copper/gold, females without gold. Tools, weapons, ornaments in these male graves express personal status/power “entirely unknown in all other Old European societies.” **Key thesis:** “The prominent males in the Varna society were **not chieftains of the Indo-European type** and were not buried separately with pomp and human or animal sacrifices” — probably **commerce overseers**; richness ≠ necessarily dominant/autocratic political role. Interior N Bulgaria (Vinica) continues more even male/female wealth in her contrast case.

**Communal vs private metal (thesis):** elsewhere she surmises metal artifacts (mostly ritual) stored in temples/vases were distributed by temple representatives and **owned communally**; after mid-5th mill. on the Black Sea littoral, Varna’s extraordinarily rich individual male and female graves mark change; geographical location suggests stimulation by contact with Kurgan people “for whom private ownership was the norm.” Life-sized clay masks (e.g. Grave 2) with gold diadem, mouth/teeth, ring pendants she places among stiff-nude / White Death Goddess examples (**figurine typology thesis**). Compare Renfrew’s ranking/prime-value theses and Sherratt’s inequality circuit — three attributed models, no wiki-voice verdict.

### Demise — Kurgan Wave 1 and Cernavoda I (Gimbutas thesis)

Under the [[kurgan-hypothesis]] Wave 1 (c. **4400–4300 B.C.**), the Varna littoral is among the groups **most affected** (with Karanovo-Gumelniţa, [[vinca-culture|Vinča]], Lengyel; [[cucuteni-trypillia-culture|Cucuteni]] survives). First half 4th mill.: Black Sea coastal Varna culture **replaced** in E Romania and Bulgaria by Kurganish **Cernavoda I**: fortified sites on high river terraces; few small surface/semisubterranean dwellings (sites generally ≤100×200 m); stock including horse, hunting, fishing, primitive agriculture; gray badly baked crushed-shell ceramics with stab-and-drag, knobs, cord/fingernail/shell impressions; **no painted pottery**; few stylized figurines; Old European symbolic designs disappeared; no cereal grains despite hoes/grindstones/sickle blades; ubiquitous horse bones; tools mostly bone + maceheads, perforated hammer-axes, flint scrapers/knives, few copper awls/chisels. Disruption of Varna, Karanovo, and Vinča triggers dislocation cascade west and north in her narrative. **Record as invasion/replacement thesis**, not established site biography; later chronology and social models may supersede 1991.

## Whittle densification (1996) — ancestral-veneration reading as POSITION

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 4–5. Mid-1990s cal BC; pre-aDNA. **Do not promote Whittle’s anti-hierarchy / ancestral-veneration reading to wiki voice** — it is one POSITION against Renfrew-style ranking and Gimbutas-style elite/Kurgan frames already on this page.)*

### Discovery, scale, regional setting (as of Whittle 1996)

Whittle reports discovery **1972** by chance above the former Varna estuary; meticulous excavation to **1986** found ~**281** graves, mid–later **5th millennium BC**; large, multi-period use; men, women, children; some gender differentiation; empty graves/cenotaphs; nearby pile-dwellings poorly investigated. He places Varna among large extramural burial grounds of the Black Sea / NE Bulgaria “**new history**” zone (with Durankulak >1000 graves, Cernavoda), sharing material culture with the **Karanovo VI–Kodžadermen–Gumelnița** bloc and ordered tells with palisades/ditches (Poljanica, Ovčarovo, etc.)—not as an isolated anomaly. Smaller NE Bulgarian cemeteries (Golyamo Delčevo, Vinica, Devnya, etc.) hold only a tiny % of settlement populations and show limited rank differentiation in his reading—**communities of the dead not strongly internally ranked**, matching living communities (**position**).

### Conventional hierarchy reading (others) vs Whittle’s challenge

Abundance and nature of goods in some graves and treatment of some cenotaphs prompted the view that the cemetery reflected a **markedly divided society** serving a very wide area—implying extensive social/political control. Whittle asks whether that interpretation can be sustained and rejects over-general Copper Age packages: permanent sedentism → craft specialisation → accumulation → social differentiation; Varna as major power differences; priests/chiefs/state-like society (**positions he resists**). Compare Renfrew’s ranking/prime-value theses and Gimbutas’s commerce-overseer / private-ownership theses above—**three attributed models, no wiki-voice verdict**.

### Grave 43 (inventory as of Whittle 1996)

Whittle’s inventory: man ~40–50, extended, head N; pots by head/chest; copper axe and stone perforated hammer-axe with beaten gold shaft (“sceptre”); heavy gold rings both upper arms; gold discs/beads/appliqués on clothing head to legs; beaten gold tube interpreted as penis cover by right hip; gold discs by knees + copper hammer-axe, axe, chisel, awl, needle + stone axe; **~990 gold objects >1500 g**; bone and *Spondylus* ornaments—only extended burial with such exceptional abundance/variety in his account.

**Whittle’s POSITION against simple elite reading:** against burial of a socially pre-eminent lineage head at peak competition. Scale of difference (six hammer-axes/axes vs one as male badge elsewhere); gold soft/easy to work—abundance may mean different value; gold in only **61** graves, with **34 cenotaphs holding ~60% of the gold**—special significance not simple wealth. Alternative: man of **religious/symbolic importance for the whole community** in life or death; goods as gifts of the living from a wider group than the family; mediation with ancestors as concern of the whole community.

### Cenotaphs and mask graves — ancestral veneration POSITION

~**56** empty/cenotaph graves: ~10 with some human bones (possible secondary burial); ~**40** well-provided empties (gold, copper, flint, *Spondylus*, pots)—style like the main tradition; possible substitute burials or ancestral rites. Graves **2, 3, 15** mask graves with life-size clay faces gold-ornamented (diadem, ear rings, eye discs, mouth strip, amulets)—excavator female-associated. Graves **1, 4, 36** exceptionally rich empties (e.g. grave 4 ~**1500 g** gold; grave 36 **857** objects ~**790 g** incl. astragalus, animal figures, cross-headed sceptre)—goods placed relative to imaginary bodies; red ochre and black organic matter; phased ritualised fills.

**Whittle’s POSITION:** more plausible as **ancestor worship** than as absent travellers’ graves; founding ancestors enhanced over time; offering pits possibly long open; Varna a testament **more to the spiritual world than to secular wealth or internal divisions**. Cenotaphs exist elsewhere but none so furnished; shorter regional history (memories of beginnings, descent tracing) + settlement mobility cycles make ancestral fixing to places important; few known settlements in the immediate Varna region. Either the cemetery is the key to a social-difference scale otherwise unsupported, **or** (Whittle) an **extreme form of a wider ancestral-veneration pattern** rooted in historical circumstance.

### Copper, gold, exchange (findings + metal theory)

As of Whittle 1996: copper production in SE Europe probably began **independently** of SW Asian metallurgy (even if the idea was borrowed); by the 5th mill. SE metallurgy possibly more sophisticated than SW Asia; simple 6th mill. beads/hooks; heavy shaft-hole hammer-axes require casting (>1000°C). **Varna: 82/281 graves with copper**; Durankulak 99/>1000; men/women/children receive copper; Varna shaft-hole axes largely men’s graves; Varna area without local source but plentifully supplied from central-southern Bulgaria. Gold techniques simple (beating); Varna greatest range/forms; major 5th mill. gold findspot (**~6 kg** in graves); gold rare Greece, central-S Bulgaria, Vinča area, forest-steppe. Graphite-painted Karanovo VI–Gumelnița wares; gold-painted dish in grave 43 (method unknown).

**Metal theory (Whittle POSITION):** copper and gold acquired to be **used, displayed, worn, given away—not accumulated**; rarity of hoards suggests interrupted exchange cycles; private accumulation unlikely given shared tell space/labour ethic; metal as constituent of social transactions; vigorous acquisition in regions of “newer histories”; copper transformations as metaphor for domestication/fertility; grave goods as offerings to ancestors; rivalry if any for esteem/reputation not personal accumulation—honouring ancestors (all but one of the richest Varna graves in his reading) brings honour on self.

*Spondylus* (ultimate Aegean source), copper, and Melian obsidian mark long-range networks; workshop-specialisation claims elsewhere he doubts when excavation is limited. Ceramic “Varna group” vs Karanovo VI bloc: shared identity ideas without political unity (**position**).

### Aftermath notes (Ch.5)

Black Sea coast cemeteries/tells largely **lapse** in the ~4000–3500 BC reconfiguration; sea-level rise (Varna estuary / Durankulak partly below present; end 5th mill. sea ~6–7 m lower) he treats as incomplete climatic explanation—mainly relocate coastal sites, not cause inland tell abandonment. Gold scarce post-Varna in transition/EBA Bulgaria and N Greece—supply/exchange reorientation (possible Mesopotamian urban pull; copper sources shift Balkan→Caucasus by Yamnaya horizon) may be **symptom as much as cause** (**position**). Conceptual shift from copper-as-fertility metaphors toward animal burials expressing similar ideas is his alternative to steppe intrusion as primary driver of changing metal acquisition.
