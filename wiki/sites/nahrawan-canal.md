# Nahrawan Canal

The giant Tigris-fed irrigation system east of the Tigris — the largest
hydraulic work of the ancient Diyala region and the type case, in
[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]]'s analysis, of a state-built irrigation regime that
outlived the administrative capacity needed to maintain it.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Traced by [[jacobsen-thorkild|Thorkild Jacobsen]]'s 1936–37 reconnaissance and
systematically surveyed in 1957–58 by the Diyala Basin Archaeological Project
([[adams-land-behind-baghdad-study|Land Behind Baghdad]]). The project excavated
the massive Sasanian weir at Al-Qantara (site 718), found beneath a later
Islamic weir, and cut sections across the Nahrawan bed showing the main canal
never recovered its former volume after the deliberate breach of 937 CE.
Repeated field re-traverses disentangled the succession of canal networks from
superimposed levees, brick sizes in offtakes, and terminal-occupation pottery —
a procedure that became a charter demonstration of [[archaeological-survey]] on
canal landscapes.

## Occupation Sequence

Adams reported (1965) that the system's Islamic-period nomenclature divided it
into the Katul (Samarra to Baʿquba), the Tamarra (Baʿquba to Nahrawan town),
and the Nahrawan proper below. No contemporary record names the builder of the
Katul al-Kisrawi ("Cut-of-Chosroes") feeder; Adams followed Jacobsen in
attributing it inferentially to Chosroes I Anosharwan (531–579 CE), and flagged
as "admittedly inconclusive" a possible Parthian anticipation of the lower
course (the Medar canal). Estimated maximum feeder capacity was ~250–300 cumecs
from channel width, though slope evidence suggests sustained flow nearer 50–100
cumecs; Adams argued only a fraction of design capacity was ever carried. The
system underwrote the Sasanian settlement maximum on the plains (~437 sites,
~3,489 built-up ha) and, per early Abbasid tax calculations, ~5,000–6,000 sq km
of cultivation.

Decline was punctuated, not gradual: Ibn Raʾiq deliberately breached the canal
in 937 in a failed defensive maneuver, cutting off the lower Diyala's water and
contributing to the Baghdad famine of 946; Muʿizz al-Daulah closed the breaches
after twenty years, but flow never fully recovered. The last serious repair
attempt was by the engineer Mujahid al-Din Buhriz in 1140. Yaqut (ca. 1224)
described the region as ruined mounds, blaming Saljuk-era warring sultans
interested only in "collect[ing] taxes and consum[ing] them." Final abandonment
came by ca. 1150, with ʿAberta lingering as the last outpost on an irregular
trickle.

## Significance

The Nahrawan anchors Adams's second irrigation "epoch": a landscape-scale,
state-financed system that made near-total cultivation of the basin possible
while making prosperity "peculiarly dependent on the central government."
Adams argued its weakness was organizational, not material — "the powerful,
centralized administrative apparatus that was needed for their maintenance
turned out to be less durable than the irrigation works themselves" — and
noted that surviving Sasanian sources name no royal irrigation officials and
the cleared sluices lack closing mechanisms, suggesting water allocation
remained decentralized even as construction required central initiative. The
breach-driven, localized pattern of medieval abandonment (versus the gradual,
uniform second-millennium-BCE declines) is his key evidence that artificial
maximum-scale irrigation created a new kind of systemic fragility. See
[[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] and [[adams-land-behind-baghdad-1965]].

## Key Debates

- [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate]] — the Nahrawan is the empirical centerpiece
  of Adams's argument that maximal irrigation *followed* strong states rather
  than generating them, and that dependence ran state→irrigation, inverting
  Wittfogel's causal arrow.
