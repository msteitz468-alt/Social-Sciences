# Exmoor

Sandstone, shale, and slate upland of North Devon / northwest Somerset, studied by [[tilley-christopher]] (with Wayne Bennett) in *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010|Interpreting Landscapes]]* (2010, Ch. 7). Defining traits for Tilley: **absence of rock exposures** except coastal cliffs and sparse valley/stream beds; near-invisible lithic "monuments."

## Discovery and Excavation History

Quinnell and Dunn's patient recording of stone settings underpins Tilley's second-phase walking interpretation. Systematic survey with Bennett 2007–08 after familiarisation walks 2004–06. Gillings (Lancombe III) and related unpublished work cited.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed, Tilley 2010.)* Sparse prehistoric settlement relative to Dartmoor/Bodmin; possible Neolithic lacuna and continued hunter-gatherer lifestyle into the Bronze Age. Monument contrast: monumental ridgetop barrows vs miniaturised geometric stone settings at valley heads and high interior land.

## Significance

**"Stalking with stones" thesis (attributed):** settings as hunter-related places linked to red-deer continuity and landscape-body metaphor — not monumental display. Comparative Ch. 10: Exmoor lacks Bodmin-style perspectival sequencing on rows/circles. Anchors sandstone/slate strand of [[geological-identity]].

## Key Debates

Interpretive hunting reading is Tilley's; sparse dating and near-invisible monuments limit verification. Four-way non-identity applies.

## Historiography

First-phase recorders (Quinnell et al.) acknowledged as unsung heroes; Tilley 2010 as rethinking relations among monuments and landforms.
