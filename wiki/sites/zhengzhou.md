# Zhengzhou (Cheng-chou) Shang City

## Discovery and Excavation History

Shang remains at Cheng-chou (northern Henan) were discovered in 1950 and were still under
excavation when [[chang-kwang-chih|Chang]] wrote in the early 1960s. As reported by An
Chin-huai (quoted by Chang), Shang settlement spread more or less continuously over ~40 km²
around the ancient city site. Chang noted the "center of Shang studies has tended to shift
from [[anyang|An-yang]] to Cheng-chou, where a longer sequence has been uncovered" — the
Cheng-chou stratigraphy giving a fuller Early-Shang-into-later sequence than Anyang alone.

## Occupation Sequence

Before the Shang occupation the Cheng-chou area held both [[yangshao-culture|Yangshao]] and
[[lungshan-culture|Lungshanoid]] farmers; Chang read the Shang settlement as evolving
immediately out of, and apparently from, the Honan Lungshanoid phase. He recorded a **five-phase
stratigraphic sequence**: Shang-chieh (earliest, on the Lungshanoid/Shang borderline — no
metal, Lungshanoid-like ceramics) → Lo-ta-miao → Lower Erh-li-kang → Upper Erh-li-kang → Jen-min
Park (latest). Bronze appears and the settlement expands suddenly in the Lower Erh-li-kang
phase, which also has the city wall.

## Significance

- **The city wall.** Roughly rectangular, perimeter ~7,195 m, enclosing 3.2 km²; surviving max
  height 9.1 m and base width 36 m, built in successive compressed *hang-t'u* layers ~8–10 cm
  thick with visible pestle depressions. An Chin-huai's labor estimate: ~1,439,000 m³ of
  compressed soil, "no less than 18 years' labor with 10,000 workers" at 330 days/year — a
  measure of the political capacity behind Early Shang urbanism.
- **Craft production.** Two bronze foundries (one ~1,050 m² south of the wall, with crucibles,
  clay molds, and charcoal); a bone workshop with 1,000+ finished artifacts (bones ~50% human);
  a pottery-kiln quarter of 14 kilns; and possible wine-making jars — a picture of full-time,
  spatially segregated craft specialization.
- **Possibly the Shang capital Ao.** Chang judged it "highly probable that the traditional Shang
  capital of Ao was located here," while treating the identification as a literary-historical
  attribution rather than an established fact.

Grave differentiation (furnished "Large Graves," two with human sacrifice, vs. numerous
unfurnished "Small Graves"), turtle-shell oracle bones appearing by the Jen-min Park phase, and
bronze ritual vessels together mark, for Chang, a stratified urban society performing "all of the
essential functions of a city" despite lacking the sheer scale of Ur, Mohenjo-daro, or
Teotihuacan.

## Key Debates

- **Shang boundaries.** Chang held that on archaeological evidence the Shang civilization proper
  was distributed only in western/northern Henan, southern Hebei, and probably western Shandong,
  explicitly rejecting (against Cheng Te-kun) claims of a much larger Shang territory — Shang
  *traits* found farther afield he read as cultural contact, not Shang presence.
- **Cross-site phasing.** Chang criticized over-generalized synchronization schemes (Tsou Heng's,
  Cheng Te-kun's) as premature, recommending analysis be confined to the few well-stratified
  sites.

## Erligang-Period Detail (Liu & Chen 2012)

Liu & Chen (2012) update the picture Chang gave from the 1963 excavation record with subsequent
fieldwork on the [[erligang-culture|Erligang]] (early Shang) city as a whole, rather than the
single wall segment Chang described:

- **City scale.** Zhengzhou Shang City covers ~25 km², comprising a rectangular inner city (~300
  ha) and a nearly round outer city (~18 km²) — considerably larger than the ~7 km-perimeter
  single-wall figure available to Chang. Population is estimated, by extrapolating Erlitou's
  density of 60–100 persons/ha, at 78,000–130,000, with a mean estimate of ~104,000; a separate
  labor estimate holds that ≥10,000 people worked 10 years to build the city walls (as of Liu &
  Chen 2012) — a somewhat larger labor estimate than An Chin-huai's figure reported by Chang, and
  not necessarily reconciled with it.
- **Sacrificial remains.** ~100 human skullcaps, many bearing saw marks, were found in a ditch near
  an architectural foundation, interpreted as remains of human sacrifice conducted near a temple;
  the bone workshop's raw materials included a large proportion of human bone, consistent with
  Chang's report of the bone workshop above but with the skullcap evidence specifically tied to a
  sacrificial context (as of Liu & Chen 2012).
- **Bronze foundries and hoards.** Two Zhengzhou bronze foundries (Nanguanwai and Zijingshan) are
  the only Erligang-culture locations with hard evidence of ritual-vessel casting. Three bronze
  hoards from Zhengzhou's outer city held 28 bronzes totaling more than 500 kg; the largest is a
  fangding tetrapod — a vessel type new at Zhengzhou — measuring 100 cm and weighing 86.4 kg. The
  hoards, buried in haste near the end of the Upper Erligang phase, are interpreted by Liu & Chen
  as safe-keeping caches during unrest, analogous in function to the later Western Zhou Zhouyuan
  hoards.
- **Early writing.** Two inscribed animal-bone fragments (twelve characters and one character
  respectively) were found at Erligang-phase Zhengzhou, stylistically similar to later Shang
  oracle-bone inscriptions (as of Liu & Chen 2012) — among the earliest known instances of this
  script tradition.
- **City decline.** Erligang expansion ceased around 1400 BCE; Zhengzhou and many regional centers
  were abandoned, ending the centralized Erligang political-economic system and giving way to the
  "middle Shang" phase of smaller successor centers (Xiaoshuangqiao, Huanbei Shang City).
- **Capital-name identification.** Liu & Chen note there is no scholarly consensus on which
  historical dynastic capital names (Ao vs. Bo) correspond to Zhengzhou specifically — a more
  cautious position than Chang's "highly probable... Ao" judgment, reflecting the persistence of
  this identification problem across the intervening decades of scholarship.

## Chang — *Shang Civilization* (1980) densification

*(Attributed — [[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Chang 1980]], Ch.5.)*

Chang treats **Cheng-chou as the type site of the Erh-li-kang phase** of Shang civilization and as
the best window on a Shang capital **before** the An-yang move. Hallmarks in his synthesis:
Loehr Styles I–III bronze vessels; hang-t'u city wall; northeast "palace" sector; specialized
workshops (bronze, pottery, bone) distributed around a ~25 km² specialist urban network; graves
with second-level ledges and waist-pit dogs (Pai-chia-chuang and related loci). He summarizes
radiocarbon dates then available (e.g. ZK-177/178) as placing Erh-li-kang roughly in the mid–late
second millennium BCE "(as of Chang 1980)" — provisional even then.

**Capital-name debate.** T'ang Lan's identification with Ao vs Tsou Heng's with Po is recorded;
Chang's "two firm points" separate archaeological phase from dynastic capital-name certainty.
**People's Park** remains are correlated with dynastic Hsiao-t'un / An-yang–period occupation.
Writing at Cheng-chou is limited (potters' marks; debated inscribed bone), underscoring An-yang's
unique epigraphic density.

Chang's larger claim: the Cheng-chou capital form is **ancestral** to the An-yang royal capital
pattern, and the center of gravity of Shang studies shifted from An-yang to Cheng-chou once the
longer sequence was known — a historiographic observation as well as a site claim.
