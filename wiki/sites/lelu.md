## Discovery and Excavation History

Lelu (also spelled Leluh) is a walled monumental complex on a small islet off the point of Kosrae Island, Caroline Islands, Micronesia, and served as the capital and chiefly center of the Kosrae polity. Kosrae itself is a 109 sq km high island (highest peak 629 m) roughly 550 km east-southeast of Pohnpei, with a fringing reef but no lagoon (attributed, [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 2017).

F.W. Christian (1899) drew the first map of the site, by which time it had already fallen to ruin. The German Südsee-Expedition — Paul Hambruch (1919) — mapped the ruins and excavated several tombs, while Ernst Sarfert (1919) collected further salvage ethnography. Ross Cordy conducted a thorough survey (1985, 1993), and Athens (ed., 1995) carried out deep stratigraphic excavations, recovering early CST pottery at the base of several residential compounds; Morgan (1988) described the site from an architectural perspective. This layered record — 19th-century mapping, early-20th-century salvage ethnography and excavation, and late-20th-century stratigraphic archaeology — is comparatively strong for a Pacific monumental site, though much of the early work predates modern excavation standards.

Contact-era eyewitness description (ethnohistorical, not archaeological fact): the French expedition *La Coquille*, under Louis Duperrey, arrived in 1824; First Officer Dumont d'Urville described "beautiful houses enclosed by high walls, well-paved streets," with roughly 800 people present (quoted in Cordy 1993:54), while ship's doctor René Lesson described "cyclopean" stone walls up to 15 feet in height (quoted in Cordy 1993:55). A later Russian expedition under Fyedor Lütke visited in 1827 (illustration reproduced, Lütke 1835).

## Occupation Sequence

The ruins cover 27 hectares, comprising a series of walled compounds — mostly residential, some containing tombs — with a canal traversing the site's center, navigable at high tide and used to bring daily tribute (food, goods) from Kosrae's land sections (*facl*). Construction resembles Nan Madol's reef-flat building technique, canals, and megalithic scale, but differs in detail.

Construction sequence (Cordy and Athens, radiocarbon-based, attributed):
1. **ca. 1st century AD**: initial occupation/use; early CST pottery suggests a possible stilt-house village.
2. **ca. AD 1250–1400** (Finlas sector): construction of artificial islets and walled compounds begins.
3. **AD 1400–1600**: major building effort — the Finbota-to-Katem area is built with stacked prismatic basalt walls; a western extension is built on the island's north end; Kinyeir and Lurun compound walls reach 6 m in height.
4. **ca. AD 1600–1650**: the open central space is filled in; construction style shifts to rounded basalt boulders, with walls of 4–5 m.
5. **AD 1650–1800**: remaining compounds are built using mixed coral stones, with low walls of 1–1.4 m.

Recent uranium-thorium dating of coral from three tombs gives AD 1310–1387 (Richards et al. 2015) — a distinct, more precise dating class than radiocarbon on the same monument type.

## Significance

Cordy's (1993) "competition hypothesis" (attributed) proposes that Kosrae's political system before AD 1000 had only two social strata (local chiefs, commoners); district-level unification added a third tier (a local chief mediating to a ruler); and island-wide consolidation — linked to Lelu's major construction phase (AD 1400–1600) — added a fourth tier, the island paramount. The proposed mechanism: differential land and population size among districts produced military advantage, leading to conquest and incorporation of neighbors and a consequent need for intermediate administrative tiers. Cordy identifies Lelu and Utwe as favored by especially large, contiguous fertile land, with population imbalance tipping competition first toward Utwe and Lelu, then to Lelu alone. Convergent models: Peoples (1990) proposes a similar population-imbalance-driven model, and Ueki (1990) proposes a sequence of population growth leading to land-management/competition changes, conflict, and a new managerial class, supported by settlement-pattern evidence from Utwa Valley (Nefalil site, a district-level center dated AD 940–1470). As a supporting proxy, Athens's (1995, 2007b) cumulative radiocarbon frequency-probability plot for Kosrae (109 dates) shows an inflection around AD 500 (population growth begins), a "spurt" around AD 800, and a sharp increase around AD 1200 — closely matching the onset of megalithic construction at Lelu (ca. AD 1200–1300); Kirch reports this as "strong support" for population growth's role, though it remains an interpretation of a population proxy rather than a direct census.

At contact in 1824, a single paramount chief controlled Kosrae, with subchiefs administering land units (*facl*) running from mountain to reef, organized into four districts, each a major drainage basin. Contact-period population is debated: most estimates are around 3,000 (Athens 2007a:276, doubting that population under the "paramount chieftaincy formed in the fifteenth century" exceeded that figure), while Ritter (1981) suggests 6,000 may be more accurate — an open, unresolved population-estimate dispute.

## Key Debates

Some traditions link Isokelekel, the legendary overthrower of the Saudeleur dynasty at [[nan-madol|Nan Madol]], to Kosrae, but Kirch notes that the term "Katau" in Pohnpeian tradition denotes a mythical sky-realm rather than a real island, cautioning against literal historical inference. Goodenough (1986:562, quoted) argues that any architectural influence between Lelu and Nan Madol most likely ran from Pohnpei to Kosrae, given that Lelu's main construction phase lags Nan Madol's by one to two centuries — possibly alongside diffusion of kava-drinking practice. The contact-period population estimate for Kosrae (roughly 3,000 vs. 6,000) also remains an open dispute (Athens 2007a vs. Ritter 1981).
