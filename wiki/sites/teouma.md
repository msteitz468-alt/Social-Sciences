## Discovery and Excavation History

Teouma is a Lapita-period cemetery on a river terrace on Efate Island, central Vanuatu, likely nearer the sea when occupied than at present. Discovered in 2004, it was excavated over five field seasons and reported in Bedford and Spriggs, eds. (Sand and Bedford, eds. 2010, as cited by [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 2017); site photographs are credited to Stuart Bedford, Australian National University, who is the likely lead excavator though the ingested claims do not name him explicitly as such beyond photo credit — flagged here for future verification. The multi-season, professionally documented excavation is a well-controlled modern dig by current standards.

## Occupation Sequence

Cemetery use began around 970–920 BC and the site was abandoned by 920–800 BC (radiocarbon dating, Petchey et al. 2015). At least 80 inhumations were recovered, with evidence of a complex, multi-stage mortuary ritual: skull removal, secondary manipulation of corpses, one cranium interred inside a Lapita pot capped with a flat dish, and another burial with three detached skulls placed on an adult male's chest. More than 73 reconstructed dentate-stamped vessels appear to mark burials.

## Significance

Kirch interprets the mortuary complexity at Teouma as evidence of ancestor worship (attributed). The site is the source of two of the most consequential biological studies of the [[lapita-cultural-complex]]:

- **aDNA (Skoglund et al. 2016, "as of Skoglund et al. 2016")**: genome-wide DNA from three Teouma individuals plus one from Talasiu, Tongatapu (all female; n=4) shows affinity to indigenous Taiwanese (Ami, Atayal) and Philippine (Kankanaey) populations with "no detectable Papuan ancestry." Skoglund et al. (2016:3, quoted) conclude "the Papuan genetic signature appeared in many Remote Oceanian populations only subsequent to initial settlement" — a small-sample (n=4), single-study finding requiring replication before promotion to wiki voice.
- **Cranial morphology (Valentin et al. 2016, n=12 adult crania)**: found to "clearly point to a Lapita–Asian biological connection... little or no affinities... to modern Australian and Melanesian cranial series but strong similarities to Chinese, Western Micronesian, and especially Polynesian" (quoted) — an independent evidence class converging with the aDNA result, though still a small sample warranting caution.

Additional studies drawing on Teouma material include an isotopic dietary study (Kinaston et al. 2014), which found a "mixed subsistence strategy... broad-spectrum marine resource foraging, consumption of wild animals especially fruit bats, and animal husbandry" (quoted), suggesting reduced plant-food reliance in the earliest colonization phase before gardens were established, and a dental-calculus microfossil study (Tromp 2016) confirming consumption of coconut, breadfruit, nuts, Malay apple, banana, taro, yam, arrowroot, and sugarcane.

## Key Debates

Teouma's aDNA and cranial evidence bear directly on the [[express-train-vs-entangled-bank-debate]] over Lapita origins, supporting a strong East Asian genetic/morphological signal at first Remote Oceanic settlement, with Papuan ancestry entering later — consistent with elements of the Triple-I synthesis position Kirch favors, though the small sample size means the finding should be treated as provisional pending replication (per the Contradiction and Replication Protocol).
