## Discovery and Excavation History

Enkapune Ya Muto Cave, in the central Rift Valley of Kenya, has produced the oldest reliable dates for decorative items — ostrich-eggshell beads — at least 39,900 years old by radiocarbon, and possibly older based on obsidian hydration (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Occupation Sequence

The eggshell-bead assemblage from Enkapune Ya Muto is part of a wider East and southern African pattern also seen at Mumba and Border Cave. No further occupation detail is recorded in the ingested source beyond the bead assemblage and its dating.

## Significance

Enkapune Ya Muto anchors the earliest secure evidence for personal ornamentation in the eastern African record, feeding Klein's argument (see [[upper-paleolithic-revolution]]) that unambiguous decorative items are essentially absent from Mousterian-age contexts but appear as part of the later behavioral-modernity package.

## Key Debates

Klein interprets the ostrich-eggshell beads at Enkapune Ya Muto, Mumba, and Border Cave as possible evidence of an exchange system analogous to the historically observed !Kung San beadwork exchange network in the Kalahari — an ethnographic analogy offered as an interpretive hypothesis, not as demonstrated behavior (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]).

Discussed in [[klein-human-career-1999]] ([[klein-richard]]) alongside [[blombos-cave]] as part of the evidence bearing on the emergence of symbolic behavior addressed in [[modern-human-origins-debate]].
