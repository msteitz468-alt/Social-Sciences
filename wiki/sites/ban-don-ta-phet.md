## Discovery and Excavation History

Ban Don Ta Phet is an Iron Age cemetery on the western margins of the Chao Phraya lowlands near the **Three Pagodas Pass** route toward the Bay of Bengal — a nodal position Higham stresses for Central Thai exchange. Major excavations were directed by **Ian Glover** and colleagues in the 1970s–1980s (Glover et al. 1984 is Higham’s cited mortuary baseline). [[higham-charles]] synthesizes the site in [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]] as the **clearest evidence** in Central Thailand for new social wealth, craft specialisation, and maritime exchange linking India, island Southeast Asia, and Viet Nam during the early–mid Iron Age.

Available radiocarbon dates, as reported by Higham, suggest occupation during the **first half of the fourth century BCE**. Large cemetery exposure revealed east–west burial rows; an encircling moat or ditch suggests a specified, possibly elite burial enclave rather than an undifferentiated village cemetery.

## Occupation Sequence

(as of Higham 2002) The excavated horizon is essentially a **single early Iron Age mortuary phase** rather than a multi-metre settlement sequence like [[khok-phanom-di]] or [[noen-u-loke]].

**Mortuary wealth.** Grave goods are far more abundant than typical Bronze Age assemblages. Glover et al. (1984), via Higham: average about **20 pots per burial** in four 1980–1 burials, plus iron tools and weapons, bronze ornaments and bowls, and stone and glass beads.

**Iron.** Socketed iron spearhead blades were often bent or ritually “killed.” The iron toolkit also includes sickle-like blades, socketed digging-stick or hoe tips, harpoons, and knives — evidence Higham folds into the broader Iron Age shift from ornaments toward agricultural and weapon forms (paralleled in sequence at [[noen-u-loke]]).

**Bronze.** Bronzes are for decoration rather than weapons in Higham’s reading. A bronze **situla** (bucket) is almost certainly a [[dong-son-culture|Dong Son]] import from northern Viet Nam. Thin **high-tin bronze bowls** (about 19–21% tin), lathe-turned to a golden colour and sometimes with incised scenes (Rajpitak and Seeley 1979), also occur at Khao Jamook; similar composition and form are widely known in India (Coimbatore; Taxila Bhir Mound late first millennium BCE; Adichanallur). Higham judges an **exotic (Indian) source more likely**, though local manufacture is not ruled out.

**Beads and ornaments.** Etched beads follow Indian decoration techniques; glass manufacture is judged highly likely of Indian inspiration, though Higham elsewhere notes growing evidence for early local carnelian exploitation and glass-making within Southeast Asia. A superb **carnelian lion** (and a broken second) takes pride of place among imports — parallels at Khuan Lukpad and Khao Sam Kaeo; Taxila Bhir lions date roughly 100 BCE–AD 200. A double-headed animal ear ornament is characteristic of [[sa-huynh-culture|Sa Huynh]] coastal Viet Nam. Unusual bird figurines include a fighting cock on a cage.

**Regional setting.** Higham places Ban Don Ta Phet within Chao Phraya’s nodal geography — mineral wealth, rice land, Three Pagodas Pass to India, Bang Pakong routes toward Cambodia, and Gulf maritime links. Copper production continued at Nil Kham Haeng in the Khao Wong Prachan Valley; other Central Thai Iron Age evidence (Ban Tha Kae glass/agate sequences, Ongbah boat-coffins and drums, Dermbang Nang Buat cast iron of Chinese technical affinity) frames Ban Don Ta Phet as one rich node rather than an isolate.

## Significance

Ban Don Ta Phet is Higham’s type illustration for several linked claims:

1. **Maritime exchange without “supine Indianisation.”** Higham rejects the assumption that coastal communities were “awakened from a long sleep by the arrival of Indian entrepreneurs.” Malagasy settlement from Southeast Asia proves long indigenous seafaring; Ban Don Ta Phet shows Central Thailand already trading with India and coastal Viet Nam by the 4th century BCE (as of Higham 2002). See [[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]].
2. **Iron Age wealth leap.** Average grave investment far exceeds Bronze Age norms at sites such as [[ban-lum-khao]] and [[nong-nor]], supporting models of increased ranking and craft specialisation ([[chiefdom]], [[southeast-asian-state-formation]]).
3. **Multi-vector exotic network.** Indian-linked beads and high-tin bowls, a Dong Son situla, and a Sa Huynh ear ornament in one cemetery demonstrate simultaneous participation in Bay of Bengal and South China Sea spheres — not a single civilizational “source.”
4. **Possible early Buddhist signal.** Higham notes that the lion often symbolised the Buddha and that it is “not unlikely that some inkling of Buddhism was already reaching Southeast Asia” — a **cautious, attributed suggestion**, not a demonstrated religious conversion of the community.
5. **Nodal resource model.** Higham proposes Ban Don Ta Phet’s occupants enjoyed easy iron-ore access and a nodal exchange position — variables he pairs with population foci, salt, and specialised bronze/iron production in the wider chiefdom-formation model.

## Key Debates

- **[[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]]** — primary site for Higham’s active-agency critique of colonial-era diffusion narratives.
- **Chronology of centralisation vs first exotic trader contact.** Higham holds strong grounds for mid-to-late first-millennium BCE centralisation but leaves the chronological relationship to initial exotic coastal contact undetermined; Ban Don Ta Phet is early and rich, but does not alone sequence cause.
- **Local vs Indian manufacture** of high-tin bowls and glass — exotic source preferred for bowls; local SEA glass/carnelian production increasingly plausible in Higham’s wider discussion.
- **Elite enclave reading of the ditch/bank** — morphologically suggestive; sample of fully published rich burials still limited (1980–1 average rests on four burials in the cited figure).
- **Buddhism inkling** — iconographic possibility, not archaeological proof of Buddhist practice.

## Historiography

Ban Don Ta Phet enters regional prehistory through Glover’s programme at a moment when Iron Age Thailand was still thinly published relative to Dong Son Viet Nam and the Ban Chiang fame complex. Higham’s 2002 synthesis elevates the site from a bead-and-bowl findspot to a structural case against passive Indianisation and for multi-directional late prehistoric exchange. Evidence quality: radiocarbon for the 4th-century BCE horizon; strong artefact provenience arguments (Dong Son situla form; Sa Huynh ear ornament type; Indian etched-bead technology and high-tin bowl chemistry); weaker for community-level demography and settlement plan beyond the cemetery enclave. Collection coverage: [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]] (secondary synthesis of Glover et al.); primary Glover excavation reports not yet ingested as separate sources.
