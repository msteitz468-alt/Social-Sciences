# Copan

## Discovery and Excavation History

Southeastern Classic capital. Long research history (Peabody, Honduran, Harvard Fash projects, etc.). [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] stress dynastic founding, Teotihuacan-linked founder iconography, and catastrophic defeat by Quirigua.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- **Preclassic:** Uir phase (c. 800–400 BC) Olmecized ceramics; Motagua jade sources nearby (blue-green jade of Olmec taste in Sierra de las Minas — 2001 discoveries cited).
- **Founding:** Yax K'uk' Mo' accedes AD 426; Hunal tomb; isotopic work points toward Caracol connections rather than Teotihuacan birthplace for the founder (as of C&H synthesis) even as Teotihuacan warrior imagery is used.
- **Late Classic:** 18 Rabbit (Waxaklajuun Ub'aah K'awiil) beheaded 4 May AD 738 after Quirigua rebellion under K'ahk' Tiliw (from 725). Hieroglyphic Stairway as dynastic manifesto (text labels Temple 25 in C&H — verify against specialist “Temple 26” convention).
- Ecological stress and deforestation enter [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] discussions for the Copan pocket.

## Significance

Southeastern hinge of the Classic world; founder ideology; exemplar of dynastic warfare ([[quirigua]]); long epigraphic sequence.



## Heritage Management Conflict (attributed — Mortensen 2006)

Lena Mortensen, "Structural Complexity and Social Conflict in Managing the Past at Copán, Honduras," in [[brodie-kersel-luke-tubb-archaeology-cultural-heritage-antiquities-trade-2006|Brodie et al. 2006]], treats Copán as a **multi-stakeholder heritage park**, not primarily as a looting-market case (contrast Luke & Henderson on the **Lower Ulúa Valley** — a different Honduran landscape).

- Stakeholders include Honduran state archaeology (IHAH), UNESCO **World Heritage** designation, foreign research projects, tourism/Mundo Maya branding, local non-Maya and **Chortí** Maya descendant communities, and conservation NGOs (e.g. Wildlife Conservation Society planning engagement ~2000–01).
- Mortensen records **naming and interpretation conflicts** (who “owns” the Maya past at a national flagship site), **Chortí protests**, and the tension between tourism revenue, research access, and conservation — a 2000–01 management plan process that, as of her account, was not finalized.
- A **1998 tomb looting** episode at the site complex enters her narrative as one flashpoint among structural conflicts, not as the volume’s Ulúa-style market analysis.

Use for [[heritage-management]] and [[public-archaeology]]; do not merge Copán management politics with Ulúa marble-vase pillage.

## Key Debates

- Founder origins (local/Caracol/Teotihuacan imagery).
- Collapse ecology in a river valley setting.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above; note founder-origin tension with C&H’s Caracol-leaning isotopic reading.)*

### Research history, setting, and density

Known since 1576 (García de Palacios); Galindo’s 1834 royal tomb excavation (first reported Maya royal tomb); Stephens/Catherwood; Maudslay from 1885 opened the era of scientific Maya archaeology; Peabody 1891–94; Morley on monuments and valley settlement; Carnegie from 1935 (Acropolis river diversion, Hieroglyphic Stairway, Ball Court); Gordon Willey Copan Valley Project 1975–77; Instituto Hondureño phases 1977–85; later North Group/Group 8N-11, Copan Mosaic Project, Acropolis tunnels. Deciphered capital name **Oxwitik**. Positioned near the eastern margin of the southern highlands as a highland–lowland trade-route capital (with [[tonina]] on the northwest highland margin). Central Copan has the highest construction density of any Maya site in their Rice & Culbert summary tables (1,449 structures/km²) — reflecting valley confinement. Greater Copan Valley Late Classic peak ~18,000–25,000 (about one-fourth of greater Tikal). Uncounted-structure correction factors for population estimates: Copan +39–50% (vs Tikal ~+10%).

### Founding (426–427), founder identity, and Early Classic sequence

Sharer & Traxler argue archaeological and historical evidence that **[[tikal]] orchestrated** the 426 takeover of Copan and establishment of [[quirigua]] as Motagua subsidiary (jadeite, Ixtepeque obsidian, Caribbean gateway). Altar Q: **K’uk’ Mo’ Ajaw** took K’awiil at *wi’te’ naah* (5 Sept 426), became **K’inich Yax K’uk’ Mo’**, arrived Oxwitik 8 Feb 427 as *ochk’in kaloomte’* (same title as Siyaj K’ak’); sponsor likely Siyaj Chan K’awiil II; *wi’te’ naah* location unspecified (possibly Tikal). Supporting threads: Hombre de Tikal text (406) names a K’uk’ Mo’ during Yax Nuun Ayiin I’s reign; Altar Q portrait shows Teotihuacan warrior regalia; **Hunal Tomb** elderly male (55–70), healed combat injuries, Teotihuacan-style shell platelet helmet; **strontium isotopes: childhood/young adult in the Tikal region of the Petén**, final years at Copan; NAA vessels from Tikal region (2), Central Mexico (5), Copan Valley (11), Kaminaljuyu-region sources. *(Wiki note: Coe & Houston 2015 synthesis above leans toward Caracol connections for founder isotopes — treat as competing readings as of their respective dates.)* Early Classic sequence includes Margarita Tomb (likely founder’s queen / Ruler 2’s mother); Ruler 4 K’altuun Hix; Ruler 7 Balam Nehn (accession 524; named on Caracol Stela 16); monument breakage ca. 553–564 possible disruption (authors float possible Calakmul/ally strike contemporaneous with Tikal’s 562 defeat); Moon Jaguar Ruler 10 (553–578) ends Early Classic era; Rosalila temple over founder’s core dedicated to the founder over 400 years later (legitimacy through ancestor cult).

### Economy, labor, and strong-state model

Carrelli’s Early Classic Acropolis estimates: founder (ca. AD 426–37) required >175,000 person-days for initial platform/buildings — about one month/year from every adult male in the Copan Valley — with labor burden rising under Ruler 2. Aoyama’s research: dynastic state managed Ixtepeque obsidian blade-core distribution. Authors classify Copan with Tikal and Calakmul as best Classic **strong-state** examples (capital qualitatively different — palaces, tombs, temples, dynastic monuments). Preclassic Los Achiotes ball court earliest in the Copan Valley; Late Preclassic hilltop defensive shift in the valley; Early Preclassic Group 9N-8 pottery with pan-Mesoamerican motifs (not proof of Olmec political control). Rosalila cache: twelve elaborate eccentric flint figures probably once royal scepters; textile fragments with the flints.

### AD 738 crisis and Terminal Classic path

[[quirigua]]’s K’ak’ Tiliw Chan Yopaat recorded inauguration under supervision (*u kabi*) of Copan’s Waxaklajuun Ub’aah K’awiil, then in **738** captured and sacrificed the 13th Copan ruler; Quirigua inscriptions also claim capture of Copan’s patron gods. North Group associated (after Ashmore/Harrison cosmogram readings) with Waxaklajuun Ub’aah K’awiil’s apotheosis after the Quirigua sacrifice. Authors’ collapse path for Copan: violence/warfare played **much less** causal role than in Petexbatun — deforestation, soil erosion, and loss of confidence in rulers mattered more; weakening triggered by loss of the thirteenth ruler and Quirigua’s breakaway, then power-sharing that decentralized authority, culminating in Ukit Took’s failure to succeed Yax Pasaj Chan Yopaat. Drought not treated as critical for Copan demise (unlike reservoir-dependent Tikal). Terminal Classic arrivals at Copan and Quirigua appear **after** demise of centralized order — more result than cause. Earliest Maya-area metal: gold-copper figurine legs (Costa Rica/Panama origin) at Copan.

*associated_with:* [[tikal]] founding sponsorship; subordinate then rival [[quirigua]]; Motagua trade; [[teotihuacan]] imagery on founder; collapse ecology in [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]].

## Densification from Martin & Grube 2008

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube 2008]], Copán chapter. Complements Coe & Houston and Sharer & Traxler densifications above; epigraphic-dynastic synthesis with tunnel archaeology.)*

### Setting, sixteen kings, and the founder (426–c.437)

Martin & Grube present [[copan|Copán]] as among the most intensively investigated Maya cities, famed for three-dimensional sculpture at the eastern edge of the Classic world and as a unique source on dynastic foundation and the Maya–[[teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]] relationship. The Acropolis is a man-made hill raised by the cumulative building of **16 kings** over roughly 400 years. Founder **K'inich Yax K'uk' Mo'** (“Radiant First Quetzal Macaw”) is known chiefly from Late Classic retrospective texts until deep excavation reached his earliest capital and, they argue, his tomb. **Altar Q** (commissioned by the 16th king in **776**, some 350 years after the founder) is the definitive dynastic statement: rulers seated in sequence on name glyphs; west face shows Yax K'uk' Mo' conversing with the commissioning king.

Per Altar Q’s top text: on **5 September 426**, then-named **K'uk' Mo' Ajaw** took the snake-footed god K'awiil and rose to kingship at the *wi te' nah* — a structure of dynastic genesis and, in Martin & Grube’s framing, seemingly of Mexican origin. The same 426 package included investiture of other regional lords, specifically the first king of [[quirigua|Quiriguá]]; location appears distant from Copán. **152 days** later Yax K'uk' Mo' “arrived here” at **Ux Witik** (principal Copán place-name), perhaps bearing a K'awiil effigy — an archetypal Mesoamerican foundation journey. Martin & Grube draw strong parallels with the **AD 378 *entrada*** of Sihyaj K'ahk' at [[tikal|Tikal]] and the Teotihuacano New Order: the founder’s mixed Mexican–Maya ties suggest he was part of that order or at least deployed its ideology at the far rim; inaugural ceremonies could have been in the Petén or even distant Mexico. Late Classic portraits give him Teotihuacan “goggle eye” ornaments; the near-contemporary depiction is more orthodox Maya. Architecture is dual: **Hunal** (Temple 16 origin) in *talud-tablero* Teotihuacan style; the **Yax** platform under Temple 26 with Tikal/Petén affinities. David Stuart’s *Uxwitza' ajaw* reading proposes a [[caracol|Caracol]] origin (hypothesis, not settled by Martin & Grube). Preclassic Copán was already significant; the founder displaced an existing elite, possibly by force. Death not precisely dated but by **437** (not much past the 9th Bak'tun of 435). **Hunal** vaulted crypt (Sharer–Sedat 1995): aged male with royal jades/shell/pyrite, unhealed broken right arm matching Altar Q’s stunted limb; chemical signatures **not** of a Copán native. Son **Popol Hol** (Ruler 2) launched a founder cult lasting nearly four centuries; Motmot interment style is said to mirror Teotihuacan practice; Margarita’s wealthy elderly woman is read as local widow/mother of Popol Hol.

### Waxaklajuun Ubaah K'awiil, peak art, and the 738 disaster

After sparse middle kings (Bahlam Nehn / Ruler 7 first to number his dynastic position and the only Copán king mentioned outside the southeast — Caracol Stela 16), **Smoke Imix / K'ahk' Uti' Witz' K'awiil** (12th; thr. 628) imprinted authority valley-wide with multiple stelae (652 package). Rosalila, carefully interred intact under the Temple 16 sequence, was in Martin & Grube’s account the last building at Copán to use stucco on such a lavish scale: with **deforestation of the valley well advanced**, firewood for lime plaster could no longer be afforded, and successors shifted to stone-carved decoration. Population rose through the Early–Late Classic transition as house mounds filled the Copán “pocket.”

**Waxaklajuun Ubaah K'awiil** (Ruler 13; acc. July **695**) presided over the artistic pinnacle then the political disaster. He controlled the Motagua, installing Quiriguá’s **K'ahk' Tiliw Chan Yopaat** in **724**; burned Xkuy in **718**. Stela A (**731**) sets his office in a four-way symbolic orientation with Tikal, Calakmul, and Palenque. Stelae C, F, 4, H, A, B, D (711–736) are cast as the highest achievement of Maya freestanding sculpture — supernatural status, not gory war scenes. By **710** he sealed Chorcha / predecessor’s tomb in Temple 26 (**Esmeralda**) with the **first Hieroglyphic Stairway**; final ballcourt (macaw façades; 16 sculptures) dedicated **6 January 738**. Less than four months later, in **April 738**, vassal K'ahk' Tiliw captured him after a fire event involving two patron deities; six days later the Copán king was **“beheaded.”** Quiriguá accounts lack a standard war term — Martin & Grube float possible duplicity. Copán’s later gloss: demise by “flint and shield” (noble battle death). **Seventeen years** of monument silence and halted construction followed; Hieroglyphic Stairway phrase “no stelae, no pyramids” may lament the hiatus. Dynasty continued: Ruler 14 inaugurated 39 days after the execution. Martin & Grube argue Copán may have been briefly subordinate to its former vassal and **never fully recovered Motagua trade wealth**.

### Hieroglyphic Stairway renewal, Yax Pasaj, Ukit Took'

Ruler 15 **K'ahk' Yipyaj Chan K'awiil** (749–761) tore out and reset the Hieroglyphic Stairway into a new Temple 26 façade, **doubling** the text; life-size ancestral kings as **Teotihuacan warriors** line the stair; Stela M (756). Upper sanctuary juxtaposes Maya full-figure glyphs with what David Stuart called a “Teotihuacan font” (pseudo-writing creating a bilingual illusion). Political argument parallels Tikal a generation earlier: fallen Teotihuacan as archetype **and** ancestral figure for the ruling line.

**Yax Pasaj Chan Yopaat** (16th; crowned June **763**, possibly as young as nine) produced no freestanding stelae; mother a lady of [[palenque|Palenque]]. Temple 11 (platform 769; superstructure 773); final Temple 16 (~776) with Mexican motifs and founder emphasis; **Altar Q** at its base with crypt of **15 sacrificed jaguars** (one per predecessor). Las Sepulturas nobles with fine hieroglyphic benches; Los Higos stela **781** as peripheral autonomy. Pocket population **20,000+** strains local support; late monuments of 800 and 805 are coarse incense-burners — last elite texts of his reign at Copán; the **810** K'atun ending is recorded only at Quiriguá (possible late rapprochement). **Deforestation**, shortage, and disease frame the end. Last ruler **Ukit Took'** acceded February **822** on unfinished **Altar L** (mimicking Altar Q: new king facing Yax Pasaj); only one of four planned faces finished, a second barely begun — “this valley had seen the last of its kings.”

*associated_with:* Teotihuacan-linked *wi te' nah* founding; 16-king Acropolis; Quiriguá 738 inversion; Hieroglyphic Stairway as dynastic charter; collapse ecology (deforestation, plaster, overpopulation) in [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]].

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel, *A Forest of Kings*, 1990]], Ch. 8 “Copan: The Death of First Dawn on Macaw Mountain.” 1990 nicknames retained with later-standard equivalents. Dramatized Yax-Pac procession monologue is NARRATIVE DEVICE. Ecological–political collapse model and power-sharing thesis are POSITION as of 1990. Compare Martin & Grube 2008 and Sharer & Traxler densifications above.)*

### Yax-Kuk-Mo’ and Early Classic dynasty

Schele & Freidel report a newly excavated temple under the Hieroglyphic Stairs (Papagayo) held a stela dated **9.0.0.0.0**; **Yax-Kuk-Mo’** (K’inich Yax K’uk’ Mo’) is recorded as king when the baktun turned, while his son (second king) owned the tree-stone—showing the founder was not a later fabrication. Notes refine: he is the dynastic founder from whom numbered successors count, yet not the true first king of the kingdom (earlier “first successor” on Stela 24). Papagayo and the Ballcourt became enduring central metaphors—temple of kings and ballcourt portal to the Otherworld. Early kings identified as of 1990: 1st Yax-Kuk-Mo’; 2nd his son; 4th Cu-Ix; 7th Waterlily-Jaguar (Stelae 15, E); 10th Moon-Jaguar; 11th Butz’-Chan. Early inscriptions often damaged by termination rituals (defacement/careful breakage) and reuse. Late Classic kings held authority as springing from Yax-Kuk-Mo’; ~400-year dynastic span until system collapse (POSITION frame).

### Smoke-Imix-God K and 18-Rabbit’s artistic peak

Recorded history “begins in earnest” with twelfth successor **Smoke-Imix-God K** (K’ahk’ Uti’ Witz’ K’awiil), reign **AD 628–695** (sixty-seven years)—Late Classic expansion to widest dominion. On **9.11.0.0.0 (AD 652)** he erected stelae bounding the valley (east Stelae 23, 13, 12; west 10, 19; plaza 2, 3) as personal sacred space / Otherworld portal; same period ending, **Altar L at Quirigua** marks Copan preeminence. Motagua and Valley of La Venta (Chamelecón) expansion—possibly one of the largest Maya royal territories of its time (ATTRIBUTED). Under him Copan nobility were the dominant elite of the southeastern region.

**18-Rabbit** (Waxaklajuun Ub’aah K’awiil) succeeded **AD 695**. Where predecessor defined valley boundaries, 18-Rabbit made the city center his stage: Ballcourt remodel with Hero Twin markers; Great Plaza forest of tree-stones **711–736** (each portraiting him as a god manifested in ritual); **Temple 22** as Acropolis masterpiece—Witz Monsters, Celestial Monster doorway laced with blood scrolls, Pauahtunob holding the sky. Themes traditional—no Pacal-style special succession agenda; focused solely on centrality of the king. Inherited nobles already governing neighboring cities. Multi-ethnic recruitment from central Honduras (Lake Yojoa, Comayagua). Private noble monuments during his reign (scribal lineage Compound **9N-8**, Structure 9N-82-Sub dedicated to God N) presented as “beginning of the end”—elite emulating royal practice in private space.

### Capture by Cauac-Sky, 3 May 738

Mid-career 18-Rabbit installed **Cauac-Sky** (K’ahk’ Tiliw Chan Yopaat) at Quirigua in a “God K-in-hand” event **2 January 725**, *u cab* “in the land of” 18-Rabbit. Thirteen years later Cauac-Sky turned on his liege, took 18-Rabbit captive in battle, and sacrificed him at Quirigua **3 May 738** (9.15.6.14.6 6 Cimi 4 Zec; “he was sacrificed 18-Rabbit-God K”). Notes: “ax” verb may be battle or *ch’ak* “decapitate”; possibly seized seeking sacrificial victims for new Ballcourt III (113 days after dedication). No archaeological evidence Quirigua dominated Copan afterward—population burgeoned, lords built, merchants traded (“business as usual”). **POSITION:** absence of conquest footprint may register Copanecs believing they could manage without a king; dynasty needed ~twenty years to recover prestige; failure “fooled the patriarchs of the subordinate lineages.” Parallel drawn to Palenque after Tonina captured Kan-Xul—no full hiatus, but lesser nobility rose as historical players.

### Smoke-Monkey, Smoke-Shell, Hieroglyphic Stairs

**Smoke-Monkey** acceded 39 days after the defeat (Venus Morningstar elongation); no associated stelae/structures; ten silent years; son **Smoke-Shell** king **18 February 749**. Smoke-Shell’s recovery strategy: ambitious building + political marriage—royal woman from **Palenque** bore heir **Yax-Pac** (Yax Pasaj Chan Yopaat), who took the throne under age twenty. Masterwork: **Temple of the Hieroglyphic Stairs** (10L-26)—longest known Precolumbian New World text (over 2,200 glyphs; notes: longest but eroded/out of order since 1898, unlikely fully decipherable—Palenque Inscriptions panels are longest *intact*); records accessions/deaths of Yax-Kuk-Mo’ dynasty; last five successors as life-sized Tlaloc-Venus war portraits including 18-Rabbit despite personal defeat.

### Yax-Pac crisis: demography, ecology, power-sharing

Schele & Freidel argue Smoke-Shell’s revival was short-term; Yax-Pac inherited swelling royal-kin pyramid, growing noble wealth, and valley ecological strain. Within 1 km of the Ballcourt >1,500 structures, ~3,000 people/km²; ≥20,000 people; best land buried under residential complexes. Cascade POSITION: foothill farming → deforestation → erosion → shorter fallows → wood demand for cooking and lime plaster → malnutrition and chronic disease among nobility and commoners (Storey, Fash apparatus in notes). Yax-Pac acceded **2 July 763**; first public act Vision Serpent altar among 18-Rabbit’s tree-stones (9.16.15.0.0).

Major program:

- **Temple 11** Reviewing Stand **27 March 769**—Xibalban ballcourt metaphor (victims rolled down stairs “as if they were the ball”); full dedication **26 September 773**—two-story cosmic statement, skeletal Maw platform, bench of twenty ancestral dynasts; sculptures not near 18-Rabbit’s artistry; vaults so wide they needed reinforcement.
- **Altar Q** before Temple 16—sixteen ancestors clockwise from Yax-Kuk-Mo’ to Yax-Pac; “last great exhortation of kingship” in the valley.
- Shift of royal ritual into **noble residential compounds** (9M-18 bench; Group **9N-8** rebuild **10 July 781**—noble equal billing with king, unlike Bird-Jaguar’s clearly subservient La Pasadita *cahal*).
- **Stela 8** / **Altar T**—only monument mentioning Palenque mother; introduces half brother **Yahau-Chan-Ah-Bac**; **Altar U**—**Yax-Kamlay** (“First Steward” ~prime minister) and Yahau-Chan-Ah-Bac as district governor; “rule by a council of brothers… ultimately failed in Copan, but it succeeded at Chichén Itza.”
- **Temple 22a** (*Popol Nah* council house built by Smoke-Monkey after 738)—throne links three siblings.
- Missing major monuments for katun 9.18.0.0.0; activity at hotun 9.18.5.0.0; **Altar G1** features Yahau-Chan-Ah-Bac in the sacred plaza center.
- **Temple 18** last Acropolis building (dedicated **12 August 801**)—reduced scale; only building in city history showing the king actually in battle (spears, cotton armor, shrunken heads, captive ropes)—“when authority fails, force is the last arbiter.”
- Late: carved incensario 802; state visit to rival Quirigua for scattering **9.19.0.0.0 (28 June 810)**—unusual (kings rarely traveled; scattering usually at homesite).

**POSITION:** power-sharing with brothers and lesser lords (Yaxchilan parallel) worked short-term; “in the end… precedents of sharing central power with nonroyal patriarchs destroyed the divinity that had sustained the Copan kingship for more than seven hundred years.” William Fash first argued nonroyal lineages competing with the royal house contributed to collapse of central power (notes).

### U-Cit-Tok and unfinished Altar L

Yax-Pac died shortly before **9.19.10.0.0 (6 May 820)**; Stela 11 at Temple 18 depicts him in watery Otherworld with ceremonial bar lacking serpent heads; verb read as *hom* “to terminate/end”—people of Copan believed Yax-Kuk-Mo’ dynasty ended with him, yet he was not the last king. **U-Cit-Tok** (Ukit Took’) seated **10 February 822** on unfinished **Altar L** (south side emulates Altar Q opposite Yax-Pac; north ritual unfinished). Schele & Freidel’s dramatic reading: sculptor stopped mid-work as central authority collapsed—“Copan’s dynastic history ended with the echoing slap of that sculptor’s sandals.” **POSITION** aftermath: residential compounds functioned another century; without king, lineages would not cooperate; Compound 9N-8 building collapsed on an occupant never dug out; within two centuries ~90% of valley population gone.

*(Dramatized procession monologue—denuded mountains, sick households, red river silt, drought—is explicit imaginative reconstruction, not epigraphic report.)*
