# Bonampak

## Discovery and Excavation History

Small Usumacinta-polity site world-famous for 1946 mural rediscovery and later documentation (incl. Bonampak Documentation Project reconstructions cited in apparatus). [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] use murals as primary evidence for Late Classic court war and ritual.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- Room murals c. AD 790: battle, captive torture, court dance, musicians; text records “Shield Jaguar” IV of Yaxchilan overseeing Bonampak lord’s accession.
- Morley Class 4 minor center politically, but unmatched pictorial record.
- Fits 751–790 breakdown narrative feeding [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]].

## Significance

Best pictorial evidence for Classic Maya warfare, court ceremony, and hierarchical overlordship.

## Key Debates

- How representative Bonampak violence is of “typical” Classic war.
- Relationship to Yaxchilan empire-building.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above; murals used as economic/ritual evidence rather than as a full site monograph.)*

### Murals as evidence for tribute, textiles, and sacrifice

Sharer & Traxler cite Bonampak murals among primary Classic pictorial sources for **elite fabrics** (elaborate cotton textiles also known from monuments and compared with Sacred Cenote cloth fragments and Rosalila textile remains at [[copan]]). Feather work known from Bonampak murals and Piedras Negras Wall Panel 3 — quetzal plumes reserved for royal use (capital offense to kill/capture per Las Casas; Motagua cloud-forest habitat). **Labeled cacao-tribute sacks** on the murals are cited as evidence that elites extracted cacao as tribute and likely sponsored regional markets — with little evidence for centralized control of most raw-material extraction or household agriculture (exception: north-coast salt facilities). Captive scenes are used against ethnohistoric claims that Mexicans “introduced” idolatry and increased human sacrifice: both practices predated foreign introduction — Preclassic/Classic incensarios, sacrifice scenes, **Bonampak captives**, and Chalchuapa mass sacrifice of 33 mostly young males. Personal-appearance conventions (warriors black and red, prisoners black-and-white stripes, priests blue) are drawn from Landa and archaeology and match mural color coding of captives and elites.
