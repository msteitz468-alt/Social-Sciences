# Göbekli Tepe

Early Neolithic monumental complex on a limestone ridge near Şanlıurfa in southeastern Anatolia (Turkey). As presented by [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] after Klaus Schmidt’s excavations, it is a dedicated ritual “mountain sanctuary” of Pre-Pottery Neolithic hunter-gatherers rather than a domestic town—and, for Mithen, a candidate turning-point in world history for the origins of farming ideology and wheat cultivation.

**Identity caution:** Ritual participants are an archaeological construct from architecture, art, and wild fauna; do not equate with later peoples, languages, or genetic populations. PPNA/PPNB are period/culture-history labels, not ethnic names.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: the site was misread in the 1960s as a Byzantine cemetery. From 1994, **Klaus Schmidt** excavated massive T-shaped limestone pillars carved from bedrock, set in sunken circular buildings with benches and carved wild animals and abstract signs. Schmidt had previously dug Nevali Çori and recognised related sculptural traditions. Mithen visited in 2002 and used the site as a narrative and interpretive climax for Neolithic origins in western Asia.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Schmidt via Mithen 2003, ch.8, 11.)*

1. **PPNA monumental phase (soon after 9600 BCE).** Pillars often ~8 feet high and ~7 tons; an unfinished pillar would have been ~20 feet / ~50 tons. No houses, fireplaces, or pits of domestic type reported; all food remains wild. Schmidt interprets a ritual meeting place for groups within ~100 km.
2. **Iconography.** Themes read by Mithen/Schmidt as fear and danger of the wild rather than mother-goddess fertility: animals described as male; limestone human with erect penis. Contrasts with Cauvin’s mother-goddess reading of early Neolithic symbolism while still supporting broader “ideology before economy” arguments.
3. **Deliberate backfill.** Early Neolithic people are said to have buried ritual buildings under tons of soil on abandonment of each phase.
4. **Later rectangular phase.** By ~8500 BCE circular monumental buildings buried; a stone wall demarcates former ritual space; new rectangular buildings beyond the wall with smaller animal-carved pillars; Schmidt still reports no domestic activity. Dedicated ritual centre until abandonment probably around 7500 BCE.

Related: Nevali Çori (Hauptmann 1983–91; later flooded by the Atatürk Dam) shows PPNB cult buildings and T-capital pillars in an art lineage Mithen traces from Göbekli (~30 km). Qermez Dere is linked by Mithen for subterranean pillar structures and deliberate backfill.

## Significance

- Type-case for monumental communal architecture among foragers at the Pleistocene–Holocene threshold—challenges settlement-first or farming-first narratives of Neolithic “complexity.”
- Anchor for debates on ideology, cooperation vs coercion, and whether ceremonial aggregation drove intensive plant use.
- Mithen links 1997 genetic results placing modern domestic wheat’s closest wild relative at Karacadağ (~30 km from Göbekli) to intensive cultivation for ceremonial gatherings—domestication as accidental by-product of ideology, not only Younger Dryas stress (theoretical; genetics date-stamped to cited 1997 work / Mithen 2003).
- Mithen’s historiographic judgment: standing at Göbekli in 2002 he “felt that it had been at Göbekli and not [[jericho|Jericho]] that the history of the world had turned.”
- Comparative symbolic lineage into Çatalhöyük, Nevali Çori, and wider PPNB imagery (raptors, cattle, pillars).

## Key Debates

- **Ritual centre vs settlement.** Schmidt’s no-domestic-debris reading (as of Mithen 2003) vs later work arguing more residential or multi-function use—record as open; do not freeze 2003 positions as final.
- **Ideology-before-farming** (Cauvin program; Göbekli as supporting case) vs economic/stress models of domestication.
- **Communal architecture as cooperation or control**—Mithen poses the same open question for Jericho’s wall/tower, Beidha, Jerf el Ahmar granaries, and Göbekli pillars.
- **Mother-goddess vs wild-danger iconography** at Göbekli and Jerf el Ahmar.
- Geographic and genetic locus of wheat domestication relative to ceremonial landscapes (Karacadağ–Göbekli cluster as of 1997 genetics).

## Cauvin densification (2000 Postscript)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]* Postscript, as of ~1998 fieldwork reports; main 1997 text predates full impact.)*

- Cauvin treats Schmidt’s deep sounding as confirming end-PPNA **Mureybetian** industry reaching SE Anatolian Taurus highlands (~8500 BC hypothesis).
- Monumental sacred art: **T-shaped pillars ~3 m** with relief animals (bulls, lion, canid, bird, large wader not previously in repertory) — enriches symbolic vocabulary beyond dominant bull + human form; significance work ongoing as of his writing.
- Still **no confirmation** (as of Cauvin) that this Anatolian “Mureybetian” already practiced cultivation — definitional “Neolithic” for him requires productive economy.
- Complements [[nevali-cori|Nevalı Çori]], [[cafer-hoyuk|Cafer Höyük]], [[catalhoyuk|Çatalhöyük]] in his Anatolian neolithisation map; later readers (e.g. Mithen) pair Göbekli with ideology-before-economy while often rejecting mother-goddess reading of its imagery.
