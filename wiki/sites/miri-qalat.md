# Miri Qalat

Baluchistan settlement represented in [[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Kenoyer 1998]] primarily through **catalogue finds** rather than a full site monograph synthesis. It marks the western highland/piedmont participation in Indus-period seal and symbolic systems.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Kenoyer’s 1998 volume does not give a full excavation history for Miri Qalat in the claims extracted for this page; the site appears as a **provenience** for geometric seals in the exhibition catalogue section. Treat excavation quality and season counts as unknown until specialist reports are ingested.

## Occupation Sequence

No periodized sequence is reconstructed in Kenoyer 1998 claims for this wiki page. Material referenced belongs to the broader Harappan-period interaction sphere in Baluchistan (associated with, not identical to, the alluvial urban centers).

## Significance

- **Geometric seal evidence:** Cat. no. 35 is a small fired steatite seal with an **incised clockwise swastika**; when stamped the impression turns counterclockwise. Kenoyer groups it with other geometric seals (e.g., yellow faience counterclockwise swastika button seal from [[mohenjo-daro]]; stepped-cross seal from [[harappa]]) as part of Indus ritual/identity symbolism — etic interpretation of use (stamping vs ornament) remains open (the faience button may not have been used for stamping).
- **Style links:** Multi-headed animal seals without script (e.g. Cat. no. 29 combining bull, unicorn, and antelope heads) are compared to a plain-boss style possibly like Miri Qalat Cat. 35 — a workshop/tradition association, not proof of political control from the great cities.

## Key Debates

- Western highland sites in the Indus system: peripheral dependents vs autonomous participants in shared symbol systems ([[indus-polity-debate]]).
- Geometric motifs (swastika, stepped cross) as pan-Indus ritual symbols vs local seal traditions ([[indus-religion]]).
- Four-way non-identity: Baluchistan findspots ≠ alluvial “Harappan people” ≠ script language ≠ genetic population.
