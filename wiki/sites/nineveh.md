## Discovery and Excavation History

Ancient Ninua (Nineveh) became the Assyrian imperial capital under
[[assyrians|Sennacherib]] (704–681 BCE), having previously been, per
[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], "a simple royal residence." The site — the mounds
of Kuyunjik and Nebi Yunus, opposite modern Mosul on the Tigris's left bank — was
excavated beginning in 1849 by Sir Austen Henry Layard, who discovered two
tablet-filled chambers in Sennacherib's palace; further tablets were recovered in 1852
by Layard's assistant Hormuzd Rassam in Ashurbanipal's palace on the same mound. Writing
boards with astronomical content were recovered in 1953 from a well at [[nimrud]],
apparently discarded during that city's sack — a related find illustrating the broader
Assyrian scribal culture. Nineveh's excavation history is thus foundational to 19th-
century Assyriology, though — as with much 19th-century fieldwork — recovery methods
and recording standards fall well short of modern single-context excavation; this is a
reliability consideration for any fine-grained stratigraphic claim about the site.

## Occupation Sequence

Sennacherib's building program expanded the city's circumference from roughly 3 km to
roughly 12 km, encompassing both the Kuyunjik and Nebi Yunus mounds. An outer wall of
limestone blocks was "raised mountain high," per the royal inscription, with an inner
wall pierced by fifteen gates; streets and squares were widened and paved. The "Palace
without a Rival" was built on Kuyunjik on a terrace over the Tebiltu river (a Tigris
affluent that had undermined the earlier palace, subsequently demolished). Sennacherib's
inscription — quoted at length by Roux — describes cedar beams from Mt. Amanus, cypress
doors bound in copper, a *bit-hilani* portico (a "Hittite palace" style term Roux notes
came via "the Amorite tongue"), copper pillars cast on bronze lion bases (a casting
technique Sennacherib claims to have invented), and an adjoining park irrigated by a
purpose-built canal running "through mountain and lowland." The **aqueduct at Jerwan**
provides archaeologically confirmed physical remains corroborating these inscriptional
claims — a genuine case of independent convergence between the textual and material
evidence classes, per this wiki's Evidence-Class Framework. Sennacherib's royal rock
reliefs at Bavian (near Jerwan), Maltai (near Dohuk), and Judi Dagh (on the
Turkish-Iraqi border) portray the king before the gods.

**Ashurbanipal's Library.** Nineveh's most significant single find is the library
assembled under [[assyrians|Ashurbanipal]] (668–627 BCE), discovered across Layard's
1849 and Rassam's 1852 excavations: over 25,000 tablets and fragments, sent to the
British Museum. Roux distinguishes two components — a smaller administrative "archive"
layer (royal inscriptions, letters, administrative texts), and a "library" layer of
literary texts (myths and legends) alongside a numerically dominant mass of
"scientific" material, chiefly divination, omen, and exorcism texts. Many tablets are
copies of older Sumerian and Babylonian originals made at royal request, some obtained
directly from Babylon; a quoted royal letter from Ashurbanipal to Sha-duna documents an
organized, coercive tablet-collection campaign directed at the temple archives (Ezida)
of Barsippa and at private holdings. Roux frames this preservation project with an
explicit irony: "the Assyrians who caused so much destruction saved for posterity a
great deal of the spiritual treasures of Sumer, Akkad and Babylon" — his own summary
judgment, quoted here as attributed voice.

Nineveh was not the only such library — temple and possibly private libraries existed
at [[assur]] and [[nimrud]], and Anglo-Turkish excavations at Sultan Tepe (near Harran)
uncovered a priest's (Qurdi-Nergal's) personal collection including the Gilgamesh Epic,
the Legend of Narâm-Sin, "The Tale of the Righteous Sufferer," and a previously unknown
"Tale of the Poor Man of Nippur." Scribal practice at Nineveh is directly evidenced:
tablets were copied verbatim or adapted and modernized, with scribes sometimes leaving
gaps for damaged originals or adding marginal notes ("ul idi," "I do not understand";
"hepu labiru," "old break"). Wax-on-wood or ivory writing boards, hinged like folding
screens, supplemented clay tablets. Tablet series were identified via numbering or
"catch-lines." Roux speculates, hedged as his own inference ("might have been"), that
the library was tied to a scribal academy (*bît mummi*, "House of Knowledge") that drew
scholars to Nineveh.

## Significance

Nineveh anchors the material record for the climax of the Neo-Assyrian empire — its
architecture (the Palace without a Rival), its hydraulic engineering (the Jerwan
aqueduct), and above all its scribal culture (Ashurbanipal's Library), which is the
single richest surviving witness to Mesopotamian literary, scientific, and divinatory
textual tradition. It is also the site of the empire's abrupt end.

## Fall and Abandonment (612 BCE)

Nineveh fell in the summer of 612 BCE to a combined Babylonian-Median siege lasting
three months (see [[chaldaeans]] for the political narrative); the Babylonian Chronicle
records that the attackers "turned the city into a ruin-mound (tilu) and heaps of
debris." Roux notes, as a historiographic aside, "no one, as far as we know, sat on the
ruins of Nineveh to write a lamentation" — a contrast with the lament traditions
composed for other fallen Mesopotamian cities. By 401 BCE, when Xenophon marched through
the region with 10,000 Greek mercenaries, he did not recognize Nineveh's walls at all,
describing only "large undefended fortifications" near Mosul — evidence of near-total
abandonment within roughly two centuries of the sack. The site was eventually
reoccupied in Seleucid times as part of a general pattern of Mesopotamian resettlement
(grouped by Roux with Mari and Arslan-Tash as sites "reoccupied after long years of
abandonment").

## Key Debates

- The historicity and scale of the reported Sennacherib-Egypt disaster before Pelusium
  (biblical, Herodotean, and Berossan traditions of plague/divine intervention) bears on
  how Sennacherib's Levantine campaigns are read, though it concerns Egypt/the Levant
  rather than Nineveh directly; see [[assyrians]] Historiography section.
- The relative weight given to Assyria as "warlike" versus "learned" in modern
  historiography turns substantially on how Ashurbanipal's Library is read against the
  empire's simultaneous record of conquest and deportation; see [[assyrians]]
  Historiography section.

## Matthews densification (2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]].)* Matthews traces pre-Layard interest in the site through Carsten Niebuhr's 1766 mapping and Claudius Rich's 1820 survey, before Botta's December 1842 trenching and Layard's November 1845 discovery of two Assyrian palaces at neighbouring [[nimrud]]; British excavation continued at Nineveh under Rassam (to 1882) and later Max Mallowan (1920s–30s, alongside Arpachiyah, [[tell-brak|Tell Brak]], and Chagar Bazar). Following Liverani (1997: 88–9), Matthews stresses that 19th-century excavation technique could not detect or excavate mud-brick, leaving the lower town and non-monumental residential fabric of Nineveh (and other imperial capitals) essentially unexplored — so urban structure, household density, social stratification, and ethnic patterning within the city remain largely unknown. He also notes archaeological evidence for the city's fall: bodies of those killed in Nineveh's final hours have been recovered at the site (Stronach 1997).
