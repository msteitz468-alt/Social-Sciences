# Cholula

Monumental center in the Valley of Puebla, home to the **Great Pyramid**, the largest pre-Hispanic structure in the Americas by volume. Per [[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz (2013)]], Cholula built a rival monumental pyramid contemporary with the construction of Teotihuacan's Sun and Moon pyramids, drawing surrounding populations to concentrate there, though rural depopulation around Cholula was less pronounced than around [[sites/teotihuacan|Teotihuacan]]. Coe & Koontz suggest (attributed) that the eruption of the Popocatépetl volcano, c. mid-first century BCE, may have driven populations toward both Cholula and Teotihuacan, with the areas nearest the volcano in both valleys abandoned at this time.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The site is known through tunnel excavations into the Great Pyramid (excavators not individually named in this source) plus ethnohistoric annals. The earliest monumental structure was a terraced/plaza complex built in **talud-tablero** style, a tradition traceable back to Preclassic Tetimpa in Puebla; its tableros bore murals of polychrome skull-like heads on a black background. A later building phase enveloped the original pyramid within a simpler, stairway-covered structure.

## Occupation Sequence

- **Classic period**: a 165-ft (50-m) polychrome mural of life-sized figures, known as "the Drunkards," depicts drinking or inebriation possibly involving hallucinogenic mushrooms or peyote rather than alcohol.
- **Epiclassic**: after Teotihuacan's decline, Cholula's builders added a stone-faced temple substructure beneath the Great Pyramid's west face, with three superimposed talud-tableros bearing textile-mosaic motifs unlike Teotihuacan's; an associated patio with four stone altars included stelae carved in Classic Veracruz interlace style. Per ethnohistoric accounts, Cholula was taken over at this time by a Gulf Coast group known as the **Olmeca-Xicallanca** (the same group associated with [[sites/cacaxtla]]), who made it their capital and developed fine polychrome pottery.
- **Post-Classic transition**: the Olmeca-Xicallanca were overthrown by the Tolteca-Chichimeca (Toltecs) "sometime around 1200," who left the Great Pyramid a ruin and shifted worship to a Temple of Quetzalcoatl beneath the modern town square. In 1359 the kingdom of Huexotzingo took over the site. In its final form the Great Pyramid covered 25 acres (10 hectares) and reached 180 ft (55 m) in height.

## Significance

Cholula is Coe & Koontz's principal comparandum to Teotihuacan for Classic-period central Mexican urbanism outside the Valley of Mexico, and the source of the "Tollan"/generic-"civilized-city" naming confusion also affecting [[unknown|Tula]] (Teotihuacan and Cholula were both loosely termed "Tollan" in Nahuatl tradition — a source of past scholarly confusion, e.g. George Vaillant's mistaken equation of Toltec Tula with Teotihuacan).

## Key Debates

- **[POSITION]** The precise archaeological identity and Gulf Coast homeland of the Olmeca-Xicallanca cannot be securely fixed (see the parallel caution on [[sites/cacaxtla]]).
