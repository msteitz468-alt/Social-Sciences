# Folsom Site

Bison-kill locality near Folsom, New Mexico (Wild Horse Arroyo), whose **1927** demonstration of a fluted point in association with extinct bison broke the Hrdlička–Holmes “no deep antiquity” orthodoxy and established Pleistocene humans in America. Densified from [[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|Meltzer 2009]] Ch. 3 (“From Paleoliths to Paleoindians”) and Meltzer’s own later site project (Ch. 9), with a complementary [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] layer.

**Identity caution:** Folsom is an etic archaeological complex—not a people, language, or genetic population. Point styles need not equal ethnic groups (Meltzer).

**Date hygiene:** Meltzer 2009 ages are **radiocarbon years BP (uncalibrated)** unless noted.

## Discovery and Excavation History

### Meltzer 2009 Ch. 3 — breakthrough narrative

*(Attributed — Meltzer 2009.)*

- **27 August 1908** floods reshaped Folsom, NM. Sometime after, Black cowboy/foreman **George McJunkin** (Crowfoot Ranch)—self-taught naturalist and former slave—noticed bones at the base of newly incised **Wild Horse Arroyo** and told others including **Carl Schwachheim**. McJunkin died before Schwachheim’s December 1922 visit.
- Schwachheim and banker **Fred Howarth** took bones (1922) to the Colorado Museum of Natural History; **Harold Cook** identified extinct bison. **Figgins/Cook** visited March **1926** to excavate a **museum display skeleton**—not expecting archaeological remains—though aware of the antiquity controversy.
- Credibility context: Cook/Figgins prior deep-antiquity claims (Hesperopithecus pig tooth; Lone Wolf Creek ~350,000 yr claim; Frederick, OK grinders with mammoth) had left their reputation low when Folsom emerged.
- **1926:** Schwachheim found a delicately fluted spear point **out of ground** before spotted (association uncertain). Figgins ordered any human remains left in place (Hrdlička had advised inviting outsiders).
- **Late August 1927:** a Folsom point **firmly between a pair of bison ribs**; telegrams invited scientists to examine **in situ**.
- Visitors including **Barnum Brown** (AMNH), **Alfred Kidder** (Carnegie), **Frank Roberts** (Smithsonian) agreed non-accidental association: human hunters killed extinct Pleistocene bison—**pre-radiocarbon** proof of Ice Age human presence. Hrdlička was in Alaska; Holmes retired.
- Kidder publicly announced first Americans ~**15,000–20,000** years ago; Brown expanded 1928 excavations; new USGS geologists independently affirmed late Pleistocene antiquity—Meltzer’s later radiocarbon work found those geological estimates off by only ~a couple thousand years.
- **Sociology of acceptance:** resolution by a **core elite** (Rudwick-style), not community-wide vote; Kidder’s deposition/association judgment carried enormous weight. Cook and Figgins were sidelined as interpreters (Matthew Effect)—prior absurd claims made “our opinions… valueless” (Figgins); not invited to major post-discovery symposia.
- Early 1960s ¹⁴C: Folsom occupation at least ~**10,800** years old; Clovis ~**11,500** BP (Meltzer’s book framing).

### Fagan layer (complementary)

In **1908** McJunkin found extinct-bison bones and a spearpoint; the find reached Jesse Figgins only in 1925. Fagan stresses field-verification by independent specialists as what persuaded a skeptical discipline; likens impact to Darwin’s *Origin*; Hrdlička remained unconvinced. Fagan’s ~10,800 BP kill date aligns with early ¹⁴C (source [[fagan-great-journey-1987]]).

### Meltzer project reconstruction (1997+)

*(Attributed — Meltzer 2009 Ch. 9.)* Meltzer’s project dates the Folsom kill to **10,490 BP** in ancestral Wild Horse Arroyo during the [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]]; cow-calf herd of **32** killed in fall peak fat; **≥28** fluted points of distant Texas Panhandle (Alibates/Tecovas) and Colorado (Flattop/Black Forest) stone (**≥190 km**); most points broken on site; camp not found (uplands eroded after 9820 BP charcoal; valley possibly deeply buried); brief stay, not overwintering. Nearest used outcrop ~190 km SE, farthest ~450 km N—triangle of three stops ~**90,000 km²** (large but not excessive for Paleoindian standards).

## Occupation Sequence

- **Folsom complex** kill/butchery; type reference for Folsom points (thinner/shorter than Clovis; fluting often full-length—flakes up to ~5 cm long yet <1.5 mm thick from both faces of points ≤4 mm thick; average just over 5 cm × 2 cm). Fluting disappears after Folsom though lanceolate form and ground haft edges persist (Meltzer).
- Stratigraphically **later than Clovis** at [[blackwater-draw]] / Clovis type site. On the Great Plains/Rockies: Folsom just after **10,900 BP** lasting until ~**10,200 BP**; makers ranged plains Canada–Texas, west into Rockies, east to Wisconsin (Meltzer).
- Common kill tactic: arroyo trap (Folsom 32 animals; Olsen-Chubbuck 190 into >2 m arroyo). Large communal kills represent **<3%** of known Folsom sites yet dominate imagery (Frison, LaBelle et al. via Meltzer). No Paleoindian bison jumps—first jumps ≤~5,000 years (Head-Smashed-In); Bonfire Rockshelter “jump” reinterpreted as secondary camp (Byerly et al.).

## Significance

- Single-site resolution of a national debate: human antiquity in the New World.
- Template Meltzer uses for how **high-bar evidence** (association + elite witnessing) ends controversy—later parallel: [[monte-verde]] 1997.
- Established search template (arroyos, megafauna bonebeds) that biased the next decade toward kill sites and the big-game stereotype of [[paleoindian]] lifeways.
- Type case for Folsom fluting technology and long-distance stone conveyance.

## Key Debates

[[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]] · [[paleoindian]] · [[fluted-point]] · whether Folsom point styles map ethnic groups · cooperative mass drives vs quiet small kills · relation of Folsom boom to Firestone et al. claimed post-Clovis population collapse (Meltzer rejects collapse; Folsom follows Clovis with seeming boom on well-dated plains—see [[younger-dryas]]).

## Historiography

*(Meltzer Ch. 3 — Folsom as pivot.)* Meltzer rejects pure vindication narratives that [[hrdlicka-ales|Hrdlička]]/Holmes only hindered science: tough criticism crystallized issues of technology, form vs age, evolution, glacial geology, and dating; rejected earlier sites (Trenton, Lansing, Vero, etc.) were not what claimed. Folsom “looked nothing like” expected Paleolithic occupation—hence **Paleoindian**, not Paleolithic, terminology. McJunkin’s long-under-credited role (Fagan/Meltzer); Cook/Figgins sidelined despite excavation labor. Scientific-history lag: type site’s full modern reconstruction waited for Meltzer’s late-1990s project. Related Folsom **habitation** evidence elsewhere: Hanson (WY), Agate Basin, Mountaineer (CO Rockies)—kill sites alone under-sample lifeways.
