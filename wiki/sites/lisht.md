# Lisht

Early 12th-dynasty royal necropolis on the desert edge midway between [[meidum]] and [[dahshur]], associated with the capital Itjtawy (“Seizer of the Two Lands”) after Amenemhat I’s move north from Thebes. Primary synthesis: [[lehner-mark|Mark Lehner]], *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* (1997).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Maspero attributed the southern hill pyramid to Senwosret I in 1882; Gautier and Jéquier excavated in 1894 (including the famous pit of ten complete seated limestone statues of Senwosret I). The Metropolitan Museum of Art ran major seasons 1906–1943. Dieter Arnold renewed architectural study in 1984–87 and is Lehner’s principal authority for construction details, casing problems, and workforce analogies drawn from related Dahshur work. Lehner notes that more traces of pyramid building (quarries SE/SW/S; ramps from quarries and harbour; wood-sleeper roads; boat-timber hauling tracks) have been found at Senwosret I’s site than at any other pyramid.

## Occupation Sequence

### Amenemhat I

If Itjtawy was the pyramid town, it lay near the desert edge by modern Lisht; the Bahr el-Libeini (possible old Nile channel) swings close to the escarpment at the pyramid foot—potential harbour. Dorothea Arnold has argued that a large unfinished bay tomb south of Deir el-Bahri may have been begun for Amenemhat I before the northern transfer (perhaps only in the last decade of a ~30-year reign, 1991–1962 BCE). After ~190 years without a large royal pyramid, expertise may have been lost: core of small rough local limestone with loose sand, debris, and mudbrick fill. Remarkable incorporation of relief-decorated blocks from Old Kingdom monuments (including Khufu’s complex) and granite from Khafre’s complex in the descending passage—brought from [[saqqara]] and [[giza]] for what Lehner calls “spiritual efficacy.”

Dimensions (Lehner via Hayes): base ~84 m, height ~55 m, slope ~54°7′. Ground-level north entrance with chapel and red granite false door; shaft on vertical axis; **groundwater has prevented modern entry to the burial chamber**. Mortuary temple on a lower terrace; foundation deposits include plaques “The Places of the Appearance of Amenemhet”; another name “The Perfection of Amenemhet is Exalted” may name the temple. Inner limestone and outer mudbrick enclosures; 22 western shafts for royal women; valley temple unexcavated (below groundwater). Reliefs of Senwosret I in dismantled first-temple foundations suggest co-regency from Amenemhat’s year 20; Senwosret seems to have rebuilt the temple. William C. Hayes noted lack of grandeur and “provincialism”: Theban elements (terraces, central shaft, open causeway, certain relief styles) mixed with revived Memphite pattern. No satellite pyramid found.

### Senwosret I

Prominent hill ~2 km south of Amenemhat I. Foundation tablets: Senwosret Peteri Tawy (“Senwosret Beholds the Two Lands”); possible town names Khenemsut / Kha-Senwosret. Base 200 cubits (~105 m)—largest since Neferirkare, class of Djedefre and Menkaure; intended slope ~49°24′, ideal height ~61.25 m.

**Internal skeleton innovation:** eight walls radiating from centre to corners and mid-sides; each of eight triangles subdivided by three cross walls → 32 compartments filled with stepped stone slabs; backing stones + casing as exoskeleton; wooden dovetail cramps (some inscribed with the king’s name—Arnold estimates ~12,000 at this complex). Casing arrangement weakened structure (patches, cracks); open construction shaft and base unevenness up to 13–15 cm. Burial chamber never entered (Arnold calculates ~22–25 m below surface, near water table—Osirian connection Lehner stresses for early 12th-dynasty deep chambers). Granite plugs ~20 tons; robbers tunnelled around them.

Causeway originally open (roofed as late as year 22); niches every 10 cubits held nearly life-sized statues (red crown north / white crown south); eight complete statues found. Dual enclosures; inner wall with 150 *serekh* panels. Mortuary temple close to late Old Kingdom layout (Teti–Pepi II); Gautier’s unweathered ten statues may never have been installed. **Last satellite pyramid** and only one known in a Middle Kingdom complex—first phase 15.75 m square at OK satellite slope, later enlarged to 18.38 m. Outer enclosure: priests’ houses, granaries, hauling tracks, ritual pits, western mudbrick boat pit. Outside: mastabas of Vizier Mentuhotep (own causeway), Imhotep High Priest of Heliopolis, Senwosret-ankh (Pyramid Texts in burial chamber). Nine small queens’ pyramids built incrementally (some substructures unfinished; Pyramid 9 mudbrick core when stone ran out).

### After Lisht

Arnold’s “second phase” of MK pyramids begins with Amenemhat II at Dahshur: the attempt to revive late OK Memphite tradition is given up for experimental combinations; royal pyramids thereafter alternate between Dahshur and the Fayum-mouth area (Illahun, Hawara).

## Significance

- **Capital landscape of the early 12th dynasty** and re-centralization after First Intermediate fragmentation (Lehner’s political arc).
- **Best archaeological sample of MK pyramid construction logistics** (quarries, ramps, sleeper roads, control notes).
- **Last classical satellite pyramid**; deep Osirian chamber placement.
- **OK material reuse as ritual practice**, not mere thrift.
- Bridge from Theban 11th-dynasty forms to Memphite revival and then Arnold’s experimental second phase.

## Key Debates

- **Itjtawy = Amenemhat I pyramid town?** Spatial hypothesis, not proven by full town excavation at Lisht.
- **Deir el-Bahri unfinished tomb:** Mentuhotep Seankhkare vs Amenemhat I (Dorothea Arnold).
- **Hayes’s “provincialism”** vs successful adaptive revival.
- **Whether Gautier’s ten statues were ever installed.**
- **Osirian motivation for water-table-depth chambers** (Lehner interpretive package shared with later MK).

## Historiography

Lisht is an MMA site *par excellence*; Arnold’s restudy made it the technical gold standard for early Middle Kingdom pyramid architecture in Lehner’s synthesis. Amenemhat I’s flooded burial chamber leaves a permanent evidential hole. This page is densified from Lehner 1997; full MMA/Arnold publications remain primary for dimensions and phase plans.
