## Discovery and Excavation History

Angkor takes its name from the Sanskrit *nagara* ("holy city"). It was first
encountered by Europeans (Portuguese missionaries) in the 16th century, long after
its abandonment as a royal capital. The site's dynastic and construction sequence is
reconstructed principally from inscriptions in Sanskrit and archaic Khmer, supported
by architectural sequencing of the temple complexes. Zhou Daguan, a Chinese diplomat,
resided at Angkor Thom for ten months in 1296 and left a firsthand description of the
city at its height, including its population and royal palace; archaeological
excavation directed by Jacques Gaucher (from 2000) has since confirmed canals, houses,
ponds, and roads, along with evidence of palace retainers, artisans, slaves, and
resident Chinese merchants described in Zhou's account — an instance of textual and
material evidence independently converging, per [[scarre-human-past-2013]].

## Occupation Sequence

The Angkor region shows Bronze Age prehistoric settlement (c. 1000–500 BCE) and
several temples pre-dating 802 CE, indicating settlement continuity before the
Khmer state's formal founding; the "first major Angkorian center," Hariharalaya,
lies southeast of the main Angkor complex. Angkor was founded as a unified kingdom by
Jayavarman II, who was consecrated *chakravartin* ("supreme ruler on earth") in 802
CE. The dynastic and temple-building sequence recorded in inscriptions runs: Jayavarman
II (r. 802–835, died c. 835 at Hariharalaya) → Indravarman I (r. 877–889; built Preah
Ko and Bakong, and a large reservoir) → Yashovarman (r. 889–910; new capital at
Bakheng hill; built the Eastern Baray, 7.5 × 1.8 km) → Jayavarman IV (brief capital at
Koh Ker) → Rajendravarman II (r. 944–968; built Pre Rup and the Eastern Mebon) →
Jayavarman V (state temple Hemasringagiri) → the Mahidharapura dynasty from c. 1080
(Suryavarman I, r. 1002–1050, built Phimeanakas and began the Western Baray;
Suryavarman II, r. 1113–1150, built Angkor Wat) → Jayavarman VII (r. 1181–1219; built
Angkor Thom, its Bayon temple-mausoleum, Ta Prohm, Neak Pean, and Preah Khan). The
kingdom controlled most of lowland Cambodia and adjacent Thailand and Laos from
roughly 800 to 1435 CE. Angkor was abandoned as capital by the mid-15th century CE,
though Angkor Wat itself was never fully abandoned.

## Angkor as a Mandala (Higham 1989)

Charles Higham (1989) analyses Angkor as the apogee of the Southeast Asian [[mandala]] —
a polity defined by circles of allegiance to a divine overlord rather than by fixed
borders (see [[southeast-asian-state-formation]]). On this reading its dynastic history is
"a progressive increase in scale" in the legitimisation of power through display, feasting,
and patronage. Higham stresses (following Vickery 1986) that the recorded royal genealogies
are partly claims to genealogical seniority rather than plain fact, and that succession
often passed laterally between brothers — a recurrent source of friction and civil war.

Jayavarman II (r. 802–850 in Higham's dating) founded the mandala and instituted the royal
cult of the *kamraten jagat ta raja* ("the god who is king," per Jacques 1985) on the Kulen
plateau in 802 CE. Its durability, following Hermann Kulke (1984), rested on the conversion
of vassal mandalas into administrative provinces (*viṣaya*/*praman*), so that usurpers
sought to seize Angkor itself rather than found rival centres — though provincial fission
persisted (e.g. after the Cham sack of 1177). The successive barays escalated in scale from
Indravarman I's Indratataka through Yasovarman's Eastern Baray to Suryavarman I's Western
Baray (up to ~70 million m³); Suryavarman II (r. 1113–1150) built Angkor Wat, and Jayavarman
VII (r. from 1181), a Buddhist, gave Angkor its final form (Angkor Thom, the Bayon, Preah
Khan, Ta Prohm, hospitals, and roads). The Ta Prohm foundation stela records 79,365 people
in 3,140 villages assigned to its upkeep, and Jayavarman VII's 102 hospitals were supplied
by 81,640 people in 838 villages — figures implying surplus mobilisation well above
subsistence.

**Kingship, taxation, and warfare.** Higham (following Mabbett 1978) characterises the
Angkorian king as pivotal but not absolutely powerful — bound by law, religion, and
tradition, ruling through patronage (land grants, honorific posts, religious foundations)
rather than bureaucratic appointment; effective rule depended on retaining regional
landowners' and spiritual leaders' devotion. In the absence of money, taxation was rendered
in produce, goods, labour, and merit, tracked by functionaries maintaining land registers.
Following Jack Goody (1971) on control of "the means of destruction," Higham identifies
three forms of Angkorian warfare — civil war for central power, subjugation of vassals, and
conflict with neighbouring mandalas (Champa, Đại Việt, and the rising Thai centres) — waged
with war elephants, elephant-mounted ballistae, and Mekong/Tonle Sap naval forces.

**Decline.** Higham reads the end of "classic" Angkor not as simple collapse but as a shift
of the mandala's centre toward the Tonle Sap–Mekong junction as tribute reoriented to the
expanding Sukhothai and (from 1350) Ayutthaya mandala. Contributing factors he records
include the decline of the *kamraten jagat ta raja* cult and rise of populist Theravada
Buddhism (which obviated the monumental sacerdotal apparatus; Hagesteijn 1984), the growth
of private grandee estates rivalling the overlord, and siltation of the water system
(Groslier 1973–74). He draws an explicit comparative analogy to the 9th-century collapse of
the Peten Maya centres (via Renfrew's 1979 catastrophe model), flagged as analogy rather
than evidence. Source: [[higham-archaeology-mainland-southeast-asia-1989]]; see
[[higham-charles]].

## Significance

Angkor anchors the Khmer Empire's political and religious history and is the
principal archaeological witness to the transition from the earlier Mekong Delta
polities Funan and Chenla into a unified, monumental Khmer state. Its temple sequence
(Preah Ko, Bakong, Angkor Wat, Bayon, Ta Prohm, Preah Khan) provides a securely
dated architectural chronology anchored by inscriptions, and its baray reservoirs and
canal networks are central evidence for debates over Khmer water management and
urban scale.

## Key Debates

**Baray (reservoir) function.** The purpose of Angkor's large reservoirs (barays) is
explicitly framed by [[scarre-human-past-2013]] as disputed, with "much debate."
Position A holds the barays were primarily symbolic — representations of the cosmic
ocean surrounding the Hindu home of the gods, i.e., a ritual function. Position B
holds they served a practical irrigation-storage role for rice fields between the
city and the Great Lake (Tonle Sap). The discovery of sluice gates distributing water
via canals to mapped rice-field systems is cited by the source as evidence supporting
the irrigation-function reading, though the source itself does not adjudicate and
frames the question as still open. Higham (1989) records the sharper mid-20th-century
form of this dispute — Groslier's "hydraulic city" against Van Liere's sceptical reading
and Bronson's low estimate — in full at [[angkor-hydraulic-city-debate]].

**Indianization vs. indigenous development.** A broader debate over Southeast Asian
state formation bears directly on Angkor's predecessors: the traditional view credits
Indian cultural stimulus (Sanskrit royal names, Brahmi script, Hindu worship) as
seminal to the rise of states such as Funan and, by extension, Angkor. Michael Vickery
(1998) instead argues that Indian influence was "merely a veneer over a solid local
culture." Both positions are recorded here as attributed scholarly stances, without
adjudication; the fuller debate is at [[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]].

**Abandonment.** Deforestation-driven siltation of the water-distribution system is
proposed (Penny et al. 2006, as cited in [[scarre-human-past-2013]]) as a contributing
factor in Angkor's eventual abandonment, alongside political pressure from the
encroaching Thai in the 14th–15th centuries — an attributed, multi-causal account
rather than a single settled cause.
