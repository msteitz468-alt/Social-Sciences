# Benin City

Capital of the Benin (Edo) kingdom in the West African forest, modern southern Nigeria. In [[connah-african-civilizations-2016|Connah, *African Civilizations* (2016)]] it is a flagship forest **city-state** case—and unusually one where **Connah’s own 1960s fieldwork** supplies core chronology, architecture, and sacrifice evidence (Connah 1975 as cited in the 2016 synthesis). All substantive claims below are attributed to Connah 2016 (including his own earlier results and Darling’s later surveys as he reports them), until independent sources densify.

**Identity caution:** Archaeological locus ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetic population. Link with `associated_with` unless identity is argued. “Edo / Benin kingdom” labels for pre-contact centuries are etic/ethnohistoric; Oba institutions and 1897 documentary snapshots must not be projected wholesale onto thirteenth-century deposits without argument.

## Discovery and Excavation History

**Connah’s own fieldwork (central to this page).** Excavations during the 1950s by **Goodwin** and by **Connah** during the 1960s (Connah 1975) revealed substantial **post-European-contact** deposits on the site of the old palace. Connah’s own work also demonstrated **occupation of the city by about the thirteenth century**—the key pre-contact chronological anchor in his synthesis.

From those excavations Connah reports:

- a radiocarbon-dated **mass burial of at least forty-one young women** >12.5 m down a well-like cistern within the old palace area, interpreted as **ritual sacrifice** indicative of centralized authority; ethnohistoric/documentary parallels for throwing sacrificial victims into pits continue to **1897** (Roth via Connah);
- **edge-laid potsherd pavements** made during or prior to the **fourteenth century**, a practice that appears to end before European contact—as at [[ile-ife|Ife]], suggesting formal architecture; postcontact **coursed-mud architecture with impluvia** is ethnohistorically and archaeologically attested;
- artistic use of **copper-base alloys** as early as the **thirteenth century**, but **no evidence of casting** those metals prior to European contact recovered in the 1960s trenches; few castings have stratigraphic context; TL-dated cores are about **fifteenth century or later** (Willett & Fleming via Connah).

**Walls and earthworks.** The innermost “Benin City walls,” dated before European contact (possibly **mid-fifteenth century**) by radiocarbon, historical documentation, and oral tradition, comprise a bank-and-ditch of up to **17.4 m** total vertical height and **11.6 km** circumference—an enormous labour investment implying powerful centralized authority (illustrative labour: 5,000 people for one dry season or 1,000 over five). Connah’s 1960s surveys revealed **>145 km** of interlocking **outer enclosures** that appeared to hint at **synoecism**—union of villages into one capital—prior to the massive innermost wall.

**Darling’s rural network.** Darling and others showed Connah’s outer network was only a peripheral part of rural enclosures covering ~**6,500 km²**, estimated length **>16,000 km**, ≥**150 million person-hours** over centuries. Connah judges these earthworks probably have more to tell about **state formation via agricultural land power struggles** than about Benin City’s urban origins alone. On distribution, character, and potsherd statistics, Darling proposed southward colonization by savanna/savanna–forest ecotone Edo speakers into rainforest from the late first millennium AD—dense enclosures contrasting with near absence among Urhobo speakers just south—but Connah stresses the survey remains “**largely undated**” (**dating flag**).

**Environment and visibility.** Extensive ditches and banks are virtually invisible in dense rainforest or regrowth and must have been built when vegetation cover was far thinner, or they would have been of little use as boundaries (von Hellermann via Connah). Connah holds it possible Benin was not always deep rainforest as today: climatic change (e.g. drier conditions 700–200 years ago at Lake Bosumtwi) and human activity (long rotational bush-fallow; secondary forest already noted early twentieth century) could have created more open past conditions.

Forest-zone written sources cover only ~500 years; Bradbury’s Benin chronology is cited among oral/historical attempts that push only a little earlier with uncertain chronologies (Connah 2016).

## Occupation Sequence

Approximate sequence as Connah synthesizes it:

1. **By ~thirteenth century** — urban occupation demonstrated in Connah’s excavations; artistic copper-base alloys in use; mass sacrificial burial in palace-area cistern as evidence of centralized authority.
2. **During/prior to fourteenth century** — edge-laid potsherd pavements (formal architecture); practice ends before European contact.
3. **Possibly mid-fifteenth century** — innermost massive bank-and-ditch walls (pre-contact).
4. **Outer enclosures / rural earthworks** — multi-century labour investment around agricultural land competition; relative dating of synoecism before innermost wall is Connah’s 1960s reading; Darling extends the pattern regionally (largely undated).
5. **European contact onward** — deep post-contact deposits dominate old palace area; coursed-mud architecture with impluvia; castings with TL dates ~fifteenth century+; horses as conspicuous consumption despite trypanosomiasis; Atlantic trade later transforms subsistence (American plants) and stimulates further urban/state development that nonetheless had **earlier origins** (Connah position).
6. **1897** — British conquest; documentary end-point for sacrificial-pit practices and kingdom independence.

**Craft and metal networks.** Slightly after Ife’s second-quarter second-millennium casting excellence, Benin craftsmen excelled similarly in copper-base alloys (Craddock & Picton via Connah). Stable lead isotope analysis indicates metal used at Ife and in an early Benin period came from faraway **France**—extent of trading networks (Willett & Sayre via Connah). Multi-craft proficiency and monumental earthworks as civil engineering sit in Connah’s wider “diversity of forest technology” list.

**Subsistence and land.** Yam–oil palm bush-fallow systems inferred for forest/fringe sites; preferred upper interfluves; 3–4 years crop / 10–15 years fallow logic generating surplus then land competition. Darling’s earthwork pattern is read as expanding farming population competing for land—aligned with Connah’s *[[productive-land-hypothesis]]*. Poorer Igbo soils may favor dispersal vs nucleation on better-varied soils (comparative note to [[igbo-ukwu|Igbo-Ukwu]]).

## Significance

Benin City anchors forest **state formation** through:

- **labour mobilization** at wall and earthwork scale;
- **ritualized centralized violence** (thirteenth-century sacrificial victims);
- **formal architecture** shared with Ife (potsherd pavements);
- **synoecism** as a possible urban-origin pathway from village cluster to capital;
- **productive-land competition** written in the rural earthwork web more clearly than in the palace art alone.

Connah places Benin with Ife among emergent early second-millennium forest city-states meeting Mabogunje-type conditions for urbanism, under an indigenous multifactor model (surplus, iron, specialization, stratification, divine kingship ideology—Oba as chief priest—and trade as one internal factor). European contact is transformative but not originary for the city/state (*[[connah-african-civilizations-2016]]*, Ch. 7 conclusion).

Because Connah excavated here, the site also anchors his methodological voice: first-person demonstration of thirteenth-century occupation against palace deposits flooded with post-contact material; survey-led discovery of outer enclosures later dwarfed by Darling’s regional map.

## Key Debates

- **Urban origins vs rural state formation.** Connah’s outer enclosures as synoecism toward the city vs Darling’s vast rural network as primarily about agricultural land power struggles—Connah leans toward the latter for the wider earthworks while retaining the city sequence from his excavations.

- **Darling colonization model.** Southward Edo-speaker colonization into rainforest is “could well be right” but archaeological survey “largely undated” (Connah)—dating flag blocks firm acceptance.

- **Forest vs more open past environment.** Earthwork visibility requires thinner vegetation; climatic + anthropogenic opening is plausible (von Hellermann; Lake Bosumtwi)—not proven landscape reconstruction for every phase.

- **Pre-contact casting.** Artistic copper alloys from thirteenth century vs casting evidence mainly fifteenth century+ / post-contact in Connah’s trenches—gap between use of cast objects and local production evidence.

- **External stimulus vs indigenous origins.** Same forest-wide debate as Ife: pre-European states/cities exist; long-distance metal (even French isotope signatures) and later Atlantic trade enhance rather than found the polity (Connah position).

- **Sacrifice and the state.** Mass burial as ritual sacrifice indicating centralized authority is Connah’s interpretation grounded in context (palace cistern) and ethnohistoric parallels to 1897—violent ritual as state capacity, not only “custom.”

All interpretive claims date-stamped **(as of Connah 2016)**; post-2016 Benin archaeology, museum repatriation research, and earthwork remote sensing are not incorporated here.

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed — Akinwumi Ogundiran in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]] Ch. 59; also Monroe Ch. 48 forest states; Sinclair earthworks note. **as of 2013**.)*

### Earthworks and emergence

Darling’s survey: **>16,000 km** concentric earthworks around **>500** settlements, enclosing **6,500 km²** in Edo-Esan (SW Nigeria); agricultural landscape from ~AD 500; walled villages coalesced AD 800–1000 into polities with hierarchies and specializations. **Benin emerged late 12th/mid-13th c.** from this process; innermost bank-and-ditch average height **17.4 m**, circumference **~11.6 km**, radiocarbon late 12th/early 13th c. (Connah 1975 via Ogundiran). Rise as political solution to **land and trade-route competition**. Coral, ivory, brass/bronze monopolized by royalty; pre-15th c. copper from [[ile-ife|Ile-Ife]]/Nupe; post-15th c. European brass primary—commemorative heads/plaques communicate authority.

**Agriculture-first path; Atlantic trade intensifies brass art after 15th c., not founding cause** (Ogundiran notes).

### Town goals and Atlantic-era intensification

Ogundiran: early towns of central Ghana and Yoruba–Edo typify **community-building** (lineages, diasporas, migrants form governing associations → expanded settlement); later 17th–18th c. militaristic-state towns typify **authority-building**. Divine kingship + town as political centre developed simultaneously AD 800–1000 in Yoruba–Edo, most elaborate at Ile-Ife from 11th c., **replicated 13th–15th c.** (including Benin). Hierarchies permeable, power diffuse even with kingship.

After 16th c., towns exploded across Bight of Benin and Gold Coast hinterlands with middle-phase Euro-African commerce and militaristic hegemonic states (**Oyo, Benin, Dahomey, Asante**) building hierarchical provincial landscapes unlike earlier weakly integrated patrimonial kingdoms. Hueda vs Dahomey contrast (Monroe): weak provincial integration vs fuller territorial integration via palaces along trading highways. 18th-c. hinterlands “the most urbanized landscape in Africa” (Ogundiran—attributed).
