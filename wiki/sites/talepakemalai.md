## Discovery and Excavation History

Talepakemalai is a stilt-house village site built over a lagoon/reef flat in the Mussau Islands, Bismarck Archipelago, Papua New Guinea. It was excavated by [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Patrick Vinton Kirch]] and team across the 1985–88 field seasons as part of the 1985 Lapita Homeland Project, with local excavator Bauwa Sagila credited by name in Kirch's first-person narrative (attributed, Kirch 2017). The anaerobic, waterlogged lagoon-bed deposits preserved wooden stilt-house posts and other normally perishable organic material — a modern (1980s), controlled excavation with strong evidentiary quality, favorably contrasting with older, looser digs per the wiki's excavation-quality-as-reliability-fact principle.

## Occupation Sequence

Talepakemalai was occupied for roughly four to five centuries, divided into an early phase (ca. 1300–1000 BC) and a later phase (ca. 1000–800 BC), identified through changes in the site's exchange network (see Significance). It is the largest known Lapita site, covering up to 80–82+ hectares, though not all of this area was necessarily occupied simultaneously; at maximum extent it could represent up to roughly 150 households, though the actual occupied population was likely smaller in practice.

## Significance

The site yielded more than 10,000 plant remains, a porpoise-bone carved anthropomorphic figure (a "Lapita god"), abundant Canarium almond and coconut shells (one coconut bearing grater marks), Trochus-shell fishhooks (both bottom-fishing and trolling types), obsidian sourced to the Admiralty Islands and the Willaumez Peninsula (New Britain), and elaborate dentate-stamped ceramics carrying anthropomorphic face motifs — among the most figurative such decoration known from any Lapita site. Talepakemalai functioned as a hub in a long-distance exchange network involving obsidian, pottery from roughly a dozen distinct clay sources in its early phase, oven stones, chert, and metavolcanic adzes imported, with Trochus-shell fishhooks, rings, and beads exported. This network contracted markedly over time: the early phase (1300–1000 BC) shows high-volume, high-diversity exchange (two obsidian sources, pottery from about a dozen clay sources), while the later phase (1000–800 BC) shows contraction to a nearer Manus obsidian source and roughly six ceramic sources — attributed by Kirch (1988b, 1997a) to declining need for homeland linkages as daughter colonies matured.

As the largest and most intensively studied Lapita settlement, Talepakemalai anchors much of the material-culture and exchange-network characterization of the broader [[lapita-cultural-complex]], including the definition of [[dentate-stamping]] as its diagnostic decorative technique.
