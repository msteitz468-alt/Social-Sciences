# Kabwe (Broken Hill)

## Discovery and Excavation History

The **Broken Hill** skull (modern place-name **Kabwe**, Zambia) is a classic Middle Pleistocene archaic *Homo* find from mining context. [[phillipson-david-w|Phillipson]] retains the older archaeological name “Broken Hill” per his convention of using names current at first substantive publication, while noting the modern toponym (as of Phillipson 2005 preface/Ch. 3).

Excavation quality is that of early industrial recovery rather than modern single-context archaeology — a reliability fact for associating fauna, industries, and the skull.

## Occupation Sequence

Not a well-controlled habitation sequence in Phillipson’s survey; the site’s importance is the **fossil** and its morphological links (e.g. similarity to the Bodo skull, Ethiopia, deposits dated ~600 ka; as of Phillipson 2005).

## Significance

Phillipson groups the Kabwe/Broken Hill skull with late Acheulean-era archaic *Homo sapiens* / *H. heidelbergensis* forms — competing designations he records without forced choice (Rightmire and others; as of Phillipson 2005). It anchors south-central Africa in Middle Pleistocene human evolution narratives alongside [[kalambo-falls]] Acheulean contexts.

## Key Debates

Absolute dating of the skull and its industry associations remain disputed in the wider literature; Phillipson’s 2005 synthesis does not settle a single calendar age. Taxonomic label (archaic *H. sapiens* vs *H. heidelbergensis*) is **position-recording**, not wiki-voice fact.
