# Meidum

Southern Memphite pyramid site where Sneferu’s builders converted a step pyramid into the first true smooth-sided pyramid (E3), anchoring the Archaic-to-classic transition in [[lehner-mark|Mark Lehner]]’s *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* (1997).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Flinders Petrie worked Meidum after his Giza seasons, recovering early plain-style wooden coffin fragments from the burial chamber (now University College London) and documenting superstructure and complex elements still fundamental to modern plans. Ludwig Borchardt’s construction analyses (including thin high straight-on ramp reconstructions from an E2 recess and “girdle stone” accretion theories discussed elsewhere for Giza) shaped early technical literature. Twentieth-century clearance of the debris mound around the standing three-step tower recovered later remains but—as Lehner stresses—no 4th-dynasty ropes, timbers, or workers’ bodies that would support a catastrophic construction-collapse model. Local casing and packing stone continued to be quarried into Petrie’s day, explaining much of the present ruin profile.

## Occupation Sequence

### Attribution and stages

Sneferu (first king of Manetho’s 4th dynasty; throne c. 2575 BCE in Lehner’s framework) is identified as builder start-to-finish: ancient site name Djed Sneferu (“Sneferu Endures”) and Sneferu’s name in site texts outweigh earlier attributions to Huni based only on the need for a large royal tomb. Stadelmann’s staging (as Lehner reports):

| Stage | Form | Chronology (Stadelmann via Lehner) |
|---|---|---|
| E1 | Seven-step pyramid | early reign |
| E2 | Eight-step completed | first ~14 years |
| E3 | True pyramid fill-out (horizontal courses) | last ~15 years; return probably years 28–29 after Dahshur work |

E3 slope ~51°50′35″ (practically that of Khufu’s Great Pyramid at [[giza]]); base ~144 m; height ~92 m. Early stages used traditional inward-sloping accretions; the final sheath used more regular courses and fine white limestone over local stone step faces. Horizontal coursing for the true-pyramid shell is first seen in the upper Bent Pyramid at [[dahshur]], then applied at Meidum.

### Interior and complex

Long north-face passage (lower part in open trench) to a chamber at roughly original desert surface—first use of **corbelling** for a burial-chamber roof (versus Djoser’s granite beams at [[saqqara]]). Chamber ~5.9 × 2.65 m, height ~5.05 m, unfinished; no sarcophagus found; vertical shaft ~6.5 m with plug recesses; cedar logs may have moved a sarcophagus. Rectangular enclosure traces (~236 × 218 m); small south satellite (badly destroyed); bedrock-cut causeway later limestone-sided (~210 m); minuscule east stone chapel with winding passages and two blank 4.2 m round-topped stelae; valley end only mudbrick walls (no full valley temple). Lehner reads the tiny chapel as possibly commemorative if Sneferu finished Meidum as cenotaph rather than tomb.

### Elite cemetery

First newly established élite necropolis since Hor-Aha’s 1st-dynasty officials’ cemetery at Saqqara: unfinished shift from older eastern-escarpment layout toward a better-organized western cemetery that Lehner calls the seed of Khufu’s Western Cemetery. Mastaba 16 of Nefermaat (Sneferu’s son; experimental paste-inlay hieroglyphs); Mastaba 17 of an anonymous prince. Other sons (e.g. Kanefer near later Amenemhat II at Dahshur) did not inherit; the throne passed to Khufu.

## Significance

- **First true pyramid** in the experimental sequence that produces the classic 4th-dynasty form; slope template for Khufu.
- **Construction vocabulary:** corbelled chambers, satellite, causeway, east chapel, enclosure—elements that become standard [[pyramid-complex]] kit.
- **Programme scale:** part of Sneferu’s three-colossal-plus-Seila mass exceeding Khufu (Lehner).
- **Social archaeology of the court:** virgin élite necropolis as organizational template for Giza cemeteries.

## Key Debates

- **Huni vs Sneferu.** Older literature assigned Meidum to Huni; Lehner follows name evidence for Sneferu throughout.
- **Collapse during construction?** Popular tower-in-rubble image; discounted by debris excavation lacking 4th-dynasty construction casualties—stripping and packing removal better explain the present form (Lehner 1997).
- **Cenotaph vs tomb.** Tiny unfinished chapel, blank stelae, unfinished chamber, and Sneferu’s dual Dahshur projects support Stadelmann/Lehner readings that Meidum (and Bent) were completed as cenotaphs; North Dahshur as likely burial.
- **Why blank stelae?** Lehner conjectures unexpected royal death and aggressive young Khufu freezing work after E3 with only dressing/inscription left—interpretive, not proven.

## Historiography

Meidum’s modern image is inseparable from Petrie’s documentation and from visual clichés of a “collapsed” pyramid that technical clearance has revised. Borchardt’s construction hypotheses remain reference points even where later work rejects them. Stadelmann’s Sneferu-wide chronology (Meidum–Dahshur–return) is the spine of Lehner’s narrative.
