# Bodmin Moor

Granite upland (~200 km²) near Cornwall's eastern boundary, treated by [[tilley-christopher]] in *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010|Interpreting Landscapes]]* (2010, Ch. 8) as one of southern Britain's best-preserved upland "fossil" prehistoric landscapes. Survey-region page: not a single excavation locus.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Base mapping for Tilley's interpretative walk is Johnson and Rose (1994) and Sites and Monuments Register work (Quinnell and others). Tilley's chapter is second-phase phenomenological field research (walking, intervisibility, alignment), not primary excavation. Excavated cairns/settlements (Colliford, Davidstow, Leskernick area literature as Tilley cites) supply chronology anchors he re-reads.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed as Tilley 2010 presents prior literature.)* Seasonal Mesolithic coastal–upland use; slight Neolithic occupation with long cairns and hilltop enclosures referencing **tors** at reserved distance; Bronze Age expansion of circles, rows, cairns, tor-cairns, and land division. Rough Tor and Brown Willy as visual dominants.

## Significance

Tilley argues (attributed) that on Bodmin **knowledge of landscape** mediated through monuments was a primary medium of power when land and staples were hard to monopolise; circles, rows, and boundaries progressively structured movement and sacred access. Tors treated as non-domesticated "megaliths" (ethnographic analogy flagged). ## Key Debates

Interpretive (Tilley's power/knowledge reading vs functional/settlement-focused survey); four-way non-identity — granite archaeology is not a single people or language.

## Historiography

Culture-historical and landscape-survey traditions (Johnson & Rose) provide the corpus; Tilley 2010 is an interpretive overlay. Dating limits and incomplete excavation coverage constrain claims — keep attributed.
