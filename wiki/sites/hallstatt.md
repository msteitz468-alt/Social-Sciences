# Hallstatt

## Discovery and Excavation History

Lakeside settlement, cemetery, and salt mines in the Austrian Salzkammergut; **type-site** of the [[hallstatt-culture]].

Cunliffe (1997) recounts the modern archaeological origin: systematic prehistory after C. J. Thomsen’s Three Age model (Stone/Bronze/Iron) and Worsaae’s stratigraphic elaborations made broad correlation possible between Iron Age material and classical Celts where sources indicated Celts lived. **Johann Georg Ramsauer** excavated the Hallstatt cemetery from **1846 to 1863**, recovering about **1,000 graves** — cremations and inhumations with weapons, vessels, and jewellery — and organic material preserved by the salt mines. Ramsauer conventionally called the graves “Celtic.” Most graves span the **seventh and sixth centuries BCE**, with a few into the early fifth; Hallstatt then declined as Dürrnberg salt developed (parallel salt working from c.600 BCE; Cunliffe has both Hallstatt and Dürrnberg mining ceasing about **400 BCE**).

Nineteenth-century work made Hallstatt the chronological and cultural label for the earlier European Iron Age after Hans Hildebrand’s 1872 bipartition (Hallstatt vs La Tène). Excavation quality is that of a mid-nineteenth-century cemetery campaign later re-studied; salt-mine archaeology adds organic and industrial evidence the cemetery alone cannot supply.

## Occupation Sequence

- **Salt exploitation** from the Bronze Age (Cunliffe: salt mined at Hallstatt from c.1000 BCE), with cemetery and settlement evidence through Hallstatt Iron Age phases (C–D floruit).
- **Cemetery assemblage** anchors seriation of Hallstatt C long swords, horse gear, and graded elite kits used pan-regionally.
- **Later fifth–early fourth centuries:** decline relative to Dürrnberg and to the northern Early [[la-tene-culture]] foci; mining cessation c.400 BCE coincides with broader west-central European reordering on the eve of historically attested [[celtic-migrations]].

## Significance

- **Names** the Hallstatt archaeological culture/period — the earlier half of the conventional European Iron Age bipartition.
- Anchors **west–east Hallstatt** comparisons and Cunliffe’s Mediterranean–barbarian contact narrative: elite warrior gear, feasting vessels, and salt-economy wealth in the Alpine zone.
- Demonstrates **four-way non-identity risk in historiography**: Ramsauer’s “Celtic” label and later culture-history equated material culture with ethnonym; Cunliffe historicizes that equation without asserting identity (see [[celticity-debate]], [[celts]]).
- Eastern communication axis (Po Valley–Veneti–east Alpine fringes–Vltava–Baltic amber) runs through the salt zone of Hallstatt and Dürrnberg in Cunliffe’s fifth-century geography.

## Key Debates

- How far Hallstatt material equals early Celtic speech or classical *Keltoi* — open; position-recording on [[celticity-debate]].
- Absolute chronology of cemetery phases vs Reinecke Ha C–D brackets (refined since 1997 by dendrochronology and calibrated ¹⁴C beyond this source).
- Relation of Hallstatt salt community to west Hallstatt “princely” centres (Heuneburg, Vix, Hochdorf) — same broad cultural horizon at elite level, not a single polity.
