# Coba

## Discovery and Excavation History

Major northern lowland capital among lakes in northeastern Yucatán. [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler (2006)]] note that Sylvanus Morley’s Carnegie Institution program (including Chichen Itza work 1924–40) also outfitted expeditions mapping Coba and [[calakmul]]. Subsequent research densified settlement, sacbe, and stela chronologies; this page follows the 2006 synthesis.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Sharer & Traxler 2006.)*

- **Setting:** Northern lowlands—rainfall usually under 2,000 mm; thin humus; cenotes principal water sources in much of Yucatán; cotton climate optimal for textile production. Early/mid Classic northern centers included Coba, Edzna, Izamal, Dzibilchaltun while south/central lowlands were dominated by [[tikal]]–[[calakmul]] rivalry.
- **Scale:** ~70 km²; peak population over 50,000—rivals Calakmul/Tikal scale in their estimates. Divine-king dynasty; ≥32 stelae (23 carved); earliest stela AD 623; Stela 20 at AD 684. Zenith ca. AD 730–1000.
- **Sacbe network and Yaxuna:** Sixteen *sacbeob*; longest Maya causeway over 100 km to Yaxuna. Yaxuna under Coba control ca. AD 600–750 to secure the western border and block [[chichen-itza]]; Terminal Classic saw Puuc buildings and western ceramic ties at Yaxuna as the geopolitical map shifted. Sacbe systems map realms more generally (Middle Preclassic Nakbe; Late Preclassic El Mirador; Classic Calakmul, Caracol, Coba).
- **Polity size:** Causeway extents used as polity proxies: El Mirador Late Preclassic ~5–6,000 km²; Caracol Late Classic ~5,500; Calakmul ~4,000; **Coba peak end Late Classic possibly ~20,000 km²**—among the largest territorial estimates in their table. Conquest-era northern lowland polities ~1,000–11,000 km² as ethnohistoric comparanda.
- **Terminal Classic pressure and abandonment:** Declined over the next 200 years as surrounded and probably attacked by Chichen Itza; abandoned by ca. AD 1200. Itza exploited central Yucatán “no-man’s-land” between Coba and Puuc/northern plains rivals; by ~AD 900 sustained campaign east and west incorporating most northern lowlands—largest/most populous state in Maya history on their reading. Yaxuna conquered; even Coba declined and abandoned, cut off from former networks. Ek Balam (midway Coba–Chichen) ended divine kingship ca. AD 900, “plausibly” the same agentic horizon though with little direct conquest evidence.
- **Postclassic reoccupation:** Reoccupation and shrine construction at Coba with Mayapan/east-coast mural and plaster styles (14th–15th centuries). Concurrent east-coast prosperity (Tulum, Tancah, Xelha; Cozumel pilgrimage) driven by bulk seaborne canoe trade. Tulum possibly founded as Mayapan-allied east-coast trading port.
- **Political model note:** Size ≠ power in Maya geopolitics (Yaxchilan smaller than Piedras Negras yet conquered it; Dos Pilas defeated Tikal with Calakmul aid). Coba nonetheless ranks among strong large northern capitals with divine-king publicity contrasting Chichen’s *multepal*-leaning imagery.

## Significance

Largest Late Classic northern polity in Sharer & Traxler’s synthesis; type-case for sacbe-defined territoriality (Yaxuna corridor); foil to Chichen Itza’s Terminal Classic expansion; Postclassic shrine reuse links Classic capitals to Mayapan-era east-coast religion and trade. Central to northern half of [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] (heartland demise ca. 800–900 vs northern zenith then decline ca. 800–1100/1200).

## Key Debates

- **Cause of Coba’s decline** — Chichen military encirclement/attack vs multi-factor Terminal Classic stress (overpopulation, environment, drought, leadership failures) that also felled [[uxmal]] and other northern centers.
- **Polity-area estimates from sacbes** — upper-bound ~20,000 km² vs Chase & Chase ~60 km military radius (~11,300 km² max) logistics models.
- Weak-state vs strong-state analogies applied to Maya diversity; Coba’s divine-king stelae place it nearer “strong-state” Classic capitals (Calakmul, Tikal, Copan) than Chichen’s council-house imagery.

## Historiography

Carnegie mapping established Coba’s monumentality; later settlement and sacbe surveys (and Yaxuna research) reframed it as a territorial state rivaling southern giants.
