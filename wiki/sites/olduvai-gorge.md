## Discovery and Excavation History

Olduvai Gorge, on the south-east edge of the Serengeti in Tanzania, is a roughly 25-mile-long, up to 300-foot-deep gorge cut by a seasonal river through lake sediments that began accumulating about two million years ago (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977). [[leakey-louis|Louis Leakey]] first visited the gorge and found stone tools there around 1931 — twenty-eight years before the site's first major hominid discovery. [[leakey-mary|Mary Leakey]] has analyzed tool industries at more than twenty occupation sites at Olduvai since the mid-1930s. On 17 July 1959, while Louis Leakey was in camp with influenza, Mary Leakey found the first important Olduvai hominid fossil: a robust australopithecine skull later named "Zinjanthropus boisei" (per [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]]). Within about two years a second, smaller-skulled but larger-brained hominid was found; it was formally named Homo habilis in 1964.

Excavation quality: the Leakeys' decades-long, systematic stratigraphic work at Olduvai — with over twenty analyzed occupation sites and cross-checked potassium-argon/fission-track dating — is presented in the source as unusually thorough among East African hominid sites, in contrast to earlier, less controlled discoveries elsewhere (attributed characterization; source's own account).

## Occupation Sequence

Olduvai's roughly 300 feet of sediment and volcanic ash accumulated over approximately two million years (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977), providing a stratified sequence against which fossils are dated relatively — a fossil found between two ash layers of known potassium-argon age is dated as falling between those dates, since fossils and non-volcanic rock cannot be dated directly. The Olduvai hominid collection came to include [[australopithecus|Australopithecus boisei]], Australopithecus africanus, and [[homo-habilis|Homo habilis]], which the authors interpret as having occupied the lake shore simultaneously (attributed). The lowest layer, Bed I, contains crude pebble-chopper tools of the Oldowan industry, which the authors state persisted for about a million years before evolving into a "Developed Oldowan" and disappearing roughly a million years ago (attributed). A second, later tool tradition, the Acheulian industry — hallmarked by the hand ax and attributed to [[homo-erectus]] — is reported to have coexisted with the Developed Oldowan for about half a million years before the Developed Oldowan disappeared. A two-million-year-old rough circle of stones, associated with only about thirty bone and stone fragments, is presented as possibly the oldest known man-made structure (as of Leakey & Lewin 1977).

## Significance

Olduvai Gorge anchors the book's account of hominid tool-industry succession (Oldowan → Developed Oldowan / Acheulian) and of multiple hominid taxa — robust and gracile australopithecines and early Homo — occupying the same landscape. The fission-track and potassium-argon cross-checks at Olduvai are cited in [[leakey-lewin-origins-1977|Origins]] as showing "fair agreement," lending the site's chronology comparatively higher confidence than some other East African localities discussed in the source.

## Key Debates

No debate specific to Olduvai is recorded in this source beyond the general four-way hominid-industry association question (see [[australopithecus]], [[homo-habilis]]): the association of stone-tool industries with particular hominid taxa (e.g., attributing the Acheulian to Homo erectus) is treated in the source as inferential, not directly demonstrated — an `associated_with`, not identity, relation per this wiki's evidence-class protocol.

**Hunting versus scavenging.** In *[[binford-bones-1981|Bones: Ancient Men and Modern Myths]]* (1981), [[binford-lewis|Binford]] reanalyzed the Olduvai fauna from Mary Leakey's *Olduvai Gorge, Vol. 3* (1971) using [[actualistic-studies|actualistic]] wolf-den and [[nunamiut|Nunamiut]] control data. He argued (attributed) that the "famous Olduvai sites are not living floors" but ravaged palimpsests, that his Factor 1 is a survivorship (destruction) signal, and that the anatomical-part composition fits hominid **scavenging** of low-utility marrow bones rather than hunting and food-sharing at [[home-base|home bases]] — challenging [[isaac-glynn|Isaac]]'s model. This launched the [[hunting-scavenging-debate|hunting-versus-scavenging debate]] that the FLK Zinj cut-mark work (Bunn & Kroll; Potts & Shipman; Domínguez-Rodrigo, above) subsequently reframed toward a mixed / early-access position. See [[binford-bones-study]].

### Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein reports Olduvai (northern Tanzania), first investigated by Hans Reck in 1913 and worked by
[[leakey-louis|Louis]] and [[leakey-mary|Mary Leakey]] from 1935; the 1959 "Zinjanthropus"
(*Paranthropus boisei*) skull, dated ~1.75 Ma by a pioneering [[potassium-argon-dating|radiopotassium]]
application, nearly doubled the accepted timespan of human evolution. The sequence (Beds I–IV,
Masek, Ndutu, Naisiusiu) spans 2.03 Ma–15 ka; Bed I fossil deposits accumulated rapidly ~1.86–1.75
Ma. Stone tools run [[oldowan-industry|Oldowan]] (Bed I–Lower Bed II) → Developed Oldowan and
[[acheulean-industry|Acheulean]] (Upper Bed II–Bed IV). The FLK Zinj site (~1.75 Ma) yielded ~2,500
Oldowan artifacts and ~60,000 bones; Bunn & Kroll read the cut-marks and skeletal-part data as
early carcass access (hunting or dominant scavenging), but Klein notes this has been contested for
~20 years (trampling pseudo-cut-marks, disputed criteria) with "no consensus in sight." The DK
"stone circle" is as plausibly tree-root fracturing as a hut base (see [[home-base]]).

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*Attributed — Domínguez-Rodrigo (Ch. 21), Sahnouni et al. (Ch. 22), Graham Connah (Ch. 2) in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]]; **as of handbook ~2012**.*

Connah notes the Leakeys established a ~2-million-year stratified palaeontological and archaeological sequence at Olduvai with K-Ar-datable volcanic ash, alongside surface excavation methods pioneered at Olorgesailie. Domínguez-Rodrigo: the **Oldowan** is named from Olduvai; formal definition Louis Leakey 1951, definitive typology Mary Leakey 1971. Only Olduvai shows Mary Leakey’s full type diversity among Oldowan sites; she subdivided Olduvai Oldowan → Developed Oldowan A/B/C. Function analyses at Olduvai 1.9–1.3 mya emphasize heavy-duty pounding unrelated to carcasses (Mora & de la Torre 2005). **FLK Zinj** (Bed I) is Domínguez-Rodrigo’s key anthropogenic assemblage for the hunting/primary-access vs passive-scavenging debate: he asserts primary access (corrected tooth marks; midshaft cutmarks; disarticulation signatures) and refutes passive scavenging for such sites — a **position**, not field consensus. Most Early Pleistocene sites >1.5 mya lack compelling anthropogenic bone–tool association except FLK Zinj; intentional reoccupation argued at FLK Zinj and EG 10 Gona.

Sahnouni et al. reassign Developed Oldowan A within Oldowan and DO B within Early Acheulean; Oldowan–Acheulean transition appears rapid with few transitional assemblages (**position**). Early *Homo* signals after ~2.3 mya (Olduvai, Turkana) retain morphological diversity (Foley Ch. 20). Industry ≠ species: Olduvai’s multi-taxon fossil record must not be collapsed into a single toolmaking people.
