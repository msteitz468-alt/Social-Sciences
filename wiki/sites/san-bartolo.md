# San Bartolo

## Discovery and Excavation History

Preclassic mural site; William Saturno and colleagues; illustrations by Heather Hurst cited throughout [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- Murals ~100 BC among oldest Maya paintings: Maize God, Hero Twins-related bloodletting, coronation themes — mythic program later seen in Classic art and Popol Vuh tradition.
- Texts 300–200 BC among earliest Maya writing, prior to many Isthmian monuments — **upends** simple models of writing diffusion from Gulf Isthmian script to Maya.
- Evidence for C&H claim that Late Preclassic is “proto-Classic.”

## Significance

Keystone for Preclassic literacy, mythology, and the independence of Maya script development.

## Key Debates

- Relationship of early Maya script to Isthmian/Epi-Olmec writing.
- Continuity of myth from Preclassic murals to Colonial Popol Vuh.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Site scale and the Pinturas murals

As of Sharer & Traxler 2006: San Bartolo (NE Petén; discovered by William Saturno 2001) covers ~1 km², >100 masonry structures; most major structures Late Preclassic (ca. 400 BC–AD 200) over Middle Preclassic foundations. Str. 1 (**Pinturas**) penultimate stage before ca. 100 BC with unique interior murals — most important Preclassic Maya mural find; dated archaeologically/stylistically ca. 100 BC. No Long Count, but a **royal inauguration scene** with hieroglyphic text including a clear *ajaw* glyph — earliest known representation of lowland divine-king accession in their synthesis. Painted glyphs on the murals rank among earliest known Maya texts (media inventory also lists stone monuments and portable objects for Late Preclassic). Evidence from Cerros, San Bartolo, and early texts associates the term *ajaw* (lord) with Late Preclassic Maya ruler title.

### Creation, maize god, and accession ritual

Late Preclassic murals depict one version of the **creation of the present world** — compared with detailed Classic creation texts at [[palenque]] (First Mother, First Father, Palenque Triad GI–GIII; present world 13/14 August 3114 BC) and [[quirigua]] Stela C’s primordial three-stone hearth. Maize god (God E / Hun Hunapu) death and rebirth appear in San Bartolo scenes. Scaffold elevation of the ruler shown at San Bartolo and Piedras Negras — part of Classic royal ritual package (headband, K’awiil scepter, transformation into *k’uhul ajaw*).

### Lowland vs southern rulership display

Authors distinguish two Late Preclassic traditions: (1) **Lowland** — rulers depicted in private settings (San Bartolo murals; personal objects e.g. Kichpanha bloodletter, Pomona earflare); public power via rituals and architectural sculpture (masks on temples at Cerros, [[el-mirador]] Str. 34, [[tikal]] 5C-54, Uaxactun E-VII-sub and Group H *witz* masks); emphasis on cosmological forces supporting the **institution** of rulership rather than individual career achievements; writing used to dedicate ritual/prestige items. (2) **Southern** — emphasis on achievements and pedigree of individual rulers on public stelae. San Bartolo is a type-case for the lowland private/cosmological pattern within the broader El Mirador–era hierarchy of sites.
