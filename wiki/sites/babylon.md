# Babylon

## Discovery and Excavation History

Babylon was founded, per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]], by the Amorite ruler Sumuabum in 1894 BCE, becoming under [[amorites|Amorite]] rule the capital of a modest kingdom before Hammurabi (r. 1792–1750 BCE, Roux's middle-chronology-adjacent dates — treat as approximate, not exact per the wiki's Chronological Uncertainty Protocol) raised it to the rank of a major capital and elevated its city-god Marduk within the pantheon.

Knowledge of Hammurabi's Babylon is overwhelmingly textual rather than architectural: eighteen years of German excavation at the site "have barely scratched the surface" of the Old Babylonian city. A high water table blocked digging below the Neo-Babylonian level (609–539 BCE) across most of the mound; in the few places deep soundings were possible, only scattered tablets and wall fragments of the First Dynasty were recovered, roughly 12 metres below the modern surface. This is a significant coverage gap: the famous Neo-Babylonian city visible today is not the city Hammurabi ruled.

The Neo-Babylonian city is known primarily from Robert Koldewey's excavations for the Deutsche Orient Gesellschaft (1899–1917), supplemented by Herodotus's eyewitness description (believed written c. 460 BCE) and the "Esagila tablet." Roux notes Koldewey's work, though extensive, still left much of the vast site unexplored.

## Occupation Sequence

**Old Babylonian (First Dynasty of Babylon, 1894–1595 BCE).** Under Sin-muballit, Hammurabi's father, Babylon controlled a small territory (roughly Sippar to Marad, Fallujah to Diwaniyah). Hammurabi's year-names record a staged expansion: early campaigns against Isin and Uruk (year 6), Emutbal and Malgum (year 10), and Rapiqum (year 11), followed by roughly two decades focused on temple-building and fortification, then a rapid series of conquests from year 30 (defeat of a coalition of Elam, Assyria, Gutium, Eshnunna, and Malgum) through year 32 (overthrow of Rim-Sin of Larsa in 1763 BCE, effective end of Eshnunna, and the destruction of vassal-turned-rival Zimri-Lim's Mari in 1761 BCE). Hammurabi took the titulary "mighty King, King of Babylon, King of the whole country of Amurru, King of Sumer and Akkad, King of the Four Quarters of the World." The First Dynasty ended when the Hittite king Mursilis I sacked Babylon c. 1595 BCE (see [[hittite-empire]]), opening the [[kassites|Kassite]] period (1595–1157 BCE), during which Babylon (as Kar-Duniash) remained the nominal capital under a foreign-origin dynasty.

**Neo-Babylonian ("Chaldaean") imperial capital (626–539 BCE).** Under [[chaldaeans|Nabopolassar and Nebuchadrezzar II]], Babylon was rebuilt as an imperial capital of roughly 850 hectares. Ancient sources report 1,179 temples, and the normal population is estimated at ~100,000, possibly up to a quarter-million. The city was bisected by the Euphrates and defended by an inner wall (two sun-dried-brick walls separated by a 7 m military road, protected by a moat some 50 m wide fed by the Euphrates) and an 8-km outer wall built by Nebuchadrezzar (three parallel walls, two of baked brick, with rubble/earth fill; per Herodotus wide enough at the top for two four-horse chariots abreast). A suburban zone between the walls held Nebuchadrezzar's "summer palace" (mound of Bâbil) and probably the bît akîtu, the New Year Festival temple, whose precise location is not established.

Eight gates pierced the inner wall, each named for a god. The best-preserved is the **Ishtar Gate** (north-western), of which roughly 12 metres of wall survives, decorated with some 575 relief animal figures in blue glazed brick — dragons (symbol of Marduk) and bulls (symbol of Adad). From it ran the **Processional Way** (Ai-ibur-shabu, "may the enemy not cross it"), more than 20 metres wide, paved in white limestone and red breccia, its flanking walls bearing 60 relief lions on each side (symbol of Ishtar) in blue-glazed ceramic. Buildings along the Way (German excavation names Nordburg, Hauptburg, Vorwerk) served defensive purposes, though the Hauptburg may also have functioned as a royal residence and repository — it yielded inscriptions and sculpture spanning the 2nd millennium to the 5th century BCE, including the "Lion of Babylon" basalt statue, whose origin is unknown and whose style is atypical of Mesopotamian sculpture.

The **Southern Citadel** (Südburg) was Nebuchadrezzar's palace, built over a smaller palace of his father Nabopolassar, with five successive courtyards and a large (c. 52 × 17 m), probably vaulted throne room decorated with glazed-brick floral and animal motifs rather than the Assyrian-style guardian colossi and orthostats.

A subterranean vaulted structure in the palace's northeast corner, with fourteen cellars and a triple-shaft chain-pump well, was long identified with the legendary **Hanging Gardens** (attributed by legend to Nebuchadrezzar, built for his wife Amytis). This identification is now doubted: later excavation of the rooms found they served merely as stores for administrative tablets. The Hanging Gardens' actual existence and location remain unresolved; this page does not assert the traditional identification as fact.

**Etemenanki** ("the Temple Foundation of Heaven and Earth"), the great ziqqurat popularly associated with the "Tower of Babel," was reportedly as old as Babylon itself; damaged by the Assyrian king Sennacherib, it was rebuilt by Nabopolassar and Nebuchadrezzar, then later destroyed entirely, leaving only foundations for archaeologists. Reconstructions based on Herodotus, the Esagila tablet, and excavation suggest a roughly 90-metre-square base of seven tiers, possibly of equal total height. See [[ziggurat]] for the broader architectural form. Herodotus reports a summit shrine containing a golden table and bed occupied by "a native woman chosen from all women" — sometimes read as evidence of a Sacred Marriage rite, though Roux notes there is no independent evidence for this reading, and it should be treated as a position, not a finding.

**Esagila** ("the Temple that Raises its Head"), the temple of Marduk — who became supreme deity of the Babylonian pantheon from the reign of Hammurabi onward — lay south of Etemenanki across Procession Street, unusually not directly abutting the ziqqurat as at most Mesopotamian temple complexes. Nebuchadrezzar's building inscription describes lavish adornment of its Ekua chapel in gold, lapis lazuli, and cedar; Herodotus corroborates a "lower temple" with a gold statue and altar (gold weight given as 800 talents, roughly 3 tons) and a reported thousand-talent annual frankincense offering. By the time of excavation the temple lay under more than 20 metres of earth and sand and had been thoroughly plundered in antiquity, yielding almost no valuable objects; its main building held shrines to Marduk, his consort Sarpanitum, Ea, and Nabû. For the akitu (New Year) festival centered on Esagila and the Processional Way, see [[mesopotamian-religion]] rather than duplicating the ritual sequence here.

**Later occupation.** Alexander the Great entered Babylon without resistance after Gaugamela (1 October 331 BCE), sacrificed to Marduk, and ordered the rebuilding of temples reportedly damaged under Xerxes — never completed. He died at Babylon on 13 June 323 BCE, probably of malaria, aged 32. During the Diadoch wars Babylon passed between Perdiccas, Seleucus (from 321 BCE), Antigonus (316–312 BCE), and back to Seleucus; the Seleucid era dating system began 3 April 311 BCE, the first continuous dating system used in Mesopotamia. Antiochus I (281–260 BCE) styled himself "provider of Esagila and Ezida" in the manner of the Chaldaean kings, in the last known Akkadian royal inscription; Antiochus IV (175–164 BCE) added a gymnasium and Greek theater, later enlarged under the Parthians. Babylon was progressively depopulated as inhabitants were drawn to the new city of Seleucia-on-the-Tigris, and it suffered further damage in the revolt of Hymeros (127 BCE) and the Mithridates II–Orodes civil war (52 BCE) — damage Roux argues exceeded anything done by Xerxes. Trajan visited in 115 CE only to honor Alexander's memory; Septimius Severus found the city "completely deserted" some 84 years later, c. 199 CE.

## Significance

Babylon anchors the wiki's Old Babylonian, Kassite, and Neo-Babylonian material: it is the seat of [[code-of-hammurabi|Hammurabi's legal stele tradition]], the symbolic and administrative center reclaimed by successive Kassite and Chaldaean dynasties, and the last great cuneiform-literate imperial capital before Mesopotamian civilization's slow, centuries-long eclipse under Achaemenid, Hellenistic, and Parthian rule.

## Economic Life

Temple institutions at Babylon and comparable cities (e.g., the E-Anna at Uruk) functioned as major landowners, employers, and traders — staffed by notables (mâr bâni), artisans (ummânê), hired laborers, slaves, and shirkê (persons consecrated to lifelong unpaid temple service), and administered by a shatammu (administrator), qipu (overseer), and tupshar bîti (head scribe). Roux attributes the rise of temple economic power to population taking refuge in cities and under clerical protection during the "dark age" of Aramaean pressure (10th–11th c. BCE per Roux's dating) — an interpretive comparison he draws to medieval European monasteries. Under Assyrian rule temples were largely tax-exempt but tightly supervised; Nabopolassar and Nebuchadrezzar left temple organization largely intact in exchange for a 20% revenue share; Nabonidus later tightened royal control over temple business (see [[chaldaeans]]).

Sixth-century BCE price data show inflation: a male slave's price rose from 40 shekels (c. 600 BCE) to 50 shekels roughly fifty years later; land bought per shekel fell from 2–4 qa under Nebuchadrezzar to 1–2 qa under Nabonidus; an unskilled laborer's average monthly wage was about 1 shekel, buying roughly 2 bushels of grain plus 3 bushels of dates. The standard silver-weight system used 60 shekels to the mina and 60 minas to the talent (biltu); minted coinage, invented by the Lydians in the 7th century BCE, did not circulate widely in the region before Darius I (521–486 BCE). The shift toward a silver standard (silver:gold ratio roughly 14–10:1) eased accounting but also fueled credit and debt expansion. Capitalist banking families such as the **Egibi** of Babylon flourished in real estate, the slave trade, money-lending, and commercial and agricultural partnerships. Roux frames the temples as having "kept alive" a civilization otherwise in economic decline for nearly six hundred years.

## Key Debates

- The **Hanging Gardens identification** at the Südburg vaulted-cellar structure is now doubted; no confirmed location for the gardens (if historical at all) has been established. Treat any specific-site claim as contested.
- The degree and manner of destruction under Xerxes (482 BCE Babylonian revolt) is disputed among ancient sources: Herodotus implies limited damage beyond the removal of Marduk's golden statue, while Arrian, Ctesias, and Strabo suggest wall-dismantling and temple destruction. Roux's own reading favors partial damage followed by gradual neglect rather than a single violent destruction — see [[chaldaeans]] for the fuller Achaemenid-period narrative.

## Historiography

Excavation quality and coverage differ sharply by period: the Neo-Babylonian city is comparatively well documented through Koldewey's systematic 1899–1917 campaign, cross-checked against Herodotus and the Esagila tablet, while the Old Babylonian city beneath it remains almost entirely inaccessible due to the water table — a reliability asymmetry that should govern confidence in any claim about Hammurabi's actual capital versus the later, better-known city. Source: [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]].

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

[[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] confirms Babylon's
two great documented peaks — under Hammurabi (r. 1792–1750, Middle Chronology) and under
Nebuchadnezzar II — as the city's defining political high points, consistent with the
Roux-derived narrative above, while adding a specific corrective on the Hanging Gardens
question.

**The Hanging Gardens question.** Van De Mieroop surveys the modern scholarly consensus
that the Hanging Gardens likely did not exist at Babylon at all: no Neo-Babylonian
building inscription, the cuneiform "Description of Babylon" tablets, Herodotus, nor the
Hebrew Bible mention them, and Koldewey's excavations found only a vaulted structure
later shown — via oil-ration tablets, including rations for the exiled Judean king
Jehoiachin — to be a storage facility, not a garden. He then cites **Stephanie Dalley's
2013** book-length argument that the "Hanging Gardens" tradition actually describes
gardens built at **[[nineveh|Nineveh]]** by Sennacherib, later misattributed to Babylon
by classical sources — a position Van De Mieroop describes as recent and not yet
broadly accepted ("too early to tell whether the ... study will convince more of them").
This is recorded here as a live, unresolved position, not a settled identification: the
existing page's own "Key Debates" section already flags the Hanging Gardens'
identification at Babylon as doubted; Dalley's Nineveh hypothesis is an additional,
distinct position in that same unresolved debate, cross-referenced to [[nineveh]] rather
than adjudicated here.

## Potts (*Mesopotamian Civilization*, 1997) — Additions

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]] unless noted.)*

### Herodotus on Babylonian canals, Nitokris, and Cyrus's capture of the city

Herodotus (*Hist.* I 193) reports Babylonia, like Egypt, was intersected with canals,
but claims the Euphrates does not overflow the corn-lands naturally as the Nile does,
requiring manual/mechanical irrigation — a claim Potts calls mistaken; it has been
suggested Herodotus crossed Babylonia during low water and never witnessed the river
in spate (Delattre 1888). Herodotus (*Hist.* I 185-7) also reports that a
Neo-Babylonian queen, **Nitokris**, diverted the Euphrates into an excavated basin
above Babylon and turned its course into a meandering channel to slow enemy (Median)
approach and river traffic; scholars dispute her identity (Cole 1994 reports she is
"usually identified" as Naqia/Zakutu, mother of Esarhaddon, but most scholars — Lewy
1952; Röllig 1979; Black et al. 1987 — identify her instead with Adad-guppi, mother of
Nabonidus, since her son "Labynetos" is thought a corrupt form of "Nabonidus"). The
account is generally thought to reflect construction actually undertaken by
Nebuchadrezzar II (604-562 BCE). Herodotus (*Hist.* I 189) further recounts that
**Cyrus the Great**, avenging a drowned sacred horse in the Diyala/"Gyndes" river, had
his troops dig 360 canals to lower its level, and later captured Babylon by diverting
the Euphrates into Nitokris's basin, allowing his troops to enter the city via the
shallowed riverbed (finding, as reported by Herodotus; historicity of specific details
disputed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]).

### The Pallacopas canal and Alexander's canal works

Arrian records that before Alexander's arrival, 10,000 Babylonians were engaged
annually for three months closing the **Pallacopas canal**, a major channel off the
Euphrates' right bank roughly 800 stadia north of Babylon, near modern Anbar. The
canal was likely dug late in the Neo-Babylonian period, perhaps by Nebuchadrezzar
(604-562 BCE), and is attested in cuneiform as *nar Pal-lu-kat* from the reign of
Neriglissar (559-556 BCE); its sluice (*bab nari*) is mentioned in Nabonidus-era
sources (555-539 BCE) (Meissner 1896, cited by Potts). Alexander's admiral
Aristobulus (quoted by Strabo, *Geog.* 16 i 11) reports that Alexander personally
inspected and cleared canals, stopping some mouths and opening others; Strabo also
records that Alexander removed artificial cataracts the Achaemenid Persians had built
to obstruct river navigation for defensive purposes, restoring Euphrates navigability
to Babylon. Strabo articulates a recurring ancient topos that responsible
river/canal management is the mark of a good ruler, its failure a mark of despotic or
corrupt rule (*Geog.* 16 i 10) (finding, Potts 1997; see [[mesopotamia-geography]] for
the fuller Euphrates/Tigris canal-management record).

### Cult-statue removals and restorations, culminating in Nabonidus and the Cyrus Cylinder

P.-A. Beaulieu (1993:242, cited by Potts) argued that removing a defeated city's cult
statue to the victor's land was meant to "actualize the rupture between the god and
his native land," itself understood as caused by the god's own anger. Documented
Babylon-linked cases include: the mid-16th-century BCE Hittite sack of Babylon
carrying off a Marduk statue, later restored by the Kassite ruler Agum (Foster
1993:273); the Elamite king Kudur-Nahhunte II's removal of Marduk's statue from the
Esagila to Susa c. 1155 BCE, along with the last Kassite king Enlil-nadin-ahi
(Brinkman 1968:89); and Nebuchadrezzar I's (1124-1103 BCE) later recovery of a statue
of Marduk from Elam. Most significantly: fearing Cyrus the Great's invasion,
**Nabonidus**, the last Neo-Babylonian king, ordered a massive gathering of the gods
of Sumer and Akkad into Babylon in **539 BCE** (Beaulieu 1993:242; confirmed by the
Neo-Babylonian Chronicle Series and Uruk letters describing statues transported by
boat). After Cyrus's conquest, the Neo-Babylonian Chronicle records the gods "returned
to their places," and the **Cyrus Cylinder** frames this restoration propagandistically
as an act of piety toward Marduk (finding, Potts 1997; see [[mesopotamian-religion]]
for the wider cult-statue-as-instrument-of-conquest pattern, including Sargon II's
709/707 BCE restoration of the Sealand gods to [[ur]] and [[eridu]]).

### Kuhrt and Sherwin-White's revisionism on Xerxes

A. Kuhrt and S. Sherwin-White (1987:69-78, cited by Potts) exposed the classical
tradition that Xerxes destroyed Babylonian temples (following a failed uprising) as
"a late fabrication without foundation." Kuhrt further argued that the "joyful
reception" topos applied to Alexander's 331 BCE entry into Babylon closely parallels
earlier accounts of Sargon II's (710 BCE) and Cyrus the Great's (539 BCE) receptions,
representing not spontaneous affection but "the final outcome of complex negotiations
forced on the citizens by an unenviable situation" (Kuhrt 1990:126). P. Briant
(1990:55, quoted by Potts) argues Babylonia flourished under Achaemenid rule and that
the Babylonian elite did not regard the shift from Persian to Macedonian domination as
progress. This is a scholarly revisionist position that directly complicates this
page's existing "Key Debates" note on the disputed degree of Xerxes-era destruction
(position; [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]).

### Seleucid continuity, contra Strabo

Potts contests Strabo's characterization (*Geog.* 16.1.5) of Babylon as "neglected and
thrown into ruins" after Seleucia's founding, calling the account "misleading." He
cites the Babylonian Chronicle Concerning the Diadochi showing Babylon was damaged by
Antigonus during the power struggle with Seleucus I (311-308 BCE) as a more plausible
explanation than "neglect." Evidence of continuity includes: renovation of the Marduk
temple/Bit Akitu around the time of a 274 BCE partial population transfer to
[[seleucia-on-the-tigris]]; ongoing cuneiform textual production; a Greek gymnasium
inscription with athletic-competition winners bearing Greek/Macedonian names and
patronymics; continued royal offerings at Babylon by the crown prince c. 308/7-294/3
BCE; a 236 BCE temple decree; and references to "men of Babylon" and citizen
assemblies in astronomical diaries through at least 169 BCE (reign of Antiochus IV).
Potts states this evidence "clearly contradicts" Pausanias's claim that "only priests
were left in Babylon" after the population transfer (attributed, Potts 1997; see
[[seleucia-on-the-tigris]] for the fuller Seleucid-period treatment, including Potts's
"Orientalization of the Occidentals" thesis).
