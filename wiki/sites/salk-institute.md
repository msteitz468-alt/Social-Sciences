# Salk Institute

The Salk Institute for Biological Studies, La Jolla, California — fieldwork site for Bruno Latour's 1975–77 ethnography that became *[[latour-woolgar-laboratory-life-1979|Laboratory Life]]* (with [[woolgar-steve|Steve Woolgar]]). Treated here as an **ethnographic locus of modern laboratory science**, not an archaeological site; `site_type: other`.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Not excavated. Founded under Jonas Salk; Latour was hosted in Roger Guillemin's neuroendocrinology laboratory with Salk Institute support (plus Fulbright and NATO fellowships). Salk wrote the Introduction to *Laboratory Life* (February 1979), welcoming "in-house" sociological observation while registering doubts about the authors' picture of science.

## Occupation Sequence

Continuous biomedical research institute from the 1960s. The wiki's documentation window for social-science purposes is **Latour's fieldwork 1975–77** and the 1979/1986 published account of Guillemin-group practice (TRF/H programs, chemistry/physiology sections, technicians and "doctors").

## Significance

Anchors the charter case of [[laboratory-ethnography]] and [[laboratory-studies]]: a high-status, instrument-rich neuroendocrinology lab whose fact-production (especially TRF) Latour and Woolgar reconstruct as literary and political work. Dedication of the book: "To The Salk Institute."

## Key Debates

Scientist reception of laboratory ethnography; ethics and politics of observing elite science; whether one institute generalizes (authors later stress networks beyond the lab walls).

## Historiography

Known in STS almost entirely through *Laboratory Life* and secondary commentary. Not a substitute for institutional history of the Salk; claims about lab culture are **date-stamped to mid-1970s Guillemin-group observation** as reported 1979/1986.
