# Teotihuacan

Central Mexican metropolis of the Classic period; Street of the Dead axis; multiethnic urban center. Principal Mesoamerican urban type-case in [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 13–14 teaching — population, craft specialization, and monumental scale against which [[cahokia]], Maya centers, and North American ceilings are compared.

## Discovery and Excavation History

W&O (2007) present Teotihuacán through settlement-history, pyramid dimensions, workshop counts, and foreign barrios rather than as an excavation-history monograph. Later Aztecs believed giants built the pyramids and gods were buried beneath them (textbook note). Specialist projects (Sun/Moon excavations, apartment-compound mapping, isotopic/enclave studies) and post-2007 political-organization debates require Mesoamerican literature.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]]; mid-2000s narrative.)*

- **Valley of Mexico backdrop:** Internally drained basin with a large lake (fish, fowl, turtles, algae, reeds); agriculture dependent on rainfall and small streams; no navigable rivers. By ~1000 BCE small villages/hamlets; few larger sites (Tlatilco, Cuicuilco); two-tier hierarchy. 800–500 BCE density rose — ≥10 sites >50 ha. 500–200 BCE: Cuicuilco maybe ~7,500; early irrigation signs; Cuicuilco’s large early pyramid anticipates Teotihuacán pyramids. Brumfiel (via W&O): tribute imposition by elites in largest communities important for growth of smaller settlements; little convincing population-pressure evidence at the critical period.
- **Locational advantages:** Large areas for irrigated agriculture and large springs; nearby obsidian; maguey (fiber, pulque) and nopal (including cochineal for red dye); natural trade route to eastern Mesoamerica.
- **Explosive growth:** Before ~200 BCE the area relatively culturally unimportant; 150–1 BCE growth unmatched; city to 6–8 km² (~one-third eventual maximum); A.D. 1–150 average population probably 60,000–80,000, rising to 100,000+ by A.D. 300 (as of W&O 2007).
- **Monuments:** Pyramid of the Sun — 198 m base sides (area comparable to Khufu’s Great Pyramid), 64 m high; ~1 million m³ fill. Pyramid of the Moon — 150 m base, 45 m high, more architecturally sophisticated trapezoidal platforms; ≥20 other temple complexes.
- **Urban fabric and crafts:** By ~A.D. 100, hundreds of workshops; perhaps ≤25% of population as craft specialists (obsidian, ceramics, precious stones, slate, basalt, shell, feathers, basketry, leather, etc.); household production may have been state-taxed. Laid out in quadrants by Street of the Dead and E–W streets; basic residence large windowless adobe/volcanic-rock compounds often ≥60 m on a side with frescoes. Foreign enclaves including **Oaxaca Barrio** (ceramics/urns/burials indistinguishable from Oaxaca >400 km south) persisted centuries.
- **Diet and hinterland:** Large quantities of nopal and other cactus, maize, beans, squash; Nichols showed extensive irrigation; even at peak ~80% of animal remains were deer. At maximum size the city depopulated much of the rest of the Valley of Mexico — only one other major settlement ~A.D. 500, a small fraction of Teotihuacán’s size; rural abandonment correlates with city growth (drawn or coerced). By A.D. 500 Teotihuacán-style ceramic vases in high-status burials on Gulf Coast, Oaxaca, elsewhere — trade more plausible than pan-Mesoamerican military control (as of W&O 2007).
- **Decline:** Size and influence began to decline before A.D. 600; after A.D. 600 styles disappeared from rest of Mesoamerica as if an exchange network were beheaded. Possible causes: eclipse by more productive polities, Maya-area encroachment, nearer centers (Tula, Xochicalco) blocking raw materials/food. Selective burning ~A.D. 650 targeting temples and elite residences with idol-smashing — internal revolt or external attack unclear; ~40,000 reinhabited but never regained former prominence.
- **Maya comparison:** Early Maya civilization largely its own creation with only minor Teotihuacán influence; Tikal and Dzibilchaltún never attained Teotihuacán’s population/physical size or comparable residential diversity/occupational specialization (as of W&O 2007).

## Significance

- One of the largest preindustrial cities of the Americas; benchmark for [[urban-revolution]] and [[craft-specialization]] in Mesoamerica.
- Illustrates rural abandonment / forced or voluntary nucleation analogous to Adams’s Mesopotamian hinterland-transfer models at [[uruk]].
- Comparative yardstick: W&O judge [[cahokia]] not functionally equivalent in specialization, product diversity, class stratification, or productivity.
- Multiethnic barrios evidence long-distance identity maintenance within an urban capital — identity cautions: archaeological Teotihuacán ≠ single ethnos/language/genes.

## Key Debates

- **Political form:** highly centralized state vs. alternative corporate/collective models (post-2007 literature denser than W&O; record textbook as mid-2000s synthesis).
- **Military empire vs. trade/hegemonic influence** across Mesoamerica.
- **Causes of decline** and meaning of selective burning ~650.
- **Cuicuilco as possible first Valley of Mexico “state”** and relationship to Teotihuacán’s rise (volcanic destruction of Cuicuilco often discussed in specialist literature beyond W&O detail).
- Four important early complexity areas (South Gulf Coast, Valley of Mexico, Valley of Oaxaca, Maya highlands/lowlands) coalesced only after centuries of competition — Teotihuacán as Valley of Mexico culmination, not sole Mesoamerican origin.

### Service (*Origins of the State*, 1975, attributed)

Elman Service's synthesis (1975), drawing on Sanders, Price, Flannery, and Coe, read Teotihuacan's rise through his [[integrative-theory-of-the-state]]: the valley's ecologically diverse "symbiotic regions" made simple reciprocal exchange inefficient and favored a centralized redistributive authority, with redistribution and political centralization reinforcing each other in a positive-feedback "path toward civilization." Service treated irrigation as merely "permissive" — an enabler of dense population, not a cause of the city or its bureaucracy — a position within the [[origin-of-the-state-integrative-vs-conflict-debate]] against Wittfogel-style hydraulic determinism. He attributed the Classic city's fall (as of Service 1975) not to simple Chichimec conquest but to competitive displacement: Teotihuacan's redistributive hinterland network had already failed to compete with newer regional powers (Tula, Cholula) before nomadic raiders, lacking cavalry, delivered an opportunistic coup de grâce — an instance of his [[law-of-evolutionary-potential]]. See also [[archaic-state-formation-pathways]]. (Source: [[service-origins-state-1975]].)

### Adams (*The Evolution of Urban Society*, 1966, attributed)

[[adams-robert-mcc|Adams]] treats Classic Teotihuacan as the **theocratic** starting point of the central Mexican sequence, homotaxially paired with Protoliterate [[uruk|Uruk]]: all-embracing religious style intensity and theocratic leadership; unmatched (in his comparison) monumental grid scale; craft luxury demand without managerial writing; growing politico-military controls and end-of-occupation militarism. Post-Teotihuacan break → Tula/Toltec transitional militarization; terminal [[aztec-empire|Aztec]] conquest state. Mexican trajectory more **pulsed** (fragmentation and craft decline between Teotihuacan, Tula, Tenochtitlan peaks) than Mesopotamia's smoother social-differentiation ramp — leans toward intermediate curve B. Mid-1960s chronology and political-form inferences date-stamped. See [[theocratic-to-militaristic-sequence]], [[adams-evolution-urban-society-study]].

### Sanders & Price (*Mesoamerica*, 1968, attributed)

[[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968|Sanders & Price]] (1968) made Teotihuacan the type-case of Mesoamerican **primary urbanism**, drawing on Sanders's Teotihuacan Valley Project and Millon's then-in-progress Mapping Project. Their reconstruction (1968-vintage, date-stamped): the valley was farmed for ~1,000 years by swidden ("tlacolol") cultivators and was marginal to the western Basin of Mexico until, between ~300 BCE and the turn of the era, population growth accelerated sharply (doubling per generation), settlement shifted to the alluvial plain, and by the Tzacualli phase 80–90% of the valley's population — and nearly all irrigation-dependent cultivators — had concentrated into a single nucleated center. They treat this **nucleation as a "social invention"** for socioeconomic integration and control, its subsistence base perennial-spring and floodwater irrigation of the alluvial plain (per Millon, peak ~19 km², ~85,000 people).

Against a purely religious-priestly reading, they emphasized (from Valley Project and INAH data) a more **militaristic** orientation — trophy bones, ritual cannibalism, projectile points, and an archaeologically visible enforced-nucleation program compared to Spanish *congregación*. They read Teotihuacan's wide influence (Gulf Coast, Maya area) as, at its strongest, **political control over local labor** — most tellingly foreign-style monumental architecture — proposing direct colonization at Kaminaljuyú (a "Cacao Route") versus more equivocal mercantile contact at Tikal. The scale contrast with Tikal (Teotihuacan's Ciudadela–market compound alone ≈ all of Tikal's civic architecture) they attributed to hydraulic vs. swidden agriculture and imperial centralization. The city's rise is the empirical anchor of their [[symbiotic-region|symbiotic-region]] and revised [[hydraulic-hypothesis-debate|hydraulic]] arguments; water allocation (scarcer than land) remained the chief source of local conflict in Sanders's own fieldwork, offered as a live analogy for why hydraulic systems favor centralized authority. (Source: [[sanders-price-mesoamerica-1968]].)

*Source depth: textbook synthesis (as of [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]]) + Adams 1966 comparative layer + Sanders & Price 1968 primary cultural-ecological layer.*


### Coe & Houston 2015 — Maya relations (attributed)

[[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston]] treat Teotihuacan as the Early Classic “military giant” whose war cult and iconography intersect Maya politics especially at [[tikal]] (AD 378 *entrada* of Sihyaj K'ahk'; Spearthrower Owl; Yax Nuun Ahiin) and in Esperanza-phase [[kaminaljuyu]]. Two schools: military intrusion vs elite emulation; isotope work (Wright) complicates pure invasion models. Maya-style ceramics and glyphs at Teotihuacan (Tetitla; Moon Pyramid burial jades) suggest possible Maya dynastic presence in the central Mexican city. Decline of Teotihuacan before AD 600 reorders Mesoamerican interaction spheres heading into Late Classic Maya florescence.

## Coe & Koontz (2013)

[[sources/coe-koontz-mexico-2013|Coe & Koontz]] date the Classic period at Teotihuacan to c. AD 150–650 in their periodization, noting most textbooks give AD 250–900 based on lowland Maya Long Count monuments — the two chronologies are not equivalent and should not be mixed (see [[concepts/long-count]]). René Millon (University of Rochester) directed the **Teotihuacan Mapping Project**, establishing that the city covered over 8 sq. miles (20 sq. km), was "fully urbanized," the largest Pre-Columbian New World city, and was laid out on a grid plan oriented 15°25' east of true north shortly after the time of Christ. See [[thinkers/millon-rene]]. By the early fourth century AD the city reached its estimated peak population, per Millon ~125,000 and possibly up to 200,000 — a figure carried at *(as of Millon; per Coe & Koontz 2013)*.

The Mapping Project's central finding was that most of the city consisted of walled residential **apartment compounds** with few, controlled entrances, ranging 4,300–75,000 sq ft (400–7,000 sq m). The city was cosmopolitan: a western "Oaxaca ward"/barrio housed Zapotecs practicing their own customs and burial rites (see [[cultures/zapotec-civilization]]); an eastern ward held people linked to lowland Veracruz and the Maya area. Isotope analysis of the Oaxaca-ward skeletal remains indicates many individuals were returned to Monte Albán or another Zapotec enclave soon after birth, before coming back to Teotihuacan to live out their lives — attributed as a practice beginning early in the city's history.

At the **Temple of Quetzalcoatl** (within the Ciudadela, completed early in the third century AD, the last monumental public structure built at the city), excavations by Rubén Cabrera Castro, Saburo Sugiyama, and George Cowgill recovered over 200 dedicatory burials of sacrificed individuals, many in warrior costume with obsidian projectile points and shell/canine-tooth pectorals — evidence Coe & Koontz read (attributed) alongside contemporary warfare imagery as showing "incontrovertible evidence for warrior cults" by c. AD 250. This anchors a **[POSITION]** on Teotihuacan's political organization: the older assumption of a purely priestly/theocratic government is contrasted, per Coe & Koontz, with "a more complex picture, with warriors also playing key roles" as co-rulers alongside priests — a live position, not adjudicated here. Isotope analysis (oxygen/strontium) of individuals buried at the Pyramid of the Moon (c. AD 350) and among the Ciudadela's sacrificial victims (Christine White and colleagues) shows many were not raised locally — some carrying Maya-highland signatures — a scientific finding date-stamped to the cited bioarchaeological analyses.

**Ethnic identity of the city's builders** remains a further **[POSITION]**: the early Spanish historian Torquemada attributed the city to the Totonac (though Totonac presence in Veracruz is otherwise a late/Epiclassic phenomenon); "some scholars claim an Otomí occupation of the city, others hold for the Popoloca"; Coe & Koontz themselves favor Nahua affinities based on continuities with the Toltecs and Aztecs "in both sacred and secular features." None of these positions is adjudicated in wiki voice.

**Collapse (POSITION set, attributed):** by the end of the sixth century AD the city's major palaces (Xalla and others along the Avenue of the Dead) were burned in what Coe & Koontz call "a targeted conflagration," with incense-burner production ceasing and central obsidian workshops closing; direct Teotihuacan influence over Mesoamerica had ceased by AD 600. Coe & Koontz record multiple non-exclusive causal positions, none adjudicated: (1) internal political/economic crisis compounded by trade-route disruption and migrant/trade siphoning by a rising rival polity such as [[xochicalco]]; (2) George Vaillant's earlier environmental-degradation hypothesis, attributing collapse to deforestation for lime-burning and resulting erosion/desiccation; (3) a regional-aridity hypothesis, worst in the Valley of Mexico during the Classic, which may have opened the region to northern-frontier peoples. Coe & Koontz further note that other Mesoamerican regional centers maintained or grew their populations as Teotihuacan fell, arguing against a single catastrophic agricultural collapse and instead suggesting regional centers "siphoned trade and people from Teotihuacan" — with the mid-to-late sixth-century destruction of elite culture possibly carried out "by people who knew that culture well and may themselves have been Teotihuacanos." Post-collapse, a reduced Coyotlatelco-phase occupation persisted at about a quarter of the former population.

This collapse POSITION set is distinct from the Maya-specific [[debates/classic-maya-collapse-debate]] and from [[phenomena/societal-collapse]]; Coe & Koontz explicitly draw a parallel between Teotihuacan's Epiclassic-era eclipse and the roughly contemporary abandonment of Cacaxtla and Xochicalco and the Classic Maya collapse (AD 800–900) as evidence of possible parallel or linked Mesoamerican-wide upheaval, without asserting a shared cause.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006 (Maya interaction)

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Maya-area interaction only; does not replace Valley of Mexico syntheses above.)*

### Place in Mesoamerican interaction and trade cycles

Sharer & Traxler place the ancient Maya within larger [[mesoamerica]] interacting with Olmec (Gulf), Zapotec/Mixtec (Oaxaca), **Teotihuacan and Tula** (Central Mexico), and southeastern Central American societies. Trade-control cycle sketch: Early Classic Teotihuacan Pacific colonies and [[tikal]] alliances for E–W commerce, with Tikal influence to [[copan]]; signpost for Teotihuacan contact is appearance of Teotihuacan-style **cylindrical tripod vessels** throughout the Maya area (often with Maya- and Teotihuacan-style motifs). After Late Preclassic southern decline, Teotihuacan (rose last centuries of Preclassic) took interest in Pacific-coast alliances to reestablish agricultural/trade prosperity; possible close alliance with [[kaminaljuyu]] to monopolize highland obsidian and jadeite; also involved in lowland fortunes. *Ochk’in kaloomte’* / *kaloomte’* titles at powerful dynasties (e.g. Tikal) possibly carry Teotihuacan-linked military legitimacy.

### Pacific coast: Balberta and Montana

As of Sharer & Traxler 2006 (citing Arroyo, Bove, Chinchilla, Medrano et al.): **Balberta** (Guatemala Pacific coast) reached apogee ca. AD 200–400 as capital of a large polity interacting **reciprocally** with Teotihuacan primarily as trading partners (likely cacao export); Pachuca and Zaragoza obsidian and Central Mexican/Gulf Coast vessels present; nothing demonstrates Teotihuacan caused state origins at Balberta. Balberta declined by ca. AD 400 and was replaced by **Montana**, with unprecedented Pacific-coast architecture (~1 km² civic core; 200 × 110 m platform, 7 m high, supporting 18-m temple); local versions of Teotihuacan pottery and censers suggest colonists; excavators conclude Montana secured cacao, cotton, and rubber for Teotihuacan and may have staged trade with Kaminaljuyu and the lowlands.

### Highlands and lowlands: partnership, not simple takeover

[[kaminaljuyu]]: commercial Teotihuacan ties on coast and at Tikal by 200–400, but Kaminaljuyu ties to Central Mexico only after ca. AD 400; thereafter revival with jade, quetzal feathers, El Chayal obsidian exported and prestige goods imported; talud-tablero adapted **locally**; green obsidian and stuccoed vessels from Central Mexico. Isotopic analyses of Early Classic Kaminaljuyu tomb elites: born and raised in Maya highlands, not Central Mexico; sacrificed retainers from more distant lowland Maya regions — casting serious doubt on a Teotihuacan **takeover** of Kaminaljuyu (Carnegie project first identified Maya–Teotihuacan Early Classic contact).

[[tikal]]: Lost World Group talud-tablero-inspired architecture beginning by ca. AD 300; AD 378 *entrada* of Siyaj K’ak’ and death of Chak Tok Ich’aak I; positions without single adjudication include Teotihuacan orchestration via Spearthrower Owl / Yax Nuun Ayiin I **or** local exiled faction, homeland possibly Kaminaljuyu or Teotihuacan. Burial 10 and Stela 31 synthesize Central Mexican warrior imagery with Mutul dynastic continuity. Coggins: K’awiil image combined with Central Mexican atlatl → “mannequin scepter.” [[copan]] founder Altar Q portrait and Hunal tomb show Teotihuacan warrior regalia even as strontium points to Petén childhood (as of Sharer & Traxler). Becan: Teotihuacan figurine cache in cylindrical vessel at Str. XIV (within Early Classic fortification context).

### Against Teotihuacan-as-origin externalism

External-stimulus models that cast Teotihuacan as transforming Maya chiefdoms into secondary states are contrasted with internal models: independent Preclassic complexity; [[el-mirador]]/Kaminaljuyu as **primary states before** Teotihuacan; Classic divine-king system collapse from internal stresses. Authors conclude continuous multiethnic external interaction **plus** internal processes. Early Classic is an era of **expansion** of already-existing Maya states, not their birth under foreign influence.

*associated_with:* Maya partners [[tikal]], [[kaminaljuyu]], [[copan]]; Pacific staging Montana / Balberta; *entrada* debate at Tikal; primary-state priority of Preclassic Maya capitals.

### Martin & Grube 2008 — *ochk'in kaloomte'*, *entrada*, and Copán founding imagery

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube 2008]]. Short Maya-interaction addendum only.)* Martin & Grube treat the high title *kaloomte'* as restricted to the strongest dynasties; when prefixed *ochk'in* (“west”), it **asserts legitimacy derived from Teotihuacan**. They place the late-4th-century **AD 378 *entrada*** of Sihyaj K'ahk' at [[tikal|Tikal]] as the transforming New Order that pulled a swathe of the Central Area into Teotihuacan’s political-cultural sphere, then note a later **spurt of dynastic foundations** — including [[copan|Copán]] and [[quirigua|Quiriguá]] during Sihyaj Chan K'awiil II’s reign — that share inscriptional or stylistic links with Teotihuacan or Tikal. At Copán, the 426 investiture of K'inich Yax K'uk' Mo' at the Mexican-linked *wi te' nah*, dual *talud-tablero* (Hunal) and Petén-style architecture, Late Classic “goggle eye” founder portraits, and the Hieroglyphic Stairway’s Teotihuacan-warrior ancestors and “Teotihuacan font” sanctuary text are read as New Order ideology carried to the southeastern rim and later revived as ancestral charter after Teotihuacan itself had fallen — parallel to Tikal’s Late Classic Mexican revival programme, not as proof of Teotihuacan birthplace for the founder.

*associated_with:* *ochk'in kaloomte'* Teotihuacan legitimacy; 378 *entrada* / New Order; Copán–Quiriguá 426 foundations as peripheral echo.

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990 (Maya lowland interaction)

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel 1990]], Ch. 4 and notes. Short Maya-interaction addendum only; does not replace Valley of Mexico syntheses or later *entrada* densifications above. 1990 nicknames: Smoking-Frog, Curl-Snout, Stormy-Sky, Great-Jaguar-Paw.)*

Schele & Freidel treat Teotihuacan as the source from which lowland Maya elites **borrowed** the Tlaloc-Venus conquest-war costume (balloon headdress, spearthrower, goggle Tlaloc, owl, Year Sign, jaguar suit) and probably associated rituals around the A.D. 378 Tikal–[[uaxactun]] horizon — initially as a rationale for kingdom-taking war, then fully Maya-ized for centuries as a war/sacrifice complex timed (later) by Venus and Jupiter stations. They explicitly **reject** Teotihuacan military conquest or throne usurpation of [[tikal]]: if their kinship reconstruction holds, unbroken Tikal royal descent (Great-Jaguar-Paw → Curl-Snout → Stormy-Sky) spans the Teotihuacano-imagery horizon. Physical presence at Tikal is framed as small numbers of trader-ambassadors with warrior escorts in a fifth–sixth-century Mesoamerican exchange web, not occupation; green Teotihuacan-source obsidian in Late Preclassic lowland caches (Altun Ha, Nohmul) and mutual style exchange predate 378, but what was “exchanged this time was not just goods, but a whole philosophy” of conquest war (POSITION). Intensive Tikal–Teotihuacan interaction ~100 years (through Stormy-Sky), then shifted toward Kaminaljuyu; broader contact continued until ~8th century. Astronomical timing of the war complex may be Maya innovation — Teotihuacan art lacks recorded dates, so calendrical import cannot be tested. Katun celebration is rejected as a Teotihuacan import (depends on [[long-count]]). Ballcourt Marker form at Tikal is compared to markers in Teotihuacan Tlalocan murals. Ritual complex survived Classic collapse at Chichén Itzá and northern Postclassic sites and may have returned to central Mexico via Cacaxtla/Xochicalco. None of this equates costume, trade goods, or talud-tablero with language, genes, or a single ethnos (four-way non-identity).

*associated_with:* lowland war-ritual borrowers [[tikal]], [[uaxactun]]; later star-war network [[caracol]], [[naranjo]], [[calakmul]].

## The Oaxaca Barrio (Flannery & Marcus, eds., 1983)

[[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983) documents Teotihuacán's relations with the [[zapotec-civilization|Zapotec]] of [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]]: [[paddock-john|John Paddock]] reported the **Oaxaca barrio** (Tlailotlacan), a residential enclave where migrants from Oaxaca used Zapotec-style tombs, urns, and gray-ware ceramics (confirmed by neutron-activation sourcing) within the Teotihuacán grid — one of Mesoamerica's clearest cases of a resident foreign-ethnic quarter. [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] read carved monuments and murals at Monte Albán (e.g., the Lápida de Bazán) as recording visits by Teotihuacán emissaries. [[blanton-richard|Blanton]] and Kowalewski compared the two cities' settlement hierarchies (rank-size), and Blanton contested Sanders and Santley on whether the two centers' growth trajectories are comparable.

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996) — Oaxaca–Teotihuacan relations

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996, Ch. 15; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

**Superpower context.** Teotihuacan (~20 km², many times larger than Monte Albán) grew spectacularly in the 2nd century AD after still being small at ~100 BC. Between AD 350–550 (~Monte Albán IIIa) Marcus & Flannery note its military colony at Matacapan (Veracruz), noble traders at Kaminaljuyú, and influence into the Maya lowlands; ambassadors almost certainly traversed Zapotec territory. They suggest Teotihuacan **may have prevented** Zapotec expansion north of the [[cuicatlan-canada|Cuicatlán Cañada]].

**No war evidence; Millon’s “special relationship.”** They find **no evidence** of Monte Albán–Teotihuacan war. The Lápida de Bazán and South Platform scenes are read as records of summit/diplomatic visits (including Teotihuacan visitors at the inauguration of 12 Jaguar), not conquest. Citing [[millon-rene|René Millon]] (with Cowgill and Rattray on foreign enclaves), they endorse the Oaxaca barrio as evidence of “a kind of **special relationship**” between the two cities — closer/different from Teotihuacan’s other foreign ties — favoring elite diplomacy and gift exchange over military takeover of either capital.

**Elite imports, not mass trade.** Occasional Teotihuacan-style vessels at Monte Albán Period III (cylindrical tripods with hollow slab feet; ring-based orange hemispherical bowls) are **largely restricted to elite areas** such as the North Platform — interpreted as noble-to-noble gifts rather than widely traded commodities. By AD 200 Marcus & Flannery also place a Zapotec enclave at Teotihuacan among markers of the Zapotec “golden age” sphere of influence.

## Basin of Mexico Survey (Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979)

The systematic regional surface survey reported in [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]] (study page [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]) revised the city's population upward across its growth sequence: from ~40,000 at the Tzacualli-phase nucleation (accepting [[millon-rene|Millon]]'s figure) to a Middle Horizon peak the survey team put at 125,000 for the city itself, out of 154,455 people counted across the whole Teotihuacan Valley/Temascalapa survey area — meaning at peak roughly 81% of the entire surveyed regional population resided in the city. By Phase Four, 94–97% of the Teotihuacan Valley's population had nucleated at the city (accepting Millon's 40,000 figure for that phase).

Sanders, Parsons & Santley (1979) argued this nucleation reflected direct state intervention: they attributed dense Middle Horizon resettlement of the upper piedmont around the city to "competition and monopolization of the better lands by the farming population of the city itself," and interpreted population concentrations along the Cuautitlan River as evidence of a state-organized irrigation-development scheme, concluding "the only way to explain the population size and distribution is in reference to the economic and political needs of Teotihuacan." They estimated roughly two-thirds of the city's population farmed the surrounding alluvial plain, with the remainder — by their reckoning about one-third, following Millon (1976) — as non-food producers (craft specialists, administrators, traders).

The survey team's causal model treated hydraulic agriculture and state formation as mutually reinforcing rather than as a simple one-way cause: they acknowledged this reasoning risked circularity ("we have strong doubts that this can be done even with ethnographic data") but held that Teotihuacan's early hydraulic investment gave it a decisive advantage over ecologically similar Puebla-Tlaxcala polities. On the city's later Middle Horizon decline, they proposed a two-tier account — the immediate trigger was military pressure from rising rival centers, above all [[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979|Cholula]] and Xochicalco ("the rapid decline of Teotihuacan is probably causally related to the rise of Cholula, and possibly Xochicalco"), while the underlying ("processual") cause was collapse of the city's long-distance trade network; they noted archaeological evidence of intentional burning of residences consistent with a violent transition. Regional survey data across the whole Basin showed population density held under ~65 people/km² for roughly 2,000 years outside the Teotihuacan hinterland, which the authors read as evidence of a persistent regional [[carrying-capacity]] ceiling that only intensified (chinampa/irrigation) agriculture later broke.
