# Mohenjo-daro

Major Mature [[harappan-civilization|Harappan]] city on the Indus system (modern Pakistan). Type urban site in [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 10 for Indus [[complex-society]] — planned streets, drains, Great Bath, craft specialists — with famously weak mortuary wealth differentials relative to Mesopotamia/Egypt (as of their 2007 synthesis).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Harappan civilization discovered early 20th century (Marshall et al.); long neglected relative to Egypt/Mesopotamia for lack of pyramids/palaces and later maturity (as of W&O 2007). Script remains undeciphered; no self-name, king lists, or internal historical accounts (Possehl via W&O). W&O cite Jansen on planning extent, Sarcina on house plans, and Flam on Indus course shifts affecting the city. Full trench histories and conservation campaigns require specialist monographs.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007|Wenke & Olszewski 2007]] ch. 10; Possehl chronology as followed in textbook.)*

- **Ecological setting:** Indus + now-extinct Ghaggar-Hakra (legendary Saraswati); Mughal: Ghaggar-Hakra supported communities including two cities as large as Mohenjo-daro and Harappa. Agriculture mainly via river irrigation/flood inundation (Egypt-like), rivers more unpredictable than Nile; circumscribed by highlands, Himalayas, Arabian Sea, Great Indian Desert. Flam: during Early/Mature Harappan the Sindhu Nadi ran west of the modern course — Mohenjo-daro ~25 km SE, outside worst flood zone; eastward shift late 3rd millennium BCE may have exposed the city to floods and encouraged relocation.
- **Neolithic background (regional):** Mehrgarh (Kachi Plain) shortly after 7000 BCE — multi-roomed mudbrick, cereal farming + hunting; independent zebu domestication discussed; cotton in southern Asia ~7000–5000 BCE (timing unclear) (as of W&O 2007).
- **Early Harappan (~3200–2500 BCE):** Continuity in subsistence/local trade; discontinuity in urbanism, stratification, specialization. Possehl: 463 sites, mostly ~5 ha; population more than tripled 3200–2600 BCE; little public architecture.
- **Mature Harappan (after ~2600 BCE, ~600-year span):** Three huge communities ~80–85 ha — **Mohenjo-daro, Harappa, Ganwariwala** — evenly spaced; >800 sites over ~1.25 million km² (larger than first Egyptian state or Sumer+Akkad+Assyria combined, as of W&O 2007). Mohenjo-daro ≥2.5 km², perhaps ~40,000 inhabitants. Jansen: only central platform mound (~100 ha) planned, with suburban unplanned areas — still orderly vs Mesopotamia. N–S street ~9 m with drains; private toilets/showers on municipal sewers; fired-brick courtyard houses (Sarcina: two basic plans; ~77% ordinary houses). Western raised “citadel”/Great Bath mound: Great Bath ~12 × 7 m, 2.5 m deep, bitumen-lined, dressing rooms; adjacent platforms debated (granaries etc.). Public architecture more utilitarian/administrative than Egyptian/Mesopotamian ceremonial mega-monuments.
- **Economy and crafts:** Craft specialization and mass-produced goods; possible administered noncapitalistic redistribution; seals may mark ownership/transactions or ritual. Staples: barley and wheat; cattle, sheep, goats, pigs, fowl; horse rare until end. Cemeteries show less mortuary differentiation than Mesopotamia/Egypt. Thousands of urban residents included goldsmiths, potters, weavers, brick masons, architects, and other specialists (W&O comparative craft list).
- **Decline (~1900–1000 BCE):** Style heterogeneity; ~score of skeletons in Mohenjo-daro streets (invasion/massacre reading — Kennedy rejects Wheeler’s massacre interpretation). Multi-causal gradual abandonment: river-course shifts (Flam), floods/tectonics (Raikes), millets drawing populations south (Possehl), deforestation/overgrazing/marine regression (Thapar), and contested Aryan/Indo-European invasion narratives now often demoted (Renfrew: Indus people may already have been Indo-European speakers — as of W&O 2007 presentation). Post-collapse power shifts toward Ganges after ~1100 BCE with Harappan cultural legacies.

## Significance

- Largest known Harappan settlement and central place (with Harappa and Lothal optimally spaced per locational theory — W&O).
- Challenges stereotype of civilization as pyramid-and-palace package: advanced municipal water/sewage “unequaled until Romans” (textbook phrasing), high cultural homogeneity, large territorial influence, yet little archaeological wealth inequality.
- Comparative [[pristine-state]] case: major evolutionary dynamics treated as primarily self-contained (minor outside influences). Long-distance trade volume low and mainly luxuries — W&O also float foreign trade as unusually powerful stimulus and note takeoff coinciding with some burned-building warfare evidence (internal textbook tension; record both).
- Four-way non-identity: archaeological Harappan ≠ single ethnos/language/genes (Kenoyer via W&O: mosaic of ethnic groups, languages, adaptations).

## Key Debates

- **State vs chiefdom:** ≥3 administrative levels / settlement hierarchy support state classification; Service-era chiefdom labels rested on limited monumental architecture, weak wealth differential, weak militarism — challenged by fortification, stratification, writing, hierarchy (Jacobson via W&O). Shaffer: urban literate culture without usual hereditary elites, centralized state, and warfare package. Kenoyer: competing elite classes (merchants, ritual specialists, resource controllers) sharing ideology/economy.
- **Piggott authoritarian stereotype** (1950) recycled despite contrary evidence (Shaffer via W&O).
- **Decline causes:** invasion vs hydrology/climate vs economic shift vs multi-factor — open; multi-causal gradual abandonment preferred in W&O summary.
- **Script and language family** undeciphered — do not assert Dravidian or other identity without attributed argument.
- **Planning extent** (Jansen planned core vs unplanned suburb) and Great Bath/platform functions.

## Possehl, *The Indus Civilization* (2002)

[[possehl-indus-civilization-2002|Possehl (2002)]] treats Mohenjo-daro at chapter length as "the epitome of all Mature Harappan settlements," likely a virgin-soil "founder's city" established during the Early Harappan–Mature Harappan Transition. His working site-size estimate is ~100 ha, explicitly provisional given subsurface prospecting evidence of buried remains beyond the visible mounds. He rejects Wheeler's fortress reading directly: the "citadel" retaining walls held fill for an elevated platform and gave it "the appearance of a citadel, but that was not its function" — see [[indus-polity-debate]]. The Great Bath is read as elite/ritual space, its abandonment marking the onset of the city's decline; the adjoining "Granary"/Warehouse, "College of Priests," and "Assembly Hall" labels are, in his own account, unsupported functional guesses based on architectural form alone, not artifact evidence — a standing methodological caution he applies to the whole excavation record. Possehl states the site shows gradual abandonment by ~1900 BCE with no destruction layer ("simply left to deteriorate"); calibration status for this date is not given in the source. He records, without adjudicating, the scattered street skeletons at findspots such as "Deadman's Lane" and the "HR Area tragedy," feeding the [[mohenjo-daro-massacre-debate|massacre debate]] but supplying no reanalysis himself. See [[harappan-civilization]], [[possehl-gregory-l]].

## Kenoyer 1998

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]], *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* 1998.)*

### Size, population, and plan

Kenoyer places Mohenjo-daro in the top tier of Indus cities (**+200 ha** surface estimate; inhabited area **greater than 250 ha**), larger than Fairservis’s 76.6 ha lower-town base for a ~41,250 population. Kenoyer allows considerably more people than Fairservis calculated but doubts continuous full occupancy — population likely fluctuated with festivals and trading seasons. Layout: high rectangular **western mound** (citadel rises up to ~12 m; historical Buddhist stupa/monastery on north) plus extensive northern/southern/eastern lower-town mounds (DK-G, VS, HR, Moneer; >80 ha) divided by four major N–S and four E–W streets. Major excavations still cover only a small proportion of the site. High water table has blocked investigation of lowest levels; Kenoyer expects Kot Diji Phase grid planning (already demonstrated at [[harappa]], [[nausharo]], Kalibangan, Rehman Dheri, Kot Diji) is likely present.

### Walls and gateways

City walls/gateways were controversial; recent studies (as of Kenoyer 1998) suggest each major mound had enormous mud-brick walls with gateways. Wheeler identified a possible SE citadel gateway (never fully published). Corings on HR perimeter revealed great mud-brick walls/platforms that, if city walls, would exceed Harappa’s in impressiveness. Ringstones out of context in streets/dumps are explained by in situ ringstone bases for wooden pillars at [[dholavira]] gateways.

### Architecture, wells, sanitation

- Average house walls ~70 cm thick; many houses ≥2 stories; some walls thick enough for three; courtyard privacy from side-lane doorways; carved alabaster/marble latticework in wealthy houses (also at Harappa). Some largest public buildings may have been entirely wood.
- **Wells:** 10–15 m deep, wedge-shaped brick cylinders, rope grooves; private wells in most houses/blocks; public wells on main streets. **Michael Jansen:** **>700 wells** citywide from excavated density. Kenoyer: private wells probably householder-built; abundance of private vs public wells suggests urban water purity concerns (not evidence for caste society).
- Bathing platforms, terracotta drain pipes, latrines (early excavators under-recorded; better at Harappa under HARP). Street/drain maintenance failures drove multi-century mound aggradation (same process as Harappa).
- Brick ratios 1:2:4; Jansen notes the ratio in citadel mound overall plan — possibly metrology and/or unknown ritual definition (Kenoyer). Multiple building orientations (Jansen) may reflect construction episodes and axial precession over centuries.

### Public buildings: Great Bath, “granary,” college, assembly hall

Northern citadel: so-called granary, [[great-bath]], and college; stupa covers eastern quarter with buried buildings beneath; southern **assembly hall** with double row of square columns and floor brick lines (possible seating). Kenoyer: high visibility legitimized rulers’ power.

**[[great-bath]]:** ~12 × 7 × 2.4 m; bitumen and gypsum waterproofing; N/S stairs; colonnades; SW drain through corbeled arch along granary terrace; eastern well; not for swimming; special religious purification rather than ordinary bathing (eight-room ordinary bathing building immediately north).

**“Granary” / great hall:** First hammam, then state granary; Wheeler 1950 reconstruction via Minoan/Roman analogies; foundation 50 × 27 m on tapered platform, 27 blocks by narrow passages, timber superstructure sockets; built **before** Great Bath (bath exit drain cuts NE foundation corner). Kenoyer (citing Fentress): poor recording, no charred grain/storage vessels/sealings → doubts state-granary ID; prefers **“great hall.”** Consensus: little evidence for massive granaries at Mohenjo-daro or Harappa.

Lower town: DK-G courtyard (market/assembly?); “house of a chief”; HR **House AI** (possible temple or leader’s house; brick circle for sacred tree?; seals and stone sculpture fragments resembling the [[priest-king-statue]]).

### Sculpture, tablets, seals, Localization

- Formal **stone sculpture school** unique to Mohenjo-daro among Indus cities ([[priest-king-statue]] type); gold fillets paralleled at Harappa and [[allahdino]]; multi-neighborhood elite residence; probably not direct political control over Harappa or Dholavira.
- **Copper tablets** almost exclusive to this site (only eight inscribed copper tablets at Harappa, different technique); possible local medium of exchange.
- Unicorn seals >60% of Mohenjo-daro seals vs ~46% at Harappa (Ratnagar via Kenoyer); local unicorn figurine styles also at [[chanhudaro]] and Harappa.
- **Jhukar Phase** continuity into Localization Era at Mohenjo-daro, Chanhu-daro, Amri, Jhukar — pottery/seal style change, script loss, technological continuity (Kenoyer Ch. 9).

## Wright (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wright-rita-p|Rita P. Wright]], *[[wright-ancient-indus-2010|The Ancient Indus]]* 2010, chs. 5, 9, 11.)*

**Aachen non-invasive survey.** Because the Pakistani government banned further excavation at the site (salinity/erosion damage), the RWTH Aachen team under Michael Jansen used non-invasive methods to redraw the settlement plan: unmanned hot-air-balloon aerial photography compared against historic excavation photos, "vacuum cleaner" surface-debris removal, selective core drilling (50–60 feet) for stratigraphy, and magnetometry/electrical-conductivity survey. Conductivity survey (M. Cucarzi 1987) identified a mudbrick platform 700 m long, 6 m wide, and 6 m deep in the lower town, a second in the upper town, and an empty corridor/street dividing the two. Jansen's team also concluded the city was built in a short period, "perhaps only a few years," equipped from the outset with vertical water-supply wells (Jansen 1984:145).

**Founding platforms.** Two massive constructed platforms in the VS and DK-G areas required an estimated four million cubic meters of clay/sediment plus millions of bricks (Jansen 1984:13). Jansen terms these "founding platforms," interpreting their primary function as flood protection and, secondarily, as a possible "iconographic element of elevating specific areas" (1984:15); Wright floats, as her own conceivable extension, a possible symbolic link to the Kirthar Mountains visible from the site.

**Wells and sanitation.** Individual houses had bathing/toilet areas on outside walls built of specially fitted, gravity-sloped bricks draining into street drains or cesspits; some toilets had brick seats, others were holes over chutes; ceramic soak pits were also used. At Mohenjo-daro specifically, wells were shared by one to three dwellings and access to wells/sewage was not restricted, as far as the evidence shows, to a particular social segment. Maintenance of the well and drain network required a substantial, largely invisible labor force (Jansen 1993b:121) whose social status is unknown; Wright notes the sanitation system likely under-delivered in practice ("green slime" observed in excavated pits).

**Great Bath construction and abandonment.** The Bath itself was a watertight pool 12 × 7 × 2.4 m, built of precisely fitted brick with millimeter-wide joins, sealed with gypsum plaster and bitumen, flanked by a pillared gallery, corridor, and rooms, with a double-walled well as its water source and a drain roofed with a corbelled vault 1.8 m high. Ardeleanu-Jansen (1984:54) reconstructs four construction phases, ending with the tank filled with debris, its pillar rows destroyed or partly reused as a production area, then buried under a later drain — evidence the Bath was abandoned as a functional space "while the Harappans were still in occupation of the site." Jansen (1993b:17) suggests a possible ritual significance tied to a general veneration of water; Wright explicitly declines to adjudicate this against a purely civic-communal reading.

**House differentiation (HR-A area).** Houses VIII, X, and XI illustrate differential space allocation: House VIII had two stories, entrance foyers, interior courtyards, a large private well, six or more interior rooms, a bathing platform, and a floor area of 300 m² (courtyard ~100 m²), with terracotta piping running into the street drain at High Lane (Jansen 1993b:64). Houses X and XI, by contrast, were fronted by exposed soak pits along their street exteriors, near where House II's drain emptied waste — Wright reads the odor and proximity as a daily marker of lower social position. House I, HR-A (>25 rooms, 675 m²) yielded over 600 artifacts, 98% of which Jansen (1993b:68) classifies as "prestige" goods "not intended for ordinary, everyday household use," including the bearded limestone head HR-910 and an alabaster statue in three pieces, plus fifteen unicorn-motif seals — evidence, per Wright, of correlation between larger house size, scarcer materials, and higher relative status.

**Statuary catalogue and the Priest-King.** All Mohenjo-daro stone human sculptures except one are male and fragmentary; interpretations of subject vary between rulers (Ratnagar 1981), ancestral figures, or clan members (Kenoyer 1998). Cataloged pieces include two torsos (L-950, SD-694), four head-and-upper-torso pieces (DK-1909, DK-B-1057, L-898, HR-910), a male head/torso in three pieces (HR-163), a poorly preserved head identified as female by long hair (L-127), and two lost-wax copper-alloy female "dancing girl" statuettes (HR-5721, DK-12728). Five animal sculptures include four reclining caprids and two seated ram-on-plinth figures. The "Priest-King" statue (DK-B-1909, found in DK-B area, Block 2, Room 1, apparently having rolled below floor level when the building collapsed — Marshall 1931:356ff.) is bearded, hair parted and pulled back with a fillet, and wears a robe with a trefoil pattern possibly once painted red; Ardeleanu-Jansen (1991:167) speculates its head may originally have belonged to the L-950 torso. Wright states the "Priest-King" attribution by early excavators "has been generally rejected" by later scholars, since no religious structures have been identified at any Indus site and the statues occur in ordinary residential contexts, not cultic ones — she identifies the Priest-King as "the idealized signature" of an older, now untenable model of Indus society as homogeneous and undifferentiated under a single ruling figure.

**Hoards.** Twelve hoards are recorded at Mohenjo-daro (DK and HR-B areas, under floors or near blocked doorways, unconnected to structures), against seven at Harappa, several at [[chanhudaro]], three at [[lothal]], and one at [[allahdino]]. Contents include copper/bronze weapons and tools, vessels, scale pans, gold/silver ornaments, and semiprecious stones (agate, carnelian, lapis lazuli, turquoise, among others). Paul Rissman (1988) divides Mohenjo-daro hoards into three types — copper scrap/tools, precious-metal-and-stone caches, and mixed assemblages — concluding gold, silver, copper, and semiprecious stone were the era's highest forms of material wealth and that hoards, though not confined to restrictive contexts, represent items of "great secular value" (1988:218), plausibly held by artisans or merchants (Rissman 1988; Kenoyer 1998). Two relatively intact houses associated with hoards lacked statuary or elaborate drainage, leading Wright to infer a different social standing for hoard-owning households than for statuary-owning ones. Wright floats, as an explicitly speculative possibility warranting further study, that Indus hoards may reflect commodity-money-like value denominators analogous to Mesopotamian "monies" of barley, copper, and silver (Powell 1996), consistent with the standardized Indus weight system.

**Religion (negative finding).** John Marshall (1931) identified several Mohenjo-daro buildings as probable temples or shrines by analogy to Minoan and Hindu religious architecture, and read the Great Bath as evidence Harappans regarded bathing as "a religious duty" (1931:75); Marshall himself noted no object confirmed a religious function. Wheeler (1968) likewise proposed House I (HR-A) as a possible temple. Wright states plainly that no religious structures have been positively identified using consistent criteria at Harappa, Dholavira, or any other Indus site, and concludes "it is not possible to say anything about an Indus 'state' religion at this time," critiquing Wheeler's fuller invocation of Great Mother Goddesses, a "priest-king" god, and a proto-Hindu/Buddhist "substrate philosophy" (D. Miller 1985) as unsupported by contextual evidence.
