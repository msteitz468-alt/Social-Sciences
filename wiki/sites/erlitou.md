# Erlitou

## Discovery and Excavation History

Erlitou, in Yanshi County, western Henan, was identified in 1959 by **Xu Xusheng**, who was
explicitly searching for a capital of the Xia dynasty recorded in ancient texts as lying in the
Yiluo River valley. It has been excavated by the Institute of Archaeology (Chinese Academy of
Social Sciences) continuously since. The site is the type locus of the [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou
culture]] and the focus of the [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate|debate over its identification with the
Xia dynasty]].

## Occupation Sequence

The location held three small late Yangshao settlements (c. 3500–3000 BCE) and one small early
Longshan settlement (c. 3000–2600 BCE), then lay largely vacant for ~500–600 years before
Erlitou-culture people arrived. It grew to **c. 300 ha** at its Phase II–III peak — the largest
urban center in the region — organized around a rammed-earth **palatial complex** demarcated by
four intersecting roads ~20 m wide. Phase II built the palaces; Phase III enclosed a 10.8 ha
palatial town with rammed-earth walls, abandoned the Phase II palaces, and saw the first bronze
ritual vessels. One estimate (Liu 2006) puts the Phase III population at 18,000–30,000 (mean
~24,000). Unlike many Neolithic sites and the later Shang capital at [[anyang|Anyang]], Erlitou has
**no spatially separate cemetery**: burials and houses are superimposed throughout.

Elite burial no. 3 (an adult male, 30–35 years) contained bronzes, jades, lacquer, ceramics,
cowries, and a **dragon-shaped artifact of ~2,000 turquoise and jade pieces** laid over the
skeleton.

## Significance

Erlitou anchors the emergence of the first archaeologically demonstrable **state** in the Central
Plain and the origin of the East Asian **piece-mold bronze ritual-vessel** tradition that the Shang
inherited. Liu & Chen (2012) classify the Erlitou polity as a **territorial state** (after Trigger
2003) — a single ruler governing a large area through provincial and local administrators — whose
political economy turned on procuring metal and salt from its periphery and monopolizing bronze
ritual-vessel production.

## Key Debates

- **Is Erlitou the Xia capital?** See [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]]. Liu & Chen decline the
  identification; most Chinese archaeologists accept it.
- **Chronology.** Conventional dates (~1900–1500 BCE) versus a later AMS range (~1750–1530 BCE)
  remain unreconciled (as of Liu & Chen 2012).
- **Glyphs as writing.** More than forty pottery glyphs resemble oracle-bone forms but are not
  accepted as a writing system.

## Shelach-Lavi (2015) densification

*(Attributed — [[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]].)*

Shelach-Lavi dates the main Erlitou-culture occupation ca. 1900/1850–1550 BCE across four
phases; the government-sponsored Xia-Shang-Zhou Chronology Project (1990s) radiocarbon-dated
the culture to ca. 1900–1600 BCE, but recently published dates push its beginning into the
eighteenth century BCE. He gives the type site as ~300 ha, with an ~11-ha rammed-earth palace
city (palaces I and II), a bronze foundry, and a turquoise workshop; bronze production at this
stage was small-scale (17 surveyed vessels, mostly small *jue* cups). He treats the
Phase III peak-population estimate of 18,000–30,000 as "inconclusive," and — because the site
is not fortified and no elite cemetery has been found — argues there is currently no clear
evidence for a qualitative "state" threshold at Erlitou; he places the "step-like" sociopolitical
change he sees as marking statehood one phase later, at Erligang (Shelach and Jaffe 2013), not
at Erlitou itself. He is correspondingly cautious about labeling Erlitou's public rammed-earth
structures "palaces," preferring to treat the "state" characterization of the site as a live
question rather than a settled fact — see [[erlitou-xia-dynasty-debate]].
