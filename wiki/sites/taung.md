# Taung

## Discovery and Excavation History

The first South African australopithecine find came from **quarrying at Taung** in 1924: a complete juvenile skull (~3 years old). Raymond Dart (1925) stressed its intermediate ape–human status and named *Australopithecus africanus* on this single immature specimen (as of Phillipson 2005). Contemporary archaeological impact was limited because Southeast Asia and Europe were then believed to be the main theatres of early human evolution (as of Phillipson 2005).

No artefacts are associated. Bones were likely brought by a predator (Berger & Clarke 1995 via Phillipson). Age estimates range ~2.8–1.0 Ma with consensus toward the earlier part; species attribution of the juvenile is still doubted by some (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Occupation Sequence

Not a stratified habitation site in the Olduvai/Turkana sense; a single type discovery from quarry breccia, later joined by the Sterkfontein–Swartkrans–Kromdraai–Makapansgat cave system for South African australopithecines (see [[sterkfontein]], [[swartkrans]]).

## Significance

Type locality of *A. africanus* and a landmark in shifting evolutionary focus toward Africa — though that shift was delayed relative to the find date (as of Phillipson 2005). Anchors the South African early hominid record alongside [[sterkfontein]] and [[swartkrans]].

## Key Debates

Dating precision and taxonomic assignment of an immature type specimen remain classic problems; treat chronology as **disputed/approximate**.
