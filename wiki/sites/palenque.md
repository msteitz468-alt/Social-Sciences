# Palenque

## Discovery and Excavation History

Western lowland capital (Lakamha in native sources per C&H orthography notes). Famous for Ruz’s 1952 Pakal tomb discovery and subsequent epigraphic explosion. Houston’s reading of Cross Group “sanctuaries” as *pibnal* (“sweat bath”) — symbolic of divine birth — is highlighted in [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- Late Classic dynastic peak under K'inich Janaab Pakal I (tomb dedicated AD 683) and successors. Cross Group temples dedicated to the Palenque Triad of gods; *pibnal* naming links architecture to birth symbolism known from ethnology/ethnohistory of Mesoamerican sweat baths.
- Ahkal Mo' Nahb (from AD 721) major sculptural patron (preface research highlight).
- Water control: underground conduits and pressure (Kirk French via C&H).
- AD 711: Tonina captures a Palenque king — western frontier warfare.

## Significance

Canonical case of Classic Maya royal funerary architecture, mythic triad theology, and high epigraphic density. Anchors discussions of [[kuhul-ajaw]] ideology and scribal culture.

## Key Debates

- Architectural meaning of Cross Group (*pibnal* vs earlier “sanctuary” labels).
- Western Maya relations with Tonina and the Usumacinta.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Research history, toponym, and architectural style

Sharer & Traxler sketch early fieldwork: Antonio del Río investigated Palenque in 1787 (report published 1822) with haphazard methods; Stephens and Catherwood documented the ruins among many others. Deciphered capital name **Lakamha’**; Emblem Glyph titles identify *k’uhul ajaw* of the Palenque house. Regional Classic style: **Palenque mansard** roofs among major lowland architectural trajectories (with central Peten/Tikal, Usumacinta, Copan mosaics, Puuc veneer, Chichen synthesis). Jade mosaic masks reported in royal tombs (alongside Calakmul and Tikal). Lounsbury showed some 8th-century Palenque events timed to Jupiter. Late Classic Palenque texts show structural parallels to ethnohistoric/contemporary Mayan narrative forms (per Fought and related work as cited).

### Dynasty, creation theology, and succession flexibility

1970s work by Lounsbury, Schele, and Mathews reconstructed the royal dynasty before Pakal — a milestone in dynastic decipherment. Palenque texts give the most detailed Classic version of creation: First Mother, First Father, Palenque Triad **GI–GIII** (present world at *bak’tun*-cycle start 13 August 3114 BC; triad born 2360 BC; GII ~K’awiil; GIII may be a sun-god aspect) — compared with Late Preclassic [[san-bartolo]] mural creation scenes and [[quirigua]] Stela C’s primordial three-stone hearth. Patron deities of the capital are the Palenque Triad (as *wayib* set). Classic succession was typically father-to-son but with exceptions including **mother-to-son at Palenque**. Royal tombs with apotheosis rituals (cinnabar, reentry) include Pakal’s funerary complex among exemplary cases.

### Regional politics and Terminal Classic fate

Sharer & Traxler place Palenque among major Classic states in the multi-capital landscape, most of the southern/central lowlands dominated by the Tikal–Calakmul rivalry and allies. A retrospective **Siyaj K’ak’** mention at Palenque may recall a role in founding that house (authors’ suggestion; not presented as settled). In 611 Calakmul’s **Scroll Serpent** struck Palenque (probably then a Tikal ally) — recorded as the “axing of Lakamha,” demonstrating Calakmul paramountcy after the 562 defeat of Tikal. Moral-Reforma Stela 4 (Simon Martin) shows a subordinate taking the headband under Calakmul then later under Palenque (~30 years after 662). A Piedras Negras *sajal* was captured by Palenque ca. 725. Cortés’s 1524–25 Honduras march passed near the Palenque vicinity without visiting it as a living capital (only major living center visited was [[tayasal]]). Terminal Classic: Palenque among capitals with most pronounced drop in domestic activity and population where Late Classic peaks had been; southwestern lowlands and interior heartland hit first and hardest; riverine trade-bypass models list Palenque with Piedras Negras, [[yaxchilan]], [[quirigua]], and [[copan]] as Usumacinta/Motagua sites vulnerable when seaborne Yucatan routes rose.

*associated_with:* [[calakmul]] 611 campaign; [[tonina]] western frontier (see Coe & Houston AD 711 capture); Usumacinta peers [[piedras-negras]], [[yaxchilan]]; creation-text comparison with [[san-bartolo]].

### From Coe 2012 — Pacal’s tomb and Mesa Redonda

[[coe-breaking-the-maya-code-2012|Coe 2012]] Ch. 8 treats Ruz’s 1952 discovery of the Temple of the Inscriptions sarcophagus (Pacal / K’inich Janaab Pakal) and the 1970s **Mesa Redonda** conferences as the social engine of dynastic decipherment: [[lounsbury-floyd|Lounsbury]], [[schele-linda|Schele]], Mathews and others reconstructed Palenque’s dynasty and the Palenque Triad (Berlin). Phonetic *pa-ka-l* readings and age-at-death debates are filed as epigraphic claims, not wiki-voice physiology.

## Densification from Martin & Grube 2008

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube 2008]], Palenque chapter. Complements Coe & Houston, Sharer & Traxler, and Coe 2012 densification above.)*

### Setting, Baakal, and dynastic crises before Pakal

Martin & Grube place Palenque on a limestone shelf at the northern edge of the Chiapas highlands overlooking the Tabasco plain toward the Usumacinta. Ancient kingdom name **Baakal** (“Bone”); city name **Lakamha’** (“Big Water”); early capital/home **Toktahn**. Near absence of monolithic stelae; effort went into architectural sculpture and stucco. History strife-torn; greatest artworks and longest texts emerged as reactions to defeat and dynastic breakdown. Early dynasty known almost entirely from retrospective 7th-century texts (Berlin, Schele, Mathews, Lounsbury apparatus). Classic founder **K’uk’ Bahlam I** (431, four years); “Casper” (435–~half century); Butz’aj Sak Chiik’s 490 dedication of Lakamha’ may mark historical founding shift from Toktahn. **Lady Yohl Ik’nal** (583–604)—one of very few Classic women with full royal titles and a full term—suffered Snake-kingdom “axing” of Lakamha’ (~599). Second sack **4 April 611** under Calakmul’s **Scroll Serpent**; Ajen Yohl Mat and Janaab Pakal died within a year. **Muwaan Mat** “reign” 612–615 uses the ancestral deity’s name; uniquely woeful 613 language *satay k’uhul ixik, satay ajaw* (“lost is the divine lady, lost is the lord”); authors reject equating Muwaan Mat with Lady Sak K’uk’ (male deity) and allow metaphorical reign or masked foreign supervisor. In 615 twelve-year-old **K’inich Janaab Pakal I** acceded; mother Lady Sak K’uk’ presents the “drum major” crown on the Oval Palace Tablet and likely retained practical power into his formative years.

### Pakal (615–683), Palace, and the Temple of Inscriptions tomb

Martin & Grube stress Pakal is the most famous Maya king largely because of his burial, not only his long productive life. Wife Lady Tz’akbu Ajaw (~626); sons later Kan Bahlam II and K’an Joy Chitam II. Earliest contemporary inscription of the reign: Olvidado Temple 647—first since Casper’s bowl ~160 years earlier. Greatest energies to the **Palace**: House E (*sak nuk naah*, “White Skin House,” white-painted) completed 654—Oval Palace Tablet throne room for later inaugurations; House C 661. House C narratives restore regional power (Santa Elena captives 659; Nuun Ujol Chaak of Tikal “arrives” in prisoner-arraignment phrasing), but authors note gains quickly lost to [[piedras-negras]] and Calakmul. Died 683 in his 68th regnal year; Temple of Inscriptions finished under son Kan Bahlam II (~690).

**Ruz’s discovery (from 1949):** floor slab with twelve plugged holes; stairway ~25 m down; vaulted chamber with huge sarcophagus unequalled in the Maya world. Lid: king’s rebirth from Underworld jaws on the world tree/cosmic axis; coffin sides ancestral figures as trees (parents twice, Lady Yohl Ik’nal, Ahkal Mo’ Nahb I, K’an Joy Chitam I, Kan Bahlam I). Body in cinnabar with copious jade (mosaic mask, rings every finger, cube and sphere in hands); psychoduct up the filled stair; lid designed for re-entry. Tortuguero (~61 km west) shared Baakal titles; Bahlam Ajaw (644–679) conquered Comalcalco 649—westernmost Maya centre.

### Kan Bahlam II, Cross Group, and the 711 capture

**K’inich Kan Bahlam II** (684–702), already 48 at accession, spent 18 years creating some of Palenque’s finest architecture—arguably outdoing his father. Completed father’s mortuary temple (three sanctuary tablets, ~617 glyph-blocks, second-longest Maya text body); façade calls him “10th in the line” only if “aberrant” reigns are excluded. **Group of the Cross** (Temples of the Cross, Sun, Foliated Cross) dedicated together **692**: coordinated programme for each Palenque Triad god, patriline fixed in supernatural foundation narrative; inner sancta as symbolic sweat-baths; each tablet shows him as mature king and as child. Special attention to July 690 planetary conjunction; only figural stela for 13th K’atun 692. Pragmatic base: September **687** successful attack on highland rival [[tonina]] (disappearance of Tonina Ruler 2 may mark a fatal encounter); later reinstalled Moral-Reforma under Palenque patronage; influence to La Mar and Anaite—but by as early as **692** he had suffered Tonina defeat and lost this region. Died 16 February 702 age 66; main tomb still unidentified as of 2008.

**K’inich K’an Joy Chitam II** (second of Pakal’s sons) replaced him 702 at age 57; baah ch’ok since 684. Palace northern gallery; Palace Tablet mysteriously discusses **Ux Yop Huun** as if pictured as king (possible regent). In **711** Tonina forces penetrated the capital in a star war and seized the 66-year-old king; Tonina sandstone panel shows him bound but dignified, still in royal finery (date best read 26 August 711). Long seen as political demise; David Stuart’s evidence (as reported by Martin & Grube) that he was restored—named at Piedras Negras 714, local ceremony 718—presumably as Tonina vassal or tribute-payer.

### Later kings to ~799 and collapse

Nephew **K’inich Ahkal Mo’ Nahb III** (721–): Temple 19 (734) accession with Janaab Ajaw personating mythic investiture of 3309 BCE; reliant on high nobles; captain **Chak Suutz’** took a [[piedras-negras]] *sajal* of Yo’nal Ahk II/III ~725 (Tablets of the Scribe and Orator). **Upakal K’inich / K’inich Janaab Pakal II** (secure date 742 installing a secondary lord). **K’inich Kan Bahlam III** known almost solely from Pomona 751; Tonina records a further Palenque defeat about this time. Last major ruler **K’inich K’uk’ Bahlam II** (acceded 764): Tablet of the 96 Glyphs (783)—widely seen as finest Classic glyphic calligraphy—recounts House E 654 dedication and later accessions in the same building. Last known king **Janaab Pakal III** acceded **799** (incised blackware from a modest residential burial; day name “6 Death” read as possible Mexican influence). Last Baakal reference on a Comalcalco brick ~814. Collapse: sudden end of dynasty/history in early 9th century, gradual depopulation, squatters in the Palace for centuries; famous skeleton of a man killed in a gallery collapse, left unburied.

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel, *A Forest of Kings*, 1990]], Ch. 6 “Children of the First Mother.” Popular-scholarly narrative; **interpretive FLAG**. 1990 nicknames retained with later-standard equivalents in parentheses. Dramatized funeral/accession scenes are narrative devices, not primary evidence. Dynastic theology and succession model are THEORY/POSITION as of 1990 — compare Martin & Grube 2008 and Coe & Houston 2015 densifications above for revised orthography and chronology.)*

### Dynastic lists, female succession, and three lineages

Schele & Freidel report that combined king lists (Temple of Inscriptions sarcophagus and panels) reconstruct Palenque’s dynasty for ten generations culminating with **Chan-Bahlum** (later standard: K’inich Kan Bahlam II). They argue the royal obsession with comprehensive dynastic lists hints of succession trouble. Core political problem as reconstructed: **Pacal** (K’inich Janaab Pakal I) inherited the throne from his mother **Lady Zac-Kuk** (Lady Sak K’uk’) in violation of normal Maya patrilineal succession; his central task was to justify that departure.

On Pacal’s sarcophagus sides, ancestors rise with fruit trees as an ancestral orchard; Pacal doubled portraits of two women—mother Lady Zac-Kuk and great-grandmother **Lady Kanal-Ikal** (Lady Yohl Ik’nal)—and also doubled non-ruling father **Kan-Bahlum-Mo’**. Schele & Freidel state Lady Kanal-Ikal and Lady Zac-Kuk are the only women they can be sure ruled as true kings—neither consorts nor regents (contrasted with Lady Wac-Chanil-Ahau of Naranjo). Because nobility reckoned membership through males, when the throne passed through these women it entered the fathers’ lineages: they count **three descent lines** in Palenque’s dynasty, breaking the equation of single patriline and dynasty.

- **Lineage 1:** founder **Bahlum-Kuk** (K’uk’ Bahlam I) through eight successors to Lady Kanal-Ikal; Pacal still claimed right from that founder.
- **Lineage 2:** Kanal-Ikal’s unnamed husband; notables include king Ac-Kan and his brother the first Pacal (never high king; likely father of Lady Zac-Kuk). No contemporary inscriptions from Lady Kanal-Ikal or her children (evidence gap explicit).
- **Lineage 3:** begins with Pacal the Great; sons Chan-Bahlum and **Kan-Xul** (K’inich K’an Joy Chitam II) belonged to the lineage of father/paternal grandfather Kan-Bahlum-Mo’—a lineage with no independent legitimate claim to produce kings.

### Pacal’s accession, building campaign, and Temple of Inscriptions

Pacal acceded at age twelve while his mother still lived, after she had ruled three years; Oval Palace Tablet (House E / *Zac Nuc Nah*, “White Big House”) shows him receiving a headdress from his mother—most successors inaugurated on a throne that once sat below this tablet. Schele & Freidel portray Lady Zac-Kuk as a masterful politician who very probably kept real power until death in **640**—twenty-five years after Pacal’s accession; only then did Pacal’s major archaeological building program appear. Temple Olvidado dedicated **647** (first post-parental project); then Temple of the Count, subterranean Palace galleries, Houses E, B, and C (House C **659**).

In his early seventies Pacal began the nine-terrace **Temple of the Inscriptions** mortuary pyramid: limestone coffin block set in a pit before the pyramid rose around a vaulted internal stair; wall slabs planned for **640 glyphs** of katun history, last columns reserved for death. Lid imagery as Schele & Freidel read it: fall down the World Tree into Otherworld jaws, sun-in-transition symbolism, resurrection like Hero Twins after Xibalba (iconographic THEORY). A spirit tube (*psychoduct*) connected sarcophagus to temple floor for Vision Serpent communication with descendants. Pacal died in his eightieth year; tomb opened by Alberto Ruz **1952**. *(Dramatized funeral scenes—Chan-Bahlum and Kan-Xul equipping the body, captive sacrifice, genital bloodletting—are narrative devices.)*

Temple of Inscriptions tablets record a woman taking the throne **22 October 612** when Pacal was nine—name uses a screaming-bird glyph that is both a Palenque Emblem Glyph variant and the name of the **First Mother** (“Lady Beastie” in 1990 scholarly nickname), born before present creation. By using the goddess’s name for Lady Zac-Kuk, Pacal (as reconstructed) declared her analogous to mother of the gods and himself like-in-kind to the **Palenque Triad**.

### Chan-Bahlum and the Group of the Cross

Chan-Bahlum waited **132 days** after Pacal’s death to accede (**10 January 684**). Outer Temple of Inscriptions piers show six-year-old Chan-Bahlum presented as heir (June 17–22, **641**, summer-solstice “became the sun” with GI), merged with GII iconography (serpent-foot, smoking-ax); six toes mark the historically polydactylous king. Concurrent with finishing the funerary temple he began the **Group of the Cross**—Temples of the Cross, Foliated Cross, and Sun—as pictorial-textual treatise redefining dynasty as transcending lineage. Triadic plan deliberately recalls Late Preclassic royal architecture (Cerros et al.), even though it orients against Palenque’s logical northward plain view (THEORY).

Each temple’s central rear chamber holds a *pib na* (“underground building”)—miniature house-portal to the Otherworld. Inner panels: Schele & Freidel identify the small muffled figure as dead Pacal facing Chan-Bahlum in Xibalba, reenacting power transfer; outer panels show full regalia as reigning monarch. Key Cross Group dates as of 1990: accession-rite/Venus contexts and group dedication **July 23–26, 690**; *pib na* activation **10 January 692** (eighth solar anniversary of accession). On 23 July 690, Jupiter–Saturn–Mars–moon conjunction (<5° in Scorpio) read as First Mother (moon) rejoined by three children (planets)—omen opening dedication (Lounsbury / Dütting & Aveni apparatus; notes refine as *way* companions of the Triad).

### Cross Group theology and legitimization strategy

Central icons as Schele & Freidel interpret them:

| Temple | Central image | Power transfer |
|---|---|---|
| Cross | World Tree / Serpent Bar / Celestial Bird (axis of cosmos) | Pacal gives sun-monster scepter |
| Foliated Cross | Maize tree from Waterlily Monster | Pacal gives Personified Bloodletter |
| Sun | Sun Jaguar shield and crossed spears; war/sacrifice | Pacal holds eccentric flint and flayed-face shield |

Sanctuary doorjambs name each *pib na*: **Wacah Chan** (World Tree), **Na Te Kan** (maize tree), **Mah Kina ????-Cab** (shield stack). House dedication next day names Chan-Bahlum’s house **Mah Kina Bahlum-Kuk Na** (“Lord Bahlum-Kuk House”), joining three historical patrilineages under the founder.

Schele & Freidel assert the Cross Group texts are the only full Classic-period statement of creation mythology and its relation to *ahau*. First Mother (Lady Beastie) and First Father GI’ born in previous creation; children of the Triad born 754 years into present creation. **Heinrich Berlin** first recognized the Palenque Triad GI–GIII (GI Venus/decapitation; GII God K/lineage and blood; GIII Jaguar Sun/Ahau-Kin). Tablet of the Cross sequence (authors’ paraphrase): Lady Beastie born 7 Dec 3121 BC; GI’ 16 June 3122 BC; new creation 13 Aug 3114 BC; Lady Beastie self-crowned 13 Aug 2305 BC at age 815 as first crowned ruler (*zac uinic* / Jester God headband); legendary **U-Kix-Chan** bridges myth to Olmec-era deep past; historical kings from Bahlum-Kuk through Chan-Bahlum I. Chan-Bahlum manipulates textual order to foreground First Mother over earlier-born First Father; contrived calendric symmetry links Pacal’s birth to Lady Beastie’s—shared sacred substance.

Twofold legitimization strategy (THEORY): (1) Lady Zac-Kuk like-in-kind to First Mother of the Triad; (2) Pacal’s birth date replicates the goddess’s temporal symmetry. Parallel for the public: Zac-Kuk’s clan = old creation; Pacal’s new patriline = “new creation”; transmission of sacred kingship essence between kings irrespective of gender or family. Chan-Bahlum further redefined succession as ecstatic communion with the dead king in the Otherworld rather than lineage alone. Later kings with descent problems simply evoked his explanation—no subsequent king had to restate proofs.

### Scale, reception, and collapse coda

During the combined lives of Pacal and Chan-Bahlum (AD 603–702), Schele & Freidel report Palenque became a major western power extending to Tortuguero west and Miraflores east. Pacal’s tomb engineering then unique in the New World; sarcophagus lid world-famous; Chan-Bahlum’s tablets popularized since Stephens and Catherwood 1841. **POSITION:** Palenque’s definition of dynasty transcending lineage did not save the polity from Classic collapse—descendants of Pacal followed others into failure of kingship.
