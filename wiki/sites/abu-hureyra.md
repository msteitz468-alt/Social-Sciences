# Abu Hureyra

Multi-period open-air settlement on the Euphrates floodplain in northwest Syria, known archaeologically as a major Late Pleistocene–early Holocene village sequence: sedentary plant-and-gazelle hunter-gatherers, [[younger-dryas|Younger Dryas]] abandonment, and later Neolithic farming and herding. The site was drowned under Lake Assad after dam construction. This page densifies claims from [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen, *After the Ice* (2003)]]; absolute dates and domestic rye status are date-stamped to that synthesis.

**Identity caution:** “Abu Hureyran people” in the literature names occupants of an archaeological locus, not a self-conscious people, language community, or genetic population.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: **Andrew Moore** conducted a two-season salvage excavation in 1972 before flooding, recovering early dwellings and rubbish but no cemetery. Flotation recovered fragile plant remains; **Gordon Hillman** identified no fewer than 157 plant species brought to the village (and suspected ≥100 more without archaeological trace), with multi-season collection consistent with year-round residence. From about two tons of bone, **Peter Rowley-Conwy** and **Tony Legge** showed that only adults, newborns, and yearlings were killed—pinpointing principal gazelle slaughter to early summer when that age mix is present. Later Neolithic phases and exchange goods (including Cappadocian obsidian) are reported in Mithen’s synthesis of the same salvage corpus and comparative work.

Lubbock narrative scenes of village life in *After the Ice* are reconstructions grounded in these finds, not excavation observations.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Mithen 2003, ch.5–6 and later comparative chapters.)*

1. **Sedentary hunter-gatherer village (~11,500 BCE scene date).** Soft-stone-cut subterranean circular rooms (~3 m across); outdoor plant processing; grinding stones and lunates; dependence on wild plants and seasonal gazelle. Gazelle principally hunted for only a few weeks each summer when vast herds passed; visitors exchanged southern Turkish / Cappadocian obsidian for Mediterranean dentalium already present at the site.
2. **Younger Dryas disruption.** After more than a thousand years of hunter-gatherer occupation, the village is abandoned under YD climatic stress (with regional peers such as [[eynan-ain-mallaha|‘Ain Mallaha]] and [[hayonim-cave|Hayonim]]); Late Natufian life becomes more mobile.
3. **Early domestic rye (Hillman; as of Mithen 2003).** A few domestic-form rye grains dated 11,000–10,500 BCE—presented by Mithen as the oldest domesticated cereal grains then known—plus typical cultivation weeds; intensified care of wild rye under YD stress is argued to have unintentionally domesticated it, yet the village was still abandoned and domestic rye is said to have reverted to wild.
4. **Neolithic return (~9000 BCE).** Return as farmers with mud-brick houses and wheat/barley on alluvium; gazelle hunting continues for another millennium; then a switch to sheep/goat herding and tell formation.
5. **Later links.** Obsidian at the site is traced (via multi-stage exchange) to Cappadocia; herding by ~7500 BCE is placed in regional animal-domestication debates; Mureybet resettlement across the Euphrates is linked by Mithen to Late Natufian abandonment of Abu Hureyra.

## Significance

- Flagship multi-period case for SW Asian [[sedentism]] before full farming, then for early cereal cultivation and later ovicaprid herding on the Euphrates.
- Landmark archaeobotanical and archaeozoological analyses (Hillman; Rowley-Conwy & Legge) as methods cases for seasonality and plant use.
- Central to Mithen’s narrative of YD stress, early domestication experiments, and the path toward the [[agricultural-revolution|agricultural revolution]] / Pre-Pottery Neolithic.
- Dam flooding makes the salvage corpus a closed archive—reliability and incompleteness (no cemetery recovered) are part of the site’s historiography.

## Key Debates

- Whether Hillman’s domestic rye (as of 2003) remains the earliest cereal domesticate, and whether it was a sustained local lineage or a failed experiment that reverted to wild (single-site finding; do not promote to timeless wiki voice).
- Causes of initial sedentism: Mithen rejects overpopulation/starvation pressure for Early Natufian/Abu Hureyran village life, favoring social opportunity models (e.g. Valla aggregation-extension) elsewhere in the same chapters.
- Local versus multi-centre origins of domestic wheat, barley, pulses, and flax; 1997 genetic work cited by Mithen pointing toward SE Turkish Karacadağ as a wheat locus (~200 km north of Abu Hureyra)—needs verification and later genetic revision.
- Sheep/goat domestication timing and geography relative to gazelle-drive economies (regional debate; Abu Hureyra herding by ~7500 BCE as one node).

## Historiography

Known primarily through Moore’s 1972 salvage and subsequent specialist publications summarized in popular and synthetic form by Mithen 2003. The site’s inundation under Lake Assad freezes fieldwork and makes re-excavation impossible—claims rest on the published salvage assemblage and later reanalyses.

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]* and Postscript.)*

- **Natufian:** intensive wild-cereal harvest 11,000–10,000 BC then cereal rarity under desiccation; knot grass and milk vetch mass use — no deliberate cereal specialisation independent of local abundance.
- **Postscript chronology:** Younger Dryas desiccation significant ~**10,600 BC** (not 11,000) from Abu Hureyra 14C + Roitel charcoal; almond/terebinth persist reduced in steppe — moderate climatic impact; not Cauvin’s trigger for the Neolithic Revolution.
- **PPNB:** appears in diffusion and animal-domestication discussions (goat import patterns with Halula).

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Ch. 4; densifies Hillman, Legge & Rowley-Conwy, Molleson via Barker.)*

- **Climate archive.** Abu Hureyra plant remains show moister springs/summers and gathering of wild einkorn and rye in Bølling–Allerød; Younger Dryas cold/dry caused decline of tree fruits, asphodels, cereals; site abandoned as park woodland retreated (attributed).
- **Early Natufian permanent settlement without agriculture.** Clay/timber shelters; 150+ plant species; systematic wild einkorn/barley + ~20 other foods; mass spring gazelle kills (Legge & Rowley-Conwy) = hunting not herding; occupation early spring–late autumn; possibly year-round via storage — “one of the most convincing as well as earliest examples… of a permanent settlement sustained without agriculture” (Hillman 2000; Hillman et al. 1997 via Barker) (attributed). Intensive flotation produced far more non-cereal seeds than older grain-cache excavations (method point).
- **PPNB reoccupation.** Rectangular houses vs Natufian circular shelters; mixed farming: emmer, einkorn, barley, legumes + sheep/goats; diet heavily roughly ground cereals (Molleson); Pottery Neolithic softer cereal foods (less dental abrasion) (attributed).
- **Analytical use.** Barker’s flagship case that **sedentism ≠ farming** — permanent settlement without agriculture attested here. Cross-links: [[natufian-culture]], [[sedentism]], [[animal-domestication]], [[jericho]], [[barker-graeme]].
