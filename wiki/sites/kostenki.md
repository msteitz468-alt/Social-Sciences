# Kostenki

Cluster of Upper Palaeolithic locales on the west bank of the Don (Kostenki and Borshevo villages); eastern European anchor for settlement scale, Early Upper Palaeolithic network structure, and mammoth-bone architecture in [[gamble-palaeolithic-societies-europe-1999|Gamble 1999]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Pioneering 1930s excavations (Efimenko 1958; especially Kostenki 1 horizon 1) are classics that, Gamble argues, **revolutionized the scale at which Palaeolithic settlements were conceived**. Rogachev’s plan shows main excavation ~760 m²; subsequent work roughly doubled the area. With Predmostí, Kostenki is important for area excavation and horizontal (not only stratigraphic) information (Gamble 1999, attributed; Praslov & Rogachev 1982). Colluvial/loess loams over humic beds with volcanic ash yield ideal bone preservation.

## Occupation Sequence

Around Kostenki and Borshevo: **~25 major locales over 6 km in three clusters** (as of Gamble 1999 summarizing Praslov, Rogachev, Anikovich, Klein). In ravine and lower terrace: pits, constructions using mammoth bones, numerous post holes, and possibly subterranean dwellings. Despite searching, no other Palaeolithic locales—not even flint scatters—are known in the immediate Kostenki area; nearest major locale Avdeevo ~300 km west.

**Chronology outline (Gamble Table 6.5, as of 1999):** Kostenki–Streletskayan/Sungirian NE ~33–24 ka bp within EUP 33–21 ka bp; later Aurignacian and Gravettian technocomplex spans; Willendorf/Kostenki phase 24–20 ka bp with shouldered points from Don to Moravia/Austria/southern Poland, mammoth-bone houses, storage pits, female/animal carvings (Svoboda via Gamble). Pre-33 ka recovery environments change: lakes/shores that preserved flagships earlier (e.g. Boxgrove, Terra Amata) are rare; Kostenki’s colluvial/loess setting is a key NE preservation pathway.

Open-air UP burials at Sunghir, Kostenki, and [[dolni-vestonice|Dolní Věstonice]]–Pavlov are treated as cultural development rather than mere recovery bias (Gamble 1999, attributed).

## Significance

### Settlement complex scale

The 6 km / 25-locale complex is Gamble’s type case for **EUP complex scale** in the northeast: dense clustering without local satellite flint scatters, implying regional emptiness or extreme focalization of activity—nearest peer Avdeevo hundreds of kilometres west. Large horizontal exposures made multi-feature “settlement” thinking possible long before many western European open-air programs.

### Transfers and extended networks

Aurignacian Kostenki: shells from the Black Sea ~500 km (Hahn/Floss via Gamble)—regularly exceeding the ~100 km “landscape of habit” limit that stone transfers more often respect. Such shell transfers help Gamble argue EUP extension of networks beyond Middle Palaeolithic effective ranges.

### Mammoth economy and structures

Gravettian NC/NE mammoth middens include Kostenki 1/1 among DV I–II, Predmostí, Pavlov, Milovice, Spadzista, etc. Soffer (via Gamble): remains from kills and natural “mammoth graveyards”; generally lack cut marks but carnivore-gnawed. At Moravian locales mammoth bone is not burnt/stored in pits—**contrast Kostenki 1/1 and Central Russian Plain post-LGM**, where storage/burning patterns differ (Gamble 1999, attributed). Mammoth-bone constructions and possible subterranean dwellings place Kostenki among the architectural high end of EUP “social occasions.”

## Key Debates

- **Chronology of earliest UP at Kostenki** and relation to modern human dispersal — dense post-1999 literature; keep Gamble layer dated **(as of Gamble 1999)**.
- **Industry labels** — Aurignacian, Streletskayan, Sungirian, Gravettian/Willendorf–Kostenki phases as technocomplexes, not peoples.
- **Isolation of the complex** — genuine regional emptiness vs survey bias (Gamble notes searching failed to find nearby scatters).
- **Mammoth bone** — architecture/fuel/storage vs collected carcass bone (Soffer dual pathway).

## Historiography

Soviet-era large-area excavation made Kostenki globally influential for settlement archaeology (Efimenko–Rogachev tradition). Gamble 1999 uses that historiography to argue for network-scale analysis of EUP Europe rather than only culture-stratigraphic succession.
