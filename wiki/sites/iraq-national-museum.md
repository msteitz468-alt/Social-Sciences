# Iraq National Museum (Baghdad)

The **Iraq National Museum** in Baghdad is the principal national repository of Iraq's archaeological collections and a **load-bearing case** in heritage literature for conflict-related cultural destruction and the [[illicit-antiquities-trade]]. Primary in-vault synthesis: [[brodie-neil|Neil Brodie]], "The Plunder of Iraq's Archaeological Heritage, 1991–2005, and the London Antiquities Trade," in [[brodie-kersel-luke-tubb-archaeology-cultural-heritage-antiquities-trade-2006|Brodie et al. 2006]]. Related: [[renfrew-loot-legitimacy-2000]], [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Not an excavation site. **Founded 1923**; present building location **1966**, enlarged **1986** (as of Brodie 2006 citing Ghaidan & Peolini; Schipper). Before the 1991 Gulf War, roughly **10,000** objects on display — less than **5%** of total holdings (prehistoric through Islamic). Material from major Mesopotamian excavations and from regional museums concentrated here under successive Iraqi antiquities administrations.

## Occupation Sequence (institutional)

| Phase | Events (attributed — Brodie 2006 and sources he cites) |
|---|---|
| Pre-1991 | National collection growth; display fraction of storerooms |
| 1991 Gulf War | Building survived with some nearby-bomb damage; objects packed in Central Bank vaults suffered flooding damage; regional museums also hit |
| 1991–94 regional crisis | **11** regional museums robbed; ~**3,000** artifacts + **484** manuscripts reported stolen (1991–94 window) |
| Apr 2000 | Museum reopened; water damage visible on many objects, esp. ivories |
| Mar–Apr 2003 | Closed for imminent war; staff moved **8,366** display objects to secret underground storage; padding/sandbags for unmovable pieces; staff evacuated **8 Apr** when fighters occupied the compound |
| 10–12 Apr 2003 | Ransacking: galleries, offices, basement storerooms; cars, equipment, computers stolen; files scattered |
| 21 Apr 2003 onward | Col. **Matthew Bogdanos** investigation; local amnesty; secret storage of 8,366 display pieces discovered early June (had been presumed stolen) |

## 2003 Loss and Recovery Figures (attributed — Brodie citing Bogdanos)

Numbers shifted between early media estimates and inventory work; use **Bogdanos-linked figures** as the chapter’s preferred assessment:

- **DoD briefing 10 Sep 2003:** at least **13,515** objects stolen; **~3,500** recovered (amnesty ~1,700; Iraq raids ~900; abroad ~750).
- **Final report as of Jan 2005:** more than **13,864** stolen; **1,935** returned under amnesty; **3,424** seized in Iraq and abroad.
- **Exhibition galleries:** **40** good-quality objects stolen, **16** damaged; selective (“knowledgeable”) theft pattern; **15** display pieces recovered by Jan 2005 including iconic pieces such as the Warka Vase / Warka Head and Bassetki statue (as reported).
- **Basement:** **103** small plastic boxes emptied; **10,686** pieces stolen including **5,144** cylinder seals; keys to 30 cabinets lost in the dark left other cabinets intact; Bogdanos notes possible **inside knowledge**.
- Offices/labs thoroughly ransacked; ~**40** vehicles taken.

**Context:** By March 2003 the museum ranked **second** (after the Central Bank) on a Pentagon ORHA list of sites to secure — guidance without command force (Brodie citing Lawler; Renfrew).

## Broader 1991–2005 archaeological landscape (same chapter)

Brodie embeds the museum in a wider Iraqi crisis: southern site destruction (e.g. estimates of **30–50%** of Isin destroyed in reporting he cites); Khorsabad execution of looters (1997 report); London antiquities market as outlet; UK **SI 1519** (Iraq) inverting burden of proof for certain imports; observed collapse of seal auctions after tighter controls. Site looting and museum theft are linked but not identical phenomena.

## Significance

- **Conflict-heritage exemplar** for Hague Convention debates, military cultural-property training, and import-restriction advocacy.
- **Market connection** — portable high-value sealed objects (cylinder seals) ideally suited to the illicit trade; excavated seals especially valuable because provenienced and fake-resistant (Brodie citing Biggs; Muscarella).
- Anchors Position A casework on [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]] without settling the causal dispute with Position B ([[cuno-who-owns-antiquity-2008]]).

## Key Debates

- Scale of 2003 losses: early media inflation vs inventory (record both, prefer dated Bogdanos figures).
- Responsibility for delayed securing of the museum (military priorities vs conspiracy narratives — Brodie reports both as *claims circulating*, not verdicts).
- Demand-side collecting policy vs nationalist/military protection frames — [[antiquities-market-looting-debate]].
