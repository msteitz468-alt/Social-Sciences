# Karanovo

Major **tell** in Upper Thrace (Bulgaria) whose stratified sequence is a **chronological backbone** for the SE European Neolithic. Featured throughout *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Whittle 1996]]* Chs. 3–5.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Long excavation history under Bulgarian archaeology; Whittle draws on published house plans, sections, and pottery phasing. Excavation quality varies by campaign — prefer primary reports when ingested.

## Occupation Sequence

Early Neolithic Karanovo I–II building phases through middle and later Neolithic/Copper Age levels (Whittle’s illustrations include successive rebuildings and house plans). Exact absolute dates as of mid-1990s calibrated framework.

## Significance

Type sequence for SE European tell-dwelling farmers; anchors discussion of first generations, house symbolism, and long-term village continuity vs change.

## Key Debates

Relationship of Karanovo farmers to Greek early Neolithic and to Carpathian basin complexes ([[starcevo-koros-cris-culture]]); demic vs indigenous models — see [[farming-spread-europe-debate]].

## Historiography

Culture-historical Balkan tell archaeology; densified via Whittle 1996 survey.

## Whittle densification (1996)

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 3–5. Chronology mid-1990s calibrated BC; pre-aDNA. Archaeological tell ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes.)*

### Early sequence and regional density (as of Whittle 1996)

Whittle’s opening Ch.3 vignette stages **Karanovo phase II** (~6000 BC context as of his framework): rectangular mud/daub houses in ordered rows; tens of buildings; clay figurines he links to the domestic world and ancestors; burials under some houses; mound already centuries old and destined (with interruptions) for ~2500 further years of use. He reports the mound rising **~12 m** and covering **over 4 ha**, with **seven major phases** in the classic sequence.

As of Whittle 1996: Karanovo-type sites in central-southern Bulgaria by **~6000 BC** (Macedonia/Crete soon after 7000; Thessaly by ~6500; Hungarian plain roughly that horizon)—a south–north radiocarbon pattern often read as colonising movement, which Whittle reframes as slow indigenous becoming with limited possible sea colonisation into dense zones (**position** on [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]).

**Nova Zagora region** (Karanovo’s region): ~12 early sites estimated in a 30×10 km area; Azmak ~25 km west as another focus; early tells also Chevdar, Kazanluk.

### Houses, layout, and tell interpretation (Ch.3)

Whittle reports **Karanovo I–II** one-roomed post-framed houses **~7×7 m or less**, oven on side/back wall, closely spaced on common orientation along wood/sand lanes; early settlement extent estimates from ~15–30 to 60+ houses; a figure of **300+** inhabitants for later stages is recorded but **not** to be assumed for every stage/site.

He contrasts more solid southern tell architecture (Anza mudbrick; Karanovo rows of post-framed houses) with northerly Starčevo–Körös brief occupations and pit-dwellings/huts suited to mobility—yet stresses gradual development even in the south. The tell, in his reading, is a **slow-growing symptom of gradual internal change**, not the stamp of rapid external innovation (**position**). He distinguishes ordered tells from dispersed “flat” sites and warns against automatically ascribing greater sedentism to tells. Site locations like Karanovo allow **floodwater cultivation** possibilities that weaken any hard link between cereals and fully sedentary settlement (**attributed**).

Early SE package elements he places on this horizon (small riverine communities; often painted pottery for presentation more than cooking; house-centred figurines; sheep/goat usually dominant among domesticates): shared with the broader first-generation SE world including [[starcevo-koros-cris-culture]]. Figurines he prefers as **ancestral** figures rather than a mother-goddess pantheon (**position** vs Gimbutas-type readings).

### Later Neolithic / Copper Age continuity with breaks (Ch.4)

Whittle reports existing tells Karanovo and Azmak **continued** (heights of several metres by the later sequence) but **not continuously**—breaks between **Karanovo IV–V** and **V–VI**; Azmak unoccupied in Karanovo IV and V equivalents. Houses larger, often two-roomed (**Karanovo VI** ~8–12 × 5–6 m), uniform with grinding platform and closed oven; Azmak VI two larger buildings (19×9 and 18.4×6.3 m). Abandoned mounds still functioned as **ancestral places**. Nova Zagora: ~20 investigated sites vs ~12 earlier; spacing up to ~5 km. Shared later material culture with the **Karanovo VI–Kodžadermen–Gumelnița** bloc and the NE Bulgaria “new history” of ordered tells + extramural cemeteries (see [[varna]]).

**Ai Bunar** (central-southern Bulgaria, Karanovo V–VI horizon) is among the copper extraction sites he lists; graphite decoration on some Karanovo VI–Gumelnița pottery at high temperatures—skilled but, he argues, not necessarily restricted specialist craft.

Ceramic regional styles (Maritsa/Karanovo IV–V mosaic → late Karanovo VI bloc) he rejects as political tribes: better as **shared identity ideas without political unity** (**position**; four-way non-identity).

### End of Karanovo VI and “transition” (Ch.5)

Whittle records many/most established Bulgarian tells abandoned with claimed humic hiatus—“transition period” lasting **centuries**; end of Karanovo VI–Kodžadermen–Gumelnița—but variation poorly known. At **Ezero**, EBA levels directly succeed Karanovo VI in the main area (hiatus on one side is earlier, IV–VII). Some NE Bulgaria tells unused in the transition; some ends with burning, but earlier deliberate burning for cycle ends is also known; danger of mistaking a **gradual process for a single event**. Ezero–Karanovo VII EBA later 4th mill.: plain dark pottery, few figurines, copper then arsenical flat axes; apsidal stone-footed houses. Late Vinča settlement-pattern change may precede Karanovo VI end; Karanovo VI end may be ~**4000 BC** while late Cucuteni–Tripolye continues—**non-synchronous** reconfiguration (**position** against single kurgan/steppe shockwave; see [[baden-culture]] for his multi-causal reading).
