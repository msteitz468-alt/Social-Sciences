# Thulamela

Thulamela is a nine-hectare **Iron Age** stone-walled hilltop settlement (**c. 1440–1640 CE**) in the
far north of [[kruger-national-park|Kruger National Park]], South Africa, overlooking the Levhuvhu
River near its confluence with the Limpopo. Part of the broader **Zimbabwe Culture** tradition (see
[[mapungubwe|Mapungubwe]] and [[great-zimbabwe|Great Zimbabwe]]), it is the central case study of
[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012|Meskell's *The Nature of Heritage* (2012)]], where it functions as
a microcosm of the book's argument: heritage as compensation for past deprivation, reliance on
public–private funding, and the eventual dominance of "nature" over "culture." All claims below are
attributed to Meskell 2012 and the specialists she cites.

**Identity caution:** Archaeological locus ≠ people ≠ language ≠ self-conscious group. The site's
attribution as "Venda" vs. "Shangaan" was itself contested during excavation (below); ethnonymic
links are `associated_with`, not identities.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Thulamela was **surveyed in 1990**, first test-trenched in **1991**, and excavated over four years,
**1993–1997**, overseen by technical and environmental committees drawn from national parks,
universities, and local Shangaan and Venda communities (Nemaheni 2004). It was celebrated as the
**first collaborative archaeological project between black and white South Africans** at the moment
of democratic transition, emblematic of the "Rainbow Nation," and drew heavy media coverage
(including the US PBS *Nova* series). It was declared a **national monument in 1999**.

Meskell is critical of the excavation's standards (attributed): the appointed lead, **Sidney
Miller**, was a **civil engineer, not a professional archaeologist**; local masons rebuilt the walls;
the work earned Miller an MA but the **final report was never published** and, in her assessment,
"fell short of international scientific standards." Funding came from corporate and NGO donors —
**Gold Fields** (a mining conglomerate) was the first corporate donor after gold artifacts were
found; **WWF South Africa** was approached for environmental-education funding; and **NORAD** gave
R1 million from 2003 for a proposed Environmental Education Centre and Museum that was **never built**
(only laminated posters at a picnic site). The **February 2000 Kruger floods** washed away
photographs, ceramics, and metal objects from the picnic-site display.

## Occupation Sequence

Thulamela's stone-walled enclosures, gold and ivory working, and imported trade goods (West African
metal gongs, glass beads, Chinese porcelain, seashells) place it within the Indian Ocean–linked
Zimbabwe-tradition political economy, **c. 1440–1640**. It was abandoned c. 1640 and succeeded by
Venda-associated settlement after c. 1690 (radiocarbon from Dzata). Meskell reports the walls
"falling/neglected" during her fieldwork, exemplifying broader institutional disregard.

## Significance

Thulamela is the most prominent Iron Age site in Kruger and the emblematic post-apartheid heritage
project. Its afterlife, in Meskell's reading, dramatizes the failure of "heritage as therapy":
management plans were repeatedly drafted (Miller 1997; a 1997 plan by international expert Amareswar
Galla; Nemaheni 2003) but **never fully implemented**; visitor numbers collapsed (430 in the first
half of 2001; 911 for all of 2005); and by 2009 SANParks asked the impoverished local municipality to
fund wall restoration. The excavated objects (gold beads and more) are held in **University of
Pretoria** vaults — an unresolved point of contention, with some beads waiting ten years to be
cleaned and returned and items reported missing.

## Key Debates

- **The reburial controversy.** Two skeletons were exhumed with gold grave goods; Miller named the
  male **"King Ingwe" (King Leopard)** and the female **"Queen Losha,"** although the two lived
  roughly 150 years apart (Steyn et al. 1998) — the bestowal of Venda names on unidentified remains
  caused controversy (Nemaheni 2004). A reburial ceremony (**31 May 1997**), led by the Makahane
  clan, mixed Shangaan and Venda dance with a Venda goat sacrifice and blood on three stones, plus a
  Christian plastic cross over each grave (removed 1999). **Community disagreement was open**
  (position): elder **Freddie Munzhelele** (b. 1929), present at the exhumation, objected that
  "none of this was explained to me... It was only whites and a few other people" and that no snuff
  was offered to the ancestors; other elders opposed disturbing the bones as against Venda tradition;
  younger members were more supportive; a senior SANParks official called the rushed reburial "a sad
  mockery." Disputes ran over whether remains could contact metal (community insisted on linen and
  wood, not aluminum).
- **Whose site? (inter-ethnic stakeholding).** Miller and senior archaeologists treated Thulamela as
  a **Venda** site, privileging the Makahane clan over the **Mhinga (Shangaan)** group; a Shangaan
  *sangoma*, German Mabasa, asked by the community forum to "throw the bones," divined that the site
  was indeed Venda (Nemaheni's account).
- **Alleged misconduct around the gold (reported, unverified).** Meskell relays — explicitly flagged
  as unverifiable testimony — reports that gold was stored under the archaeologist's bed, that
  auctioneers were contacted within hours of gold-bead finds, that gold was "ransomed" until staff
  were paid, and that a student wore ancient blue glass beads taken from spoil heaps (rationalized by
  a senior professor as an established "Afrikaner archaeology" pattern). Kruger's warden apologized in
  writing (1997) for "conflicts of personalities."

All interpretive claims date-stamped **(as of Meskell 2012)**; post-2012 dating not incorporated.

## Historiography

Thulamela's record rests on a sub-standard, partly unpublished excavation led by a non-specialist,
supplemented by Meskell's own **archaeological ethnography** (including a National Science
Foundation–funded 3D digital scanning project) and the accounts of participants and elders (Nemaheni
2004; Steyn et al. 1998; Miller 1996/1997). The site's interpretation was shaped by post-1994
"Rainbow Nation" politics and by contested ethnohistoric projection of Venda identity onto medieval
remains — reason for the four-way non-identity cautions above. Source:
[[meskell-nature-of-heritage-2012]].
