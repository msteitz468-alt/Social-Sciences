# Aksum (Axum)

Highland capital and type-site of the Aksumite state in modern Ethiopia (Tigray), with a nuclear area Connah maps around Aksum and the Red Sea port of Adulis (Eritrean coast). Densified from [[connah-african-civilizations-2016|Connah, *African Civilizations* (2016)]], principally Ch. 5. All interpretive claims are attributed to Connah 2016 and specialists he cites.

**Identity caution:** Archaeological locus ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetic population. “Aksumite,” “Ethiopian,” “Semitic contribution,” and South Arabian script influence are not population-replacement claims. Do not equate the sacred-city survival of Aksum with continuous ethnic essence. Link with `associated_with` unless identity is argued.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Connah (2016) combines classical texts, readable indigenous inscriptions and coins, oral tradition (ranked least valuable for origins), and stratified excavation/survey.

- **Classical sources:** Pliny (~AD 77) and the first-century *Periplus* name Adulis, Koloè (possibly Kohaito or Matara—identification uncertain), and Aksum; classical writers indicate a powerful kingdom able to conquer parts of South Arabia in the sixth century CE, already partly urbanized and exporting ivory, rhinoceros horn, tortoiseshell, and obsidian via Adulis.
- **Inscriptions and coins vs [[meroe|Meroë]]:** Unlike Meroitic texts, Aksumite and earlier highland inscriptions (Ge’ez, Sabaean, sometimes Greek) can be read. Stone inscriptions record military exploits (Ezana’s apparent [[meroe|Meroë]] expedition, fourth century; Kaleb’s South Arabia campaigns, sixth) and ruler names. Coins may record as many as twenty-four kings and document fourth-century Christianization by replacing crescent and disc with the cross.
- **Major excavated components Connah reports:** German expedition’s Enda Mika’el, Enda Semon, and Ta’akha Mariam (monumental multiroomed structures provisionally called palaces; Ta’akha Mariam ~80 × 120 m); Anfray’s Dongur Mansion (~40 rooms, ~3,000 m², ~seventh century CE—elite villa, not necessarily royal); Chittick’s hillside excavations behind the main stelae group (erosional cycle); D Site Kidane Mehret and K Site Maleke Aksum (nonelite); Matara (~20 ha) between Adulis and Aksum; Adulis port excavations; Michels’s stratified random survey (40% of 500 km² Aksum–Yeha; ~260 sites); Bard et al. five phases at Bieta Giyorgis (fourth century BCE to possibly ninth century CE).
- **Oral tradition:** Connah ranks oral tradition least valuable for urbanization/state emergence—often fantastic and beyond Aksumite range—yet the Queen of Sheba–Solomon royal-origin tradition offers some support for a Semitic *contribution* indicated by other sources (linguistic/cultural influence frame, not modern ethnicity).

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Connah 2016 Ch. 5; chronology explicitly disputed among specialists he cites.)*

### Chronological frameworks (dating flag)

Connah summarizes: Anfray/Michels long chronologies; Fattovich Pre-Aksumite start possibly tenth century BCE; Munro-Hay short Aksumite coin-based sequence (first–early seventh century CE); Negussie long chronology for Aksum but short for Matara; later Phillipson and Fattovich suggested abandoning “Pre-Aksumite” and its subdivisions. Connah prefers a realistic timetable of contrasting periods in the first millennium BCE, first millennium CE, and second millennium CE.

### First millennium BCE antecedents

- Connah reports antecedents of Aksumite culture as early as the eighth century BCE (formerly Pre-Aksumite), with large settlements at Yeha (Ethiopia) and Matara and Sembel (Eritrea)—Sembel belonging to the possibly earlier Ona Culture—showing inscriptions, sophisticated stone masonry, sculpture, and metallurgy alongside continued stone tools, plus contacts with South Arabia and lesser contacts with the Nile Valley.
- **Yeha:** Ashlar sandstone temple (parts still ~9 m high; Fattovich: originally ≥13 m, ~19 × 15 m), dated seventh or sixth century BCE with South Arabian parallels—possibly older than the Athenian Parthenon and implying highly organized society; South Arabian inscriptions, tombs, and Grat-Be’al-Guebri (Anfray: palace); Yeha may possibly have been an early urban centre.
- **D’mt:** Sabaean inscriptions suggest a mid-first-millennium BCE state D’mt in Tigray and adjacent Eritrea with modest urban growth at Yeha and Matara, then disintegration late BCE—but Phillipson and Fattovich questioned the evidence for D’mt. Connah still holds it probable that Aksumite society developed from first-millennium BCE regional cultures. Early first-millennium BCE culture was influenced by South Arabia (especially Saba) yet showed originality as language/script shifted toward Ge’ez.
- **Kidane Mehret D Site:** Peasant settlement of the eighth to fifth centuries BCE, then Late Aksumite sixth–eighth century CE farming village ~1 km north of Aksum.

### Aksumite first millennium CE

- **Complexity package (~2,000 years ago as Connah’s framing):** Urban centres; own writing; gold, silver, and bronze coinage; distinctive masonry architecture; unique monuments implying quarrying and engineering; extensive internal and external trade; significant international political role; among the first states to adopt Christianity.
- **Stelae and elite tombs:** Largest stelae carved as multistoreyed buildings—Stela 3 ~21 m standing; Stela 2 ~28 m; Stela 1 nearly 33 m, which Phillipson called a strong candidate for the largest single monolith humans ever proceeded to erect—associated with subterranean multichambered tombs. Stela 1 likely fell during erection (~fourth century CE / ~AD 400 per Phillipson/Munro-Hay), near official Christianization under Ezana (~AD 333 per Munro-Hay)—possibly abandoned for ideological reasons. Only ~8.5% of the largest stela’s length was set underground, so the centre of gravity was miscalculated or uncalculated.
- **Christianizing burial:** Tomb of the False Door (late fourth/early fifth century) lacks associated stela but retains door carving; twin tombs traditionally attributed to sixth-century kings Kaleb and Gabra Maskal mark a further stage.
- **Gudit Stelae Field:** ~594 large stones SW of Aksum, interpreted as middle-class rather than ruler/elite cemetery; nonelite burials from mid-second to mid-fourth centuries and possibly much of the first five centuries CE; GT II yielded early third-century high-quality glassware despite robbing.
- **Palaces and nonelite city:** Monumental complexes provisionally identified as palaces; Munro-Hay suggests some may have been village centres around landlords’ houses. K Site Maleke Aksum: nonelite industrial/craft occupancy within the city in fifth–sixth centuries CE. Connah argues Aksum was an extensive first-millennium CE city; Chittick’s hillside sequence shows erosional impact, suggesting eventual decline may link to environmental deterioration under high population density.
- **Matara urban hierarchy:** Phases ~500–300 BCE and Aksumite ~AD 700 after long abandonment (date as reported; site poorly published); elite villas, intermediate houses, and two-to-three-room dwellings suggest social hierarchy; Roman/Byzantine goldwork hoard; amphora-pipe water supply to church baptistery; key plan of Aksumite urban layout.
- **Adulis and engineering satellites:** Adulis yields Aksumite-style stone buildings and mixed Aksumite/Mediterranean artefacts. Kohaito dam ~67 m long, ~3 m high (Pankhurst: “one of the greatest engineering feats of the Aksumites”). Mai Shum reservoir is storage, not irrigation (Sulas et al. via Connah).
- **Nuclear geography:** Anfray’s map places the nuclear state around Aksum and Adulis, distinguishing eastern (more prosperous) and western (political-power centre) provinces, with possible Late Aksumite capital shift east. Michels linked fertile soil of the Aksumite Plain to development of political organization (~260 sites in surveyed Aksum–Yeha sample).
- **Coinage:** First tropical African state to mint its own gold/silver/bronze coinage (~third–seventh centuries CE), similar to Byzantine weight/standard/form; many gold coins in Greek—likely introduced for international trade; issue ceases as external trade declines; ~90% of known coins from northern Ethiopia (mainly bronze); gold coins mostly from South Arabia and less certainly India.
- **Subsistence:** Mixed agriculture—wheat, barley, teff, pulses, grape, cotton; cattle-dominant livestock with ox-drawn ploughs (unique in tropical Africa per Connah); ivory exploitation. Michels classed Aksumite Plain soils good to excellent; heartland capable of storable cereal surplus. Teff/injera pottery only late Aksumite, so teff less significant earlier. Laurel Phillipson shows cheap stone tools continued into late first millennium CE for wood, pottery finishing, hides, leather, parchment, sometimes ivory—alongside early bronze and iron at Yeha by seventh/sixth century BCE.

### Decline and afterlife

- Connah argues the economic/political base was seriously weakened by Arab extension of control over Red Sea trade; coinage ceases early seventh century; plague, drought, and environmental deterioration may have contributed; political centre shifted south toward Lalibela/Lasta. An Ethiopian state survived but in the earlier second millennium CE lost previous urban character—rulers leading semi-nomadic “moving capitals” (Pankhurst) of up to ~100,000 exhausting local food and firewood. Legend of Gudit (late tenth century) is flagged as legend.
- Rock-hewn churches (e.g. Beta Giyorgis at Lalibela, ~10.6 m high single-block church) document Christian technical and resource-marshalling capacity. Late second millennium: “static capitals” with stone castles (Gouzara; Gondar with twenty successive emperors before the nineteenth century). Aksum remained a reduced religious centre (Ark of the Covenant tradition; coronations); archaeological continuity claimed via churches using Aksumite ruins/features, though the last thousand years were under-studied until recently.

## Significance

- **Early tropical African complexity package:** Urbanism, writing, coinage, masonry, engineering monuments, long-distance trade, and early Christianity make Aksum a central comparative case in Connah’s African [[civilization]] / [[complex-society]] survey—despite highland geography that “seems an unlikely setting” for state emergence.
- **Bridge, not pure isolation:** Connah rejects pure isolation; Ethiopia and Eritrea at times formed an important zone of cultural integration—Ullendorff’s “bridge between Africa and Asia.” Post-seventh-century relative isolation after the rise of Islam is real but not Gibbon’s “thousand years of sleep”; isolation had strategic benefits (Connah’s closing paradox: join or reject the outside world from the “roof of the world”).
- **[[productive-land-hypothesis]]:** Highland environmental diversity and fertile soils (~27% good, ~13% fairly good in figures Connah reports); multiple crops per year; plough agriculture. Constraints (rocky land, gorges, epidemics, malaria in lowlands, recurrent famine) are mostly episodic and of little long-run inhibiting effect on complexity growth. Localized population pressure on more productive land under drought-famine stress could empower controllers of better land as elite power base; control of people was as important as control of land.
- **Trade as intensifier, not sole cause:** Wide trade (Roman eastern Mediterranean, Egypt, Nubia, South Arabia, Persian Gulf, India, Sri Lanka)—raw materials/exotica for manufactures/luxuries. Adulis as “front door”; possible damage to Nile trade contributing to [[meroe|Meroë]]’s post-first-century decline (suggestion). Apogee coterminous with Red Sea trade, yet Ethiopian state survived second-millennium comparative isolation. Aksum as entrepôt on a web of regional routes (*Periplus*: Adulis–Koloè three days, Adulis–Aksum eight; Cosmas: Aksum–Aswan thirty days; Aksum–Lake Tana/Sasu fifty days). Ivory as high-value export (Phillipson: major Mediterranean supplier). Origins of sociopolitical change argued to pre-date large-scale long-distance trade takeoff.
- **Stratification and ideology:** Absolute monarch (coins, thrones); royal/elite palaces and tombs; temples then churches; storeyed stelae; Kobishchanov’s “mania for the gigantic” to instil awe. Luxury-good distribution implies elite beyond royalty; large labour forces imply peasants/slaves; Matara houses and Gudit burials indicate intermediate ranks. Functional specialization (builders, metalworkers, potters, ivory carvers, merchants, scribes, coiners, soldiers, clergy). Religion (South Arabian-related polytheism → distinctive Ethiopian Christianity from the fourth century) legitimized elite power and later sacred-city survival of Aksum.
- **Technology rank:** Connah ranks Aksumite technology among the most sophisticated in precolonial tropical Africa (stelae, Nefas Mawcha, Kohaito dam, Mai Shum)—with noted mechanical limits on the largest stela.

## Key Debates

- **Pre-Aksumite term and long vs short chronologies:** Major dating dispute (Anfray, Michels, Fattovich, Munro-Hay, Negussie, Phillipson, Bard et al.); Connah’s three-millennium-block simplification is a pragmatic response, not a specialist consensus chronology.
- **D’mt state historicity:** Inscriptional suggestion vs Phillipson/Fattovich doubts.
- **Urban status without walls:** Towns/cities apparently lacked surrounding walls, leading Phillipson to question their urban status; layout evidence remains limited (Matara plan; Michels on Aksum’s changing form).
- **Palace vs landlord villa:** Dongur and peer complexes—royal palaces, elite villas, or village centres around landlords’ houses (Munro-Hay).
- **Population estimates:** Michels’s peak AD 450–750 at 39,603 then collapse after 850—Phillipson says significantly underestimated. Butzer and Bard et al. link humid mid-first-millennium BCE to mid-first-millennium CE intensification then seventh–eighth century soil degradation; Phillipson links growth to environmental collapse, possible sixth-century plague, and capital shift to NE Tigray.
- **Trade vs indigenous origins:** Temptation to treat the state as product of international trade vs Connah’s multi-causal synthesis (productive land, stress, stratification, ideology, trade intensifier).
- **South Arabian influence vs originality:** Script/language and architectural parallels without equating to population replacement.
- **Stela fall and Christianization timing:** Engineering failure vs ideological abandonment near Ezana’s conversion—both remain interpretive.
- **Comparative self-destruction:** Connah later analogizes Great Zimbabwe’s resource overexploitation to his Aksum reading—comparative hypothesis, not proven single cause.

## Oxford Handbook densification (Mitchell & Lane 2013)

*(Attributed — David W. Phillipson in [[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013]] Ch. 55; also Monroe Ch. 48; Edwards Ch. 54 on Meroe relation. **as of 2013**.)*

### Pre-Aksumite not a single Sabaean-defined state

Societies of the northern Horn in the first half of last millennium BC were conventionally lumped as ‘pre-Aksumite’ defined by South Arabian traits; that collective definition is **no longer tenable**. Indigenous occupations (Ancient Ona around Asmara; Kidane Mehret/Ona Negast near Aksum) show microlithic industries, rare copper alloy, cattle/sheep/goats, wheat/barley/(less certain) t’ef, dry-stone rectangular houses—Arabian elements poorly represented. Concept of a single ‘pre-Aksumite’ state (whether or not called **D‘mt**) now appears doubtful; non-elite show stronger local continuity than formerly believed (Phillipson 2013).

**Yeha / Mekaber Ga‘ewa:** temples safely described as Sabaean in style (late 8th–7th c. BC elite prerogatives); small numbers of South Arabian specialists (e.g. masons) may have arrived, but separate identity short-lived—do not equate elite architecture with population replacement or whole-polity foreign origin.

### Aksumite kingdom

Flourished first seven centuries AD as successor to Beta Giyorgis elite centre. Max territory likely included Massawa/Adulis, south/central Eritrean highlands, greater Tigray; 4th-c. Nile Valley incursion likely; 6th-c. rule over parts of southern Arabia. **Major ivory export** via Red Sea; Christianity adopted mid-4th c. (kings) but widespread rural adoption took 100–150 years; coinage (gold/silver/copper) from late 3rd c., gold with Greek until 7th c. Stelae Park elite tombs (3rd/4th c.)—largest stela 517 tonnes, would have been 33 m high. Kingdom **largely self-sufficient**; local metallurgy, ivory carving, glass, potting, stone-knapping from local forebears. **Overseas connections previously overstressed vs local continuity** (Phillipson).

Monroe: Aksum rose with Red Sea commerce as [[meroe|Meroë]] declined; Roman/Byzantine goods; Adulis/Matara hierarchy; decline with Islamic maritime control by 8th c.

### Decline; Zagwe and Lalibela

Late 6th–early 7th c.: over-extension under Kaleb, possible environmental stress (scale questioned: Sulas et al. 2009), debased coinage, capital shifts east in Tigray, coinage ceases, Mediterranean ivory declines—linked to growing **Arab control of Red Sea** trade routes. Medieval Tigray churches retain Aksumite architectural features; hiatus ~1000–1100. **Lalibela** multi-stage development; Zagwe southward transfer of authority and self-conscious Aksumite style as legitimacy prop; Zagwe ascendancy earlier than traditional early-12th-c. date (Phillipson).

Arabic traditions of mid-7th-c. Muslim refugees at Aksum should be read cautiously; firmer Islam via Dahlak islands and eastern plateau from perhaps 10th c. (Insoll; Fauvelle-Aymar & Hirsch via Phillipson).

Edwards notes possible Axumite military role in Meroitic end has **slight evidential bases**.
