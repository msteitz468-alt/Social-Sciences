# Monte Negro

## Discovery and Excavation History
Mountaintop site above Tilantongo in the Mixteca Alta, excavated by [[caso-alfonso|Alfonso Caso]] and Jorge Acosta as part of the Monte Albán project era.

## Occupation Sequence
A single, brief occupation contemporary with late [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] I (Ramos phase in Mixteca terms).

## Significance
In [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983), [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]] reinterpreted Monte Negro against the older view of it as an ancestor of Monte Albán: on the volume's reading it is a short-lived Ramos-phase urban experiment in the Mixteca, contemporary with (not ancestral to) Monte Albán I — a supersession of the pre-1965 consensus recorded here as a `[CONTRADICTION]`-resolution, including a conflict between an early uncalibrated radiocarbon date and ceramic cross-dating.

## Key Debates
Its chronological placement is part of the wider revision of the Caso-era Oaxaca sequence.

## Zapotec Civilization (Marcus & Flannery 1996)

*(Attributed — [[marcus-joyce|Marcus]] & [[flannery-kent-v|Flannery]], *[[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-1996|Zapotec Civilization]]* 1996, Chs. 12, 14–15; study [[marcus-flannery-zapotec-civilization-study]].)*

**Transitional Monte Albán I comparison.** Marcus & Flannery use Monte Negro (mountaintop ~60 km NW of Monte Albán, above Tilantongo) as a **contemporaneous smaller mountaintop center** illuminating the Monte Albán I transitional society between Rosario-phase chiefdom and Period II state. Pottery and architecture show strong ties to the Valley of Oaxaca; they float **possible Zapotec colonization** of the Tilantongo Valley in Late Monte Albán I, while noting no Building J “conquest slab” yet identified for Tilantongo (“Black Mountain”). **Secret temple passageways** at Monte Albán and San José Mogote are “reminiscent of those seen earlier at Monte Negro”; Dainzú Complex A also has a roofed passageway of Monte Negro type — shared architectural tradition within the expansion sphere.

**Not ancestral to Monte Albán.** Aligns with the Cloud People revision: Monte Negro is a short-lived peer/frontier experiment, not the ancestor of Monte Albán.

**Abandonment by IIIa.** By the “golden age” / Monte Albán IIIa need-for-defense discussion, the mountaintop site was **largely abandoned** as the Zapotec lost many outer Monte Albán II provinces (alongside eventual abandonment of Quiotepec in the [[cuicatlan-canada|Cuicatlán Cañada]]).

## Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas (2022) — fortified hilltop wave and abandonment

*(Attributed — [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski & Nicholas]], *Ancient Oaxaca: The Monte Albán State*, 2nd ed. 2022.)* [[caso-alfonso|Caso's]] 1937–9 excavations at Monte Negro found local expressions of highland Oaxaca and wider Mesoamerican styles in architecture, bodily ornamentation, pottery, braziers, and ritual practice in both domestic and public/temple contexts (Acosta and Romero 1992). Monte Negro was one of two Level II mountaintop towns in the Central Mixteca Alta after 300 BC (along with Cerro Jazmín), part of a broader shift to fortified hilltop terrace settlement across the region. Temples there (and at Valley of Oaxaca Period I civic-ceremonial centers) featured a new brazier form — a cylinder with a man's face, down-turned feline-associated mouth, perforated eyes for a glowing-coal effect, and headdresses bearing glyph C ([[zapotec-writing|Cocijo]]) with a maize cob or vessel of water (Acosta and Romero 1992; Caso and Bernal 1952). Most Mixteca Alta hilltop sites of this wave, including large ones like Monte Negro, were newly built and abandoned by **100 BC–AD 250** after perhaps only a few generations of occupation — consistent with, and densifying, the volume's chronological revision noted above.
