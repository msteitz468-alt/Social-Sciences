# Mehrgarh

Early farming settlement on the Kachi plain of Baluchistan (Pakistan). In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is the earliest known farming village of its type in southern Asia and a deep developmental sequence toward later Indus urbanism ([[harappan-civilization|Harappan]] / [[mohenjo-daro|Mohenjo-Daro]]).

**Identity caution:** Dental resemblance to indigenous Mesolithic South Asians is a biological observation, not proof of continuous ethnic identity; farming package ≠ migrant people. Culture ≠ genes ≠ language ≠ people.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: excavated by the French Mission working with the Pakistan Department of Archaeology under **Jean-François Jarrige**. **Lorenzo Constantini** identified domesticated wheat and (in larger quantities) barley from plant impressions in mud-brick chaff temper, plus gathered jujube and date—implying a wetter Kachi plain than today. **Christophe Moulherat** et al. identified cotton fibres stringing copper beads inside a corroded copper bead from a ~5500 BCE burial.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Jarrige et al. via Mithen 2003, ch.43.)*

1. **By ~7000 BCE:** farming village with more than a hundred inhabitants; multi-roomed rectangular mud-brick houses and storerooms—earliest known settlement of its type in southern Asia in Mithen’s framing.
2. **Crops:** domestic wheat and abundant barley; gathered jujube and date.
3. **Animals:** cattle and sheep bones become progressively smaller through layers while goat and gazelle sizes stay largely unchanged—interpreted as local domestication of humped cattle (zebu) and wild sheep; gazelle remains wild and declines.
4. **Exchange:** turquoise and lapis lazuli (likely Afghanistan); seashells from the Arabian shore ~500 km south; dentalium-like shells recalling Natufian exchange aesthetics without implying Natufian identity.
5. **Cemetery:** formal cemetery of ≥150 burials; underground tombs with chambers reused by shifting older bones—Mithen reads increasing importance of family ties.
6. **Dentition:** large teeth like indigenous Mesolithic South Asians; healthy, low caries, fluoride staining—appears to challenge pure immigrant-farmer ancestry from the west in favour of local people adopting farming means/ideas (position; phenotype ≠ population identity).
7. **~5500 BCE:** cotton fibres in copper bead—earliest known cotton use by ≥1,000 years (as of Mithen); cotton seeds with cereals in likely storage; wild vs domestic status uncertain, cultivation suspected (*G. arboreum* lineage). Cattle dominate economy (possibly ploughing/transport/milk); fine painted pottery appears.
8. **Later (to 4000–3500 BCE):** wheel-thrown coarse pottery, jasper bow-drills for beads, naturalistic figurines, stamp seals, copper smelting crucibles—foundation path toward Indus cities in Mithen’s long narrative.
9. **Wider frame:** West Asian barley/wheat/goat package reaches environmental limits east of the Indus where monsoon reverses seasonality; Jeitun and Iranian-plateau towns as related rim settlements; Zagros foothill expansion after 8000 BCE as first steps toward South/Central Asian farming.

## Significance

- Earliest substantial farming village of South Asia as of Mithen’s synthesis; dual local animal domestication (zebu, sheep) plus imported cereal package.
- Revises the antiquity of cotton use by more than a millennium relative to classic Indus assumptions.
- Key node linking SW Asian Neolithic expansion to the Indus urban trajectory without reducing Mehrgarh to a passive colony.
- Dental evidence as a caution against simple demic-diffusion stories.

## Key Debates

- **Immigrant farmers vs local adoption** of the farming package—dental evidence challenges pure western immigrant ancestry (as of Mithen 2003); do not equate with modern genetic results without new sources.
- Local vs introduced status of early cattle/sheep vs goats.
- Wild vs cultivated cotton at 5500 BCE.
- How continuous the path is from Mehrgarh seals/copper to Harappan cities (long developmental claim vs punctuated change).

## Possehl, *The Indus Civilization* (2002)

[[possehl-indus-civilization-2002|Possehl (2002)]] treats Mehrgarh (Kachi Plain, Baluchistan, at the base of the Bolan Pass) as the key regional case for local, non-diffused domestication, drawing on Jarrige, Jarrige, Meadow, and Quivron (1995). Period I is aceramic, with mud-brick dwellings and a subsistence base dominated by domesticated naked six-row barley; wheat is present but, per Meadow (1998), less plausibly a local domesticate than barley. Zebu cattle (*Bos indicus*) were locally domesticated, per Meadow, with sheep likely locally domesticated from wild stock and goats kept from first occupation; long-distance materials (lapis lazuli, turquoise, marine shell) appear even in the earliest graves. Pottery begins in Period II (the "Burj Basket-marked" horizon, c. 5500 BCE by regional cross-dating). Mehrgarh III, the Togau phase, shows a documented population discontinuity: skeletal (biodistance) analysis by Hemphill, Lukacs, and Kennedy (1991) finds the Togau cemetery population shares affinities with populations "from the west... the Iranian Plateau and the Near East," distinct from the earlier Stage One population. This is a biodistance finding about population affinity, not a claim about language, ethnicity, or self-identification — filed `associated_with`, per the four-way non-identity rule, and date-stamped to the 1991 study. See [[possehl-gregory-l]], [[harappan-civilization]], [[early-harappan]].

## Barker 2006 (*Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory*)

*(Attributed — [[barker-graeme|Graeme Barker]], *[[barker-agricultural-revolution-prehistory-2006|The Agricultural Revolution in Prehistory]]* 2006, Ch. 5; genetics **(as of Barker 2006)**; densifies Jarrige/Costantini/Meadow via Barker’s synthesis.)*

- **Sequence.** ~9 m aceramic Neolithic, four phases; top level with Jeitun-like pottery fifth millennium BC; third level calibrated eighth millennium BC; earliest level 4 to eighth and possibly **ninth** millennium BC — contemporary with PPNB beginnings in SW Asia (Jarrige via Barker). Complex multi-roomed unmoulded mud-brick storage structures from phase 1 (no doorways/domestic artefacts → communal storage); formal cemeteries with flexed burials and exotic grave-goods (lapis, turquoise from Iran; shell; textile traces) (attributed).
- **Plants (Costantini).** Naked six-row barley dominant (~90% basal plant remains); also einkorn, emmer, durum wheat, two- and six-row hulled barley, date; wild jujube and morphologically wild two-row barley; wild barley grows around site. Costantini argues **local barley domestication**; durum could hybridize with *Aegilops squarrosa*; dates native — “no reason” first crops had to be introduced domesticates from SW Asia (position as Barker reports).
- **Fauna (Meadow).** Early meat mostly game (gazelle, deer, wild ass, wild goat/sheep, boar, nilgai, wild cattle, water buffalo, elephant…); domestic dog, sheep, goats, cattle identified as humped zebu *Bos indicus*; morphological mix of domestic-sized and larger (wild) animals; hoof imprints of domestic sheep/goats. Later phases: game declines, zebu becomes major livestock. Cattle and sheep size diminish rapidly, goat size stable → Meadow: fully domestic goats from outset, cattle and sheep still being domesticated. Copper bead with cotton fibre c.5500 BC (Moulherat et al. 2002) earlier than usual Harappan cotton assumption (attributed).
- **Independent parallel domestication reading (Barker position).** Geometric microliths with silica gloss as sickles; polished axes/adzes, stone bowls, digging-stick weights. Architecture/cemeteries somewhat PPNB-like but farming systems and technologies differ significantly. Barker: at least as reasonable to see separate parallel domestication in this part of South Asia as to assume eastwards PPNB colonization/acculturation. Human dental morphologies imply distinct breeding population from SW Asia (Lukacs). mtDNA (Loftus et al. 1994; Hanotte et al. 2002 **as of Barker 2006**): separate Indian zebu domestication. Communal storage and elaborate ideologies suggest site unlikely to be absolute initial agricultural stage in Baluchistan (attributed). Four-way non-identity: site ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes. ## Kenoyer 1998

*(Attributed — [[kenoyer-jonathan-mark|Kenoyer]], *[[kenoyer-ancient-cities-indus-1998|Ancient Cities of the Indus Valley Civilization]]* 1998.)*

### Indus Valley Tradition anchor

Kenoyer places Mehrgarh at the base of the Bolan Pass on the Kachi Plain with [[nausharo]] and Pirak as the deep sequence for the **Early Food-producing Era** of the [[indus-valley-tradition]] (Periods IA–IIA ca. 6500–5000 BCE in his tables): domestic wheat and barley, cattle, sheep, goats; rectangular mud-brick houses with storage cubicles; bitumen-coated baskets; coarse chaff-tempered ceramics only at period’s end; shell and colorful-stone ornaments with axes and chert blades in graves. French Mission under [[jarrige-jean-francois|Jarrige]] excavated 1974–1986, resumed 1996–97 (as of Kenoyer 1998).

### Indigenous food production narrative

Until the 1970s, sparse South Asian early-domesticate evidence encouraged Near Eastern → Iran/Baluchistan diffusion models; post-1976 Mehrgarh excavations “changed this forever” toward indigenous food production on the Indus margin (Kenoyer). First settlers cultivated barley and domestic wheat; hunting then domestic sheep/goat; **local domestication of humped zebu** (*Bos indicus*); later water buffalo with wheat/barley as staples into Indus cities. Wild barley in earliest Neolithic levels. Kenoyer reconstructs seasonal highland–piedmont mobility becoming permanent reoccupation at the Bolan-river camp; no significant climate/rainfall change required; modern Kachi agro-pastoral migration after spring harvest offered as analogy (not identity).

### Architecture, burial, hierarchy, seals

- Period IA aceramic Neolithic (ca. 6500–6000 BCE): square/rectangular houses of hand-formed plano-convex mud bricks with finger impressions, four+ internal compartments — **hand-formed mud brick** long before Kot Diji mold-made 1:2:4 bricks and ~2600 BCE kiln-baked bricks (brick history Ch. 3).
- Neolithic burials (ca. 6500–4500 BCE) often with sacrificed animals, ornaments, tools (sometimes children more elaborately ornamented); Chalcolithic Period III (4500–3500 BCE) simple graves with few ornaments despite craft/trade — Kenoyer links this shift to keeping valuables in circulation for the living (continuity into sparse Indus urban mortuary wealth vs Mesopotamia).
- Large multi-room houses vs smaller structures (with Nausharo) as class hierarchy signals in Regionalization.
- Later Regionalization seals: clay, bone, ivory, rarely copper — mostly geometric; square steatite knobbed seals at Rehman Dheri and Harappa become common Indus status markers — independent invention of elite control systems relative to Mesopotamian cylinders (Kenoyer).

### Urban template and catalogue crafts

Kenoyer links (without asserting direct continuity) Indus city **cardinal grid planning** to compartmented Neolithic Mehrgarh house plans and Early Chalcolithic geometric seals as a **cultural template** for organizing space. Catalogue: Period VI female figurine ca. 3000 BCE (elaborate coiffure/appliqué ornaments); Kechi Beg polychrome jar Period IV ca. 3300 BCE; Faiz Mohammed Gray Ware with fish motifs **produced at Mehrgarh** and traded regionally in Period VII ca. 2800–2600 BCE (despite western Baluchistan type-site name).
