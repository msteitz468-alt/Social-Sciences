# Mehrgarh

Early farming settlement on the Kachi plain of Baluchistan (Pakistan). In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is the earliest known farming village of its type in southern Asia and a deep developmental sequence toward later Indus urbanism ([[harappan-civilization|Harappan]] / [[mohenjo-daro|Mohenjo-Daro]]).

**Identity caution:** Dental resemblance to indigenous Mesolithic South Asians is a biological observation, not proof of continuous ethnic identity; farming package ≠ migrant people. Culture ≠ genes ≠ language ≠ people.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: excavated by the French Mission working with the Pakistan Department of Archaeology under **Jean-François Jarrige**. **Lorenzo Constantini** identified domesticated wheat and (in larger quantities) barley from plant impressions in mud-brick chaff temper, plus gathered jujube and date—implying a wetter Kachi plain than today. **Christophe Moulherat** et al. identified cotton fibres stringing copper beads inside a corroded copper bead from a ~5500 BCE burial.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Jarrige et al. via Mithen 2003, ch.43.)*

1. **By ~7000 BCE:** farming village with more than a hundred inhabitants; multi-roomed rectangular mud-brick houses and storerooms—earliest known settlement of its type in southern Asia in Mithen’s framing.
2. **Crops:** domestic wheat and abundant barley; gathered jujube and date.
3. **Animals:** cattle and sheep bones become progressively smaller through layers while goat and gazelle sizes stay largely unchanged—interpreted as local domestication of humped cattle (zebu) and wild sheep; gazelle remains wild and declines.
4. **Exchange:** turquoise and lapis lazuli (likely Afghanistan); seashells from the Arabian shore ~500 km south; dentalium-like shells recalling Natufian exchange aesthetics without implying Natufian identity.
5. **Cemetery:** formal cemetery of ≥150 burials; underground tombs with chambers reused by shifting older bones—Mithen reads increasing importance of family ties.
6. **Dentition:** large teeth like indigenous Mesolithic South Asians; healthy, low caries, fluoride staining—appears to challenge pure immigrant-farmer ancestry from the west in favour of local people adopting farming means/ideas (position; phenotype ≠ population identity).
7. **~5500 BCE:** cotton fibres in copper bead—earliest known cotton use by ≥1,000 years (as of Mithen); cotton seeds with cereals in likely storage; wild vs domestic status uncertain, cultivation suspected (*G. arboreum* lineage). Cattle dominate economy (possibly ploughing/transport/milk); fine painted pottery appears.
8. **Later (to 4000–3500 BCE):** wheel-thrown coarse pottery, jasper bow-drills for beads, naturalistic figurines, stamp seals, copper smelting crucibles—foundation path toward Indus cities in Mithen’s long narrative.
9. **Wider frame:** West Asian barley/wheat/goat package reaches environmental limits east of the Indus where monsoon reverses seasonality; Jeitun and Iranian-plateau towns as related rim settlements; Zagros foothill expansion after 8000 BCE as first steps toward South/Central Asian farming.

## Significance

- Earliest substantial farming village of South Asia as of Mithen’s synthesis; dual local animal domestication (zebu, sheep) plus imported cereal package.
- Revises the antiquity of cotton use by more than a millennium relative to classic Indus assumptions.
- Key node linking SW Asian Neolithic expansion to the Indus urban trajectory without reducing Mehrgarh to a passive colony.
- Dental evidence as a caution against simple demic-diffusion stories.

## Key Debates

- **Immigrant farmers vs local adoption** of the farming package—dental evidence challenges pure western immigrant ancestry (as of Mithen 2003); do not equate with modern genetic results without new sources.
- Local vs introduced status of early cattle/sheep vs goats.
- Wild vs cultivated cotton at 5500 BCE.
- How continuous the path is from Mehrgarh seals/copper to Harappan cities (long developmental claim vs punctuated change).
