# Huaca Prieta

Preceramic midden mound at the mouth of the Chicama Valley, north coast Peru — the excavation that first established preceramic sedentary coastal occupation in the Andes.

## Discovery and Excavation History

[[bird-junius|Junius Bird]] excavated Huaca Prieta and reported briefly in 1948, noting other prepottery middens in the region. Per [[moseley-maritime-foundations-1975|Moseley (1975)]], Bird's work established a preceramic, sedentary coastal occupation predating pottery — though contemporaries still tended to read such sites as early farming villages despite abundant seafood remains. Bird's 1963 study of the textile assemblage remains (as of Moseley 1975) the only preceramic fabric collection given detailed microscopic reconstruction of faded color patterning, with warp/weft positions plotted on graph paper.

## Occupation Sequence

Summary resolution as of Moseley 1975 (fuller stratigraphy in Bird's reports, not yet ingested):

- Deep stratified midden; subterranean dwellings comparable to those at [[aspero]] and Culebras 1; elaborate residential terracing with masonry foresets (contrasted with simpler terracing at Aspero and Punta Grande).
- Large masonry summit walls built by group labor. **Position recording:** Bird (1948) interpreted them as stabilizing the summit for use as a living area; Moseley (1975) reads them as defensive — "the top of the mound has a distinctly fortified appearance" — linking them to inferred preceramic inter-community conflict. Unresolved.
- Moseley's population estimate (Table 5.1, his own figures, flagged conservative): 150–350.
- Sea-urchin collecting may have been important here and at some other northern sites, though rare at most early coastal settlements (Moseley 1975, citing Wendt 1964; Engel 1963).

## Significance

- Anchor site of the coastal-preceramic revolution in Andean archaeology and a genealogical foundation of the [[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]].
- **Textile and gourd art:** of >10,000 gourd specimens, 13 bore incised/pyroengraved designs; reconstructed textile motifs include bicephalic avian figures with serpentine bodies, a serpent-crab composite, and interlocking avian design bands (Bird 1963, as reported by Moseley 1975). Bird (1963:265) argued the angular, conventionalized style is a product of the twining medium's structural constraints, not a stage in any "natural-to-geometric" evolutionary sequence (attributed).

## Key Debates

- Summit walls: residential stabilization (Bird 1948) vs. fortification (Moseley 1975) — see above.
- Interpretive frame: farming village (1940s–50s readings) vs. maritime settlement ([[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]]).

## Historiography

This page rests on Moseley's 1975 secondary reporting of Bird's fieldwork; Bird's primary reports (1948; 1963) and the modern Dillehay re-excavations are not yet ingested — dates and finds are as of Moseley 1975.
