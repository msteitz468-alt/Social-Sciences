# Ohalo II

Last Glacial Maximum hunter-gatherer campsite on the western shore of Lake Tiberias (Sea of Galilee), Israel. As presented in [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]], it is among the best-preserved LGM settlements known, with brushwood dwellings near oak woodland and a plant/animal assemblage of exceptional diversity for the period. The wiki slug follows the conventional archaeological designation **Ohalo II**.

**Identity caution:** “Ohalo people” denotes site occupants in archaeological prose, not an ethnonym or genetic population.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: brushwood huts burned (cause unknown); rising lake levels then gently flooded the site and protected it from decay. A 1989 drought exposed charcoal hut rings; **Dani Nadel** (Haifa University) began meticulous excavation of plant and animal remains described as astonishing for the LGM, with further work when water levels fell again in 1999. Waterlogged/submerged preservation is a reliability fact—organic survival is exceptional and not representative of typical LGM sites.

Lubbock scenes of camp life in *After the Ice* are narrative reconstructions, not field observations.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Mithen 2003, ch.2–3.)*

- **LGM camp** on the western shore of Lake Tiberias: brushwood dwellings; forest-steppe setting far from ice-sheet tundra; plant diversity including wild relatives of later domesticates (wheat, barley, peas, lentils, flax) in the wider east-of-Galilee landscape Mithen treats as historically critical.
- **Economy:** wild cereal harvesting by beating grain into baskets (precise timing required; ethnographic analogy to recent North American wild-grass collection is flagged as inference method); gazelle and other game (fallow deer, wild ass, wild goat, aurochs, hartebeest, wild boar, small game) plus fish among the bones.
- **Abandonment after fire:** Mithen uses the burned camp to illustrate forager mobility as risk management—people could leave because they lacked farmers’ sunk investment in land, pens, stores, and crops (interpretive contrast, not ethnographic observation of Ohalo decisions).
- Coastal Mediterranean foraging at the LGM remains largely speculative in Mithen’s account because post-glacial sea-level rise flooded coastlines before excavation—sampling bias for coastal contemporaries of Ohalo.

## Significance

- Best-preserved LGM open-air settlement in the southern Levant as of Mithen’s synthesis—anchors reconstructions of pre-Natufian forager diet, seasonality, and technology.
- Demonstrates deep pre-agricultural plant know-how in the forest-steppe cradle of later SW Asian domesticates without implying that Ohalo occupants “were becoming farmers.”
- Method case for lake-level exposure archaeology and organic preservation bias.
- Baseline contrast for later [[natufian-culture|Natufian]] villages (e.g. larger-scale plant processing at [[eynan-ain-mallaha|‘Ain Mallaha]]).

## Key Debates

- How far LGM wild-cereal use anticipates later cultivation vs remaining opportunistic broad-spectrum foraging (Mithen keeps Ohalo contemporaries on the wild-harvest side of the domestication threshold).
- Seasonality and residential mobility of the camp (year-round vs seasonal—not settled as a single verdict in the 2003 synthesis).
- Representativeness: exceptional preservation may overstate LGM dietary breadth relative to open dry sites.
