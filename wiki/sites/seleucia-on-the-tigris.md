# Seleucia-on-the-Tigris

Founded by Seleucus I Nikator sometime after 305/4 BCE as the new administrative and
commercial center of Seleucid Mesopotamia, replacing [[babylon]] in that role while
Babylon itself continued as a functioning cult city (see [[babylon]] for the
continuity-not-decline correction to Strabo's "neglected and thrown into ruins"
characterization). Cuneiform sources distinguish the city by the epithet **al
sarruti**, "city of kingship" (Sherwin-White 1983:269, cited in
[[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]). Potts states Seleucia was
comparable in the Seleucid east only to Alexandria and Antioch (per Strabo).

## Discovery and Excavation History

American excavations were conducted by the University of Michigan in 1927-1936;
Italian (Turin) excavations resumed from 1963. The Italian-excavated "Archive
Building" was destroyed by fire, possibly during Avidius Cassius's Roman incursion of
AD 165/6, and yielded roughly **25,000 clay bullae** (sealings) — an exceptional
administrative archive by volume, though almost entirely Greek in epigraphic character
(finding: [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]).

## Occupation Sequence

### Foundation and scale

Ancient population estimates for the city range widely by source and period: Pliny
records **600,000** shortly after founding; Orosius records **400,000** near the end
of Antiochus IV's reign (c. 165 BCE) (Oelsner 1986:400 n.539, cited by Potts). The city
had a rectilinear Greek street plan, agorai, and a theatre — the standard apparatus of
a Hellenistic *polis* (attributed, Potts 1997).

### The Archive Building bullae and the fiscal-administrative record

Of the roughly 25,000 clay bullae recovered from the fire-destroyed Archive Building,
most bear epigraphs relating to a **salt tax** (Greek *halike*), alongside a general
sales tax (*eponion*), a slave-sale tax (*andrapodikon*), and a canal tax (*ploion
Euphratou*) (McEwan 1988:417, cited by Potts). Strikingly, only **one fragmentary
cuneiform tablet and one cuneiform-legend bulla** were found among the ~25,000 largely
Greek-style seal impressions — Potts reads this as indicating administrative and
fiscal **Greek dominance** at the documentary level even though the broader population
of Seleucid Mesopotamia remained substantially Babylonian (attributed synthesis,
[[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]). G.J.P. McEwan (1988) argued from
this Greek-epigraphic dominance that Uruk (which shows comparable Greek-language
administrative bullae without Greek settlement — see [[uruk]]) became a Greek *polis*;
Potts calls this claim, extended by analogy from Seleucia's administrative record,
"insufficiently evidenced" — Sherwin-White (1982:54) argues the Greek-language
inscriptions on such bullae reflect only the language of royal administration, not the
ethnic composition of the population (position, cited in Potts 1997; see
[[uruk]] for the fuller version of this debate).

### Elephants and the Seleucid military establishment

Potts documents Seleucia and the wider Seleucid Mesopotamian corridor as a hub for the
Seleucid war-elephant corps: Seleucus I received 500 war elephants from the Mauryan
king Chandragupta (c. 305-285 BCE) in exchange for peace; elephants were stabled at
Apamaea in Syria and transported overland via Bactria and the "Great Khorassan Road."
A Bactrian governor sent 20 elephants to Babylonia's satrap on 26 March 273 BCE for
Antiochus I's use against the Ptolemies in the First Syrian War (van der Spek
1993:97, cited by Potts). The entire Seleucid elephant herd was destroyed by Rome at
the Battle of Apamea in 161 BCE (finding, [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts
1997]]; see [[mesopotamian-trade]] and [[meluhha]] for the ivory-import background that
predates and parallels the elephant trade).

### The 274 BCE population transfer from Babylon

Potts records a partial forced or induced transfer of Babylon's population to
Seleucia in 274 BCE, roughly contemporary with a renovation of Babylon's Marduk
temple/Bit Akitu; he cites this renovation, alongside continued cuneiform textual
production, continued royal offerings at Babylon, and a citizen assembly attested
in astronomical diaries through at least 169 BCE, as evidence that Babylon was not
simply abandoned in Seleucia's favor, contra Pausanias's claim that "only priests
were left in Babylon" (position, [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]];
see [[babylon]]).

### Pre-Alexander Greek-Mesopotamian contact (background)

Potts situates Seleucia's foundation against several centuries of prior
Greek-Mesopotamian contact predating Alexander's 331 BCE entry into Babylon:
Mesopotamian cylinder seals found on Crete, Mitanni Common Style and Kassite seals
from Thebes (Boeotia), Neo-Assyrian conquests reaching Cyprus, Greek mercenaries
serving Achaemenid and rival rulers from 547 BCE (including Xenophon's Ten Thousand at
Cunaxa), and ethnic Greeks resident in Babylonia by the Neo-Babylonian period. A
terracotta sarcophagus excavated by Leonard Woolley at [[ur]], beneath an
"Achaemenid"-period house, contained roughly 200 clay sealing impressions of Greek,
Egyptian, Babylonian, Assyrian, and Persian origin, plus a dozen Greek coin
impressions, dated by an Athenian coin (c. 450-440 BCE); E. Porada interpreted this as
a **study collection** assembled by a non-Greek seal-cutter producing pseudo-Greek
work, evidence of Greek glyptic influence in Mesopotamia well before Alexander
(Porada 1960:228-34, cited by Potts; see [[ur]]).

## Key Debates

### The Hannestad-Keall debate on Hellenistic ceramics as evidence of colonization

Potts presents a live scholarly dispute over how to interpret the appearance of
Hellenistic ceramic forms (fish-plates, amphorae, kantharoi, unguentaria) across
Seleucid Mesopotamia and Bactria, directly relevant to how Seleucia's own material
culture should be read:

- **Position 1 (L. Hannestad, 1983):** pottery reflects Greek settlement patterns
  directly — "a strong Greek influence on the pottery of a given site or area reflects
  a correspondingly strong Greek colonization" — with a "radical change" occurring at
  the start of the Seleucid period rather than under the Achaemenids. E. Strommenger
  (1967:33) similarly noted a rupture in Uruk's ceramic tradition between
  Neo-Babylonian/Achaemenid and Hellenistic/Parthian periods.
- **Position 2 (E.J. Keall, publishing Parthian Nippur):** eschews Hellenocentric terms
  like "amphora"; argues the persisting shapes/sizes reflect "a continuum of centuries
  of pot making in an 'indigenous mode'" more than imported fashion, and that Greek
  influence at Nippur was "much less significant than... continuity with the earlier,
  long-standing tradition."

Potts's own synthesis (attributed, his interpretive judgment) sides against
Hannestad's colonist-settlement explanation as poorly supported by the cuneiform
documentation on ethnic Greeks, reading the ceramic changes instead as adoption of new
"Hellenistic tastes, of the palate" (foodways) rather than demographic replacement.

### "Orientalization of the Occidentals" — Potts's programmatic thesis

Potts closes his treatment of the Seleucid period arguing that the 19th-century
concept of "Hellenism/Hellenization" (attributed to German historian J. Droysen)
reflected "the colonizing spirit of early modern Europe" (citing Briant 1990:40) and
is, "in a post-colonial world," a "hollow claim." He argues the dominant pattern in
southern Mesopotamia under Seleucid rule was "thoroughgoing continuity of Asiatic
social, religious, economic and political forms," concluding that evidence such as
Antiochus I's traditionally-styled Babylonian temple dedications and the full
integration of Greek-named individuals into Babylonian legal life at Uruk (see
[[uruk]]) attest to "the Orientalization of the Occidentals, rather than the
Hellenization of the Orientals" (Potts's own closing programmatic thesis,
[[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]]).

## Significance

Seleucia anchors the wiki's evidence for what Seleucid rule actually did to southern Mesopotamia: a new Greek-planned royal capital whose fiscal administration ran in Greek, set beside near-total continuity of Babylonian religious, legal, and scribal life at [[babylon]] and [[uruk]]. It is the test case for the Hellenization-vs-continuity question (Hannestad–Keall debate above) and the material anchor of Potts's "Orientalization of the Occidentals" thesis.

## Historiography

Seleucia's evidentiary record is lopsided by design of preservation and excavation
history: the ~25,000-bulla Archive Building gives an exceptionally dense fiscal record
but is almost entirely Greek in script, so conclusions about the city's *administrative*
language dominance should not be silently extended to claims about its *demographic*
composition — a distinction Potts insists on and that this page preserves (see the
Hannestad-Keall debate above). Source: [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts,
*Mesopotamian Civilization*, 1997]].
