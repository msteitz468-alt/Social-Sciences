# Assur

Assur (Ash-shur), the cult-city of the god Ashur, sits on a defensible hilltop above the Tigris at the entrance to the Fatha gorge (Jabal Hamrin), protected by both river and canal, controlling the Tigris-valley route to Kurdistan and the Upper Jazirah. Per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]], the city was occupied since the Early Dynastic period or earlier and later became the eponymous seat of Assyrian kingship, though Roux stresses "no evidence that Assur was independent before the second millennium B.C."

## Discovery and Excavation History

The site's archaic Ishtar temple is described by Roux as artistically "mediocre" relative to comparable Early Dynastic sculpture elsewhere (see [[mari]]). The Assyrian King List, found at Khorsabad and published by A. Poebel in 1942, is a key textual source for the city's early rulers; no excavator or excavation-year detail is given in the source material used for this page.

## Occupation Sequence

Assur was occupied successively by Sargon, Narâm-Sin, and Ur III kings during the Early Dynastic through Ur III periods, functioning as a provincial center rather than an independent polity. The Assyrian King List names 17 early kings of Assur, nominally placed in Early Dynastic times and described as having "lived in tents." Roux interprets this cautiously: the list may represent parallel, not successive, dynasties — comparable to a similar problem in the Sumerian King List — and "lived in tents" may indicate these figures governed a tribal group rather than the city itself. Several early royal names (Tudia, Ushpia, Sulili, Kikkia) are neither Semitic nor Sumerian and are tentatively identified by Roux as possibly Hurrian; this ethnolinguistic attribution is Roux's own inference from onomastics, not a certain identification, and is itself an instance of the four-way non-identity caution — a name's linguistic affiliation does not establish the bearer's ethnicity, ancestry, or self-identification.

After the fall of [[ur|Ur III]], Assur became independent under Puzur-Ashur I (c. 2000 BCE), opening a dynasty that used Akkadian royal names echoing Sargon and Narâm-Sin. Ilushuma and Erishum I left inscriptions recording temple-building for Ashur, Adad, and Ishtar; Ilushuma raided southern Iraq during the reign of Ishme-Dagan of Isin (1953–1935 BCE).

## Significance

Roux's own interpretive claim (attributed, not settled fact): the true architects of later Assyrian power were Western Semites (Amorites) who flooded into northern Iraq in the early 2nd millennium BCE, traced by Roux to Halê, an Amorite tribal chief who settled between the Khabur and the Tigris — whose descendants, in his account, built northern Mesopotamia, including Assyria, into "a large, prosperous and powerful kingdom in which the true Assyrians played a very small role." This is a strong causal/interpretive claim about the ethnic origin of the ruling dynasty and should be read as such — another instance of the wiki's four-way non-identity caution applying directly: an "Assyrian" polity that, on Roux's own framing, was substantially built by non-"Assyrian" (Amorite) rulers.

Samsi-Addu, whose sons Ishme-Dagan (at Ekallâtum) and Iasmah-Addu (at [[mari]]) governed on his behalf, is listed on the Assyrian King List despite being, per Roux (quoting an unnamed Danish Assyriologist), "in fact a usurper, later rejected by the Assyrian tradition." Roux argues the polity Samsi-Addu built should properly be called the "Kingdom of Upper Mesopotamia" rather than "Assyria" or a "First Assyrian Empire," since it "did not originate in, and was not built upon the men and the power of the city-state Assur" — a naming and classification dispute treated here as a position, not a resolved fact.

## Key Debates

- The ethnic/linguistic identity of Assur's earliest kings (possible Hurrian names) versus later Amorite-descended dynasts — an explicit case of the four-way non-identity problem.
- The classification dispute over whether Samsi-Addu's realm constitutes a "First Assyrian Empire" or should instead be termed the "Kingdom of Upper Mesopotamia" (see [[mari]] for the Iasmah-Addu side of this history).

Assur was later destroyed in 612 BCE; see [[assyrians]] and [[chaldaeans]] for the fuller narrative of Assyria's fall, which lies outside the scope of this range's source material.

## Historiography

The Assyrian King List's early section shares the Sumerian King List's structural problem of potentially conflating parallel dynasties into a single linear sequence — a source-critical caution Roux applies explicitly. Attribution of Assur's earliest royal names to Hurrian, rather than Semitic or Sumerian, origin rests on onomastic inference alone and should be flagged as tentative wherever cited.
