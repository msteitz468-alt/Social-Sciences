# Barkaer

Early Neolithic monumental site in Jutland, Denmark. In *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998, Ch. 1), [[bradley-richard|Richard Bradley]] uses Barkaer as the opening **paradigm case** for a wider European problem: features long classified as enormous **longhouses** re-read, in the modern excavation report he cites (Liversage 1992), as **massive funerary monuments** built at the beginning of the local Neolithic.

## Discovery and Excavation History

For nearly thirty years (1947–1975), Barkaer was taken to hold two of the largest Neolithic houses in Europe (attributed, Bradley after the Glob tradition). Excavation began 1931; main campaign 1947–49 (~3,000 m²); Glob expected a well-preserved Neolithic settlement. Final publication only with Liversage 1992 after Glob’s death — a delay Bradley uses to show how field preconceptions and changing intellectual climates shape observation.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed via Bradley 1998 / Liversage 1992 line.)*

Two enormous rectangular structures side by side, each just under 90 m long × ~7 m wide, defined by stone "walls," internal partitions (coloured sand, stakeholes), outer postholes; laid out on the same alignment facing a "village street" on raised ground near a lake. Each appeared divided into nearly thirty compartments (~6 × 3 m), often without doorways between screens. Anomalous features for houses: stone cist toward the east end of one structure (pots, amber bead; no bones in acid soil); further features with upright posts, complete pots, amber, copper; massive post rows at eastern ends with decorated vessels. Wider North Sea Early Neolithic mortuary evidence (post-settings; long mounds with bay divisions by uprights) made the "longhouses" reinterpretable as two mounds.

Glob eventually accepted rectangular mounds but held that underlying postholes were wooden houses of the same dimensions — "houses of the living… converted directly into the houses of the dead." Bradley/Liversage-line sequence: massive funerary monuments built over **ephemeral** settlement traces; sparse pre-mound artefact densities; postholes partly clustered where excavators sought house walls.

**Agriculture context (attributed):** Korup Lake pollen was undated by Iversen; later radiocarbon outline spreads clearances ~600 years; early human impact limited; plantain + cereal phase later and still ~90% tree pollen; episode dated *after* the Barkaer barrows — occupants need not have grown cereals; Liversage notes subsoil unsuited to cultivation.

## Significance

Anchors Bradley’s thesis that **[[monumentality]]** is among the earliest clear Neolithic signals in parts of north-west Europe, complicating models in which farming surplus precedes ceremonial investment. Events do not follow the expected sequence: monument + early domesticates before intensive land use. Links: [[long-mound]], [[death-of-the-house]], [[neolithic]], [[agricultural-revolution]].

## Key Debates

House vs tomb classification (Glob conversion reading vs Liversage/Bradley funerary-first); how far Barkaer generalizes beyond Jutland; chronological relation to earliest local domesticates (update with post-1998 14C as ingested).
