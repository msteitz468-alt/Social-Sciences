## Discovery and Excavation History

Huaca de los Reyes is a monumental complex in the middle Moche Valley, on the north coast of Peru, and is part of the Caballo Muerto Complex — eight mound complexes distributed across 3.6 sq km. Construction began before roughly 1800 BCE and the site remained in use for approximately the following nine centuries. Three radiocarbon dates from Mound F are reported by Thomas Pozorski, the site's excavator, as 3680 ± 80 BP, 3310 ± 80 BP, and 3140 ± 60 BP (Pozorski 1975:248); the calibration status is unstated in Moore 2005 (raw BP figures with stated ± error are given, without a calibration statement), and should be treated as calibration-unspecified pending verification.

Huaca de los Reyes belongs to the Cupisnique/Initial Period Formative tradition of the Moche Valley, predating the later Moche state by roughly two millennia, and should not be conflated with [[moche-culture]].

## Occupation Sequence

The complex is organized along a single east-west axis with progressive restriction of access, per Moore's architectural analysis: large open plazas (Plazas I and II), capable of holding great numbers of people, give way to a smaller, more elaborately and individually decorated inner plaza (Plaza III), and finally to an innermost mound-top shrine containing a single modeled clay head (possibly feline), visible to only a select few. The site's figural friezes — anthropomorphic bipedal figures and feline/jaguar motifs — face outward and eastward, designed to be experienced by people moving through the site rather than viewed from within enclosed rooms.

Pozorski (1980:101) also documents strong bilateral symmetry and mathematical/proportional design at the site: "Fractional relationships of 1/4, 1/5, 1/6 and 1/12" recur in the architectural spacing — for example, "Plaza III is 1/5 the size of Plaza II, which is 1/3 the size of Plaza I" — with columns arrayed in multiples of 4, 6, 8, and 12.

Per Moore's comparative Table 3.3, Huaca de los Reyes is grouped (with Purulén and Montegrande) among Formative sites where ceremonial architecture is permanent and unique at the regional/interregional level, incorporating public-distant visibility: ritual practice is classified as axial movement; permanence as multigenerational; ubiquity as regional; visibility as personal/public-distant. It is cited (with Purulén, Las Aldas, and Sechín Alto) as a site where processional movement involved ascent across three or more planes via progressively restricted spaces, with staircases steep enough to preclude litter-carried nobles — "individuals accomplished the final ascent of the procession on foot."

## Significance

Thomas Pozorski (1980:101–104) estimated the total labor investment at approximately 350,000 person-days. Assuming a single architect's coherent vision, he argued this implied construction within under 25 years, achievable by a workforce of 240 people laboring roughly two months per year during agricultural off-seasons, concluding that "organizational efforts need not have been as substantial or as rigid as might be supposed" (Pozorski 1980:104).

T. Pozorski and S. Pozorski (1994:64), comparing Huaca de los Reyes to Pampa de las Llamas-Moxeke, argue that "the function of Huaca de los Reyes was fundamentally religious": its large, accessible plazas were designed to be "inspirational to large groups of commoners," while its smaller, less accessible, more individualized plazas served "as areas of meditation or worship for more select groups" possessing specialized esoteric knowledge. Moore notes this as an internal nuance worth preserving: the Pozorskis themselves concede a religious/canonist-type reading of this specific site even while elsewhere (at Pampa de las Llamas-Moxeke) arguing for secular/political centralization.

Moore's own synthesis reads Huaca de los Reyes as expressing "a canon of ritual knowledge in which felines and anthropomorphs were stylized and well defined," with a clear separation between an inner group holding direct cult access and a larger outer public — consistent with his canonist model of religious authority (see [[formative-stage]]). Moore interprets the site's architectural emphasis on axial movement and procession as evidence for canonist, rather than ecstatic-shamanic, religious authority: "it seems much more probable that the major ceremonial constructions of the Formative reflect the continuities and institutions of ritual authorities employing a canon of sacred knowledge." Moore also notes the apparent absence of a large resident population at the site may indicate it was symbolically separated from everyday life.

## Key Debates

**Conklin versus Pozorski on construction sequence.** William Conklin (1985) argued Huaca de los Reyes was built cumulatively across at least eight distinct construction phases. Thomas Pozorski, the site's excavator, argues instead — based on detailed excavation data — that "all eight of Conklin's architectural construction phases (save for a portion of Phase 8) are in fact contemporaneous with one another and were built as a single, integrated, planned unit" (Pozorski 1995:338). Moore does not adjudicate between these positions.

Huaca de los Reyes also serves as a key case in the wider Pozorski/Burger–Salazar-Burger dispute over whether large-scale Initial Period monumental architecture on the Peruvian coast signals state-level political centralization or reflects cumulative construction by nonhierarchical, religiously motivated communities; see [[formative-ceremonial-architecture-debate]].
