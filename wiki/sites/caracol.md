# Caracol

Large [[classic-maya]] capital on the Vaca Plateau of western Belize. As of [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]], Caracol was a key player in the conflicts that led to [[tikal]]’s Early Classic political downfall and later a Late Classic mega-site with an extensive causeway network and intensive terraced agriculture.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Sharer & Traxler (2006) synthesize Caracol primarily from epigraphy and settlement archaeology rather than a single discovery narrative. As of their account, the city occupies the Vaca Plateau at about 500 m elevation; intensive mapping and excavation (notably work by Arlen and Diane Chase, as cited in the polity-size literature they summarize) document a vast Late Classic urban footprint, sacbe system, and agricultural landscape. Monumental texts—especially Altar 21 of Kan II (AD 633)—carry much of the reconstructed Early Classic political history. Settlement-density and terrace surveys underpin population and polity-size estimates used in comparative lowland urbanism.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]] unless noted.)*

- **Late Preclassic foundation:** Site origins are relatively obscure in the texts; Sharer & Traxler report Late Preclassic foundation and note Late Preclassic E Group architecture in the El Mirador Basin package also attested at Caracol (alongside [[el-mirador]], Wakna, Uaxactun, and [[tikal]]). Early Long Count notations at Caracol use horizontal bar-and-dot numbers comparable to southern-area practice.
- **Early Classic dynasty and Tikal subordination:** Te’ K’ab’ Chaak is associated with dates 331 and 349. Yajaw Te’ K’inich I accession 484; Kan I accession 531 under an unidentified overlord (Sharer & Traxler judge likely [[tikal]]); Stela 16 (534) lists ancestors plus Copan’s Balam Nehn. Yajaw Te’ K’inich II accession 553 under Tikal’s Wak Chan K’awiil—Caracol was ally/subordinate of the Mutul polity; none of his own monuments acknowledge this dependence. Altar 21 (his son Kan II, 633) records the later shift from Tikal alliance to [[calakmul]] alliance.
- **War with Tikal and the 562 star war:** In 556, Tikal under Wak Chan K’awiil defeated a Caracol lord. In 562 Tikal suffered a star-war conquest recorded on Caracol Altar 21. Simon Martin (as cited by Sharer & Traxler) argues the victor-name outlines match Calakmul’s Sky Witness rather than Yajaw Te’ K’inich II—so Calakmul orchestrated the conquest with Caracol assistance; Wak Chan K’awiil likely captured and sacrificed, disappearing after 562. Sharer & Traxler argue Calakmul’s Early Classic strategy of surrounding Mutul turned critically on flipping Caracol from Tikal ally to Calakmul ally.
- **Late Classic growth under Kan II and Calakmul ties:** Dramatic Late Classic urban expansion: Sharer & Traxler report a city of roughly 28–50 km²; population estimates 30–60k for the site proper and >100k for the polity (Tikal-scale). Seven *sacbeob* radiate from the Caana Complex (longest ~8 km to Cahal Pichik); terraced agriculture and reservoirs supported density; “middle class” tomb wealth appears among nonelite burials. Causeway-network extent is estimated at ~5,500 km² polity scale (comparable to Late Preclassic [[el-mirador]] causeway estimates). Caracol, with Dos Pilas and Naranjo, acknowledged [[calakmul]] overlords in inscriptions. Yuknoom Chan of Calakmul is known only from a single Caracol reference (619, overseeing an event with Kan II); Tajoom Uk’ab’ K’ak’s Calakmul accession (622) is also recorded at Caracol. In 626 Kan II launched two attacks into [[naranjo]] territory without immediate Calakmul response; Calakmul’s Yuknoom Head then conquered Naranjo in 631 to stabilize the alliance.
- **680 star-war defeat and century-long hiatus:** In 680 Naranjo attacked and defeated Caracol in a star war, forcing king K’ak’ Ujol K’inich II (658–ca. 680) to flee. Thereafter Caracol entered a major monument hiatus lasting over a century. Sharer & Traxler link the 680–798 quietude to monument destruction, construction slowdown, Tikal’s resurgence, and the Calakmul alliance’s downfall.
- **Terminal Classic revival then abandonment:** Revival under K’inich Joy K’awiil: 798 new B Group ball court; Stela 11 for 800; Altar 23 (800) shows Ucanal and B’ital rulers as bound captives. Successor K’inich Toob’il Yopaat inaugurated ~804; Stela 18 for 810; altars record alliance with former foe Ucanal; further Caana construction. Stela 17 (849) depicts Kan III and another lord seated as apparent equals—illustrating reliance on subordinates. Sharer & Traxler report Caracol maintained most of its population and thrived until the end of the Terminal Classic, then was rapidly abandoned ca. 1000–1050. Terminal Classic Seibal’s refounding (830) involved an overlord from Ucanal in confederation with Caracol.

## Significance

Caracol is central to the Early Classic “Great Game” between [[tikal]] and [[calakmul]]: as of Sharer & Traxler 2006, flipping Caracol into the Kan alliance enabled the 562 conquest of Tikal and a century of Mutul eclipse. As urban form, it exemplifies strong-state-scale lowland capital organization—sacbe realm mapping, terraced intensification (≥40,000 ha with Xunantunich/Maya Mountains systems), and nonelite mortuary wealth. It also illustrates that settlement size and military power need not move together in lockstep across the lowlands, and that some heartland centers briefly adapted through the Terminal Classic before final abandonment. Royal tombs with apotheosis-related ritual (cinnabar, reentry) are cited among Caracol, Palenque, Tikal, and Copan.

## Key Debates

- **Authorship of the 562 victory:** Whether Caracol’s own king or Calakmul’s Sky Witness (Martin’s reading of Altar 21 name outlines, as reported by Sharer & Traxler) was the primary victor—and thus how far the “Caracol–Calakmul alliance” model should be weighted.
- **Polity size and military radius:** Chase & Chase’s ~60 km control-radius model (~11,300 km² max) and causeway-based estimates (~5,500 km² for Caracol) vs denser central-lowland averages; size ≠ power comparisons with smaller Dos Pilas and Yaxchilan.
- **Weak-state vs strong-state analogies:** Sharer & Traxler place Calakmul, Tikal, and Copan among the best Classic strong-state examples; Caracol’s sacbe network, agricultural intensification, and overlord acknowledgments place it in that comparative conversation without equating size with centralized capacity.
- **Collapse trajectory:** Survival as a Terminal Classic enclave then rapid 11th-century abandonment vs drought, trade realignment, and warfare accounts applied unevenly across the southern lowlands.
- **Cyclical k’atun prophecy models** (Puleston; Rice): chronological coincidence of K’atun 11 Ajaw 534 with the run-up to Tikal’s defeat by the Calakmul–Caracol alliance is noted as a supporting coincidence, not a demonstrated mechanism.

## Historiography

Caracol’s modern scholarly profile is dual: epigraphic (Altar 21 and the Martin–Grube hierarchical reconstruction of Calakmul–Tikal politics, as synthesized by Sharer & Traxler) and settlement-archaeological (Chase & Chase urban and agricultural mapping that underwrites polity-size and “middle class” arguments). As of Sharer & Traxler 2006, Early Classic political history is disproportionately text-driven and retrospective (Kan II’s Altar 21 recounts the Tikal–Calakmul shift), while Late Classic scale is archaeological. Multiple/family burials and nonelite tomb wealth at Caracol figure in comparative mortuary discussion. Claims about population (>100k polity), causeway polity extent, and abandonment ca. 1000–1050 are source-attributed estimates and should not be promoted to wiki voice without independent convergence. Primary synthesis source for this page: [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006]].

## Densification from Martin & Grube 2008

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube 2008]]; [[martin-simon|Martin]] & [[grube-nikolai|Grube]]. Complements Sharer & Traxler densification above.)*

### Client of Tikal, flip to Snake, and the 562 star war

Martin & Grube state that Caracol was a key player in the diplomatic and military manoeuvring of the Early–Late Classic transition; a mysterious setback produced a building/monument hiatus spanning most of the 8th century, after which the city revived as one of the more vibrant Terminal Classic centres. Ancient name **Uxwitza'** (“Three Hill Water”). First royal name Te' K'ab Chaak appears retrospectively (AD 331, 349) as possible dynastic progenitor. History firms with Yajaw Te' K'inich I (accession 484) and K'an I (531); K'an I’s accession is “supervised” by an unclear higher authority (divine being or regional [[overkingship]]).

With **Yajaw Te' K'inich II** (accession 553), they give a clear Early Classic geography: Caracol moves from [[tikal]]’s orbit to that of the Snake kingdom. Altar 21 (commissioned by son K'an II in 633) reveals that Yajaw’s inauguration was supervised by Tikal’s Wak Chan K'awiil — subordination suppressed on Yajaw’s own Stela 14 and only later narrated. Relations collapse within three years: Tikal “axes” its erstwhile client (April 556); in April 562 Wak Chan K'awiil falls victim to a star war. On the victor’s name, Martin & Grube follow the Chase observation that surviving outlines do not match Yajaw Te' K'inich but fit **Sky Witness** of the Snake; Caracol ascribes star-war triumphs not to itself while noting major roles, and thereafter falls under Snake regional hegemony (Scroll Serpent still supervises Caracol events before 583). Century-long prosperity after the flip sets the minor capital on the road to metropolis.

### K'an II vs Naranjo; hiatus 680–798; Terminal revival

**K'an II** (618–658) is cast as perhaps Caracol’s most successful king: settlement and sacbe expansion; wealth spreading beyond the leading elite. Snake diplomatic roster includes Yuknoom Ti' Chan (619), Tajoom Uk'ab K'ahk' (622–630), and Yuknoom Head’s conquests (631, 636). Combined campaign against [[naranjo]]: K'an II attacks near Naranjo (626–627); star war on Naranjo December 631 (Snake seat then firmly at [[calakmul]]); hieroglyphic stairway long seen as Caracol occupation monument at Naranjo — Martin & Grube note it may originally have stood at Caracol and been carried as trophy in a later reverse. Overlap accession of K'ahk' Ujol K'inich II (22 June 658) while K'an II still lived (29-day co-reign).

In February 680, during K'ahk' Ujol K'inich’s 22nd year, Uxwitza' suffers a star-war assault by Naranjo; “60 days later” the king “arrives” back — two months of exile. This is the last clear record of him: Martin & Grube define **Caracol’s Hiatus AD 680–798** (118 years) as political crisis with halt in major construction; traditional enemies Tikal and Naranjo flourish while ally Calakmul declines. Only Stela 21 (702) and shadowy Ruler VII / Tum Yohl K'inich interrupt the silence.

**Terminal revival** under K'inich Joy K'awiil (B-Group Ballcourt; accession 799; earliest date 798 tracking back 467 years to Te' K'ab Chaak; Stela 11 and Altar 23 of 800 with Ucanal and Bital captives). Successor K'inich Toobil Yopaat (c. 810–830+) allies ceremonially and militarily with Ucanal’s Papamalil — among the last Classic war records — and likely sets the plain 7 Ajaw altar for 10.0.0.0.0 (830). K'an III (Stela 17 / Altar 10, 849) places monuments in outlying groups and shares prominence with subordinates — autocratic kingship adapting, in their reading, to magnate power. Last king Ruler XIII leaves all-glyphic Stela 10 (~859); no further elite activity.

## Densification from Schele & Freidel 1990

*(Attributed — [[schele-freidel-forest-of-kings-1990|Schele & Freidel 1990]], Ch. 5 and endnotes. 1990 nicknames retained; later phonetic forms in parentheses. **Star-war / Venus-timed warfare is theory/position of Schele & Freidel**, not wiki-voice fact. Note: Schele & Freidel center **Caracol agency** in the 562 Tikal defeat more heavily than Martin’s later reading of Altar 21 victor outlines as Calakmul’s Sky Witness—see [[caracol]] Key Debates and densifications above.)*

### Lord Water, Altar 21, and the 556–562 wars on Tikal

As of Schele & Freidel, Caracol dynastic history that survives begins AD 495; **Lord Water** (as of 1990; Yajaw Te’ K’inich II in later literature) accedes **18 April 553** (9.5.19.1.2), recorded in part on Stelae 6 and 14 and on **Caracol Altar 21** (C5-004). Altar 21 was found in **1986** on the central axis of a ballcourt—128-glyph circular text beginning with the birth of commissioning king **Lord Kan II** (as of 1990; later K’an II) and narrating Lord Water’s aggression against [[tikal]] (C5-071).

On **11 April 556** (9.6.2.1.11), following the end of Katun 6, Caracol conducts an **“ax-war”** action “in the land of” the ahau of Tikal (C5-005). Shortly after, on 17 September 557, Tikal’s **Double-Bird** (as of 1990; Wak Chan K’awiil) raises Stela 17 for a one-katun anniversary—last recorded public history at Tikal for a long time (C5-006). On **1 May 562** (9.6.8.4.2) Altar 21 records a **“star-at-Tikal”** war event; Schele & Freidel treat this class of event as usually lethal to the loser and identify it as **Tlaloc-Venus war** of the type earlier used against Uaxactún—“The long darkness at Tikal had begun” (C5-007). Endnote C10-037: Houston recognized the war events; ax-event and star-over-Tikal Emblem at Venus stationary point; authors **accept Chase & Chase (1987)** that the Tikal hiatus results from Caracol conquest; post-war Caracol population +325% and rich tombs vs Tikal burial poverty (Coggins 1976). Chapter typology (C5-068): **ax-war** (556) framed as non-fatal vs **star-war** (562, and later 631 Naranjo) as Venus-associated and usually decisive—**authors’ typology**.

They argue correspondence of Caracol’s victory claim to “all-out destruction” at Tikal shows the claim was not fabrication: Lord Water’s war broke Tikal’s pride, independence, and prosperity—though extent and duration of political dominance remain uncertain (C5-008; [[overkingship]]). From this period they report Caracol-region influence in Tikal art and funerary practice; stelae cease; temple building slows; well-stocked tombs give way to meager goods; they speculate Double-Bird was captured and killed and remaining *ahauob* cut off from trade (C5-009). Only one rich tomb under the hiatus—Burial 195 of the twenty-second Tikal successor—never permitted public monuments (C5-010). Uaxactún, part of Tikal’s hegemony, ceases monuments at 9.6.0.0.0 for ~200 years; authors stress the irony that Tikal was undone by the same Tlaloc-Venus form it had used against Uaxactún ~180 years earlier (C5-026). Endnote C10-042: no epigraphic usurper Caracol kings at Tikal and no victory monument as at Naranjo—victors desecrated history and banned public monuments rather than installing a foreign line.

### Lord Kan II, Calakmul bond, and the Naranjo campaign

Lord Water reigns ~46 years; older brother (born 575) becomes king 26 June 599 for 19 years; younger brother accedes **9 March 618**, takes Lord Water’s name, and launches the campaign that defeats [[naranjo]]—this is **Lord Kan II** (C5-011). On **9 January 619** Kan II acts “in the land of” a [[calakmul]] ahau—authors mark the start of a bond that would prove fateful for Tikal and Naranjo (C5-012).

**Naranjo wars (attributed narrative + Venus theory):**
- **28 May 626:** full-scale campaign vs Naranjo (Caracol Stela 3); Venus at stationary point as Morningstar—**position Schele & Freidel believe favorable for victory** (C5-017).
- **4 May 627:** second war/sacrificial event at Caracol; Naranjo stairway calls it a ballgame (authors: player as captive); death recorded 4 October 630—possibly aged Ruler I (C5-018).
- **27 December 631:** decisive **star-war** defeat of Naranjo when Venus as Eveningstar first appeared over Naranjo (**authors’ astronomical reading**); texts at Caracol and Naranjo Stairs do not name the Naranjo king as captive (C5-019).
- **4 March 636:** further attack; Kan II records personal capture of lord **18-Rabbit** (as of 1990 nickname; not the later Copan namesake) when Morningstar was past maximum elongation (**theory**) (C5-022).
- **24 November 637:** first-katun reign completion celebrated **on Naranjo’s Hieroglyphic Stairs**, not at home (C5-023).
- **6 December 642** (9.10.10.0.0): Naranjo forced to dedicate the stairs glorifying Caracol triumph; last public written record at Naranjo for the next ~40 years (C5-027).

**Domination mechanism (theory):** conquerors claim tribute (obsidian, shell, heirlooms, craftsmen, cloth, artists); without standing armies to garrison cities, stripping wealth and king made policing unnecessary—loss of prestige destroyed political influence and ability to recruit hinterland population/goods (C5-024). Effacing enemy monuments and impoverishing cities so they could erect no others took away “history”—most devastating result of defeat (C5-025).

### Failed empire, silence, and reverse

Unlike Smoking-Frog’s Uaxactún triumph, Schele & Freidel argue Kan II and Calakmul allies **failed to quell hatred or consolidate submission**; short-term failure fired Petexbatún challengers of [[dos-pilas]], who would revive Naranjo and jar Tikal—yet whose vengeance would “seal the Maya doom” even as they rejuvenated defeated dynasts (C5-028). Chapter synthesis: Caracol conquered Tikal then with Calakmul conquered Naranjo; later Tikal attacked Calakmul while Naranjo rampaged south toward Caracol; Caracol’s response was to **“duck and hide”**; fortunes declined as Tikal and Naranjo reemerged (C5-060). After Kan II, **~70 years of inscription silence** until the end of Katun 17 (as of 1990 knowledge) (C5-062). C10-059 ranks star-shell events (including Caracol vs Tikal/Naranjo) as best candidates for wars yielding territorial conquest plus political dominance.
