## Discovery and Excavation History

Angkor Borei is a major early historic urban site on the Mekong Delta margin in Cambodia, brick-walled and moated, and a principal archaeological anchor for the polity Chinese sources call Funan. Higham (2002) reports Miriam Stark’s programme encountering an Iron Age cemetery deeply stratified below the later walled city — “a finding of the greatest significance in any investigation of early state origins” (R4-059; R5-008). French colonial scholarship (Pelliot on Chinese texts; Malleret at Oc Eo; aerial canal survey by Paris) first framed the delta as Funan’s heartland; modern excavation has made Angkor Borei the best-documented inland partner to the port city of Oc Eo (as of Higham 2002).

## Occupation Sequence

**Iron Age roots (from 3rd/4th century BCE).** Stark et al. (1999), as reported by Higham, document five metres of cultural deposits commencing by the third or fourth centuries BC, with an Iron Age inhumation cemetery under historic layers (R5-008, R5-013). Nearby Go Hang yields glass, agate, and carnelian beads resembling Noen U-Loke forms with radiocarbon 54 BC–AD 130 (R5-008). Pre-state Iron Age communities were already positioned for maritime exchange.

**Funan-period city (c. 1st–6th centuries CE and later).** Historic occupation with thin orange ceramics falls at least within the 1st–6th centuries AD. Brick walls enclose about 300 ha. Barays include one of roughly 20,000 m²; wall and baray dating remain key research goals (as of Higham 2002; R5-013). Vishnu statue style suggests brick temples by the 7th century.

**Canal network and capital geography.** Between AD 100–550, Higham reports cities linked by canals across the delta. Oc Eo is a rectangular enceinte 3 × 1.5 km behind five ramparts and four moats (~450 ha), bisected by a large canal; aerial and pre-WWII work show Oc Eo canals as part of an extensive network, some seaward. Canals radiate to Ta Kev (probable actual port) and Da Noi, thence an arterial canal of nearly 90 km to Angkor Borei — “probably the pre-eminent centre.” One Chinese report placed the capital 500 li (~200 km) from the sea (R5-009, R5-012). Canals are undated but assigned to Oc Eo occupation (2nd–7th c.) with multi-function drainage, transport, and settlement foundation by analogy.

**Oc Eo material culture (regional context).** Malleret recovered Roman medallions of Antoninus Pius and Marcus Aurelius, Mediterranean-inspiration jewellery, a Chinese mirror, Iranian coinage, Indian-inspired jewellery, and Indian script from late 1st–early 2nd c. AD on portable objects, later writing to the 5th c. (R5-010). Local manufacture blended introduced and local techniques: Indian glass-bead making in clay crucibles of long local ancestry; pot anvils matching millennia-old local forms; bivalve stone moulds for tin pendants matching Mekong-valley bronze moulds of the previous 1,500 years (R5-011).

**Agriculture.** Fox and Ledgerwood (1999), applied by Higham, identify likely flood-recession farming for the deep-flood delta: floodwater trapped behind bunds/reservoirs and released as floods retreat; high yields from annual silt; no ploughing needed. The Angkor Borei basin and delta terrain are well adapted (R5-014) — modern/traditional analogy applied retrospectively, flagged as such.

**Epigraphy and state transition.** Funan Sanskrit inscriptions (Go Thap, Nak Ta Dambang Dek; Coedès early-6th-c. stylistic dates) name kings such as Jayavarman and Rudravarman, marsh reclamation (likely drainage canals), and official appointments (R5-018). Delta temple/cremation sites (Nen Chua, Go Thap, Go Xoai) AD 400–650 show Hindu/Buddhist monumental and mortuary practice (R5-015). Higham summarises a rapid early-centuries-AD state transition: walled cities, brick temples, canals, writing, monumental statuary; Iron Age extended inhumation replaced by cremation; exotic cults and Sanskrit royal names (R5-019).

## Significance

Angkor Borei is central to [[southeast-asian-state-formation]] as secondary-state formation at a maritime silk-route node. Higham argues the lower Mekong, though flood-prone marsh behind mangroves, was strategically nodal for east–west exchange and controlled Mekong-valley flow from a rich hinterland (Central Cambodia through Dang Raek onto Khorat) (R5-007). The site’s Iron Age–to-city continuity challenges any model of pure colonial foundation without local roots. Funan decline is attributed by Higham preferentially to Chinese bypass of Mekong mouths for island transit routes as interior agrarian states rose (R5-019, R5-061). Multi-variable process model (prestige goods, manufacture, canals, coastal raiding, Indic political-religious forms as selective adoption): R5-059–062; see [[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]].

## Key Debates

- **Unitary Funan vs competing delta polities** — unresolved as of Higham 2002 (R5-019).
- **Chinese documentary reliability** — Higham warns not to take Chinese accounts literally; multi-hand transmission and Sinocentric state templates (R5-017, R5-054).
- **Indianisation** — Higham rejects passive “blotting-paper” absorption of Indian culture by a stone-age society; Funan complexity is “most logically conceived as a process in a particularly favoured area, within a local cultural matrix to which the development of exchange relations with India contributed” (R5-055, R5-059).
- **Wall/baray chronology** — still a research goal as of Higham 2002.

## Historiography

Pelliot’s reconstruction of Funan from Chinese texts, Malleret’s Oc Eo excavations during WWII, and Paris’s aerial canal mapping established the colonial-era frame. Coedès’s Indianised-states synthesis remains the historiographic baseline Higham revises with archaeology and multiplier-effect theory (vs Coedès 1968; Wheatley 1983). Stark’s LOMAP programme grounds Angkor Borei’s Iron Age roots. This page rests on [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]]; full Stark monographs and post-2002 work are not yet ingested. Do not equate the archaeological city, the Chinese exonym “Funan,” Khmer speakers, or any genetic population.
