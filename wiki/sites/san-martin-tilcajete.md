# San Martín Tilcajete

Settlement complex in the Ocotlán arm of the Valley of Oaxaca, the principal center of the valley's southern subvalley during the Formative and — in the period after [[monte-alban]]'s founding — the most prominent apparent holdout against the new capital. Spencer and Redmond's excavations at its El Mogote and El Palenque loci (attributed) document a local polity that expanded and built its own civic-ceremonial complex during Monte Albán Early I–Late I before being destroyed/incorporated, which they read as evidence that the Monte Albán state expanded partly by conquest within the valley.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Recorded and mapped by the Valley of Oaxaca Settlement Pattern Project surveys (1970s); intensively excavated by Charles Spencer and Elsa Redmond (AMNH) from the 1990s, with modern stratigraphic methods and radiocarbon dating — a high-quality excavation record by regional standards.

## Occupation Sequence

Village occupation from the Early Formative; grew as the Ocotlán subvalley head-town (El Mogote) in the Rosario and Early I phases; new civic-ceremonial complex at El Palenque (Late I), including a multiroom palace; burning and abandonment of the El Palenque complex around the Late I/Nisa–Pe transition, followed by reorganization under [[monte-alban]] (attributed to Spencer and Redmond's excavation reports as summarized in ingested syntheses).

Per [[blanton-ancient-oaxaca-2022|Blanton et al. (2022)]] summarizing Redmond and Spencer (2013; Spencer and Redmond 2004): the El Palenque complex included an eight-room ruler's palace and a precinct of three temples across a plaza, linked by a vaulted underground tunnel from the principal temple, with two thick-walled single-room buildings (possible priests' residences) behind. El Mogote was burned during Monte Albán Early I (Spencer and Redmond 2001), with a sparsely settled zone to its south read as a possible buffer against enemies of the expanding Monte Albán polity. Domestic excavations show comals increasing by Late I and Cocijo effigy imagery entering Late I burials as small figurines declined (Carpenter 2019).

## Significance

Tilcajete anchors the debate over whether the Monte Albán state formed by coercion/conquest or by collective confederation: Spencer and Redmond treat its destruction as conquest by an expansionist Monte Albán, while Blanton, Feinman, Kowalewski, and Nicholas (2022) read the same valley-wide record as predominantly cooperative synoikism with holdouts. See [[monte-alban]] and [[collective-action-theory]].
