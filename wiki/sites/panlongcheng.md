# Panlongcheng

## Discovery and Excavation History

Panlongcheng, near Lake Panlong in Huangpi County, Hubei, was excavated by the Hubei Provincial
Institute of Archaeology (Hubeisheng), with findings reported through Hubeisheng 2001a (as of Liu &
Chen 2012). It was initially an [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]]-period settlement of roughly 20 ha
engaged in pottery and bronze work, then expanded during the Upper Erligang phase to ~290 ha
(measured by its outer rammed-earth walls), with a 7.5 ha inner walled palatial town.

## Occupation Sequence

Panlongcheng functioned as a communication node linking Yangzi/Han river routes to the Henan
Central Plain. During its Erligang-period expansion it became the largest [[erligang-culture|Erligang]]-period
outpost in the middle Yangzi region and, per Liu & Chen (2012), the chief Early Shang outpost of
that region — one of the fortified southern nodes of Erligang expansion (alongside Dongxiafeng,
Nanguan, and Donglongshan to the west, and Daxinzhuang/Qianzhangda to the east). Panlongcheng's
double-ringed concentric wall design and its material culture — bronze styles, construction
techniques, palatial layout — closely resemble Yanshi and [[zhengzhou|Zhengzhou]], indicating close
affiliation with the Central Plain Erligang core (as of Liu & Chen 2012). The site was abandoned by
the late Shang period; the regional center role in the middle Yangzi subsequently passed to Wucheng
(Zhangshu, Jiangxi), which grew from a 61-ha Upper Erligang-phase enclosure to a 400-ha site by the
Yinxu (late Shang) phase.

## Significance

Elite tombs at Panlongcheng contained large numbers of bronze vessels and crucibles; finds include
proto-porcelain vessels and bronze ritual vessels (he, jue, zun, tiliangyou). Panlongcheng is a key
data point for Liu & Chen's model of Erligang expansion as a tributary core-periphery political
economy: as a fortified outpost closely resembling the Central Plain core in construction and
material style, it illustrates how the Zhengzhou/Yanshi core projected political and craft-production
influence southward along river-communication routes to secure metal and other resources (attributed
interpretation, as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Key Debates

Whether Panlongcheng's bronzes were cast locally or imported from Zhengzhou is disputed: no casting
molds for ritual vessels have been found on site, and one clay-residue study (Nan et al. 2008)
argues for local production from local clay — a finding described as controversial (personal
communication, Jin Zhengyao 2009, as cited in Liu & Chen 2012). Rich local copper deposits in the
area are cited as circumstantial support for local production, but the absence of vessel-casting
molds leaves the question open; this wiki records it as an unresolved position rather than a
settled finding.
