# Monte Verde

Late Pleistocene settlement on Chinchihuapi Creek in southern Chile (Meltzer 2009: ~50 km from the Pacific via the Río Maullín). Excavated by [[dillehay-tom|Tom Dillehay]], it is the site whose **MV-II** component—sealed by peat and dated on average to **~12,500 radiocarbon years BP**—broke the Clovis-first exclusive model after a 1997 expert site visit (as of [[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|Meltzer 2009]] and earlier [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] / [[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] layers).

**Identity caution:** Monte Verde occupants are an archaeological population defined by a settlement assemblage, not a named people, language, or genetic cluster. Do not equate with later Andean or Mapuche identities without separate argument. Ethnobotanic parallels with Mapuche plant use are recorded as analogy, not identity.

**Date hygiene:** Ages from Meltzer 2009 on this page are **radiocarbon years BP (uncalibrated)** unless noted; he deliberately avoids cal BP for late Pleistocene comparability (see [[radiocarbon-dating]]).

## Discovery and Excavation History

### Meltzer 2009 (Ch. 4) — principal densification

*(Attributed — [[meltzer-david]], *[[meltzer-first-peoples-2009|First Peoples in a New World]]* 2009.)*

Woodsmen finds reached Dillehay; excavations **1977–1985** with ~80 collaborators. After occupation, damming created a **water-saturated, culturally sterile, grass-matted peat** over ~**600 m²** of the MVII surface, stalling normal decay—wood, plants, hide, and coprolites preserved. Excavation required non-metal tools and saturation protocols (geological fluke of preservation, not routine open-air recovery).

Dillehay’s **1989 Vol. 1** established natural vs cultural plant/animal patterning before full artifact publication—a novel burden that Thomas Lynch and others found hard to judge without the later volume. **1997 Vol. 2** (~1,000 pages, 32 co-authors) detailed mastodon bone through sediment chemistry and residues; *Nature* called it among the most impressive interdisciplinary archaeology volumes of a generation. Meltzer treats the overkill as necessary under what he calls the **“Jackie Robinson Rule”**: a pre-Clovis site must convince skeptics with room to spare.

**January 1997 site visit.** At Dillehay’s invitation, Paleoindian experts (proponents and skeptics, including Dena Dincauze and C. Vance Haynes; Meltzer among them) studied the final volume, examined Kentucky and Valdivia collections, then visited the site. Final evening discussion in Puerto Montt: **all agreed MVII was archaeological and ~12,500 years old**. A later *American Antiquity* statement drafted by Donald Grayson and Meltzer was signed after a cooling-off period, urging others to judge the volumes themselves. Coverage reached the *New York Times*. This was **not** a Folsom-style in-situ point verification; it was a multi-evidence consensus after monographic publication.

**Haynes’s later second thoughts.** Haynes later stressed only six unequivocal artifacts (those resembling North American Paleoindian points). Meltzer calls this a red herring: wood, modified bone, ivory, mastodon meat, knotted strings, and exotic plants remain; even six artifacts leave a 12,500-year-old archaeological site. Majority opinion was not reversed (as of Meltzer 2009).

**Fiedel critique.** Stuart Fiedel identified mapping/count/provenience discrepancies in the volumes; published in popular *Discovering Archaeology* with a short response window before the Clovis and Beyond 1999 conference—felt like an ambush (per David Thomas, via Meltzer). Dillehay clarified provenience and photos; hall gave Dillehay a standing ovation; Fiedel was denied stage time. Media/professional-norms dispute as much as pure stratigraphy.

**2008 seaweed/algae affirmation.** Dillehay et al. (early 2008) reported half a dozen previously unrecorded algae/seaweed species (including seaweed on a stone tool) in hearth sediment with ¹⁴C affirming the age. Long-time skeptic Daniel Sandweiss said the results “remove any lingering doubts about the antiquity of human presence at that site.” Meltzer stresses coastal resources for early diet and health.

### Mithen 2003 / Fagan layers (complementary)

As of Mithen 2003: prior critics included Thomas Lynch (reworked recent artefacts) and Dena Dincauze (radiocarbon misinterpretation); 1997 panel including Meltzer, Haynes, James Adovasio, and Dincauze accepted peat-sealed human artefacts and uncontaminated dates ~12,500 BCE in Mithen’s calendar framing (different date register from Meltzer’s ¹⁴C BP). Fagan (1987/2004) treats Monte Verde as the one pre-Clovis site that “has stood the test of time,” with ~twelve wooden dwellings, clay-lined braziers, 60+ edible plant/seaweed species, and a quartzite biface sourced ~60 km away (attributed).

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed primarily to Dillehay via Meltzer 2009; complementary Mithen/Fagan.)*

1. **MV-II / MVII (principal component, multi-lab average ~12,500 ¹⁴C BP).** Range reported **11,790 ± 200 to 13,565 ± 250 BP** on wood, charcoal, tusk, and bone. Largest assemblage: tools, huts, food debris. Architecture: wishbone-shaped sand/gravel foundation (~3.9 × 3 m) with wood stubs/hide (work/cure hut) ~35 m from rectangular “row house” huts (~20 m long) with foundation timbers, juncus string on stakes, hide drapes; communal and clay-lined hearths; child’s footprint (~13 cm). Fauna: mastodon, paleo-llama, fish, shellfish, birds, seaweed, eggshells—not a Clovis-style mammoth kill. Plants: ~75 species, more than off-site comparative samples; ~1/3 charred; ~1/4 exotic from coast (~30 km; seaweed on tools), high Andes, or grasslands to ~600 km; wild potato skins; juncus/scirpus seeds in wooden basins; boldo–seaweed chewed plugs as medicinal “prehistoric chaw.” Artifacts dominated by wood (digging sticks, mortars, lances) plus bone/ivory and ~715 stone pieces (bolas, cores, basalt perforator, grinding slabs, knives, unifaces, spear points); many minimally modified stones with unnatural sphericity (Collins); >40 distant stone sources; coastal bitumen hafting.
2. **MV-I / MVI (possible older component).** Separate area: couple dozen stone specimens and three small charcoal basins dating **>33,000 BP**; lacks MVII organic preservation; some artifacts unequivocal but Dillehay not fully committed—focus remained MVII. **Not** the basis of 1997 consensus acceptance.
3. **Regional aftermath.** After Monte Verde’s abandonment, South America held varied hunter-gatherer economies (Mithen/Fagan narratives); Meltzer stresses pre-Clovis South American complexes lack coherent technological links to Clovis (no Clovis *sensu stricto* south of Panama).

## Significance

- Load-bearing site for the [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]]: acceptance of pre-Clovis occupation at ~12,500 ¹⁴C BP (as of 1997 consensus Meltzer narrates; 2008 seaweed consolidates) ends exclusive Clovis-first as viable orthodoxy in his synthesis.
- **Geographic paradox:** ~16,000 km from NE Asia/Beringia; presence in southern Chile by that age implies earlier departure and forces route rethink—interior [[ice-free-corridor]] closed ~28–12 ka cannot deliver people to Chile 500 years before corridor opening (as of Meltzer 2009 geology).
- Revises First Americans image from nomadic plains big-game specialists toward forest plant-gatherers in a wet-site residential settlement.
- Challenges pure blitzkrieg [[megafaunal-extinctions|overkill]] timing: people present long before Clovis and mass extinction (Meltzer/Mithen positions; see [[pleistocene-overkill-debate]]).
- Template for how high-bar evidence ends controversy (parallel Meltzer draws with [[folsom-site]] 1927).

## Key Debates

- **Clovis-first vs pre-Clovis** — Monte Verde as the decisive 1997 case in Meltzer’s narrative (later literature continues; do not freeze 2009 as final).
- **MV-I ≥33,000 BP** — Dillehay cautious; not equated with accepted MV-II.
- **Fiedel provenience critique** vs monographic defense and 2008 reaffirmation (positions).
- **Route:** ice-free corridor vs Pacific [[coastal-adaptation|coastal entry]]; missing intermediate settlements; no pre-Clovis sites in interior corridor region as of Meltzer 2009.
- **Colonization pace:** not an “Oklahoma Land Rush”; landscape learning slows dispersal (Meltzer thesis).
- Relation to other early candidates ([[meadowcroft]], Pedra Furada, Taima-Taima, Paisley, etc.)—none peers of Monte Verde’s acceptance status in Meltzer’s 2009 weighing.

## Historiography

Monte Verde is both a research act and a disciplinary episode: peat seal + multi-specialist monographs + elite site visit + media amplification + Fiedel ambush dynamics + 2008 seaweed consolidation. Meltzer is a participant-historian (1997 visitor; *American Antiquity* co-drafter) and flags *caveat lector* bias while arguing legitimate multi-reading of evidence was under-explained in popular media. Site-visit epistemology (Haynes: “one day on the site… worth all the words”) still matters after Folsom even though lab antiquity cannot be watched in the field. Earlier Mithen/Fagan layers are downstream of Dillehay’s monographs; primary excavation reports remain the evidence base.

## Fagan (1987/2004) — complementary account

*[[fagan-great-journey-1987|Fagan]] treats Monte Verde as the one pre-Clovis site
that "has stood the test of time," in explicit contrast to overturned early-date
claims (Pedra Furada, Calico Hills, the amino-acid-dated California skulls)
(attributed).*

- **Setting and finds.** An open settlement ~15 km from the Pacific in humid
  sub-antarctic forest with exceptional organic preservation. [[dillehay-tom|Dillehay]]
  (with a Universidad Austral de Chile team) identified ~twelve wooden dwellings
  in two rows, clay-lined braziers, outdoor fireplaces, and a wishbone-shaped
  structure along ~400 m of stream bank; finds include modified bones of 5–6
  mastodon (possibly scavenged), 60+ species of edible wetland/forest plants and
  seaweeds, wooden hafts and digging sticks, and 200+ minimally-modified stones
  including one clear quartzite biface sourced ~60 km away (attributed, Fagan).
- **Dating.** Main occupation ~12,000–14,500 BP (~1,000 years before Clovis);
  Fagan gives ~14,500 years in the 2004 update. A deeper ~33,000-BP level
  (later MV-I) is reported but its human origin was **unverified** at the time of
  writing — Fagan withholds judgment on it (attributed; radiocarbon).
- **Significance for Fagan.** Dillehay's full two-volume Smithsonian monograph
  (1989, 1997) — a rigor Fagan calls rare in American archaeology — drew attention
  to the importance of **plant foods** to the earliest Americans, correcting the
  big-game-hunter stereotype (attributed). Alan Bryan cited Monte Verde to recast
  the first Americans as "generalized foragers."
- Source: [[fagan-great-journey-1987]] ch. 8; see [[peopling-of-the-americas-debate]].
