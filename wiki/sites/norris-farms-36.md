# Norris Farms No. 36

Late prehistoric cemetery in west-central Illinois, box feature in [[milner-moundbuilders-2021|Milner 2021]] Ch. 7 (“Violent Death at Norris Farms No. 36”). Landmark bioarchaeological case for **intergroup violence** in the late prehistoric Midwest; associated with [[oneota-culture|Oneota]]-related communities.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Well-documented excavation of a small bluff-top cemetery (~**2+ feet** of earth on a high bluff over the Illinois River floodplain, barely modifying ridge contours). Cemetery grew as graves were added, mostly on the valley-facing side. Sample on the order of **≥260 burials** (as of Milner 2021).

## Occupation Sequence

Cemetery serving a late prehistoric village community (~**1300 CE**); not a platform-mound capital. Burials often with personal ornaments, tools, pottery; some age/sex patterning but without extreme elite outlier assemblages of Mississippian mound centers. **About one-third of adults** show evidence of violent trauma (Milner) — wounds consistent with warfare/raiding rather than rare interpersonal assault alone.

## Significance

- High trauma frequency anchors Milner’s broader argument for serious late prehistoric **warfare** in parts of the Midwest amid fourteenth-century climate stress and settlement clustering.
- Comparative baseline for “deteriorating relations” after ~1000 CE (palisades, village nucleation).
- Method: bioarchaeology as evidence class for violence rates self-report and ethnohistory cannot supply for prehistory.

## Key Debates

Representativeness for the wider region; warfare vs other violence; demographic profile of victims; relation to Vacant Quarter / climate narratives.
