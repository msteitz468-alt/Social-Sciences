## Discovery and Excavation History

Cerro Sechín is a relatively small but iconographically exceptional shrine on the Sechín branch of the Casma Valley, Peru, within the broader Sechín Alto Complex landscape. As of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]], it is one of several exceptional monuments along the Río Sechín — including the colossal U-shaped Sechín Alto, U-shaped Sechín Bajo, and non-U Taukachi-Konkan — each architecturally distinct. Scarre ed. 2013 summarizes the megalithic wall reliefs and cites Donnan (1993) on their possible foreshadowing of later Moche combat/sacrifice themes. Excavation-quality detail beyond construction sequence is limited in the ingested syntheses.

## Occupation Sequence

Moseley reports a two-stage construction history:

1. **Early phase:** modest multi-room adobe sanctuary with large polychrome paintings of felines and a fish.
2. **Megalithic phase (before 1200 BC):** filled in to create an expanded one-story rectangular platform ornamented on all sides by a unique megalithic facade of **incised monoliths** forming a giant mosaic. A plate caption in Moseley dates the important site to **1290 BC**.

**Iconographic program (attributed interpretation):** dual procession of armed men with axe-like clubs diverging from the back and converging on the front central entrance marked by banner monoliths — Moseley reads this as the first grand iconographic statement of *tinku* rituals and duality convergence. The most common motifs are **dismembered humans** (severed corpses, heads, limbs); "what they symbolize is not known" (Moseley). Peaceful coexistence among early creeds was the apparent norm, but violence is not absent in these megaliths; Moseley wonders whether the pikes were emblems of authority that later evolved into staffs of office (link to later [[staff-god]] iconography is speculative, not identity).

## Significance

- Anchors the **Casma Valley Initial Period** as a landscape of multiple concurrent creeds rather than a single Vatican-like monopoly: Sechín Alto as apex of the U-shaped tradition; Cerro Sechín as a smaller, different shrine with explicit dual-procession and dismemberment imagery.
- Provides early coastal monumental iconography of armed processions and body fragmentation that Donnan (via Scarre) and Moseley variously link interpretively to later north-coast warrior-sacrifice themes (`associated_with` later [[moche-culture]] imagery — not ethnic descent).
- Type illustration of Initial Period **huaca** facades as public ideological display.

## Key Debates

- **Meaning of dismemberment motifs:** unknown as of Moseley 2001; range from sacrificial documentation to mythic narrative to martial emblem — do not promote any reading to wiki voice.
- **Pozorskis’ multi-valley religious spheres:** Thomas and Shelia Pozorski (via Moseley) see Sechín Alto and Pampa de los Llamas-Moxeke as complementary moiety-like formations with six-valley influence (Chao–Huarmey); Moseley calls such spheres tenable yet speculative because creed frontiers were permeable.
- **Link to Moche:** iconographic foreshadowing is attributed scholarly comparison, not demonstrated cultural continuity across a millennium-plus gap.

## Historiography

Moseley’s 2001 synthesis embeds Cerro Sechín in his Initial Period U-shape / ecumenical-landscape argument and dual-organization (*tinku*) reading. Scarre ed. 2013 foregrounds Donnan’s comparative link to Moche. Primary excavation monographs are not yet ingested; treat construction dates and motif counts as of Moseley/Scarre.
