# Ur

Major southern Sumerian city (Tell al-Muqayyar). Famous for Woolley's excavations; in *[[adams-heartland-of-cities-1981|Heartland of Cities]]* it anchors Henry T. Wright's appendix survey of the Eridu–Ur margins and appears in Adams's alluvial period maps.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Woolley and successors; this page currently holds **survey-context** densification from Adams/Wright 1981, not a full excavation synthesis.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Adams 1981 / Wright appendix.)*

- Southern capital in historical sequences (especially Ur III); Wright's Eridu–Ur survey anchors the southern margins.
- Wright: Ubaid hierarchy Ur ~**10 ha** (with Eridu ~12 ha); multi-period population and size series through ED, Ur III, Larsa–OB, Cassite, Neo-Babylonian+.
- Jacobsen notes Lagash area east of Shatt al-Gharraf still unsurveyed as of 1981 — Ur/Eridu coverage does not close all of Sumer.
- Adams catalog and period maps place Ur within wider watercourse and hierarchy shifts (date-stamp 1981).

## Significance

- Type southern capital (especially Ur III) in historical sequences.
- Southern-margin survey counterpart to central floodplain intensive coverage.

## Key Debates

- Relationship of Woolley excavation narratives to regional survey demography (Wright appendix vs site monographs).
- Ur III hydraulic and settlement intensity relative to Adams's second vs third watercourse configurations.
- Coverage gap: Lagash area still unsurveyed as of Jacobsen's 1981 foreword — Ur/Eridu do not close all of Sumer.

## Roux (1992) — Early Dynastic and Ur III Additions

*(Attributed — [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]].)*

### Early Dynastic: First Dynasty of Ur and the Royal Cemetery

A-annepadda and his father Mesannepadda, founder of the First Dynasty of Ur (successor to the First Dynasty of Uruk), were confirmed as historical figures via Woolley's winter 1922–3 excavation at al-Ubaid, which recovered a marble tablet inscription: "(To) Ninhursag: A-annepadda, King of Ur, son of Mesannepadda, King of Ur... has built (this temple)." Roux calls this the first case in which "one of those early Sumerian princes long held as mythical was proven to have actually existed"; he dates Mesannepadda's reign to roughly 2560 BCE.

The Royal Cemetery of Ur, excavated by Sir Leonard Woolley, dates to the period immediately preceding the First Dynasty of Ur (c. 2600 BCE, slightly before Mesannepadda). Famous finds include gold vessels and daggers, gold-and-lapis "ram caught in a thicket" statuettes, gold and silver bulls'-head harp ornaments, "Queen Puabi's" (formerly read "Shubad") gold headdress, and Meskalamdug's gold helmet — Roux calls the period's goldsmithing, evidenced here, a peak "unequalled in any other contemporary civilization" (his own comparative judgment). Of 17 royal tombs with legible inscriptions, only two male names (Meskalamdug "hero of the good land," Akalamdug "son of the good land") carry the title *lugal* ("king"), and two female names (Ninbanda, wife of Meskalamdug; Puabi, spouse of an unnamed king) carry *nin* ("queen") — a pattern Roux finds "disconcerting" despite most tombs having been plundered in antiquity, which could otherwise explain missing inscriptions.

Collective/retainer burials at Ur ranged from 3 to 74 attendants (mostly female) accompanying the primary burial, described by Roux as "practically a whole royal household"; within Mesopotamia the practice is attested only at Ur (and possibly Kish), though Roux cites broader comparanda (Egypt's First Dynasty, the Scythians, the Mongols, Assam, the 13th-century-CE Comans).

**Open interpretive question — see [[ur-royal-cemetery-debate]].** Woolley's theory, endorsed provisionally by Roux as plausible but explicitly flagged as contestable, holds that the Cemetery's occupants (Meskalamdug, Akalamdug, Puabi, and unnamed others) may have been considered "living gods"/"substitute gods" — human representatives of the city's divine couple — entitled to take courtiers and retainers with them into the afterlife. Supporting textual evidence is limited to one epic, "The Death of Gilgamesh," describing a king going to the grave with his retinue, combined with the tradition that Gilgamesh himself became a god of the Netherworld only a few generations removed from Meskalamdug. Roux: "this theory, as all others, is open to criticism, and the drama of the Cemetery of Ur remains a mystery." This should be treated as an open, unresolved interpretive question, not settled fact.

Roux also rejects Woolley's separate claim regarding water-borne clay/sand flood deposits excavated at Ur: 7 of 14 test pits dug by Woolley (1929–1934) show such deposits, the deepest and thickest lying between two Ubaid-period occupation layers, which Woolley claimed represented "the biblical Flood." Roux states this claim "lacks convincing support," concluding instead that the deposits reflect localized inundations from river-course changes, not a Near-East-wide cataclysm (other, thinner Ur flood deposits are dated to roughly 2800–2600 BCE).

### Third Dynasty of Ur (Ur III, c. 2112–2004 BCE)

Ur III was founded by Ur-Nammu, governor of Ur under Utu-hegal of Uruk; after Utu-hegal's seven-year reign ended in his deposition, Ur-Nammu took the titles "King of Ur, King of Sumer and Akkad." Ur-Nammu reigned 2112–2095 BCE (Roux's dates), restoring order — he "freed the land from thieves, robbers and rebels" — and is traditionally credited with the oldest known Mesopotamian law code. Roux notes, however, that a newly found tablet indicates the code's actual author was Ur-Nammu's son **Shulgi**, not Ur-Nammu himself — an explicit revision of the code's attribution within this very source; any existing wiki content crediting the code directly to Ur-Nammu should be reconciled with this correction. Surviving law fragments show compensation-in-silver penalties for offenses such as physical injury, rather than the death or mutilation seen later in Hammurabi's Code or Hebraic law — Roux reads this as evidence of "a society far more polished and civilized than is usually imagined" (his own evaluative judgment). Ur-Nammu also dug canals, fortified towns, and built ziqqurats at Ur, Uruk, Eridu, Nippur, and other cities — see [[ziggurat]] for the Ur ziqqurat's detailed architectural description and the competing theories of ziggurat function. Ur-Nammu died "abandoned on the battlefield like a crushed vessel" (a quoted lament) in an unrecorded war.

Shulgi (reign 2094–2047 BCE, 47 years per Roux) completed his father's temples and ziqqurats, reformed the calendar, introduced the "royal gur" (roughly 6 bushels) as a standardized grain measure superseding local measures, and founded scribal schools at Ur and Nippur — "to which we owe so many masterpieces of Sumerian literature." From his 24th regnal year he waged annual campaigns in Kurdistan against the Hurrian/Lullubi stronghold of Simurrum (captured or "devastated" nine times) to protect trade routes, and had a fortified wall built between the Tigris and the Zagros; the region became a Sumerian province by 2051 BCE. In southwest Iran he pursued marriage-alliance diplomacy (daughters married to rulers of Warahshe and Awan), took Susa (installing a Sumerian governor and building a temple to Inshushinak), crushed an Anshan revolt, and recruited Elamite soldiers as a border "foreign legion." He took the Naram-Sin-derived title "King of the Four Quarters" and was worshipped as a living, then posthumous, god, with offerings made twice monthly at his statues empire-wide and a month of the Sumerian calendar named for him.

Amar-Sin (Shulgi's son, reign 2046–2038 BCE) continued temple-building and northeastern campaigns, was likewise deified ("the god who gives life to the country"), and died, per a late omen-text, of infection from a shoe-bite; he was buried beside Shulgi in an underground mausoleum at Ur near the Royal Cemetery, found intact though plundered.

Under Shulgi and Amar-Sin, the Ur III "empire" (Roux's own scare quotes) comprised three zones: a periphery of nominally independent allied states (Elam, Mari) tied by marriage alliance; conquered provinces under a civilian (*ensi*) or military governor (*sagin*/Sumerian, *shakkanakkum*/Akkadian), often locally recruited; and the Sumer/Akkad heartland itself, former city-states now administered as provinces by *ensis* reduced to royal appointees. See [[temple-economy]] for the detailed bala-tax, Drehem-archive, and state-production economic record, including Roux's caution that the surviving archives document only the state sector.

Shu-Sin (Amar-Sin's brother and successor, reign 2037–2029 BCE) restored temples, campaigned successfully in the Zagros, and helped his son-in-law (king of Simanum, north of Mardin) suppress a rebellion, building what Roux calls "the first P.O.W. camp in Mesopotamia" near Nippur. His year-4 date formula records building "the fortress of MAR.TU...which keeps away Tidnum" — the first explicit sign of rising Amorite pressure on the western frontier (see [[amorites]]).

Ibbi-Sin (Shu-Sin's son), enthroned 2028 BCE, saw the empire "disintegrate" almost immediately: Eshnunna seceded in his 2nd year, Susa in his 3rd, and Amorite incursions had breached defenses by his 5th year. Surviving correspondence between Ibbi-Sin and his general Ishbi-Irra (a native of Mari) documents the crisis — a grain-supply mission to Ur blocked by MAR.TU raiding, and Ishbi-Irra's request for, and receipt of, command over Isin and Nippur's defense. Ibbi-Sin defeated the MAR.TU militarily but faced famine and eroding authority regardless; in his 11th year (2017 BCE) Ishbi-Irra proclaimed himself king in Isin, the very city he had been charged to defend, while around the same time an Amorite sheikh, Nablânum, was crowned in Larsa, 25 miles from Ur. The Elamites then invaded, taking Ur in 2004 BCE: the city — whose walls Ur-Nammu had built "as high as a shining mountain" — was sacked and burned, and Ibbi-Sin was taken captive to Anshan, where he died. A later Sumerian lament commemorates the destruction as a national catastrophe ("O Father Nanna, that city into ruins was made... Ur — its weak and its strong perished through hunger..."); this is a literary lament-genre text, cited here in attributed voice for its content and register, not as literal casualty data. See [[amorites]] for Roux's causal analysis of Ur III's fall (Amorite invasion and internal secession as "the real causes," the Elamite sack as the final blow).

## Postgate (1994) — Gulf Trade and the Ur III/OB Administrative Archives

*(Attributed — [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]].)* See [[mesopotamian-trade]] for the fuller regional network this material feeds.

Ur's merchant archive, excavated by Woolley in the city's merchant-housing quarter on the south-east side, documents — as analyzed by Oppenheim and Leemans — sea-borne trade down the Persian Gulf to **Tilmun** (Bahrain), where excavation has confirmed a contemporary sea-port with further connections south and east. Imports up the Gulf to Ur included luxury items and, importantly, bulk **copper**: a surviving memorandum records copper imported from Tilmun with weights given in both "Tilmun stone" and "Ur stone" standards, reflecting differing weight standards across trade zones. Survey and excavation now situate the copper's mineral source in **Magan** (Oman) — Postgate treats the Oman = Magan identification, and the Indus = Meluhha identification, as no longer controversial. Mesopotamian exports southward (mostly unrecovered archaeologically, being perishable or manufactured) included, in the Ur III period, textiles, wool, leather, sesame oil, and barley to Magan; earlier Larsa-period texts (reign of Sumu-ilum) record tithes and gifts to Ur's main temple from luxury goods (copper, ivory, exotic woods, carnelian, pearls, shells) brought back by traders who travelled to Tilmun "on their own" — implying an element of private, independent trading initiative alongside institutional trade. Rim-Sin-era texts record silver, textiles, and sesame oil exported to Tilmun in return.

The Nanna (moon-god) Temple at Ur functioned as a bank, supplying trading capital for Tilmun voyages in the Ur III and Old Babylonian periods; merchants gave thank-offerings (including silver boats) on safe return, sometimes noted as given "from the prompting of his heart" — a free-will offering distinguished from the temple's compulsory *arua* dedications.

Ur's Ur III and Old Babylonian **administrative archives** are, more broadly, one of the evidentiary anchors for Postgate's economic reconstruction of the period: Old Babylonian Ur inflation data (barley, fish, and oil prices rising 60x, 24x, and 5x respectively under the last Ur III king, Ibbi-Sin) is used as converging evidence, alongside the geographic contraction traceable in the latest-dated Ur III tablets found at each provincial site, for reconstructing the dynasty's territorial collapse — a methodological exemplar of using tablet date-distributions as a proxy for political control (attributed, Postgate 1994).

## Current Consensus (Van De Mieroop 2016)

**The Third Dynasty of Ur was a bureaucratic state, not a totalitarian regime.** Van De Mieroop states the current-consensus correction directly: "The Ur III state was not a totalitarian regime whose inhabitants were fully subjected to the bureaucracy, so labor had to be recruited by offering sufficient compensation." He argues the "local economies and hierarchies survived quite independently, even if they had to pay taxes and homage to the king at Ur" — evidenced by (a) the ease with which regions reverted to local practice immediately after Ur III's collapse, without disruption, which would not have been possible had their economies actually become specialized, interdependent components of a single system; (b) traces of private, unrecorded economic activity running parallel to the state archive; and (c) an explicit sampling-bias caution, since "almost all the texts derive from the state bureaucracy and describe the state's activities" — the bulk of surviving state records creates a false impression of totalizing control that is an artifact of preservation, not social reality. This should be read alongside, and as a caution against overstating, the Roux narrative above of Ur III as an "empire" with civilian and military hierarchies.

**Steinkeller's core/periphery model.** Van De Mieroop credits Piotr Steinkeller's model of the Ur III state as two-part: a heartland of roughly twenty provinces (the former city-states of Sumer, Akkad, and the lower Diyala) administered by civilian *ensi*-governors, often from prominent local families with hereditary succession; and a militarily-administered "eastern periphery" between the Tigris and Zagros under generals (*shagina*) reporting to a royal chancellor (*sukkal-mah*). Ur III centralization, on this model, was built on top of — and depended on — continuity of city-state elites rather than replacing them.

**The bala system's province-by-province collapse.** Consistent with the Roux narrative of Ur III's fall above, Van De Mieroop's account (via the geographic contraction traceable in the dynasty's own administrative archive) supports reading the collapse as a province-by-province unravelling of the bala tax-and-service system rather than a single sudden event — the same evidentiary pattern Postgate uses tablet-date distributions to trace (see above).

See [[the-state]] for the fuller Van De Mieroop treatment of Ur III's structure and the totalitarian-model correction, and [[amorites]] for the "Who were the Amorites?" debate Van De Mieroop records as open.

## Potts (*Mesopotamian Civilization*, 1997) — Additions

*(Attributed — [[potts-mesopotamian-civilization-1997|Potts 1997]] unless noted.)*

### Gold's "explosive appearance" in the Royal Cemetery

Gold (Sum. *ku-sig17*, Akk. *hurasu*) is sporadic in southern Mesopotamia before its
"explosive appearance" in the Royal Cemetery at Ur (ED IIIa) — only a Ubaid-period
gold-wire fragment at Ur and a Late Uruk goat amulet at [[uruk]] predate it (Moorey
1985:76-77; Maxwell-Hyslop 1971:1, cited by Potts). Goldworking techniques evident at
Ur include gilding, chasing, inlaying, casting, filigree, cloisonné, hard soldering,
and granulation (Alexander 1976); Ur III/Old Babylonian texts document controlled
goldsmith workshop operations in which gold loss during purification/lamination was
kept to a minuscule 0.27% (0.02-0.025 g per shekel). A twenty-year-old scholarly
consensus favoring the Pactolus (Lydia/Croesus) region as gold source, based on
platiniridium inclusions, has been overturned since platiniridium occurs in gold from
multiple sources (Maxwell-Hyslop 1977); cuneiform sources cite gold from Hahhum,
Mardaman, Meluhha, Harali, and Egypt (finding, Potts 1997). See
[[ur-royal-cemetery-debate]] for the fuller interpretive debate over the Royal
Cemetery's status.

### Drinking tubes and Royal Cemetery banquet practice

Drinking tubes found in the Royal Cemetery of Ur, together with banquet scenes in
glyptic (Selz 1983), suggest wine or beer was drunk communally from large mixing vats
via tubes, akin to the Greek *krater*, rather than from individual cups — a practice
that changed only with the Achaemenid-period introduction of Greek/Mediterranean-style
wine-sets (attested at Deve Höyük, Qalat al-Bahrain) (attributed, Potts 1997).

### Ivory quantities at Ur

Substantial ivory quantities are attested at Ur III Ur: UET III 1498 records 21+ minas
(~10.5 kg) of ivory; UET III 751 records a 38-mina (19 kg) tusk; a further UET III 1498
entry records 40 minas (20 kg) used by a woodworker for chair-making. D. Collon
concluded the ivory always derived from the Indian elephant (*Elephas maximus*) and
that elephants themselves were imported only intermittently (Collon 1977:222; finding,
Potts 1997). This Ur III ivory trade is part of the longer Mesopotamia-South Asia
exchange network treated on [[meluhha]] and [[mesopotamian-trade]], and long predates
the Seleucid-era war-elephant corps documented on [[seleucia-on-the-tigris]].

### Intramural infant burials under house chapels

At Ur in the Isin-Larsa period, Leonard Woolley found unusually high concentrations of
infant burials beneath domestic "house chapels" — up to 32 infant graves under a
single chapel — part of a wider pattern of intramural, sub-floor house burial that
J.N. Postgate (1990:234) observed was common at Ur and Sippar but absent from
excavated areas of [[nippur]]; Postgate suggested the difference may have been
pragmatic (urban families lacking rural land) rather than symbolic (attributed,
Postgate 1990, cited by Potts 1997; see [[nippur]] for the comparative absence).

### Woolley's terracotta sarcophagus and the Greek seal-impression study collection

A terracotta sarcophagus excavated by Woolley at Ur, beneath an "Achaemenid"-period
house, contained roughly 200 clay sealing impressions of Greek, Egyptian, Babylonian,
Assyrian, and Persian origin, plus a dozen Greek coin impressions, dated by an
Athenian coin (c. 450-440 BCE). E. Porada interpreted this find as a **study
collection** assembled by a non-Greek seal-cutter producing pseudo-Greek work,
evidence that Greek glyptic influence reached Mesopotamia well before Alexander's
331 BCE entry into Babylon (Porada 1960:228-34, cited by Potts 1997). See
[[seleucia-on-the-tigris]] for the wider pre-Alexander Greek-Mesopotamian contact
Potts situates this find within.

### Seleucid-era abandonment and the Euphrates's shift

Ur appears to have been abandoned not long after Alexander's death: the latest
securely dated finds are two tablets from the reigns of Alexander and Philip
Arrhidaeus (323-316 BCE). Woolley and J. Oelsner attribute Ur's desertion to a shift
in the Euphrates's course away from the city (Oelsner 1986:75-77, cited by Potts
1997) — contrast the variable continuity documented at [[uruk]] (which persisted
under the Seleucids into the Parthian period) and at [[babylon]] over the same
period.

### Human grave goods and the Royal Cemetery's retainer burials

Potts argues Mesopotamian burial was, from the Ubaid period onward, overwhelmingly
primary and individual, with little evidence of true collective burial. He holds the
Royal Graves at Ur — containing retainers of a high-status individual — do not
qualify as "collective burial" on this reading, since their primary purpose was
interment of a single individual, with the additional persons functioning as "human
grave goods" (attributed, Potts's own interpretive position; cf. Steinkeller's 1990/1980
analysis of a comparable pre-Sargonic Lagash burial, DP 75, connecting extensive grave
goods to human-sacrifice practice, and H. Schutzinger's reading of the up-to-eighty
individuals in a single Royal Grave as themselves treated as grave goods rather than
persons receiving proper funerary rites). See [[ur-royal-cemetery-debate]] for the
fuller treatment, including Roux's own more cautious framing above.
