# Thebes (Waset)

Upper Egyptian political and religious centre (modern Luxor region), capital and Amun cult hub across Middle and New Kingdoms. Narrative densification from [[wilkinson-toby|Wilkinson]] *[[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010|Rise and Fall]]* (2010); institutional temple frame from [[kemp-barry-j|Kemp]] *Anatomy* (2018).

**Naming:** Greek “Thebes”; Egyptian Waset. Not Boeotian Thebes.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Intensive excavation and epigraphy since the nineteenth century (Karnak, Luxor, West Bank). Wilkinson uses Thebes as pivot of reunifications (MK, early 18th Dynasty) and of late-period disasters (Assyrian sack).

## Occupation Sequence

Regional capital in reunifications; New Kingdom “Estate of Amun” monumentalization; Third Intermediate / Late Period political fragmentation; foreign occupations.

## Significance

1. **Amun temple city** and processional religion (Kemp NK temples).
2. **Imperial and civil-war capital** in Wilkinson’s narrative arcs.
3. **Valley of the Kings / Deir el-Medina** labour and royal burial complex.

## Key Debates

Capital vs Memphis dualism; scale of Amun estate power vs king.

## Historiography

Stub from Wilkinson/Kemp ingests; specialist Karnak/West Bank monographs outstanding.

## Wilkinson densification (2010)

*(Attributed — [[wilkinson-toby]], *[[wilkinson-rise-fall-ancient-egypt-2010]].)*

- **Reunification capital** in FIP–MK (Mentuhotep) and early 18th Dynasty (Ahmose/Theban loyalists).
- **New Kingdom** Amun estate, Deir el-Bahri (Hatshepsut), imperial wealth display.
- **Late New Kingdom–TIP** high-priest power, dual rule with northern dynasts.
- **Assyrian sack of Thebes** (Ashurbanipal era) as national trauma in Part V.
- **Place of Truth** necropolis workforce (Deir el-Medina complex) — strikes under Rameses III and tomb-robbery scandals in late Ramesside narrative.

## Assmann densification (1996/2002)

*(Attributed — [[assmann-jan]], *[[assmann-mind-of-egypt-1996|The Mind of Egypt]].)*

- **Theban theocracy.** Herihor's assumption of royal titulary and a new year-count (ca. 1080 BCE) institutionalized the [[theology-of-will]] as a "god-state" ruled by Amun's oracle — the closest Egyptian approach to direct theocracy in Assmann's typology; sovereignty expressed as control over "social time" (the calendar reset).
- **Holy city semantics.** Piye's victory stela has the army lay down arms and don priestly linen before entering Thebes — "completely unprecedented" theocratic staging in royal texts, per Assmann.
- **Late Period festival culture.** The Decade Festival (Amun-in-Luxor crossing to Medinet Habu/Djamut — whence Greek "Thebai") and the split of Amun into three "generations" transposed onto the gods functions once embodied by the king: "the symbolism of rule was replaced by a symbolism of time" (Assmann).
- Assmann suggests the Assyrian sack of 663 BCE "dried up for all time" the mythodynamics of holy war on Amun's behalf, pushing the Saite dynasty toward a strikingly secular self-presentation.
