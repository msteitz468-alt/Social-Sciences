## Discovery and Excavation History

Noen U-Loke is an Iron Age moated settlement and cemetery in the Upper Mun Valley of Northeast Thailand (Khorat Plateau). Higham reports the site was chosen for large mortuary exposures after prior Fine Arts Department work (Wichakana 1991). The mound covers about 12 ha and is ringed by up to five moats; nearby Non Muang Kao is larger (~50 ha, multiple channels) (as of Higham 2002).

Over two seasons Higham and Rachanie Thosarat excavated 220 m² to a depth of five metres, recovering 126 inhumation graves divided into five mortuary phases (MP) by depth and stratigraphy. Higham presents the sequence as covering virtually the entire regional Iron Age duration (as of Higham 2002). The research design (Higham & Thosarat) aimed to sample social ranking over time, technology, subsistence efficiency, and inter-community friction — warfare as a possible stimulus to leadership and iron weapon production — and to address the unresolved nature, purpose, and dating of surrounding banks and moats, which may extend more than 100 m from the mound edge (as of Higham 2002; R4-046–047).

## Occupation Sequence

**MP2 (earliest iron graves, c. 300–200 BCE preferred).** Six burials fall between radiocarbon brackets of 400–200 BCE preceding and 200–1 BCE later; Higham suggests earliest Iron Age interments within 300–200 BCE, preferably earlier. Iron first appears as ornaments (a woman with iron bangles and neck ring/torc), then as tools and weapons (a man with socketed iron hoe/axe and large socketed spearhead plus bronze spears, shell disks, bronze bangles/torcs, and a tiger-canine necklace). Pottery remains very similar to latest Bronze Age Ban Lum Khao (as of Higham 2002; R4-048).

**MP3 (probably first two centuries CE).** Burials form two tight multi-age clusters. First agate pendants and glass beads open exotic exchange; complete young pig skeletons appear as offerings. A major mortuary innovation is filling graves with white silicified rice (Higham notes a possible spiritual analogy to later Cambodian Great Lake village offerings of heated unhusked rice — ethnographic analogy, not direct evidence). Agate and especially carnelian expand in this phase; Theunissen et al. (2000), as cited by Higham, suggest a potential Central Thailand source rather than automatic Indian origin for agate/carnelian (as of Higham 2002; R4-049).

**MP4 (peak wealth).** Four north–south-oriented clusters show elaborations: clay-lined/sealed graves on beds of silicified rice; Phimai Black pottery (thin, burnished, lustrous black — Higham infers a specialised manufacturing centre); abundant spindle whorls (local weaving) while potting tools are rare. Woman burial 113 had a gold-and-agate necklace, two agate pendants, 38 bangles, 64 finger rings, seven toe rings, silver toe/finger rings, and an iron knife with fabric. The richest man (burial 14, aged 35–40) wore 75 bronze bangles per arm, three bronze belts, silver ear spirals covered in gold, many glass beads, an iron knife, and pottery around the cranium; infants could also be richly furnished (as of Higham 2002; R4-050).

**MP5 (after c. 300 CE, possibly a century or more later).** Same north-oriented inhumation tradition continues, but rice is no longer placed in graves, clustering ceases, and bronze range and quantity fall markedly. Iron offerings now include sickles and a tanged spearhead. One young man was found face-down with an iron arrowhead (~6 cm) embedded in his spine — Higham’s most graphic single indicator of interpersonal violence at the site (as of Higham 2002; R4-051).

**Technology package.** Higham synthesises local iron smelting/forging of laterite ore for ornament, agriculture, forest clearance, and conflict; local bronze casting of spirals, belts, and torcs with a surge in exchanged ingots; possible local glass-bead manufacture; two salt-making sites west of the main site accumulating in the Iron Age; high-quality mortuary ceramics; continued use of stone adzes; many spindle whorls and fabric remains (as of Higham 2002; R4-052).

**Moats and banks.** Basal deposits show an old stream channel and swampy conditions; sections across moats show slow-moving water deposits. Boyd et al. (1999), as cited by Higham, reconstruct a low-lying swampy habitat. Encircling banks and water-laid moat deposits at six Upper Mun sites have radiocarbon dates all within AD 1–500 (McGrath and Boyd); Higham concludes later Iron Age occupants invested energy in earthworks to control water, though purpose remains elusive — defensive, dry-season water storage, and/or fish supply are all possible. He notes a 7th-century Jayavarman I inscription mentioning autumn war when the enemy’s moats were dry as a later textual analogy only (as of Higham 2002; R4-053).

**Subsistence.** Rice is the subsistence mainstay (carbonised rice, burnt rice in ritual, iron agricultural implements), matching a regional surge in settlement number and size. Noen U-Loke at 12 ha contrasts with Non Muang Kao at least four times larger. Fauna are dominated by domestic cattle, pig, and water buffalo; surprisingly few fish bones appear despite fish placed in early mortuary vessels; shellfish come from swamps, fields, and streams (as of Higham 2002; R4-054).

**Conflict.** Iron and bronze weaponry from MP2; iron projectile points rarely enter mortuary offerings (except the embedded spine example) but dramatically increase in later contexts — Higham treats this as a potential indication of conflict and as a stimulus to leadership (as of Higham 2002; R4-055).

**Regional parallels.** Non Muang Kao (~10 km SE), excavated by O’Reilly (2000), preserves successive clay floors with postholes; clay-lined N–S graves with MP4-like goods; foundation occupation mid-Noen U-Loke sequence ~50 BC if the relative dating holds — a hint of growing population and new settlement foundation (as of Higham 2002; R4-056). Pasak Valley sites (Ban Chaibadan, Ban Pukree) show close mortuary parallels (glass, bronze bangles, iron tools, gold) (R4-060). Higham links richer late Iron Age interior burials contemporary with Oc Eo’s coastal emporium to the broader process feeding [[southeast-asian-state-formation]] (R5-020, R5-058).

## Significance

Noen U-Loke is Higham’s (2002) best-documented window on Mun Valley Iron Age social ranking, craft intensification, water control, and conflict beyond the Han imperial frontier. It anchors the contrast with [[dong-son-culture]] warrior chiefdoms under Qin–Han pressure: shielded by the Truong Son and Lao uplands, Mun–Chi communities followed a trajectory of indigenous complexity, population growth, and international trade rather than rapid incorporation as Chinese commanderies (as of Higham 2002; R4-003). The site feeds models of secondary state formation via a “multiplier effect” — high-status mortuary energy, iron weaponry, industrial salt, iron agriculture, and water-controlling walls contemporaneous with Mekong Delta offshore exchange (R5-058). Linked phenomena and debates: [[chiefdom]], [[southeast-asian-state-formation]], [[indianisation-southeast-asia-debate]], [[ban-non-wat]], [[ban-don-ta-phet]].

## Key Debates

- **Moat function** — defensive vs dry-season water vs fishery; dating AD 1–500 for encircling earthworks at several Upper Mun sites does not settle purpose (Higham 2002).
- **Iron origins in Chao Phraya/Mekong** — independent recognition of iron ore properties vs maritime introduction of iron knowledge and Indian glass/carnelian; Higham holds both possible for the region (R4-001–002).
- **Agate/carnelian sources** — formerly assumed Indian; geochemical work (Theunissen et al. 2000 via Higham) raises Central Thailand as a potential source (R4-049).
- **Sa Huynh / Cham inland infiltration** — Higham speculates that Mun Valley urn burials (Ban Kan Luang, Ban Sawan) *might* reflect Cham-speaking people crossing Truong Son passes; weak analogy only (R4-030).

## Historiography

Earlier Mun Valley work by Quaritch-Wales (Thamen Chai, Muang Phet) found iron to basal layers and treated iron as Indian contact with first-millennium AD occupation — a model Higham’s later radiocarbon and mortuary sequences revise downward into the mid–late first millennium BC (as of Higham 2002; R4-037). Williams-Hunt (1950) aerial recognition of Mun–Chi moated/banked mounds established the settlement hierarchy Higham’s excavations sample. Moore (1986) size groups (small ≤20 ha, intermediate 21–40 ha, large up to 68 ha) frame Noen U-Loke as intermediate within a landscape of differentially large centres (R4-043, R4-058).
