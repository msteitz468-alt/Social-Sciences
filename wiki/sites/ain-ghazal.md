# ‘Ain Ghazal

Large Pre-Pottery Neolithic B town on the Wadi Zarqa near Amman, Jordan. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is the type-case for late PPNB population boom, public ritual (plaster statues and special buildings), and economic collapse into nomadic pastoralism—three to four times the size of [[jericho|Jericho]] at its peak.

**Identity caution:** Site name and archaeological culture labels (PPNB) are not ethnic, linguistic, or genetic identities.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: excavations directed by **Gary Rollefson** from 1983 onward recovered two caches of plaster statues—the only intact plaster statues then known (fragments known from Nahal Hemar and Jericho). First cache: 12 statues + 13 busts in one pit (large figures oriented E–W); second cache ~200 years later more standardised, including three two-headed busts; reed-bundle cores coated in plaster. Architectural sequence includes apsidal “neighbourhood” structures, repeatedly refloored circular shrines, and a slope-top “temple” (no plaster floor; red-painted hearth, limestone slabs, anthropomorphic pillar). Small clay spheres, discs, and pyramids may have been accounting devices (e.g. field allocation). Turkish obsidian and Red Sea coral document long-distance exchange.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Rollefson / Mithen 2003, ch.10–11.)*

1. **Boom.** Town grows to roughly three–four times Jericho’s size; population explosion from natural growth plus refugees from eroded abandoned villages; terraced slopes; by the 6500 BCE narrative horizon soils exhausted and valley sides barren. Fauna include ≥45 wild species—some meat possibly from desert hunter-gatherers as domestic meat dominates prestige/economy.
2. **Public ritual.** Plaster statues mark a shift (Rollefson/Mithen) to more public and possibly centralised religious activity; special buildings as above. Denise Schmandt-Besserat uses later Babylonian analogy to read statues as banished ghosts or a pantheon (two-headed figures; breast-exposing poses)—analogy flagged, not identity of religions.
3. **Peak and collapse.** Peak ~30 acres and >2,000 people; by ~6300 BCE advanced decline—abandoned houses, shabby new builds; soil exhaustion/erosion, deforested landscape, long travel for crops/fodder, polluted river, catastrophic infant mortality; return to hamlets. Mithen generalises “complete economic collapse” for PPNB towns of the Jordan valley.
4. **Drivers.** Ice-core evidence of low temperatures and unreliable rainfall/drought ~6400–6000 BCE; Mithen states relative impacts of human farming vs climate on the barren landscape are “quite impossible to untangle.”
5. **Aftermath.** Town life unsustainable; replaced by nomadic pastoralism continuing to the ethnographic present in Mithen’s framing; ‘Ain Ghazal becomes a seasonal gathering place for goat herders among ruins.
6. **Exchange.** Cappadocian obsidian (via multi-stage exchange); coral and coloured stones; late PPNB interaction sphere with traders, Bouqras craft goods, Çayönü copper, etc.

## Significance

- Largest well-known southern Levantine PPNB mega-site and the unique source of intact plaster statues.
- Case study for early urban-scale stress, environmental degradation, and the farming-to-pastoral transition in the Jordan valley.
- Symbolic and ritual evidence for public cult and possible administrative tokens within a pre-pottery economy.
- Foil for more durable settlement trajectories in Mesopotamia after ~6500 BCE (Mithen’s contrast toward “civilisation” pathways).

## Key Debates

- Human overexploitation vs mid-Holocene climate as causes of collapse—explicitly entangled in Mithen’s account; no sole `caused_by` from climate.
- Meaning of plaster statues (ancestor/ghost cult, pantheon, social display)—Schmandt-Besserat’s Babylonian analogy is a position, not a finding.
- Degree of centralisation implied by “temples” and statue caches.
- Role of wild-game procurement and desert foragers in a domestic-meat economy.
- How far Jordan-valley collapse generalises to all PPNB towns vs local trajectories.
