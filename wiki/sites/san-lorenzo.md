## Discovery and Excavation History

Matthew Stirling discovered San Lorenzo in 1945 after being led to a colossal stone head reported as a curious "eye" seen along a trail (Coe & Koontz 2013). Michael D. Coe (see [[thinkers/coe-michael-d]]) directed a Yale University archaeological-ecological project at the site from 1966 to 1968, excavating, among other features, the site's drain system in 1967. Ann Cyphers of Mexico's National Autonomous University has directed extensive further investigations since 1991 (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Occupation Sequence

San Lorenzo was first settled around 1700 BCE, possibly by Mixe-Zoquean-speaking migrants from the Soconusco region, and was thoroughly Olmec in character by 1500 BCE (Coe & Koontz 2013). For roughly three centuries thereafter it was, in the authors' words, "several times larger than any other settlement in Mesoamerica," prompting archaeologists John Clark and Mary Pye's assessment that "San Lorenzo had no equals, only peers." One stone-sculpture workshop, shown by Cyphers to have specialized in recarving monuments, ceased operating around 1200 BCE, coinciding with a marked decline in site activity and population; Coe & Koontz note the cause is unclear — possibly a shift in the river's course disrupting distribution networks, or, more speculatively, internal or external unrest, "although the evidence for this is not as abundant as once thought" (Coe & Koontz 2013).

The site radiocarbon-dates the onset of Olmec civilization there to "around 1200 BC" under older, uncalibrated readings; calibration moves the true date roughly 300 years earlier, toward 1500 BCE (Coe & Koontz 2013) — a caution against comparing calibrated and uncalibrated Olmec chronologies uncritically.

The settlement itself was mapped as an artificial plateau roughly 150 ft (45 m) high and about 3/4 mile (1.2 km) north-south, built up to a depth of 23 ft (7 m), with long ridges on its northwest, west, and south sides arranged in mirror symmetry; Coe & Koontz report the (unconfirmed) suggestion that the whole plateau was intended as a giant bird-effigy, never completed.

## Significance

San Lorenzo is treated by Coe & Koontz as the earliest of the major Olmec centers and, on the "mother culture" reading (see [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]]), the seat of Mesoamerica's first state-level society. More than 124 Early Preclassic sculptures have been recovered, including ten colossal heads up to 9 ft 4 in (2.85 m) tall, interpreted as portraits of individual rulers wearing distinctive headgear resembling football helmets. David Grove demonstrated that the large flat-topped basalt "altars" — some up to 40 metric tons — functioned instead as thrones, their niche figures holding either a Maize God infant (a royal-descent motif) or a rope binding captives (a warfare/conquest motif) (Coe & Koontz 2013).

Coe & Koontz frame the rise of the first Mesoamerican state at San Lorenzo as resulting from (1) an environment of high agricultural potential — year-round rains, wet-season river inundation, abundant fish — and (2) differential elite access to the best river-levee land, explicitly likened to "the gift of the Nile." The settlement was socially stratified: elite palatial structures, including a "Red Temple" with basalt drains, columns, and attached stone-sculpture workshops, occupied the summit, with more modest houses on the terraced slopes, as documented by a survey led by Roberto Lunagómez.

A drain system excavated by Coe's team in 1967 comprised 560 ft (170 m) of pecked basalt troughs with covers and three subsidiary lines, believed to have drained ceremonial pools; comparable drains are known from [[sites/la-venta]] and Laguna de los Cerros. Basalt for the monumental sculptures was sourced from Cerro Cintepec in the Tuxtla Mountains, some 50 miles (80 km) away, and is presumed to have been moved by balsa raft down to the Gulf and up the Coatzacoalcos River before being dragged or rolled to the site. Thousands of obsidian artifacts recovered — blades, dart points, bone-working tools — were sourced via trace-element analysis to highland Mexico and Guatemala, since no natural obsidian occurs in the Gulf Coast heartland.

Diet centered on fish (snook) and domestic dog, with only slight interest in deer or peccary hunting; human bones bearing butchering and burn marks were also recovered, which Coe & Koontz interpret, in attributed voice, as "an indication of their cannibalistic propensities." Marine toad (*Bufo marinus*) bones were also found, possibly connected to the hallucinogen bufotenine.

San Lorenzo lies near [[sites/el-manati]], a waterlogged ritual deposit associated with the same Olmec population, and predates the rise of [[sites/la-venta]] as the Gulf Coast Olmec center after San Lorenzo's decline.

## Key Debates

San Lorenzo's priority as the earliest major Olmec center anchors one side of the [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]]: Stirling's original claim that Olmec civilization predates the Classic Maya was vehemently opposed by Mayanist Sir Eric Thompson but backed by Alfonso Caso and Miguel Covarrubias; Coe & Koontz state that "time has fully borne out Stirling and the Mexican school" (2013) — an attributed verdict, not an uncontested wiki-voice fact, given ongoing revisionist positions questioning the primacy of the Gulf Coast heartland.

## From Flannery (ed.) 1976 — catchment and exchange links

Short attributed addendum from *[[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-1976|The Early Mesoamerican Village]]* (Flannery ed. 1976; study [[flannery-early-mesoamerican-village-study]]). Does not supersede Coe & Koontz synthesis above.

### Rossmann site-catchment analysis

David L. Rossmann applied Vita-Finzi–Higgs-style [[site-catchment-analysis]] to San Lorenzo, Potrero Nuevo, and Tenochtitlán near the Coatzacoalcos River, using Coe’s 1966–68 excavations and ~75 km² survey (mapping, soils, ethnography, land use). Peak San Lorenzo phase extent ~1150–900 B.C.; Coe house-mound estimates San Lorenzo ~1000 persons, Tenochtitlán ~1000 (Rossmann flags likely overestimate for Early Formative), Potrero Nuevo ~250. Catchments defined to 5 km from site edge; sites abstracted as circles (SL diameter 1 km); land classes by kilometer ring from Coe soil maps (lomas, *tierra de primera* levee, *tierra de potrero* savanna, marshes). Rossmann reported San Lorenzo’s near catchment dominated by upland lomas (1-km ring over 95% lomas; total catchment more than half lomas). At modern yields he argued weighted lomas could produce far more maize than Coe’s 1000 persons required; under Early Formative maize ~250 kg/ha (Kirkby), ~800 ha yearly cultivation for 1000 persons would imply reduced fallow and stress. He cautioned that raw land-type percentages mislead without institutions: inverse size–*primera* relation would imply lomas preference under cookbook Vita-Finzi logic, contradicting modern levee value; San Lorenzo as ceremonial-civic center may have commanded levee as tribute; Potrero Nuevo and Tenochtitlán might be barrios of one giant San Lorenzo catchment. Catchment sketches walking-distance resources, not the network of control (including basalt moved tens of kilometers).

### Exchange networks (Pires-Ferreira; Earle NNA)

Jane Wheeler Pires-Ferreira’s Early Formative obsidian matrices place San Lorenzo on major long-distance nets: **Guadalupe Victoria** (eastern Puebla) 62.2% of SL sample (~300 km) vs 90.8% at smaller Tabasco Chontalpa sites (~450 km) and 36.5% Oaxaca (~200 km); **El Chayal** (Guatemala, ~580 km) 21.7% at SL; **Barranca de los Estetes** only 4.8% Early Formative (possible indirect via Guadalupe Victoria–Oaxaca links). Middle Formative: breakdown/realignment of Guadalupe Victoria system and dissolution of traditional Gulf Coast–Oaxaca ties after monument destruction at San Lorenzo and La Venta’s rise (SL–Oaxaca Q-type average correlation +.38 → −.01); Barranca de los Estetes at SL rises 4.8% → 26.3%. Coe-found small flat iron-ore mirrors identical to those from a San José Mogote residential ward → proposed Oaxaca–Gulf elite exchange (Pires-Ferreira/Flannery). Earle’s nearest-neighbor analysis treats San Lorenzo with La Venta and Tres Zapotes as broadly contemporaneous highest-order ceremonial-civic centers ~1000–600 B.C. with tendency toward **regular spacing** (mutual antagonism / competition reading); Estero Rabón ~24 km west of San Lorenzo checked against a secondary-center prediction ~15 km from major centers. Marcus/Flannery scale note: San Lorenzo ~53 ha by 900 B.C.; with Tenochtitlán + Potrero Nuevo within ~3 km, regional total ≤2500 in ~80 ha — density comparable to Winter’s Early Formative Tierras Largas households/ha; San José Mogote ~half San Lorenzo size in the same comparative tables.
