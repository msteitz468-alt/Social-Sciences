## Discovery and Excavation History

Cuzco (Quechua *Qosqo*), capital of Tahuantinsuyu, was entered by Spaniards in 1533 after Cajamarca. Colonial churches and houses overlie and recycle Inca masonry (notably Santo Domingo over the Coricancha). Modern archaeology works in constrained urban contexts; ethnohistory and standing architecture carry much of the interpretive load ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]).

### From D'Altroy 2002

As of [[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]], early Inca archaeology at the capital included Valcárcel’s 1930s work at [[saqsawaman]] (recovering pre-imperial Killke pottery), Rowe’s Cuzco archaeology, and later UNESCO-era mapping/conservation; as of his writing there was still no complete published regional archaeological survey of the Cuzco basin, though estate studies such as [[ollantaytambo]] (Protzen) and Yucay (Niles) advanced knowledge of royal landscapes. D'Altroy treats Cieza de León’s mid-century interviews with Cuzco aristocrats and Polo de Ondegardo’s magistracies (including the 1559 recovery of royal mummies still venerated around the city) as load-bearing ethnohistoric frames. Factional native histories (Betanzos for Pachakuti’s descendants; the Probanza de Qhapaq Ayllu from [[khipu]]; Toledo/Sarmiento’s official history) mean “Cuzco’s past” is not a single archive.

## Occupation Sequence

Post-[[wari-culture|Wari]] local polities (Killke ceramics and related) precede imperial Inca urban form. Moseley synthesizes Cuzco as dual-organized (*hanan*/*hurin*), with royal *panaka* lineages, the Coricancha as solar and imperial pantheon focus, and a *ceque* system of ritual sight-lines radiating to *huacas* in the heartland. Sacsahuamán crowns the northern rim as cyclopean complex (temple/fort readings both attested). The "puma-shaped city" plan is a debated ethnohistoric/architectural interpretation, not established fact (Moseley records the debate).

### From D'Altroy 2002

D'Altroy reports that origin legend places Inca ancestors at Pacariqtambo and organizes allied Tambos into two sets of five [[ayllu]] — Hanan Cuzco and Hurin Cuzco — foreshadowing the dual structure of Spanish-era Cuzco. He treats Betanzos’s claim that Pachakuti spent some twenty years renovating the capital after the Chanka wars as a single-chronicle narrative of political rebuild, not a verified construction calendar. At Spanish arrival, he reports each half of elite society included five royal [[panaqa]] and five non-royal kin groups, with late Upper Cuzco *panaqa* (especially Hatun ayllu of Pachakuti’s descendants and Qhapaq ayllu of Thupa Inka Yupanki) dominant in civil, military, and religious hierarchies until the Waskhar–Atawallpa war virtually eradicated Qhapaq ayllu and burned Thupa Inka Yupanki’s mummy.

Urban layout (as of D'Altroy 2002): Hanan Cuzco northwest of the main plazas (socially and topographically above), Hurin Cuzco southeast; twelve surrounding districts, three for each of the four *suyu*; roads toward the four parts of the empire. Suburban neighborhoods formed an **ethnic microcosm** — Pacariqtambo-origin non-royal *ayllu* in southern Cayoache; Chachapoyas and Cañaris in positions analogous to their NW-empire origins; Chilque and Qolla limited or denied access for historical resistance. Each great provincial lord was supposed to keep a house in Cuzco and spend four months a year there, with one son in continuous residence to learn Quechua and Inca ways. Greater Cuzco extended several km of aristocrat/service residences, fields, canals, and *qollqa*; sacred agricultural fields (Bauer) included the first-maize planting field with harvests reserved for shrines.

**Sacred landscape:** D'Altroy reports at least 332 and probably more than 400 *wak’a* (springs, stones, peaks) organized on the [[zeqe-system]]; Sherbondy argued more than one-third were water/hydraulic; year-long cycles assigned *panaqa* and affiliated *ayllu* to shrine lines; Hanan controlled upper-valley water and canals, Hurin the lower. [[saqsawaman]] on the rocky promontory above the city is called the grandest architectural complex in the empire; tradition attributes inauguration to Pachakuti or Thupa Inka Yupanki; Cieza’s rotating provincial workforce of 20,000 (4,000 quarry, 6,000 haul, others dig/lay foundations) is a scale claim for [[mita-labor]] at the citadel. Nearby royal estates (Pisac, [[ollantaytambo]], [[machu-picchu]], Yucay, Chinchero) are treated as countryside extensions of capital politics under [[split-inheritance]] and *panaqa* holding.

## Significance

Administrative and sacred navel of the four *suyu*; terminus of the highway system; stage for mitmaq elite education and panaka estate politics. First Spanish descriptions (Cieza and others) fix the image of gold-sheathed temples that structured European reception of the Andes.

### From D'Altroy 2002

D'Altroy frames Cuzco as the convergence of a four-part world and the most elaborate ritual complex in the native Americas via the *zeq'e* network of *wak’a* (see [[huaca]], [[qhapaq-ucha]]). Provincial design often imitated capital form: “New Cuzcos” such as [[huanuco-pampa]] and Inkawasi, Tumipampa in Ecuador, and the ethnic microcosm of suburban neighborhoods. Sons of high provincial lords were held/trained in Cuzco (arms, Quechua, Inca religion/history, [[khipu]] accounting). The 1536 siege — chroniclers’ figures of ~200,000–400,000 soldiers and personnel against a few hundred Spaniards and barely more than 1,000 native auxiliaries — ended largely, D'Altroy argues, because soldier-farmers disbanded for the agricultural cycle; capture of Saqsawaman reduced direct pressure on the city.

### From Moore 2005 — ceremonial calendar and royal processions

Via [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Jerry Moore (2005)]], Cristóbal de Molina (parish priest in Cuzco, writing 1570–1584) records the Inca ceremonial year: a new-moon mid-May Sun-dedicated cycle; a June lull for irrigation/planting; a July harvest ceremony to Viracocha; the multi-phase August **Situa** cleansing and protection of Cuzco (rites of separation followed by progressive reincorporation of social groups); a September/October ear-piercing initiation for young nobles; and the November **Ccapac Raymi**, whose processional circuits moved from the main plaza (Haucaypata) out to shrines in each of the four *suyus* and back, progressively enlarging to include the hilltops around Cuzco (attributed, Moore 2005; see [[huaca]]).

Cieza de León (1998:206–209) describes Atahualpa's November 1532 procession into the plaza of Cajamarca before the first battle with Pizarro's forces: it took four hours to cover four kilometers, led by a phalanx of thousands of soldiers and the Inca's litter, followed by 5,000 more soldiers with the *ayllus* and some 70,000 total service people (not counting women), accompanied by large drums, many trumpets, and raised banners. Juan de Betanzos (1987:185–86) describes a year-long royal procession by Huayna Capac in which the Inca donned local dress for each province entered, sat on a prepared throne in the plaza, received llama sacrifices and chicha offerings, then danced, sang, and ate with the local population — Moore frames this as scripted political theater, calling it, in his own phrase, **"dynamic diplomacy"** rooted in Andean reciprocity and fictive kinship even in asymmetric relations (attributed, Moore 2005, citing John Murra 1980).

## Key Debates

- Imperial foundation date vs long local development (ethnohistory vs radiocarbon/ceramic sequences).
- Degree to which Cuzco's dual layout and ceques are uniquely Inca vs deeper Andean pattern.
- How much "Inca Cuzco" was Pachacuti-generation rebuild vs accretion.
- Competing models of central government as of D'Altroy 2002: absolute divine monarchy tempered by aristocratic negotiation; generative *panaqa* hierarchy via [[split-inheritance]] (modal scholarly view); dual Hanan/Hurin ranking of capital and empire (Upper linked to Chinchaysuyu/Antisuyu, Lower to Kollasuyu/Cuntisuyu); Zuidema’s diarchy (always two kings — minority view in D'Altroy’s presentation); dynamic rewriting of kin-group history.
- Source reliability: kin-group-centered native histories and Toledo’s political agenda in Sarmiento’s official history; Atawallpa’s factional destruction of historians and *khipu* after the dynastic war (attributed via Callapiña et al.).
