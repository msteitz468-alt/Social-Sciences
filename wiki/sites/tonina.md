# Tonina

## Discovery and Excavation History

Classic Maya capital of the western highland–lowland transition in Chiapas. [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler (2006)]] place Tonina near the northwest margin of the northern highlands as a major capital in a transitional highland–lowland valley serving important trade routes (paired geographically with [[copan]] near the eastern margin of the southern highlands). This page densifies from their synthesis; detailed excavation monographs remain outstanding for deeper stratigraphic resolution.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Sharer & Traxler 2006.)*

- **Geographic role:** Highlands generally above 800 m; geologically active; *tierra templada* and *fría* climates; permanent settlement and agriculture later than on the Pacific coast in their outline. Later defensible highland capitals of ethnohistoric fame (Utatlán, Iximché, Mixco Viejo) belong to a different Postclassic highland sequence; Tonina’s Classic career sits in the earlier lowland-linked western corridor.
- **Early Classic intersite mentions:** Chak Tok Ich’aak II’s death (AD 508) is mentioned at Tonina in the Tikal dynastic turmoil narrative—evidence of western lowland–highland communication networks.
- **Late Classic warfare:** Western frontier conflicts include the AD 711 capture of a [[palenque]] king by Tonina (noted on the Palenque page tradition and consistent with Sharer & Traxler’s war landscape). Piedras Negras narratives in the same synthesis record sajal-level warfare and hierarchy in the broader Usumacinta–western zone of which Tonina is a highland-edge peer.
- **Terminal Classic and last Long Count:** By AD 900 only vestiges of traditional polities remained in the central/southern lowlands; dynasties of divine kings dominating since the Late Preclassic had all but vanished; dedication of dynastic monuments ceased at most capitals. **The last known stela with a Long Count date is Tonina Monument 101 at 10.4.0.0.0 (AD 909).** Earlier Morley-era barometers placed the last date at 10.3.0.0.0 (AD 889) before shifting to 909 with Tonina.

## Significance

Chronological anchor for the end of the Classic dynastic monument tradition—the “last Long Count stela” site in standard teaching narratives as of Sharer & Traxler 2006. Geographic type-case of a highland–lowland trade-route capital. Western peer/rival of [[palenque]] in Late Classic warfare. Feeds [[classic-maya-collapse-debate]] as evidence that monument cessation tracks decline of [[kuhul-ajaw|divine kingship]], not disappearance of Maya peoples.

## Key Debates

- **Long Count end ≠ civilizational death:** Sharer & Traxler argue the end of Long Count monuments is only one symptom of multi-century decline of divine kings; [[el-mirador]] rose and fell without Long Count monuments; northern and lakes-region Maya continued.
- **Morley’s stela barometer** for the “Old Empire”—Uaxactun/Tikal starts, peak AD 790, last date revised to Tonina 909—historiographic rather than purely empirical framing.
- Western Maya geopolitics: Tonina–Palenque and broader Usumacinta hierarchies.

## Historiography

Tonina entered textbook chronology primarily as the terminal date-point of the Long Count stela tradition. Sharer & Traxler 2006 retain that chronological role while demoting it from a civilization-end marker to a symptom of divine-kingship’s multi-century fade. ## Densification from Martin & Grube 2008

*(Attributed — [[martin-grube-chronicle-maya-kings-2008|Martin & Grube 2008]], Tonina chapter. Complements Sharer & Traxler densification above.)*

### Hillside capital and militarist visual culture

Martin & Grube describe Tonina’s ceremonial core carved into a steep limestone ridge as a sequence of **seven terraces**; from the plain the Acropolis appears as a multi-step pyramid crowded with temples and palaces at every level. Ocosingo Valley—oaks and pines of the Chiapas highlands rather than lowland plains—~40 miles (64.5 km) south of great rival [[palenque]]. One of few highland sites with a full Classic dynasty, in close contact with lowland cultural ferment and a key military role between the two zones. Traditionally viewed as dour and militaristic; recent discoveries reveal virtuoso stucco equalling Palenque. Distinctive free-standing statues carved fully in-the-round; enthusiasm for freestanding sculpture only exceeded by Late Classic [[copan]]. Bound-prisoner sculptures are a special fixture of the city’s militaristic reputation (Monument 99 a rare female captive). Excavated by Pierre Becquelin and Claude Baudez (French Mission, 1972–1980); later INAH under Juan Yadeun. Surface remains almost exclusively Late and Terminal Classic; Early Classic thin.

Early named king **Jaguar Bird Tapir** (accession 563; Monument 168, 577) introduced three-dimensional portraiture. First foreign mention: Chinikiha throne records capture of a Tonina lord in 573. **K’inich Bahlam Chapaat** took office age nine in **615**—only months before equally youthful Pakal at Palenque, when Palenque’s dynasty was shattered. **Aj k’uhuun** and other nobles form remarkable continuity across reigns, taking special prominence during the city’s several **child-kingships**. **Ruler 2** (inaugurated 668) expanded freestanding stelae and captive sculpture; Martin & Grube now believe his reign ended ingloriously at Palenque’s hands (linking to Kan Bahlam II’s **687** victory).

### Baaknal Chaak and the struggle for regional overkingship

Tonina rebounded under warrior king **K’inich Baaknal Chaak** (crowned at 35 in **688**), inheriting Ruler 2’s *aj k’uhuun* and soon joined by Aj Ch’anaah (*yajaw k’ahk’*, “Lord of Fire”—key military office). Principal theme: conflict with [[palenque]] and struggle for overkingship of the region. In **692** he conducted a star war against Kan Bahlam of Palenque (oblique childhood-name reference); principal captive lord K’awiil Mo’. Though ~40 miles apart as crow flies, mountainous terrain made effective distance greater; primary access to the Classic heartland ran east through river valleys to the Usumacinta—where Baaknal Chaak challenged Palenque near its peak extent.

Victories celebrated in sunken **Ballcourt 1** (eastern Great Plaza)—probably dedicated **699** for Usumacinta campaigns; sloping walls with projecting torsos of sculpted captives over feather-framed shields; all identifiable captives were vassals of arch-enemy Palenque. Client captives include **Yax Ahk** of Anaay Te’/Anaite (southern Usumacinta bank between [[piedras-negras]] and [[yaxchilan]]) and **Chan Maas** of La Mar (“Rabbit Stone”), seizure fixed to **693**. By conquest Baaknal Chaak established himself as overking with widespread authority: a Bonampak column has the local king describe himself as Baaknal Chaak’s vassal (posthumous reference from 715 for a reign beginning 702). Last secure date 704; successor inaugurated 708.

### Child-king Ruler 4 and the 711 capture of Palenque’s king

**Ruler 4** (undeciphered fiery-jaguar name)—youngest known to assume sacred kingship: born 706, enthroned at two in November 708 (*ch’ok* emphasized). Reign dominated by nobles **K’elen Hix** and **Aj Ch’anaah**. Despite a toddler king, lieutenants achieved stunning success in **711**: Tonina forces penetrated the Palenque capital in a star war and seized **K’an Joy Chitam** (younger brother of Kan Bahlam). Sandstone panel: Palenque ruler crouched and twisted, still in jade jewelry, arms tied, paper strips through earlobes (garb of the vanquished); eroded date best read **9.13.19.13.3 = 26 August 711**. Latest evidence (David Stuart as reported) indicates the Palenque ruler was freed to resume kingship—ransom, tribute, or sworn vassalage. Another captive’s loincloth reads Aj Chiik Nahb (“He of Calakmul”)—direct engagement vs. allied warriors unresolved. K’elen Hix died 717 (Memorial Monument 165); by 721 Aj Ch’anaah held primary position. 1992 fifth-terrace giant stucco mural of *wayob* spirit companions (skeletal “Turtle-foot Death,” sacrificial heads on feathered scaffold)—otherworld symbolism matching burial concentration on that terrace.

### Later kings and last Long Count 909

**K’inich Ich’aak Chapaat** (Ruler 5) acceded age 14 in 723; fire-entering into Baaknal Chaak’s tomb June 730 (42 solar years and 59 Tzolk’in after 688 accession); last heard 739—captive processions strangely absent. Sparse mid-8th-century records; **K’inich Tuun Chapaat** (Ruler 6) linked to a new Palenque conflict (bound prisoner + Palenque emblem; king’s name on missing stone). Final flowering under **Ruler 8** (born 756): reworked upper Acropolis; styled *aj bolon baak* “He of Many Captives”; star-war conquest of **Sak Tz’i’** and capture of its ruler; 789 war on Pomoy; last inscription Monument 95 in **806**, then ~30 years’ silence. Relative isolation may factor in longevity (epigraphic record currently outlasts Palenque by ~110 years), but the record is broken and uninterrupted continuity is unclear. **Ruler 9 / Uh Chapaat** pedestal 837; crude half-sized Monument 158 by Ruler 10 in **904**; five years later **Monument 101**—last inscription and **last Long Count date in the entire Maya area: 10.4.0.0.0 = 15 January 909**; king’s name lost—“Tonina’s story ends as it began, in anonymity.” Late ceramics show sparse occupation perhaps a century more; colonial governance little known until Dominicans founded a church at Ocosingo (13 km west) in 1545.
