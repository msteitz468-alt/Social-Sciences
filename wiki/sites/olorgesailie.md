# Olorgesailie

A Middle Pleistocene Acheulean locality in the Gregory Rift Valley, Kenya, treated here as
documented in [[isaac-glynn|Glynn Isaac]]'s chapter of *[[lee-devore-man-the-hunter-1968|Man
the Hunter]]* (1968). Olorgesailie is a landmark example of **living-floor archaeology** and
of the [[ethnographic-analogy|ethnographic-analogy]] debate in that volume.

## Discovery and Excavation History

*(Findings — Isaac 1968.)* The site is a sedimentary formation deposited over ~50 square
miles, exposed by erosion over less than 5 square miles, up to ~200 ft (60 m) thick, its
cultural material falling within a single culture-stratigraphic unit of the "Acheulean
Industrial Complex." It was worked by L. S. B. and Mary Leakey (Leakey defined "Land Surface
7" in 1952) and later, in detail, by Isaac and his wife, under permit from the Government of
Kenya and with Wenner-Gren, Boise Fund, British Academy, and British Institute support. As a
report of controlled excavation of discrete occupation horizons, it is a comparatively strong
witness for its period.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Isaac.)* At the largest excavated horizon (the "Main Site," Member 7), Isaac
and Leakey recovered a >1,000 kg occupation floor (~19 × 13 m) of stone, bone, and rubble
dominated by the remains of at least **50 adult/subadult and 13+ juvenile extinct giant
baboons (*Simopithecus oswaldi*)** — interpreted as either sustained specialized baboon
hunting or a single mass-kill event. Isaac read the assemblages as indicating Acheulean groups
of ~4 to ~30 adults, with real stylistic idiosyncrasy suggesting group stability, and an
opportunistic subsistence pattern (meat socially prestigious, plant food dietarily dominant,
by analogy to the [[juhoansi|Bushmen]] and [[hadza|Hadza]]).

## Clark 1961 (global outline)

*[[clark-world-prehistory-1961|World Prehistory: An Outline]]* places Olorgesailie among East African lakeside camping-places of hand-axe (Acheulean) hunters — open-air living floors with fauna and bifaces illustrating Lower Palaeolithic ecology outside the classic European cave sequence (attributed, Clark 1961 ch. 2). No detailed Isaac-style living-floor analysis; use Isaac 1968 (above) for excavation depth.

## Significance

Olorgesailie anchors two arguments. **Taphonomy:** Isaac's actualistic experiments (with M.
Posnansky) showed that a protected goat skeleton lost most thin bones to weathering within ~7
years and that scavengers dispersed marked bones beyond a 30–50 yard radius — evidence used to
explain low bone density on Acheulean sites without denying hunting or scavenging.
**Analogy:** Isaac explicitly linked the baboon-bone concentration to Woodburn's report of
[[hadza|Hadza]] night baboon-drives, offered as a "plausible explanation" and flagged as an
untestable hypothesis — a textbook case of [[ethnographic-analogy]] as hypothesis-generator.

## Key Debates

Olorgesailie sits inside the volume's [[ethnographic-analogy|analogy debate]]: Isaac argued
ethnography supplies indispensable hypotheses and calibration; L. Binford and Freeman argued
archaeological interpretation must stand on independently testable propositions, not analogy.

## Historiography

The interpretation rests on Isaac's excavation and on relative (stratigraphic) dating; no
absolute dates appear in the 1968 source, and later work has refined the site's chronology and
the hunting-vs-scavenging reading (not primary-ingested here). The theoretical lens is early
**processual** — Isaac's insistence on archaeologically answerable questions and actualistic
experiment prefigures the New Archaeology.
