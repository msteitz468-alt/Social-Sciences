## Discovery and Excavation History

Chan Chan, on the north coast near modern Trujillo (Moche Valley), is the adobe capital of Chimor. Large-scale mapping and excavation (including work associated with [[moseley-michael|Moseley]]) established the sequence of walled royal compounds (*ciudadelas*), craft sectors, and hydraulic features ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]). Colonial looting of burial platforms (*huacas sepulturas*) is itself ethnohistoric evidence for ancestor architecture. [[moore-cultural-landscapes-andes-2005|Moore (2005)]] synthesizes the Chan Chan–Moche Valley Project's household and elite-architecture record (Day, J. Topic, Klymyshyn, Kolata, Conrad, Moseley and Mackey) as a case study in "house societies," attributed.

## Occupation Sequence

Moseley reports a walled urban footprint on the order of ~20 km² with a dense civic core (~6 km²) and large craft-working population. Classic *ciudadela* form (e.g. Rivero, Tschudi compounds in the literature) shows restricted access, *audiencia* U-shaped courts, storage, and burial platforms — read as successive royal seats under [[dual-organization]] and ancestor-veneration "glue." Maritime iconography (sea creatures, related friezes) continues Moche-era ocean themes into Chimor ideology. Late hydraulic stress: failed Chicama inter-valley canal project and sunken gardens (*wachaque*) as responses to water crisis (Moseley, attributed). Inca conquest transferred Lambayeque craft specialists toward Chan Chan and reorganized administration (Farfán and other centers).

Ten ciudadelas are documented, ranging 6.73–21.2 ha and containing 113–907 rooms (attributed, Moore 2005, synthesizing Klymyshyn, Conrad, Kolata, Moseley and Mackey). Each is delineated by battered adobe walls up to 9 m tall enclosing plazas, walk-in wells, storerooms, and distinctive niched three-sided *audiencias* (U-shaped rooms); access was typically restricted to a single entrance on the north wall (Moore 1992, 1996b). Seven of the ten enclose a burial platform. Storage area per ciudadela ranged from ~110 m² (Squier) to 7,620 m² (Gran Chimú); Kolata (1990:139) reports that in ciudadelas built during and after his "Late Chimú 1" phase (Velarde, Bandelier, Rivero, Tschudi), storerooms became smaller and more uniform, probably holding "exclusively precious, elite commodities" rather than bulk goods (attributed).

Alexandra Klymyshyn's (1982) typology of the 35 smaller walled "intermediate architecture" compounds (associated with nonroyal elites) ranks them by declining similarity to ciudadela features, from Type I (ramps, storage, walk-in wells, ciudadela-like subdivision) to Type VI (distinguished only by an enclosing adobe wall). Using Alan Kolata's (1982) seriation of adobe brick types, Klymyshyn concluded most elite compounds date to later Chan Chan construction phases; only four date to the earliest period (attributed). Elite/intermediate compound storerooms covered only 1–28 m² each, versus 110–7,620 m² in the ciudadelas — Klymyshyn (1982:139) concludes corporate wealth accumulation was not characteristic of nonroyal elite compounds (attributed).

## Significance

Climax center of Moseley's **Maritime-Oasis** developmental trajectory — coastal counterpart to highland Inca/Cuzco before Inca unification ([[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]] framing in the 2001 epilogue). Largest adobe city of pre-Hispanic South America in conventional surveys.

**House-society reading (Moore 2005, attributed).** Jerry Moore argues the royal ciudadelas materially qualify as Lévi-Straussian "noble [[house-society|houses]]" ([[levi-strauss-claude|Lévi-Strauss]] 1983) on three criteria: (1) definition of bounded space, (2) accumulation of corporate wealth (the storage-area range above), and (3) continued/perpetual existence — burials within the compound, sequential construction and additions, transformation into ceremonial/memorial space. On this reading, intermediate elite architecture is ambiguous (only Unit 6, associated with the Las Avispas burial platform, shows comparable evidence of maintenance/memorial function; Klymyshyn 1982:134), while commoner *SIAR* barrio dwellings clearly do not qualify. Moore interprets Chimor as a polity "in the process of transition" from kin-based to territorial-based social membership — a transition he argues was cut short by Inca and Spanish conquest. He further attributes the ciudadela walls to "an ideology of separation" marking the Chimú king as "a distinct order of being," the compound's transformation into a "house" involving both royal descendants and non-kin such as descendants of loyal courtiers (Moore 1996b, 2003b, 2004, as reported in Moore 2005).

Comparatively, William Isbell (1997) documented above-ground "open sepulcher monuments" widely distributed from far-northern Peru to the northern Chilean highlands; only nine burial platforms are known from Chan Chan plus one from Farfán. Moore argues (attributed) that Chimú avoidance of above-ground sepulchers, despite contact with sierra peoples who used them and despite adequate adobe engineering capacity, reflects a deliberate ideological choice tied to marking the king as a distinct kind of mortal, not a lack of knowledge or material constraint.

## Key Debates

- Ciudadela sequence and whether each compound equals one reign.
- Degree of centralized state vs confederated Chimor.
- Climate/water crisis vs political factors in late Chimor vulnerability to Inca conquest.
- **Funerary structure vs. royal residence.** John Rowe (1995:30) argued the ciudadelas were not occupied palaces at all: "If these compounds were palaces, where are the apartments that served as royal residences?... consider the possibility that the entire compound is a funerary structure, that the ruler resided elsewhere" — noting that the ~2,423 storerooms known across the ten ciudadelas (Klymyshyn 1987) contain almost nothing but mouse bones (Day 1982b:338), and that the compounds otherwise lack residential features (hearths, milling stones) outside open courtyards (*canchones*). Kent Day (1973, 1982a), Geoffrey Conrad (1974, 1982), Moseley, and Moore counter that Rowe's argument proves too much, since intermediate (nonroyal) compounds show the same "clean" pattern; they point to roofed *audiencias*, worn pedestrian paths, and evidence of food consumption as signs of sustained use by living people. Carol Mackey (personal communication to Moore, May 2002) proposed *audiencias* functioned as elite/royal residences, based on roofing evidence, courtyard associations, and the later ciudadelas (Tschudi, Rivero) having multiroom audiencia/patio groupings interpretable as residences for a ruler and multiple wives. Not adjudicated here; recorded as positions per Moore 2005.
- Whether ciudadelas were occupied singly and sequentially (Day 1982a) or in paired sets reflecting dualistic/quadripartite political structure (Cavallaro 1991; Netherly 1990); relative construction chronology also disputed.
- Tello and Laberinto are anomalous among the ciudadelas: neither has a burial platform. Kolata (1990:122) suggests Tello "was not functionally equivalent to the other ciudadelas... probably intended as a residence of an expanding class of bureaucrats"; Moore proposes Tello resembles a cluster of Type II intermediate-architecture units rather than a true ciudadela. For Laberinto, Moore speculates — explicitly flagged as speculative, with no causal claim about the flood — that the missing burial platform may reflect an unfinished plan or "a conscious decision not to remember a specific king or the overthrow or collapse of a once-powerful royal house," loosely noting its occupation correlates with a major flood ca. C.E. 1100.
