## Discovery and Excavation History

Chan Chan, on the north coast near modern Trujillo (Moche Valley), is the adobe capital of Chimor. Large-scale mapping and excavation (including work associated with [[moseley-michael|Moseley]]) established the sequence of walled royal compounds (*ciudadelas*), craft sectors, and hydraulic features ([[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]]). Colonial looting of burial platforms (*huacas sepulturas*) is itself ethnohistoric evidence for ancestor architecture.

## Occupation Sequence

Moseley reports a walled urban footprint on the order of ~20 km² with a dense civic core (~6 km²) and large craft-working population. Classic *ciudadela* form (e.g. Rivero, Tschudi compounds in the literature) shows restricted access, *audiencia* U-shaped courts, storage, and burial platforms — read as successive royal seats under [[dual-organization]] and ancestor-veneration "glue." Maritime iconography (sea creatures, related friezes) continues Moche-era ocean themes into Chimor ideology. Late hydraulic stress: failed Chicama inter-valley canal project and sunken gardens (*wachaque*) as responses to water crisis (Moseley, attributed). Inca conquest transferred Lambayeque craft specialists toward Chan Chan and reorganized administration (Farfán and other centers).

## Significance

Climax center of Moseley's **Maritime-Oasis** developmental trajectory — coastal counterpart to highland Inca/Cuzco before Inca unification ([[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]] framing in the 2001 epilogue). Largest adobe city of pre-Hispanic South America in conventional surveys.

## Key Debates

- Ciudadela sequence and whether each compound equals one reign.
- Degree of centralized state vs confederated Chimor.
- Climate/water crisis vs political factors in late Chimor vulnerability to Inca conquest.
