# Nabta Playa

Early Holocene playa basin in the southern Egyptian Western Desert with herder camps excavated by the Combined Prehistoric Expedition. In [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] it is the key African case for possible **independent early cattle domestication** and for Saharan pastoral trajectories before Nile farming villages.

**Identity caution:** Herder camps are archaeological; do not equate with later Nubian, Egyptian, or Saharan ethnic groups without attributed argument. Domestic cattle claims are contested positions, not settled wiki-voice facts.

## Discovery and Excavation History

As of Mithen 2003: the Combined Prehistoric Expedition (notably **Fred Wendorf**, **Angela Close**, **Romuald Schild**) worked Nabta localities including **E-75-6**, recovering huts, hearths, wells, plant remains, and cattle bones. Some cattle bones dated ~9000 BCE from rainy-season lakeside camps.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Wendorf et al. via Mithen 2003, ch.51.)*

1. **c. 9000 BCE:** short-term camps beside the seasonal lake during rains; cattle bones present. Wendorf’s ecological argument: arid eastern Sahara could not support free-ranging cattle needing daily water (unlike gazelle/hare fauna), so bones imply managed stock—**contested**; critics noted in Mithen’s account; do not promote to wiki voice.
2. **Priority claim:** if Wendorf is right, North African cattle domestication (~9000 BCE) precedes the western Asian consensus (~7500 BCE), overturning the model of cattle entering Africa from Asia ~7000 BCE.
3. **Genetic support (as of Mithen 2003):** Bradley and Loftus (Trinity College Dublin) mtDNA/divergence work concluding European and African cattle lineages separated ≥20,000 years—cited as supporting independent African domestication. **Date-stamp:** as of Bradley & Loftus 1996 / Mithen 2003; later aDNA may revise.
4. **Scenario (theoretical):** people leave the Nile after 10,000 BCE, pen wild calves as drought insurance, evolve full herding (milk/blood, rare slaughter) in the eastern Sahara by ~9000 BCE, spread into the Nile ~5000 BCE rather than out of it.
5. **c. 5800 BCE:** sheep and goats arrive from the north.
6. **c. 4000 BCE:** prolonged drought; Nabta basin abandoned.

## Significance

- Primary field support for an African cattle-domestication hypothesis independent of (and possibly earlier than) SW Asia—as argued by Wendorf and allies and relayed by Mithen.
- Anchors eastern Sahara pastoral prehistory and the environmental limits of desert herding.
- Comparative node for pastoral social/ritual entanglement with cattle (developed more fully in Acacus rock-art chapters of the same source).
- Sets up later Nile mixed economies (e.g. Fayum) in which farming supplements hunting/fishing/gathering.

## Key Debates

- **Domestic vs wild/hunted cattle** at Nabta and Bir Kiseiba—Wendorf ecological necessity argument vs critics; open as of Mithen 2003.
- **African vs SW Asian cattle priority** and direction of spread (into vs out of the Nile).
- Reliability of mid-1990s mtDNA divergence dates for independent domestication (fast-moving genetics—date-stamp).
- Climatic forcing of abandonment vs social reorganization ~4000 BCE.
