# Nabta Playa

## Discovery and Excavation History

**Nabta Playa** in the Egyptian Western Desert is a key early Holocene locality in discussions of Sahara–Nile sedentism and early food production. [[phillipson-david-w|Phillipson]] draws on Wendorf and colleagues for its place in the North African/Sahara sequence (as of Phillipson 2005).

## Occupation Sequence

Phillipson places Nabta within early Holocene wetter-phase occupation of the eastern Sahara, part of the broader trend toward permanent settlement and, later, herding/cultivation discussed in his Chs. 5–6 (as of Phillipson 2005). Exact phase names and cattle-domestication claims in the specialist literature are contested; keep all food-production interpretations **attributed** and date-stamped.

## Significance

Illustrates Phillipson’s argument that final Pleistocene–early Holocene sedentism and specialised foraging in the Nile–Sahara zone provided a base for later herding and cultivation — without treating “Neolithic” as a uniform package or calendar stage (he is wary of the label; as of Phillipson 2005).

## Key Debates

Early cattle domestication vs hunting/management at Nabta is a live specialist dispute beyond Phillipson’s survey resolution — do not promote to wiki voice from this source alone.

### Handbook densification (*Oxford Handbook of African Archaeology*, 2013)

*(Attributed — chapters in *[[mitchell-lane-oxford-handbook-african-archaeology-2013|Mitchell & Lane 2013]]*; positions as of ~2012.)*

- **E-75-6 / El Nabta Neolithic (Barich, Ch. 31):** Semi-permanent village (aligned huts, hearths, storage pits) in a wet-phase recolonization ~8100–7900 BP; domestic cattle still rare; sheep/goats late (~7000 BP at E-75-8 and Hidden Valley, Farafra). Macroplant remains and grindstones show **wild** sorghum and millet as important diet components alongside hunting — plant gathering / possible early manipulation without full domestication package (Haaland Ch. 37: late seventh-millennium BC broad-spectrum grasses at E-75-6; sorghum morphological change “suggestive” only).
- **Early *Bos* claims contested (di Lernia, Ch. 36; Gifford-Gonzalez & Hanotte, Ch. 35):** Hardest “earliest cattle” data still rest on late-1970s/early-1980s Nabta/Bir Kiseiba large-bovid bones (~8800 BP Nabta; Bir Kiseiba surface ~9500 BP) argued osteologically by elimination and ecologically (harsh rainfall supposedly requiring human water aid). Critics (Grigson metrics = larger wild animals; Smith 1986 ecology; assemblages as cultural selection) dispute both lines. **Uncontroversial** Nabta-Kiseiba domestic *Bos* dates cluster c. **5750–4550 BC**, with caprines only a few centuries later (Gifford-Gonzalez & Hanotte summarizing the field). No subsequent site has provided fresh direct faunal evidence of ~10 ka domestic cattle.
- **Caprine entry:** Earliest North African caprine dates cluster ~7800–6500 BP; di Lernia’s parsimonious picture is diffuse pastoral groups with domestic sheep/goats over a large North African area ~7200–7000 BP (routes debated).
- **Cattle cult / ritual:** Cattle pits/stone monuments first at Nabta ~6400 BP then central Sahara by end of that millennium — rapid circulation of ideas/rituals under aridification (di Lernia 2006 via Ch. 36).
- **Early ceramics:** Physicochemical work at Nabta and Bir Kiseiba shows most pots local but some vessel/sherd movement between basins (Nelson 2001 via Barich).
- **Research agenda:** Move beyond “bone obsession”; isotopes, dung, dairying residues, ethnoarchaeology of forager–herder relations, population genetics for demic vs cultural tests (di Lernia).

**Identity caution:** Nabta is a **site sequence**, not a people, language, or genetic population. Cattle claims remain **positions** — optimistic early-domestication readings vs later uncontroversial dates.
