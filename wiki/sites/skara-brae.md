# Skara Brae

Neolithic stone-built settlement on the Bay of Skaill, Mainland, Orkney — one of the best-
preserved village sites of Atlantic European prehistory in the wider literature. **This
vault’s Cunliffe layer has essentially no site-specific treatment of Skara Brae.**
*[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* (2013) does not discuss the settlement’s
house plans, middens, or excavation history in densified claims; the name is not a load-
bearing topic in the extracted ranges. What follows records (1) that coverage gap and (2)
the **Orkney ceremonial and connectivity context** Cunliffe *does* provide, which is the
landscape in which Skara Brae is usually situated by other authors — without equating
settlement archaeology with passage graves or henges as one “culture people.”

### Orkney late Neolithic context (Cunliffe 2013 — associated landscape, not the village)

Use only as **regional setting** (attributed, Cunliffe 2013):

- **Passage graves:** Orkney holds large impressive structures, most notably **Maes Howe**
  (central chamber 4.6 m square, corbelled roof once ~5 m high), built around **3000 BC** as
  part of a Boyne–Anglesey–Orkney horizon culminating a thousand-year passage-grave tradition
  (attributed). Shared solstice alignments and stone-carving values with Irish and Welsh tombs
  imply seaway contact, not a single building crew ([[newgrange]], [[megalith]]).
- **Henges and stone circles:** earliest known henges currently in **Orkney** (end of the
  fourth millennium); southern British examples (~3000 BC) may be later; origins left open
  (attributed). **Maes Howe** sits in a circular enclosure with wide flat-bottomed ditch and
  bank on the outer lip in henge style — one of the earliest henges known and a possible type
  origin (attributed). **Ring of Brodgar**: massive stone circle **104 m** diameter on the
  lip of a circular ditched enclosure; one of several monuments on the neck of land between
  lochs Harray and Stenness north of Maes Howe (attributed). Stones of Stenness and related
  ceremonial monuments form the same sacred landscape (attributed map/caption frame).
- **Grooved Ware and belief systems:** Grooved Ware (flat-bottomed, line/stab decoration;
  ~1000 years from thirty-second/thirty-first century; pan-British/Irish) is invariably
  associated with henges; some writers, noting earliest dates for both henges and Grooved
  Ware in Orkney, propose that the belief systems began in the north and spread south via
  east-coast routes and to Ireland via Atlantic seaways when passage graves were most
  elaborate. Cunliffe allows this but requires more precise chronology; at minimum, Britain
  and Ireland shared distinctive monuments and ceramics showing intense third-millennium
  social interaction (attributed position).
- **Earlier Neolithic connectivity:** Orkney vole (*Microtus arvalis orcadensis*) genetics
  imply transport from western France or Iberia in a single step, present by end of the
  fourth millennium — Cunliffe tempts Neolithic ship introduction (attributed; animal
  genetics ≠ human ethnic identity).

None of the above substitutes for a Skara Brae house-sequence page; they locate Orkney in
Cunliffe’s late Neolithic connectivity model.

## Significance

**In Cunliffe 2013:** Skara Brae itself is not used as a type-site. Orkney’s significance in
his synthesis is **ceremonial** (Maes Howe, Brodgar, Stenness, early henges) and
**ideological** (possible northern lead in henge–Grooved Ware systems), plus Atlantic
passage-grave culmination ~3000 BC. Settlement archaeology of the Northern Isles is largely
off-stage.

**In the wider field (stub pointer only):** other literature treats Skara Brae as a
stone-furniture village of the third millennium BC with rich household assemblages — that
claim set is **not** yet sourced in this wiki from an ingested monograph. Leave findings
unasserted until a primary site source is filed.

## Key Debates

- Orkney-first hypothesis for henges and Grooved Ware (position Cunliffe reports; chronology
  insufficient for resolution as of 2013).
- Relation of village settlement (when documented from other sources) to ceremonial
  landscapes of Stenness–Brodgar–Maes Howe — association of place, not identity of
  “Grooved Ware people.”
- Atlantic vs North Sea connectivity for Northern Isles Neolithic (vole genetics as weak
  ancillary signal only).
