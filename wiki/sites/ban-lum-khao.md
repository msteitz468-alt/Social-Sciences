## Discovery and Excavation History

Ban Lum Khao is a late Neolithic and Bronze Age settlement and cemetery in the upper Mun Valley of Northeast Thailand, excavated in the 1990s by [[higham-charles]] and Rachanie Thosarat as part of the Mun Valley programme that also frames [[noen-u-loke]] and, from 2002, [[ban-non-wat]]. The principal published exposure for mortuary analysis is a **14 × 10 m** trench yielding **111 graves**, analysed in detail by O’Reilly (1999) as reported in [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]]. Fine-mesh flotation (under 1 mm) was applied — Higham cites Ban Lum Khao and [[nong-nor]] as demonstrations that subsistence reconstruction depends heavily on screening intensity.

Higham and Thosarat’s work at the site also revises the Phimai-region ceramic sequence: they place a Tamyae-equivalent horizon as early as **~1450 BCE** and a Prasat (no iron) phase at **1000–500 BCE** (as of Higham 2002, citing Welch and McNeill’s broader Phimai framework).

## Occupation Sequence

### Late Neolithic baseline (~1500 BCE)

(as of Higham 2002) Higham places first occupation around **1500 BCE**, near the end of the Khorat Plateau Neolithic span he synthesises as roughly **2300–1500 BCE**. Basal pits with flotation recovered water-buffalo bones large enough to fall in the **wild** size range; one pit held eleven Eld’s deer plus sambar and muntjak; pig and domestic dog are present; hundreds of fish (*Anabas*, *Ophicephalus*) are larger than modern local specimens. Higham infers that first occupants of Ban Lum Khao — and probably other upper Mun Neolithic sites — entered a **pristine environment** not previously exploited by human groups.

### Bronze Age cemetery (c. 1400–500 BCE)

(as of Higham 2002; O’Reilly 1999) The cemetery of 111 graves falls within **1400–500 BCE**, “but no greater precision is possible at present due to a lack of datable material” (Higham). Four mortuary phases organise men, women, infants, and children in rows. Phase-4 pottery approaches the earliest local Iron Age (7th–6th centuries BCE).

**Metal and craft.** Despite **crucible and mould fragments** indicating local bronze casting, **no bronzes** were present among grave goods — shell jewellery and ceramic vessels dominate. Ceramic anvils occur in three phase-3 graves (two male, one female); adzes are mostly with infants/children; spindle whorls accompany all ages and sexes (six burials plus 88 whorls in the cemetery matrix), including phase-4 infants and women but also earlier young men — undercutting simple sex-craft mapping.

**Wealth structure.** O’Reilly’s points-based scoring found **95 of 111** burials “poor” (<10 points) and only **three “rich”** (>20), all in the final phase. Most offerings show no significant patterning by gender, age, or row (one bangle style restricted to older individuals). Phase-4 mean offerings rise about threefold (15 vs ~4.3–4.6 earlier). Infants in row 2 averaged ~6 items vs 1.5 in row 1 (statistically wealthier); Higham records both the standard premise that infant goods can index ascribed status and the alternative that they project family status, without adjudicating a ranked-[[chiefdom]] model. The dominant impression is **uniformity of treatment**; only final western-edge reoriented burials show a sharp rise in quantity and range of goods. Phase 4 also yields rare copper-alloy balls/fragments mostly with subadults — increasing mortuary investment before iron while finished bronze tools/weapons remain almost absent from graves.

**Subsistence.** Late Neolithic occupation met a low-lying marshy environment. Domestic cattle and pigs were maintained, but hunting continued throughout the Bronze Age (Eld’s deer, muntjak, sambar). Flotation samples are fish-dominated: snakehead (*Ophicephalus*) most abundant, then *Mystus*, *Anabas*, *Clarias*, swamp eel, *Wallagonia*. Species frequencies match modern local consumption and show no major change from Neolithic pits through the Bronze Age; fish were vital from swamps, streams, rivers, and by inference rice fields. Snakehead as a mortuary offering in two late burials continues into the Iron Age at [[noen-u-loke]]. Freshwater bivalve shellfish of multiple species accompany the dead — part of a wider Bronze Age symbolic shell signature Higham notes across sites including [[nong-nor]] and [[non-nok-tha]].

**Bioarchaeology.** Domett (1999) via Higham: infants under one year ~**19%** of the sample (higher than Nong Nor’s 11%); Ban Lum Khao appears relatively worse off among Bronze Age samples — higher young female death rate, lowest male stature; about half of female pelves show pregnancy evidence (vs most at [[ban-na-di]]). Higham notes Ban Lum Khao as a possible exception to long local ancestry/adaptation inferred for other Bronze Age settlements.

## Significance

Ban Lum Khao is central to Higham’s picture of **Mun Valley Bronze Age society**:

1. **Local casting without mortuary bronze.** The site severs any automatic link between on-site metallurgy and bronze-as-grave-wealth, sharpening arguments in the [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]] about what bronze *meant* socially when it first circulated.
2. **Ranking without centralization.** Uniform mortuary treatment, late enrichment only in phase 4, and ambiguous infant-row differences support Higham’s model of flexible prestige competition rather than durable chiefly strata (see [[chiefdom]]).
3. **Neolithic landnám baseline.** Basal “pristine environment” fauna give a rare ecological snapshot of first agricultural/settled use of upper Mun wetlands around 1500 BCE (as of Higham 2002), relevant to [[rice-domestication]] and Khorat Neolithic chronology.
4. **Bridge to Iron Age.** Phase-4 pottery and offering surge prefigure [[noen-u-loke]] early Iron Age assemblages while still lacking iron and abundant bronze grave goods.

## Key Debates

- **Ascribed status vs family projection in infant graves.** Higham leaves open; row-2 vs row-1 infant differences do not generalise to children or whole-row wealth separation.
- **Continuity into Iron Age ranking.** Whether phase-4 enrichment is the start of the trajectory documented at [[noen-u-loke]] and other moated Mun sites, or a local oscillation within Bronze Age flexibility.
- **Population ancestry.** Possible exception to long local ancestry claimed for other Bronze Age samples — open, not demonstrated replacement (four-way non-identity: cemetery population ≠ language ≠ genes).
- **Phimai ceramic chronology.** Higham–Thosarat revisions push Tamyae-equivalent material earlier than Welch and McNeill’s original bands.

## Historiography

Ban Lum Khao is a product of the Higham–Thosarat Mun Valley programme rather than of the earlier Ban Chiang / Non Nok Tha fame cycle. Its importance in the 2002 synthesis is comparative: a large, fully scored mortuary sample that is *poor in bronze grave goods* yet evidence of local casting. Absolute dating remains coarse (1400–500 BCE envelope). Evidence classes: material (excavation, flotation fauna), statistical mortuary analysis (O’Reilly), bioarchaeology (Domett). Collection coverage: [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]] only; O’Reilly’s 1999 analysis and full site monographs not separately ingested. Later Ban Non Wat monographs (post-2002) will reframe the upper Mun sequence — treat this page as the 2002 snapshot.
