## Overview

Tenochtitlan was the island capital of the Aztec (Mexica) empire, founded (per Aztec
tradition) around 1344–1345 CE on islands in Lake Texcoco, paired with the satellite
settlement of Tlatelolco to its north. By 1519 the twin-city complex covered roughly
5 square miles (14 sq km), was laid out on a grid known from a fragmentary 16th-century
map, and was connected to the mainland by three causeways — north to Tepeyac, west to
Tlacopan, and south to Coyoacan — each fitted with removable bridges and defensive
gatehouses. A masonry aqueduct carried fresh water from the spring at Chapultepec. The
city was, in effect, a [[chinampa]] city: canals for canoe traffic ran north–south,
bordered by lanes and crossed by larger canals, with rectangular house plots laid out
between them (Coe & Koontz 2013).

Population estimates are attributed, not settled: Rudolf van Zantwijk, working from
Aztec tribute lists, calculated that the city's warehouse stores could have supported
350,000 people; Coe and Koontz, describing the underlying data as "however flimsy,"
suggest a range of 200,000–300,000 inhabitants in 1519 — "five times the size of
contemporary London of Henry VIII" (Coe & Koontz 2013). All of Mexico between the
Isthmus of Tehuantepec and the Chichimec frontier is estimated at roughly 11,000,000.

## Discovery and Excavation History

The Sacred Precinct at the city's ceremonial core, surrounded by the Coatepantli
("Snake Wall"), lay buried beneath colonial and modern Mexico City after the Spanish
destroyed it and built administrative buildings, a cathedral, and churches over its
foundations. Per Fray Bernardino de Sahagún's 16th-century account it once held 78
buildings; roughly 40 have been located archaeologically. The precinct's excavation
began by chance: in 1978, utility workers digging for power transformers northeast of
the Cathedral struck a huge oval relief monument, the Coyolxauhqui Stone. This
discovery triggered a major, sustained excavation program directed by Eduardo Matos
Moctezuma and, in later phases, Leonardo López Luján, continuing for decades under the
Instituto Nacional de Antropología e Historia (INAH) (Coe & Koontz 2013). Excavation
quality is high by modern standards (systematic, stratigraphically controlled,
ongoing), in contrast to earlier antiquarian-era digging elsewhere in the region.

## Occupation Sequence

The Templo Mayor — the double temple of Huitzilopochtli (sun/war cult) and Tlaloc
(rain) that dominated the Sacred Precinct — was rebuilt across seven successive
construction stages. Stage II is the oldest surviving core; Stage VI is the form seen
by the conquistadores in 1519. The temple was split down its axis, with the right/south
half dedicated to Huitzilopochtli and the left/north half to Tlaloc. Other structures
in the precinct included temples to Tezcatlipoca, Quetzalcoatl, and Xipe Totec; a
tzompantli (skull rack) displaying thousands of human heads; and a large ball court
(Coe & Koontz 2013).

Beyond the ceremonial core, the city was organized into four great quarters, divided by
imaginary north–south and east–west lines meeting at the Sacred Precinct, and into more
than eighty [[calpulli|calpolli]] wards (see [[calpulli]]). Elsewhere in the city,
newly built chinampa plots supported light cane-and-thatch houses, while older, drier
ground supported stone-and-mortar houses, some two stories with patios and gardens.

## Significance

Tenochtitlan anchors the archaeological and documentary record of Aztec urbanism,
statecraft, and religion, and is the type site for understanding the [[aztec-empire]]
as a going concern rather than as later Spanish description alone. Its excavated
offering caches and monumental sculpture provide a materially independent check on
Spanish and native documentary sources, and its destruction and overbuilding by the
Spanish make its archaeology a rare, deliberately-recovered window onto an imperial
capital erased at conquest.

### Key finds

The Coyolxauhqui Stone — a relief depicting the dismembered moon goddess Coyolxauhqui,
associated with construction Stage IV and measuring 11 ft 2 in (3.4 m) along its
longest dimension — lies at the base of the temple's Huitzilopochtli side, tied to the
myth of Huitzilopochtli's miraculous birth and fratricide of his sister and brothers.
The Tlaltecuhtli monument, roughly 10 by 13 ft (3 by 4 m) and larger than the
Coyolxauhqui stone, was found embedded in the plaza floor retaining traces of ocher,
red, and blue paint; it likely covers a royal burial and was commissioned by the ruler
Ahuitzotl (r. 1486–1502). A nearby stepped depression yielded roughly 15,000 buried
offering items, including sacrificial animals and abundant sea life from the Pacific
coast of the empire — consistent with Ahuitzotl's greatest military successes having
been on the Pacific side (Coe & Koontz 2013). Tlaloc offering caches held blue-painted
Tlaloc vases in pottery and stone, along with imported shells and coral.

## Key Debates

Cecilia Klein and Elizabeth Boone reassessed the monumental female sculptures found
around the Templo Mayor — including the colossal "Coatlicue" — associating them with
the tzitzimime, female star demons linked to childbirth, war, and ancestor contact,
believed to threaten to devour humanity should the sun fail. Boone further notes that
these goddess images are shown decapitated, possibly signaling their defeat by
Huitzilopochtli (attributed to Klein and Boone via Coe & Koontz 2013). This is an
interpretive reading of iconography, not a documented native exegesis, and should be
read as attributed scholarly interpretation.

The scale of human sacrifice conducted at and around the Templo Mayor is a live,
unresolved dispute — see [[sacrifice]] for the Nicholson-vs.-skeptics position record;
Tenochtitlan's tzompantli and offering deposits are the primary material evidence cited
on both sides.

## Basin of Mexico Survey Population and Settlement Estimates (Sanders, Parsons & Santley 1979)

[[sanders-parsons-santley-basin-of-mexico-1979]] (study page [[sanders-basin-of-mexico-study]]) incorporated Edward Calnek's documentary/archival research (1970–1976) on Tenochtitlan's urban core, reporting an estimated population of 150,000–200,000 within a densely built 12–15 km² urban area — a density of roughly 12,000–13,000 people per km², which the authors noted was far higher than Middle Horizon [[teotihuacan]]'s. They described Tenochtitlan's residents as almost exclusively non-agriculturalists organized in extended-family domestic units along a fairly regular street-and-canal grid, and recorded that Tenochtitlan and Tlatelolco began as two separate island centers that later physically merged, with Tenochtitlan absorbing Tlatelolco's rulership by forced replacement late in the 15th century.

At the largest scale, the authors estimated a "Greater Tenochtitlan" zone of roughly 600 km² (from the Sierra de Guadalupe south to the Ajusco foothills) held approximately 400,000 people by the Late Horizon, encompassing Tenayuca, Azcapotzalco, Tacuba, Coyoacan, Ixtapalapa, Culhuacan, and Xochimilco among other provincial centers — which they described as the largest, densest population concentration in prehispanic Mesoamerican history, and treated analytically as a single integrated community. They put the Late Horizon Basin-wide population at roughly 1,000,000–1,200,000, with Tenochtitlan-Tlatelolco alone accounting for up to 20% of the Basin total.

On social organization, the authors summarized Late Horizon stratification as roughly 5–10% noble (*pilli*) status, with commoners divided between *macehual* (free commoners holding corporate land rights) and *mayeque* (dependent tenants on noble estates, lacking corporate membership); they treated the *[[calpulli]]* — the basic territorial corporate unit, with tax, educational, military, and religious functions — as having considerable time depth, citing comparable corporate-group evidence at Middle Horizon Teotihuacan and Monte Albán. Tenochtitlan, Texcoco, and Tacuba (the Triple Alliance capitals) are described as constituting a distinctly higher political-organizational tier than the roughly fifty other semiautonomous local centers documented for the Late Horizon Basin, with Texcoco estimated at ~30,000 and Tacuba's role as largely formal/honorific.
