## Discovery and Excavation History

Broom and Robinson made the first discoveries at Swartkrans in 1948, but the site's geology is, per [[klein-human-career-1999]], the best understood of any southern African australopithecine site, owing to C. K. Brain's subsequent research establishing the Swartkrans Formation's stratigraphy of five members. As at [[sterkfontein]], no volcanic material is present for radiometric dating, so the chronology rests on faunal correlation and taphonomic analysis rather than absolute methods — a reliability caveat that runs through the whole southern African cave sequence.

## Occupation Sequence

Members 1–3 have yielded craniodental fragments from more than 100 individuals of [[australopithecus|Paranthropus robustus]] and postcranial elements from more than 17 individuals of [[homo-erectus|Homo ergaster/erectus]]; Member 1 alone is diagnostically about 97% P. robustus material. The same members produced more than 875 flaked stone artifacts, classified as Developed [[oldowan-industry|Oldowan]], together with 77 bone fragments showing polish or wear consistent — per experimental replication cited by Klein — with use for digging up subterranean plant foods. Occasional burned bones first appear in Member 3; Klein treats this as a possible indication of incipient human control over fire, but notes the burned material is not locally concentrated and there is no evidence for hearths, so the inference remains tentative (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]).

## Significance

Swartkrans is the key type site documenting co-occurrence of [[australopithecus|Paranthropus robustus]] and early [[homo-erectus|Homo]] alongside a substantial Developed Oldowan toolkit, and its well-resolved stratigraphy (thanks to C. K. Brain) makes it a benchmark for taphonomic method in South African cave archaeology generally.

## Key Debates

Whether Paranthropus robustus made any of the stone tools recovered alongside its remains at Swartkrans is a live dispute. Klein argues Homo produced most or all of the tools, reasoning largely from the disproportionate fossil ratio (about 97% Paranthropus to 3% Homo) and from P. robustus's apelike thumb-metacarpal anatomy, while acknowledging the attribution is circumstantial and that "conceivably... robust australopithecines also made some of the tools" (as of [[klein-human-career-1999]]). The Member 3 burned bones are similarly a position, not a settled finding: they are cited as a candidate for the earliest human fire use in the region, but Klein stops short of asserting fire control given the absence of hearths or local burning concentration.

Discussed in [[klein-human-career-1999]] ([[klein-richard]]) alongside [[sterkfontein]] and [[olduvai-gorge]] in the debate over who made the earliest stone tools.
