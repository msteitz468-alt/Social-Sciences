# Çatalhöyük

Neolithic settlement mounds on the Konya Plain, central Anatolia (Turkey). One of Southwest Asia’s best-known early farming sites and — for this wiki’s knowledge layer — the principal **field laboratory** for [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]]’s reflexive methodology in *[[hodder-archaeological-process-1999|The Archaeological Process]]* (1999) and for **[[entanglement-theory|entanglement theory]]** in *[[hodder-entangled-2012|Entangled]]* (2012). Programs: [[post-processual-archaeology]]; [[entanglement-theory]]; practice concept: [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]].

**Identity caution:** The site is a material settlement locus, not a “people,” language, or genetic population. Ethnonyms and identity claims in modern heritage discourse (local villages, Turkish national narratives, international “goddess” constituencies, academic teams) are **present interests** about the past, recorded as such.

## Discovery and Excavation History

**Survey and discovery.** [[mellaart-james|James Mellaart]] began systematic survey of the Konya Plain in 1951 (British Institute of Archaeology at Ankara). The double mound was noted at a distance in 1952; formal surface discovery came on a cold November day in **1958** (Mellaart with Alan Hall and David French): burned mudbrick, ash, bone, potsherds, and obsidian tools from base to summit (~15 m / ~50 ft), material paralleling deep Neolithic Mersin (attributed, *[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967|Çatal Hüyük]]* 1967).

**Mellaart phase (1961–1965).** Four seasons of **horizontal area excavation** (not deep trenches), designed to recover building complexes and protect wall-paintings; virgin soil not reached. Building-levels numbered from the top (**O–X**), with Level VI split into **VIA and VIB**. Mellaart’s mid-1960s radiocarbon series plus plaster-layer counts placed Levels X–II roughly **c. 6500–5700 BC** over ~800 years (uncalibrated/legacy framework — superseded as absolute chronology by later calibrated project work). The 1967 monograph *[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967|Çatal Hüyük: A Neolithic Town in Anatolia]]* made the site world-famous and fixed the “shrine”/goddess reception that later teams and publics renegotiated. Study object: [[mellaart-catal-huyuk-study]] · deep analysis: [[hubs/studies/mellaart-catal-huyuk-study]].

**Hodder phase (from 1993).** Hodder directed a long-running international project that used the site to **operationalize reflexive field method** — not only to refine Neolithic culture-history. As of the 1999 method book (attributed project practices):

- Multi-specialist presence on site during excavation (fauna, archaeobotany, lithics, ceramics, soil micromorphology, human remains, ground stone, etc.).
- Laboratory specialists tour excavation areas every **one or two days** so primary interpretation is interactive.
- Diaries written into the database and cross-referenced to context numbers.
- Video clips of excavators and specialists explaining layers and assumptions, keyword-searchable with coded finds.
- Ambition for full multimedia web database, hypertext narratives, and VR as a public “front-end.”
- Multiple national teams excavating different areas with equivalent recording systems but varied traditional techniques — “different Çatalhöyüks” through different windows.

Hodder (1999) uses Building 1 (North Area) — floor/wall relationships and Space 107 dump vs activity-area sampling — as **reasoning** examples of how interpretation and recovery interlock.

## Occupation Sequence

Neolithic East Mound occupation is conventionally placed in the mid–late 8th to early 6th millennium BCE (approximate; radiocarbon-anchored sequences have been refined since Mellaart and continue to be revised — treat absolute dates as research-front). Dense contiguous building levels; sub-floor burial; elaborate wall paintings and installations in some buildings. West Mound and later periods exist; the 1999 method discussion centres Neolithic excavation practice rather than a full culture-historical synthesis.

### Mellaart 1967 densification (primary excavation synthesis)

*(Attributed throughout — [[mellaart-james]], *[[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967]]*; fieldwork 1961–65. Absolute BC figures are mid-1960s C-14 framework; do not overwrite calibrated project chronologies above.)*

**Scale and setting.** East Mound ~**32 acres**, up to ~50 ft of Neolithic deposit, on an old branch of the Çarşamba Çay ~32 miles SE of Konya at ~3,000 ft; West Mound treated as later successor settlement across the river. Mellaart ranked it the largest Neolithic site then known and, with [[jericho|Jericho]], among the first essays in “town-life.”

**Architecture and plan.** Sun-dried mudbrick houses with own walls (mutual buttressing), reed-and-mud flat roofs, **wooden ladders through roof openings** (no street-level doorways); movement on roofs. Standardized platforms, benches, hearths, ovens; under-platform burial. Horizontal clearance recovered coherent plans especially Levels II–VI; outer wall faces and gate/guard arrangements read as defence. Mellaart claimed no evidence of sack or massacre across the excavated ~800-year span. Annual white plaster (*ak toprak*) coats used to estimate building duration (extremes ~100 layers VIB, ~120 Level VII).

**“Shrines.”** Of **139 living rooms** (Levels II–X), Mellaart classed **≥40** as cult rooms — more elaborately decorated, often largest, but **same plan/construction as houses**. Criteria (his list): elaborate ritual paintings; plaster reliefs of deities/animals; horned benches and bucrania; cult-statue groups; wall ex-votos; skulls on platforms. He admitted borderline cases over earlier shrines and inferred **continuity of cult** from superimposition. Offerings common (grain, tools, pots, horns, stamp-seals, clay animals “wounded” in hunting rites); no intramural slaughter infrastructure. **This classification is the principal target of later reanalysis** (Hodder project: domestic microstratigraphy in elaborately decorated buildings; ritual/domestic continuum).

**Art and religion (Mellaart’s reading).** Polychrome wall-paintings (pigments from iron oxides, copper ores, cinnabar, manganese, soot, etc.) from pre-Level X rubbish through Level II; often **sealed under white plaster** after ritual use so walls spent most years plain white. Subject groups include geometric/kilim (textile) patterns, hands, animals, goddesses, hunts, funerary/vulture scenes, and landscape (twin-peaked **Hasan Dağ** volcano with town — probable obsidian source in his narrative). Plaster reliefs of pregnant goddesses, animals, and bucrania; portable stone and clay goddess statues (Level II enthroned “Mistress of Animals” from a grain bin as type image); schist plaque read as *hieros gamos*. Leopard-skin dress and belt-fasteners with some male burials, obsidian mirrors with some female burials — inferred as priest/priestess markers. Goddess-centred fertility cult is **Mellaart’s theology**, not wiki-voice fact; see [[mother-goddess]] and Key Debates.

**Burial.** Secondary burial under platforms (not storerooms/courtyards); extramural cemetery not found in his sample (contrast aceramic Hacılar). Model: outdoor mortuary platforms, **vulture excarnation**, then wrapped desiccated bodies under floors, often timed with annual spring redecoration. Family accumulations (e.g. 32 individuals in shrine VI.10; 28 in house VI.B.34). Red-ochre/cinnabar and blue/green painted burials; gendered grave-goods (weapons vs ornaments/mirrors). Cloth wad substituted for brain in at least one skull (VI.1).

**Crafts, trade, economy.** VIA conflagration (Mellaart ~5880 BC) preserved textiles, wood, leather. Workshops largely **outside** the excavated “religious quarter.” Obsidian (Hasan Dağ and other sources; characterization work including Cann & [[renfrew-colin|Renfrew]]), imported flint, pigments, shells; copper and lead from early levels (IX+), with metallurgical specialist input. Hans **Helbaek**: multiple crops (emmer, einkorn, naked six-row barley, pea; bread-wheat by VI); wild progenitors not local to the plain — agriculture **introduced**. Perkins/Ducos: domestic sheep/goat early; aurochs and other large game hunted; Suberde (Bordaz) as hunting contrast. Mellaart: mild inequality; important position of women; full-time specialists; strong conservatism of plan over centuries; Upper Palaeolithic “survivals” via continued hunting. Preliminary physical anthropology (Ferembach/Biernoff) used 1960s cranial “race” typology — **quarantined as period measurement construct**, not ethnicity.

## Significance

1. **Neolithic social life at high resolution** — architecture, burial, craft, symbolism as material evidence class.
2. **Method laboratory** — the most fully stated attempt to build [[reflexive-fieldwork]] into routine excavation and recording (twelve steps + four themes: reflexivity, relationality, interactivity, multivocality).
3. **Global heritage node** — Hodder (1999 Ch. 9) treats the site as a case of negotiated local–global interests: sponsors, local and national politics, tourism, goddess-spirituality tours, academic careers, CRM logics. Emancipatory and dominating uses of the same networks are both possible (Hodder’s framing).
4. **Entanglement exemplars (Hodder 2012)** — clay/plaster/brick maintenance and extraction “holes”; mudbrick walls that fall; brick-making sequences; sheep/goat cooking-pot residues vs wild-cattle history symbolism; Level VII pottery and sandier bricks; unique undecorated-period face pot never copied; West Mound decorated pottery co-fitting with larger productive houses; tanglegram of clay dependences (Fig. 9.2 with Doherty); wild-cattle decline (Levels VI–IV) as catalyst for domestic cattle and house reorganization. These are **framework illustrations** in attributed voice, not free-standing site reports — specialist monographs remain the primary material record as ingested.

## Occupation Sequence (entanglement-relevant notes)

As summarized in Hodder 2012 (with project literature he cites): East Mound habitation conventionally ~7400–6000 BCE; West Mound continues into mid-6th millennium. Houses packed without streets, rooftop movement; intramural burial; paintings and installations densest in middle levels. Pottery scarce earliest, then Levels XII–X; cooking pottery notable by Level VII with sandier mudbricks. Upper levels: larger multi-room houses, more storage/production space, hearth toward center, decorated serving pottery by late East Mound / West Mound; adult burial shifts away from under-floor platforms (Team Poznań tomb evidence cited for uppermost levels). Absolute dates remain research-front; treat as approximate and method-dependent.

## Historiography

Mellaart’s 1961–65 dig and 1967 monograph conditioned later publics and textbooks; the Hodder project is both excavation program and **epistemological experiment**, documented in method papers (e.g. Hodder 1997) and *The Archaeological Process* (1999). Critical fieldwork historiography ([[lucas-gavin|Lucas]] 2002) places Çatalhöyük reflexivity within a longer non-progressive history of recording systems. Primary Mellaart synthesis now ingested: [[mellaart-catal-huyuk-1967]] / [[mellaart-catal-huyuk-study]] / [[hubs/studies/mellaart-catal-huyuk-study]]. Method detail from Hodder 1999 remains; specialist project monographs still needed for current faunal/plant/building-biography synthesis.

### Patterns in Prehistory (Wenke & Olszewski 2007)

*(Attributed — [[wenke-olszewski-patterns-in-prehistory-2007]], [[wenke-robert]], [[olszewski-deborah]]; mid-2000s textbook synthesis—dates uncalibrated/legacy as of 2007; do not silently overwrite later project chronologies above.)*

W&O summarize Çatalhöyük (south-central Anatolia) as occupied c. 6250–5400 BCE; at times ~13 ha and ~4,000–6,000 people—several times larger than other known sites of the period; near Hasan Dağ obsidian. Houses ~25 m² mudbrick with roof-ladder access and dense packing; ~40 shrine-like rooms with fertility/death imagery (bulls, vultures, etc.); mortuary practices largely egalitarian in their reading (no infant rich graves, no stone tombs); limited exotic trade. Redman is cited calling it a “premature flash” of complexity. They contrast Jarmo (Iraqi Kurdistan; ≤200 people, uniform burials, little public architecture): the key difference in their framing is Çatalhöyük’s control of a localized strategic resource (obsidian). By ~8 kya, Jericho and Çatalhöyük remain largely regionally self-sufficient within the SW Asian village horizon.

## After the Ice densification (Mithen 2003)

*(Attributed — [[mithen-steven]], *[[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|After the Ice]]* 2003, ch. 11; Mellaart/Hodder synthesis as of early 2000s.)*

- **Discovery and scale:** Mellaart’s mound ~450 m long, ~32 acres; “most remarkable Neolithic settlement ever discovered” — status now shared or surrendered to Göbekli Tepe (Mithen’s comparative judgment).
- **Rooftop cellular plan:** houses packed without streets; access via roofs — extreme residential packing (attributed).
- **Art and installations:** bulls, breasts, skulls, vultures, wall paintings and sculptures as Mellaart’s famous repertoire; Mithen reads continuity of West Asian Neolithic mythology from Göbekli/Nevali Çori, Bouqras, ‘Ain Ghazal, early Jericho skull/raptor interests — contrasts Natufian “trust in nature” with Çatalhöyük “fear of the wild” (strong interpretation).
- **Hodder project:** from 1991 planning; doubts public buildings/priests/political leaders; questions shrine vs house distinction — Wendy Matthews microstratigraphy shows routine toolmaking in “shrines”; ritual and domestic intimately entwined so inhabitants may not have distinguished them (Hodder via Mithen).
- **Economy revised:** toward ordinary mixed farming rather than exceptional specialized economy alone (attributed synthesis).
- **Spatial order:** houses rebuilt same design same place with fixed activity areas; Hodder — age/gender/skill restricted seating/work locations; Mithen — lives ritualised under oppressive ideology of bulls, breasts, skulls, vultures (positions).

Does not overwrite later project chronologies or entanglement monographs above. Source: [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003]].

## From Roberts, *The Holocene* (1998)

*(Attributed — [[roberts-holocene-1998]]; Konya-plain catchment study, Roberts 1983, Roberts et al. 1997a.)*

Roberts uses Çatalhöyük (occupied c. 9000–8200 Cal. yr BP) as a case study in **site-catchment** environmental history. Its low-diversity alluvial catchment supported cereal cultivation and cattle husbandry rather than a broad-spectrum economy. He shows that the Konya basin held a large late-Pleistocene lake whose main phase ended ~20,000 Cal. yr BP, with several metres of Çarşamba-river alluvium deposited around the mound only *after* founding (from ~7500 BP) — so modern catchment soils misrepresent Neolithic conditions. The exploitation of newly formed alluvial soil-water is his concept of **"geological opportunism"** (Vita-Finzi; Limbrey; Roberts 1991). Contrast with the pre-farming Natufian catchment of [[eynan-ain-mallaha|Ain Mallaha]].

## Cauvin densification (2000)

*(Attributed — *[[cauvin-birth-of-the-gods-2000|Cauvin 2000]]* ch.3, using Mellaart’s seventh-millennium material as developed “explicit theology.”)*

- Cauvin reads Çatalhöyük (northern Levant diffusion stream in his model) as the place where Woman and Bull become a **structured goddess religion**: monumental relief goddess giving birth to bulls/rams; bucrania; carnivore jaws/boar tusks/vulture beaks in clay “breasts”; vulture frescoes; portable figurines (goddess on panther throne; Hacılar parallels) assembling fertility, maternity, royalty, mistress of animals — **[[mother-goddess|Mother-Goddess]]** traits.
- Bull murals with diminutive armed men = early bull-contest scenes ancestral to east-Mediterranean/Minoan ceremonies.
- Used **retrospectively** to interpret earlier Khiamian/Mureybet bucrania and female figurines — analogy/teleology risk; keep as Cauvin’s method, not wiki-voice identity of PPNA and Çatalhöyük religion.
- Late PPNB arrival in central Anatolia (Postscript): end-eighth-millennium inhabitants strongly PPNB-influenced after Aşıklı phase.

## Bellwood *First Farmers* densification (2005)

*(Attributed — [[bellwood-peter]], *[[bellwood-first-farmers-2005|First Farmers]]* 2005; brief comparative pins, not a site monograph.)*

- **Symbolic continuity (via Cauvin):** Later architectural display of cattle skulls in Çatalhöyük shrines noted as part of a symbolic complex continuous with earlier Jerf el Ahmar / Mureybet (~9000–8500 BC).
- **“Doorless cubicle” architecture:** Later-PPNB architectural style that, for Bellwood, characterizes some earliest agricultural communities far beyond the Levant — including central Anatolian Çatalhöyük (also Ganj Dareh D, Mehrgarh).
- **SE European Neolithic source (Pinhasi & Pluciennik, as of Bellwood 2005):** One position holds SE European Neolithic drawn from **central Anatolian** Neolithic (Çatalhöyük) more than from a single Levantine donor stream — more geographically precise than genetics alone at that date.

Site ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes. Source: [[bellwood-first-farmers-2005]].

## Nissen densification (1988)

*(Attributed — [[nissen-hans-j|Hans J. Nissen]], *[[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988]]* 1988, Ch. 2; synthesis of Mellaart-era exposures as of the mid-1980s, not Hodder-project reanalysis. Past tense of documentation.)*

As of Nissen 1988, excavations had uncovered about **440 m²** of densely built houses of standard size and pattern packed on an incline, with **no paths, ground-level doors, or inter-unit doors** — access and communication via **flat roofs**. From the lack of significant differences in house size/structure and the absence of communal facilities, he argued for a **socially undifferentiated community** on architectural grounds — but stressed that only about **one-eighth** of the settlement had been excavated. **Grave finds** pointed the other way: numerous multiple under-floor secondary burials after presumed exposure, with beads/stone tools rare in those deposits, versus a few burials with opulent goods (stone/wooden vessels, makeup utensils, bone belt buckles, exquisite obsidian/flint tools). He treated the contrast as clear evidence of **social differences**, yet the houses containing rich graves did not differ from the others. Rooms with bulls’ skulls/horns on walls, shelves, and “altars” might indicate a bull cult or totem animals; any cult rooms connected directly to living quarters — **no freestanding temple prototypes**. With [[jericho|Jericho]], he classed Çatalhöyük as a special early “town”-like extreme of density and stratification **not** on the main path to higher political organization via multi-tier settlement systems. Site ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genes. Source: [[nissen-early-history-ancient-near-east-1988]].

## Renfrew *Figuring It Out* (2003) — material-symbolic illustration

*(Attributed — Renfrew 2003, citing Mellaart-era synthesis.)* Çatalhöyük (~6500 BC in Renfrew's presentation) exemplifies early sedentary/farming **[[material-engagement]]**: mud-brick architecture, cereals and herds, later pottery, obsidian, textiles, plaster reliefs/paintings, figurines. Renfrew stresses that **sedentism preceded farming** in the Near East and that deity images/plastered skulls (also Ain Ghazal) are products of constitutive material symbolism, not mere reflections of prior theology. Secondary sketch only — not a substitute for Hodder-era site literature already in the vault.

## Hodder *Leopard's Tale* densification (2006)

*(Attributed throughout — [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]], *[[hodder-leopards-tale-2006|Çatalhöyük: The Leopard's Tale]]* 2006; on-disk source is the Yapı Kredi Turkish translation. Mid-project synthesis; absolute dates are mid-2000s framing and are **superseded for precision** by Bayesian results in *[[hodder-catalhoyuk-excavations-2009-2017|Excavations 2009–2017]]* 2022.)*

### The leopard puzzle

Hodder organises the book around a contrast: large wild cats (esp. leopards) dominate figurative art and installations, yet large-cat bones were essentially absent from the excavated faunal sample as of the mid-2000s (~24,000+ identified bones vs zero large-felid bones in the table he cites). He canvasses disposal, off-site processing, and taboo scenarios — combinations are likely; the puzzle is used as a wedge into wild/domestic, death/house, north-platform “clean” vs south-oven “dirty” spatial dualisms. A coda reports a later **leopard claw pendant** find that reinforces rarity without dissolving the taboo framing (attributed).

### Settlement as “kasaba” / social geography

Against pure “village” or pure “town” labels, Hodder argues agglomeration, neighbourhood clusters, dual-half layouts, and roof-street circulation produce a dense social geography without clear elite precincts. Mellaart’s “priestly quarter” is rejected: surface survey, geophysics, and excavation find houses and middens repeating across the mound (this line continues into the 2009–17 research design). Household production, sheep-centred economy, and long-distance obsidian remain key.

### House, history-making, shrine debate

Standard house: roof entry, platforms, hearth/oven south, sub-floor burial, plaster cycles. **“History houses”** accumulate more burials and installations; retrieval pits and plastered skulls (e.g. Building 42 find) are central to ancestor-focused practice. On [[catalhoyuk-shrine-house-debate]]: elaborately decorated buildings show domestic/craft residues; Mellaart’s shrine dualism is treated as etic over-partitioning (Position B strengthened). Mother-goddess primacy is challenged with sex-isotope similarity, age over sex in mortuary patterning, and re-readings of figurines (bear/animal rather than exclusively goddess) — **positions**, not wiki-voice.

### Production, materiality, personhood, change

Storage, “keeping-while-giving,” feasts, and hide/reveal cycles organise power more than surplus accumulation (attributed). Art is practice-bound and entangled with materials; upper levels see more portable art and clearer sex marking after Level VIA house independence (trend claim as of 2006). Domestication is framed as **entanglement intensification** rather than a single origin event — cross-link [[entanglement-theory]], [[domestication]].

## Hodder ed. *Excavations 2009–2017* densification (2022)

*(Attributed — [[hodder-ian|Ian Hodder]] (ed.), *[[hodder-catalhoyuk-excavations-2009-2017|Çatalhöyük Excavations: The 2009–2017 Seasons]]* , BIAA/CRP 12, 2022. Multi-author; Bayesian chronology **as of Bayliss et al. 2022**.)*

### Project phase and research aims

Fourth excavation phase (2009–2017) plus analysis/publication shifted from individual-house focus toward **social geography through time**, middle levels (South M–O / Mellaart VII–VI), and contiguous North Area buildings (North G). Conservation, UNESCO (2012), and display shelters constrained partial excavation of many buildings (floors often left). Overarching question: situate elaborate symbolism in environmental, economic, and social context; additional funded programmes on religion, cognition, and Girardian violence/sacred (published separately 2019–2020).

### Chronology (as of Bayliss et al. 2022)

- East Mound main sequence framed **~7100–5950 cal BC**; West to **~5600 BCE** (with Orton et al. 2018).
- **North Area preferred model** (`Catal_North.oxcal`): overall start **6695–6650** · end **6470–6400 cal BC** · use **195–275 years** (posterior HPDs as published).
- Building-level anchors include B.132 early North thorough dig ~**6650 cal BC** frame; B.77 fire **6555–6495 cal BC**; B.1 start revised later than 2014 IntCal20 framing.
- Major occupational **gaps** between some stacks (e.g. tens to >100 years between certain buildings); levels ≠ absolute contemporaneity.
- GDN/late-final Bayesian narrative extends late East sequence toward **~6015–5890 cal BC** end frame (as of GDN model).
- Method note: Neolithic bone collagen alkali-step failures drive strict exclusion rules at Poznań lab.

### Methods and settlement pattern

Unit → feature → space → building hierarchy; priority tours, diaries, multivocal database (continues 1999 reflexive programme — [[interpretation-at-the-trowels-edge]], [[reflexive-fieldwork]]). Geophysics (magnetometer + GPR/STREAM) maps dense occupation and open/track features beyond shelters. South Area phasing revises Mellaart height-stacking; South O burning cluster (B.76/79/80); open-space breaks and return of houses in later South letters.

### House variability and “history houses”

North and South catalogues document a continuum from short-lived small buildings to multi-phase **history houses** with many burials, horned installations, and fire-endings (e.g. B.52, B.77 with ≥39 burials, B.131 MNI ~41, B.102 “rich” production space). Burning may be intentional or accidental by building — keep as positions. Size does not equal elaboration (B.132). External “midden” spaces reinterpreted with retention walls and long-lived open areas. GDN Area (Barański) integrates late/final buildings and collapses some TP level labels into fewer architectural shells.

### Social differentiation

CRP synthesis continues to stress **limited economic dualism**: variation in burial richness, art, and house duration without clear priestly elite quarter. Post-**~6500 cal BC** dispersal and West Mound overlap mark major organisational shift (Hodder Ch.1 summary).

## Matthews densification (2003)

*(Attributed — [[matthews-archaeology-mesopotamia-2003|Matthews 2003]], using Çatalhöyük as his exemplar excavation project.)* Matthews highlights **micromorphology** — intact blocks of deposit (e.g., floor-plaster sequences) resin-impregnated and thin-sectioned — as showcased at the site, revealing food-preparation, trampling, and burning traces invisible to the naked eye (W. Matthews et al. 1997). He frames Hodder's claim to a **post-processual "reflexive methodology"** as itself contested: critics (Hassan 1997; Gamble 2001; Lucas 2001b) argue the project's field techniques and recording mechanics remain rooted in processual practice, a live methodological dispute Hodder answered in Hodder 1998. Bleda During's study of house access found a shift over time from neighbourhood-scale social units (with shrines) toward individual-building units — a "detachment of history via locality and buildings" (During 2001: 16) — while Ian Hodder and Peter Forest's structuralist readings interpret the art/symbolism as encoding oppositions (male/female, wild/domestic, life/death), sparked by what Hodder called "the violence of this imagery" (Hodder 1990: 4). Regional survey (Baird 1996) found a bimodal Konya-plain settlement pattern: one very large site (Çatalhöyük) ringed by several much smaller contemporaries.
