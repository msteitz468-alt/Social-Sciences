# Kerma

Major middle-Nile settlement and cemetery complex on the Dongola Reach (Third–Fourth Cataract sector of the Nile in modern Sudan). Densified from [[connah-african-civilizations-2016|Connah, *African Civilizations* (2016)]], principally Ch. 4 (middle Nile: Kerma, Napata, Meroë). All interpretive claims are attributed to Connah 2016 and specialists he cites until independent sources densify.

**Identity caution:** Archaeological locus ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetic population. Kerma the site is not equivalent to “Kushites,” later Nubian polities, or any continuous ethnic essence. Link with `associated_with` unless identity is argued. Four-way non-identity applies to Egyptian imports, pastoral groups, and later Napatan/Meroitic labels.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Connah (2016) presents Kerma mainly through synthesis of early cemetery work and later settlement archaeology rather than as a trench-by-trench monograph.

- **Reisner’s cemetery:** Connah reports George Reisner’s work on a cemetery of thousands of graves including eight unusually large tumuli (largest ~91 m diameter) with mud-brick internal structures; principal burials on beds with weapons and possessions were accompanied by sacrificed humans. In Tumulus X, Reisner recorded 322 sacrifices in the sacrificial corridor and estimated as many as 400 originally—evidence Connah reads as highly centralized political authority.
- **Bonnet’s settlement sequence:** Connah, citing Bonnet (1990; 1992), reports a fortified settlement of rectangular mud-brick and wood houses that evolved around a sanctuary that became the massive **Western Deffufa** (solid rectangular mud-brick mass ~27 × 52 m base, surviving height ~19 m; ≥12 masonry phases; originally a chambered temple later filled solid). The **Eastern Deffufa** is presented as a mortuary chapel. Dry climate is said to have preserved organic materials; Connah reports life expectancy ~31 years for natural deaths in the skeletal sample he cites.
- **Egyptian frontier context:** Middle Kingdom Second Cataract forts (e.g. Buhen under Senusret I, c. 1918–1875 BCE as dated in Connah’s Egyptian frame) and New Kingdom temples (including Jebel Barkal’s Temple of Amon) form the colonial backdrop Connah uses to frame Kerma’s climax during the Second Intermediate Period and its end with New Kingdom expansion. Buhen is Egyptian frontier infrastructure, not Kerma proper.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Connah 2016 Ch. 4; absolute phases approximate and dependent on Egyptian historical correlations plus Bonnet’s Pre-Kerma–Classic Kerma sequence.)*

- **Environmental frame:** Connah situates Kerma on the Dongola Reach (Fourth to Third Cataract), which he identifies as the earliest zone of [[complex-society]] development on the middle Nile (with later Napata). Sandstone reaches supported farming; granite stretches generally did not. Northern middle Nile is nearly rainless; southern half has a short wet season with rainfall rising southward. The Nile supplies water and silt along a narrow littoral; desert pastoralism, Connah argues (citing Edwards), was so important to the riverine economy that control of or coexistence with desert outsiders incentivized stronger political organization.
- **Pre-Kerma (before ~3000 BCE, approximate):** Connah, citing Bonnet, traces indigenous origins in Pre-Kerma before 3000 BCE, with substantial pastoralist roots (sheep/cattle associated with burials). He notes MacDonald’s linkage of sociopolitical change to “Mobile Elites” of savanna/Saharan margins from ~4000 BCE as a regional backdrop, not as a named people at Kerma.
- **Early and Middle Kerma (~1,500-year emergence span before Classic climax):** Connah reports a long trajectory through Early and Middle Kerma before Classic Kerma—chronology approximate as of his 2016 synthesis.
- **Classic Kerma (climax ~mid-second millennium BCE):** Connah places the kingdom’s climax in Classic Kerma during the Second Intermediate Period of Egyptian weakness. By the first half of the second millennium BCE the settlement included a very large circular wood-and-mud-brick elite building ≥10 m high (probable conical roof), which Connah likens to later ethnohistoric African audience chambers and treats as emphasizing African architectural character, contrasting with a later rectangular administrative building and Egyptian-style temple on site.
- **State and urban claim:** Connah claims Kerma as the earliest state in tropical Africa. He reports David O’Connor’s judgment that Kerma was “the earliest city in Africa outside of Egypt” and that its social complexity and political centralization were those of a state, not a complex [[chiefdom]]. Stable-isotope work Connah cites suggests a significant portion of the buried population originated elsewhere—mobility, not ethnic replacement claims.
- **Egyptian pottery at Classic Kerma:** Connah, citing Bourriau (1991), reports that Egyptian pottery at Classic Kerma is mainly commodity storage/transport jars, insufficient evidence for resident Egyptians, whereas Kerma pottery in Eighteenth-Dynasty Egypt is tableware/cooking pottery (indicating Nubian presence in Egypt).
- **End of independent Kerma:** Connah states Kerma disappeared with New Kingdom expansion (second half of the second millennium BCE), when Egyptian dominion reached at least the Third Cataract and inscriptions appear as far as Kurgus between Fourth and Fifth Cataracts.
- **After Kerma (regional succession, not continuous identity):** Connah places Napata (Kingdom of Kush) on the Dongola Reach from the ninth century BCE, focused until the fourth century BCE on a ~24 km stretch downstream from the Fourth Cataract (El Kurru, Nuri, Jebel Barkal, Sanam); local rulers with Jebel Barkal priestly sanction later ruled Egypt as the Twenty-fifth Dynasty (eighth–seventh centuries BCE). Later [[meroe|Meroë]] remains tied ritually to Napata for crowning and temple-building. These are successor polities, not “Kerma people” renamed.

### Subsistence and technology (Kerma-focused)

Connah reports mixed farming on inundated/irrigated soils in favoured reaches: barley cultivation; cattle, sheep, and goat herding; hunting and fishing; dogs and donkeys; ox-skull rows on mounds. He treats pastoralism as repeatedly reasserted against cultivation-only models. On technology, he judges Kerma deffufas and tombs indigenous in design and execution (in contrast to many later temples/pyramids showing alien inspiration with Nubian characteristics). Slag mounds are reported at Kerma (and Kawa, Argo) as well as the better-known iron industry at [[meroe|Meroë]].

## Significance

- **Earliest tropical African state/city (Connah’s frame):** Outside Pharaonic Egypt, Connah places some of the earliest African [[urbanization]] and [[state-formation]] evidence along the middle Nile; Kerma (before 2000 BCE in his summary phrasing; Pre-Kerma before 3000 BCE in the detailed sequence) is the deep root of a sequence continuing through Napata, [[meroe|Meroë]], Christian Nubian kingdoms, and later Islamic states.
- **Indigenous agency vs Egyptocentric diffusion:** Connah reports early twentieth-century scholars attributing Nubian developments to successive Egyptian influences; later work stressed indigenous character while acknowledging northern contributions. He rejects his own 1987 “cultural cul-de-sac” framing: citing Edwards, he presents Kerma and Meroë as early examples of an enduring Sudanic kingdom tradition (with Fuller on distinctive Sudanic power).
- **Not merely a [[secondary-state]] of Egypt:** Connah argues that explaining Nubian complexity as a direct result of Egyptian contact is too simple—exotic influences played a role, but important contributions and initial development came from within, yielding distinctively Nubian social complexity. Roots of Meroitic achievement, he holds (with Shinnie), go back to mid-second-millennium BCE Kerma, with cultural origins appearing contemporary with Pharaonic Egypt—so Nubian and Egyptian complexity should be read as parallel and interacting African achievements.
- **Trade corridor and [[prestige-goods-economy]]:** Connah models major long-lived middle-Nile cities as collection points for interior exports to Egypt, shifting upstream over time (Buhen → Kerma → Napata → Meroë → later Shendi/Soba/Sennar). North-bound gold, minerals, ivory, slaves, and exotica exchanged for south-bound manufactures/luxuries; imported prestige goods in elite graves and redistributed to supporters contributed to rulers’ power bases (Edwards via Connah). Diorite from west of Abu Simbel transported as far as [[giza|Giza]] (>1,200 km along the Nile) exemplifies commodity archaeology of the corridor.
- **[[productive-land-hypothesis]] / constraint–opportunity:** Connah treats the middle Nile as paradigmatic: river life versus scarcely habitable dry surroundings; irrigation, rainfed cultivation, and pastoralism exploited river, savanna, and desert margins so successfully that the area hosted some of Africa’s earliest states and cities. Origins of sociopolitical change, he argues, seem to pre-date large-scale long-distance trade takeoff even here.
- **Stratification package:** Monumental tombs, human sacrifice, and palace/temple labour demonstrate absolute monarchy; small houses and poorer graves indicate labouring free peasants and slaves; trade/manufacturing imply intermediate merchants, craftsmen, officials, and clergy. Pre-Christian integration of secular and spiritual authority (including sacrifice at Kerma) is said to have legitimized divine monarchy before later Christian “Crown and Cross” configurations.

## Key Debates

- **State vs complex chiefdom:** Connah endorses O’Connor’s state reading against a chiefdom-only model; the claim is interpretive and rests on tumuli, sacrifice, deffufa monumentality, fortification, and urban settlement scale.
- **“Earliest tropical African state”:** Connah’s own ranking claim; comparative early claims elsewhere in Africa depend on definitions of state, city, and tropical Africa as of 2016.
- **Egyptocentric residency and pottery:** Bourriau’s commodity-jar argument challenges models that treat Classic Kerma as an Egyptian colonial town on ceramic grounds alone.
- **Indigenous vs imported technology and culture:** Partitioning local production from imports and expatriate craftsmen remains difficult; Connah insists deffufas/tombs are indigenous while many later religious monuments show hybrid inspiration.
- **Cul-de-sac vs Sudanic genesis:** Shift from Connah 1987 / Alexander passive-receiver models to Edwards/Fuller Sudanic-kingdom framing is a major interpretive change across editions.
- **Pastoralism vs Nile cultivation primacy:** Edwards’s emphasis on rainfed agriculture and pastoral inputs (reported for the broader middle Nile) revises pure riverine-farming models that underwrite older environmental determinisms.
- **Secondary-state teleology:** Connah’s multi-causal synthesis (surplus on Nile silt/rain/grazing; scarce land under stress; stratification; trade as catalyst via prestige goods; alternating Egyptian control and indigenous reassertion) is offered against single-cause Egyptian stimulus models.
- **Limited pan-tropical influence:** Connah reports that middle-Nile urban/state traditions seem to have had little influence on the rest of tropical Africa (Shinnie; Alexander), with qualifications about diagnostic pottery visibility and early Islamic outliers in Darfur and Red Sea ports—not a claim of total isolation.
