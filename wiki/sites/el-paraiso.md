## Discovery and Excavation History

El Paraíso (also El Paraiso) lies in the Chillón Valley on Peru’s central coast, about 2 km from the sea. As of [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]], it is the **largest prepottery masonry monument in the Western Hemisphere**: more than 100,000 tons of stone; nine complexes across ~58 ha. A French excavation of one square complex (~50 m on a side, 8 m high) documented dual stairs, a red ceremonial court with sunken floor 4.5 × 4.25 m burned bright orange, and four 1-m charcoal pits on a surrounding bench — fire/incendiary ritual. Moseley assigns most other large coastal Preceramic monuments (except El Paraíso) to the Supe Tradition; El Paraíso anchors a distinct **Paraiso Tradition** and, for architect Carlos Williams, the starting point of the long coastal **U-shaped** civic-ceremonial tradition of inland irrigated valleys.

## Occupation Sequence

- **Plan:** two largest elongated parallel mounds form wings of a giant U (>50 m wide, >250 m long, ~7-ha plaza); base of the U only partial; same cardinal orientation as later regional U-shaped centers.
- **Construction:** multi-stage *shicra* (open-mesh reed satchel) stone fill — labor modularized as intact bags deposited, a Preceramic accounting practice.
- **Use pattern:** complexes lack domestic hearths; trash uncommon compared with Rio Seco or Aspero — users mostly non-resident. Midden protein all marine (especially small fish); industrial cultigens; fruits, achira, squash, jicama; adjacent ~150 ha floodplain probably small-canal cotton/crops. By late Preceramic, arable land short → sites >40 km up Chillón.
- **Dates:** construction underway by 2000 BC; about one-third of radiocarbon assays after 1800 BC (when pottery and intensive agriculture appear regionally) — Moseley allows possible maritime Preceramic organizational persistence after ceramic desert reclamation began.
- **U-shape origins uncertainty:** Williams treats Paraíso as U-tradition starting point (inland orientation and general shape), but Moseley notes the Paraíso economy was more maritime than agrarian; origins are also uncertain because a U-shaped configuration was superimposed atop La Galgada when pottery appeared ~1900 BC.

## Significance

Moseley (citing Williams) reads U-shaped centers as almost always facing inland mountains, opening ceremonial wings to mountain headwaters of desert rivers — architectural expression of agricultural concerns even when subsistence still heavily maritime. U-shaped sanctuaries are for Moseley the most enduring form of Andean ceremonial architecture (four millennia), later reduced in scale but retained as Chimor *audiencias* and Inca *masmas*. El Paraíso is "certainly the architectural expression of the ideological and organizational transformations of an impending new era" — the Initial Period of intensive irrigation farming and peak coastal mound-building ([[maritime-foundations-hypothesis]], [[andean-chronology]]).

Williams’s six-phase Initial Period U-shape sequence follows El Paraíso with increasing standardization and size (e.g. La Florida c. 1750–1650 BC; Garagay; San Jacinto) then late size decrease (e.g. Cardal).

## Key Debates

- **Maritime vs agrarian foundations:** Paraíso’s marine protein + industrial cultigens + U-shape facing the mountains sit at the center of MFAC vs inland-farming debates; Moseley uses it as transitional architecture, not pure MFAC poster child like littoral Aspero.
- **Origins of U-shape:** El Paraíso vs La Galgada vs later Initial Period standardization — unresolved as of Moseley 2001.
- **Labor catchment:** non-resident users imply supra-local support (real or fictive kinship), paralleling grander Supe centers.

## Historiography

Carlos Williams’s architectural seriation structures Moseley’s Paraíso Tradition narrative; French excavation supplies the best-described single complex. Primary monographs and full assay lists not yet ingested — dates and labor estimates as of Moseley 2001.
