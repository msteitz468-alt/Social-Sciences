# Snaketown

## Discovery and Excavation History

Snaketown (from the Pima word *Skoaquik*, "place of snakes") is the type site of the
[[hohokam-culture|Hohokam]] culture, on the upper terrace of the Gila River in southern
Arizona. Its wide undulating mounds drew attention from the late 19th century.
**Emil Haury** conducted the most extensive Hohokam excavations here (1934–35 and 1964–65),
investigating 167 of the site's hundreds of houses and defining **seven occupation phases**
(earliest c. AD 500, via Mound 29), as reported by [[fagan-ancient-north-america-1991|Fagan
(1991)]].

## Occupation Sequence

The site covers more than 1 km² with over 60 trash-filled mounds and an estimated 650–1,000
inhabitants. Dwellings evolved from large pithouses (extended families, clustered around a
central plaza) to smaller pole-and-brush single-family houses, with above-ground adobe
appearing in the **Classic period** (c. AD 1100–1450), when the community built **ballcourts**
and clay-capped **platform mounds** and functioned as a regional center on a large
canal-irrigation network. Snaketown declined and was abandoned in the 15th century.

## Significance

- **Hohokam irrigation and Mesoamerican links.** Snaketown anchors the Hohokam sequence and
  its canal-based agriculture; Haury compared its platform mounds to Mesoamerican forms, and
  the site yielded shell ornaments, copper bells, and macaws reaching "far into Mexico." Fagan
  flags the ballcourt–Mesoamerica connection as explicitly uncertain.
- **The migration-vs-indigenous debate.** Haury's original model (Mexican immigrants bringing
  ballcourts, platform mounds, irrigation, and pottery) was superseded by "more recent
  fieldwork" (Fish and Fish 1977; Wilcox 1985) favoring indigenous Cochise-Archaic roots — a
  central interpretive shift recorded on the [[hohokam-culture]] page.

## Key Debates

- **Origins of Hohokam complexity** — Mexican migration vs. indigenous development.
- **Pima descent.** Haury and others hold the modern Pima (Akimel O'odham) descend from the
  Hohokam — an argued continuity, not an established identity.
