# Saqqara

Memphite necropolis west of ancient [[memphis|Memphis]]; type site of the stepped stone [[pyramid-complex|pyramid]] (Djoser) and, with [[abusir]] and South Saqqara, the longest continuous royal burial landscape of Old Kingdom Egypt. Primary synthesis here follows [[lehner-mark|Mark Lehner]], *[[lehner-complete-pyramids-1997|The Complete Pyramids]]* (1997).

## Discovery and Excavation History

European and Egyptian work at Saqqara runs from early travellers through the Antiquities Service monopoly into modern institutional programmes. In Mariette’s last year (1880), foreman Mohammed Chahin opened [[pyramid-texts|Pepi I’s pyramid]]—the first in which Pyramid Texts were found; Emile Brugsch copied them and Flinders Petrie made unofficial copies. Gaston Maspero then directed an “attack on the entire front of the Memphite Necropolis, from Abu Roash to Lisht,” opening Unas, Pepi I/Neferirkare, and Teti within months—five formerly “dumb” Saqqara pyramids “spoken” in less than a year (Lehner 1997, reporting Maspero).

C.M. Firth and J.E. Quibell began scientific clearance of Djoser’s superstructure from 1924; Jean-Philippe Lauer joined in 1926 intending a short architectural stint and spent roughly seventy years restoring and reconstructing the complex from dislodged pieces (Lehner 1997). Walter Emery excavated most of the North Saqqara 1st-dynasty [[mastaba|mastabas]] between 1935/36 and 1956, establishing much of the architectural background to pyramid origins. Zakaria Goneim discovered and excavated Sekhemkhet’s unfinished 3rd-dynasty pyramid (1952–56). Gustave Jéquier excavated Pepi II’s complex (1926–36). Postwar French work (MFAS; Jean Leclant from 1966) restored thousands of Pyramid Text fragments at Pepi I and related monuments. Rainer Stadelmann, Miroslav Verner (at Abusir), Dieter Arnold, and Egyptian Antiquities programmes continued regional synthesis that Lehner draws on.

## Occupation Sequence

### Archaic necropolis and Memphis

Lehner places the founding of [[memphis]] as “The Wall,” later Ineb Hedj (“The White Wall”), near the North Saqqara escarpment when Upper Egyptian overlords moved administration to the Delta apex. A wide wadi from an old lake basin rises into the plateau—analogous, in Lehner’s reading, to the Abydos wadi path from living land to the dead. Along the cliff edge, 1st-dynasty builders erected a string of large niched-façade mastabas. Painted niching simulated wood frames and reed-mat patterns of the Per Wer and Per Nu; clay ox heads with real horns lined benches at several tombs; offering dishes survived in niches of mastaba 3503. Design innovations include stone beams over burial chambers, portcullis slabs, northern model buildings and boat pits, and—in mastaba 3507—an interior tumulus within a niched enclosure; mastaba 3038 (reign of Adjib) hid an eight-step mudbrick mound at ~49°, the closest formal precedent Lehner cites for Djoser’s Step Pyramid.

Immediately south of Djoser, two underground gallery systems more than 130 m long are ascribed by sealings to Hetepsekhemwy and Ninetjer (2nd dynasty); Stadelmann links empty desert rectangles farther west to Abydos-style valley enclosures, though others date them to the 3rd dynasty.

### Third dynasty: Djoser and Sekhemkhet

Within a reign Lehner dates c. 2630–2611 BCE, Netjerykhet (later called Djoser by New Kingdom visitors) and architect Imhotep built the Step Pyramid enclosure: limestone wall ~10.5 m high and ~1,645 m long enclosing ~15 ha. The pyramid rose in six steps to ~60 m on a base ~121 × 109 m, containing roughly 330,400 cu. m of clay and stone. Lauer’s six-stage evolution (M1–M3–P1–P′–P2) describes transformation from mastaba to stepped accretions; Stadelmann has modified details of the scheme. Builders hand-carved 1,680 recessed panels into already-laid masonry. Substructure totals more than 5.7 km of shafts, tunnels, and magazines; the Central Shaft is ~7 m square and ~28 m deep; reused limestone star-ceiling blocks form the earliest known royal star vault. Blue faience-tiled “palace” chambers and false-door stelae underlie the pyramid; the serdab statue gazes through peep-holes tilted ~13° toward the northern sky. Eastern galleries held ~40,000 stone vessels, many inscribed for royal ancestors rather than Djoser. The South Tomb replicates the granite vault and blue chambers at smaller scale—too small for a human burial; Lehner records hypotheses ranging from ka-tomb and Sed fictive burial to satellite-pyramid precursor.

Sekhemkhet’s “Buried Pyramid” southwest of Djoser never rose above the enclosure; intended ~70 m in seven steps (base ~120 m). Goneim found a unique single-piece alabaster sarcophagus with sliding end door, mortar-sealed and empty; a child’s wooden coffin in the unfinished South Tomb corridor is not Sekhemkhet.

### Fourth dynasty hiatus and return

Lehner stresses that pyramid builders largely abandoned Saqqara for almost the entire 4th dynasty—programme jumping to [[meidum]] then [[dahshur]] under Sneferu, then [[giza]] for three generations—returning with Shepseskaf’s giant mastaba (Mastabat Faraun) at South Saqqara around the end of that dynasty.

### Fifth–sixth dynasties

Userkaf returned to the east wall of Djoser’s enclosure; four successors clustered at [[abusir]]. Djedkare-Isesi opened South Saqqara; Unas built against Djoser’s southwest—smallest known Old Kingdom pyramid (~57.75 m square, ~43 m high)—with the first Pyramid Texts on chamber walls (283 of >700 known spells). Unas’s causeway (~750 m) reused blocks from Djoser’s already-ruining enclosure and carried a full economic and military relief programme. Teti, Pepi I, Merenre, and Pepi II standardized roughly 150 × 100 cubit pyramids (~78.75 × 52.5 m) with expanded texts; the Greek name Memphis likely derives from Pepi I’s pyramid name Men-nefer Pepi. After Pepi II, major complexes cease for more than 150 years; Ibi (8th dynasty) and possibly Merikare (9th/10th) mark late gasps at Saqqara. Middle Kingdom and New Kingdom use continues (private pyramids; Khaemwaset restoration of Unas; Serapeum cult).

## Significance

- **Scale:** Lehner counts 11 royal pyramids on the Saqqara plateau—more than any other Egyptian site—excluding satellites, queens’ pyramids, and Shepseskaf’s mastaba; with Abusir and South Saqqara the necropolis exceeds ~7.5 km N–S as the stone counterpart of living Memphis.
- **Form invention:** Djoser’s complex introduces the first large-scale stone pyramid, colonnade, hypostyle/portico vocabulary, torus and cavetto, life-sized statues, and “petrified” wood/reed architecture that later generations treat as hieroglyphic building types.
- **Textual corpus:** From Unas onward, Saqqara is the primary findspot of the [[pyramid-texts|Pyramid Texts]]—load-bearing for any account of Old Kingdom royal afterlife ideology.
- **Evolutionary chain:** Lehner chains [[hierakonpolis]] temple mound → Abydos valley enclosure / O’Connor “proto-pyramid” → North Saqqara mastaba interiors → Djoser as the formative sequence of the [[pyramid-complex]].

## Key Debates

- **Royal cemetery: Abydos vs Saqqara.** Emery first treated large North Saqqara mastabas as noble tombs, then as royal; others reverse the equation, treating Abydos as true royal burial ground (retainers’ stelae, combined tomb + valley enclosure area) and Saqqara as high-official cemetery (Merka stela; official titles on sealings). Cenotaph theories for Abydos remain a recorded position (Lehner 1997).
- **Djoser stage scheme.** Lauer’s six stages vs Stadelmann’s modifications; whether the Step Pyramid was planned from the start or emerged by accretion.
- **South Tomb function.** Ka house, Sed fictive death, canopic store (Lauer), statue burial / satellite precursor—Lehner lists without adjudication.
- **Pyramid Texts motivation.** Lehner reads Unas’s interior spells as independence from an “unreliable priesthood” after visible decay of earlier cults—an interpretive claim, not a wiki-voice finding.
- **Lepsius XXIX / Headless Pyramid attribution** (Merikare vs Menkauhor) and other late/anonymous monuments remain disputed in Lehner’s catalogue.

## Historiography

Saqqara entered modern Egyptology as both plunder frontier and restoration laboratory. The 1880–81 Pyramid Texts openings permanently reoriented “dumb” stone monuments into readable ritual machines. Emery’s mastaba campaign framed the Archaic–pyramid developmental narrative still central to Lehner. Lauer’s multi-decade reconstruction made Djoser’s complex the most thoroughly restored early royal landscape—and also locked certain architectural readings into the public visual record. Lehner’s 1997 synthesis is a high-level catalogue drawing on Lauer, Emery, Goneim, Jéquier, Stadelmann, Verner, Arnold, and MFAS rather than a primary excavation report; dimensions and OCR-sensitive numbers in secondary extracts should be checked against print where precision matters. Collection coverage for this vault begins with Lehner 1997; specialist monographs on individual complexes remain to be ingested.
