## Discovery and Excavation History

Izapa lies in southeastern Chiapas on a tributary of the Suchiate River — the river that divides Mexico from Guatemala — on the Pacific coastal plain. The site is large, with earthen mounds faced with river cobbles, courts and plazas, and a possible ball court (Coe & Koontz 2013). Coe & Koontz do not detail a specific excavation history in the extracted material; excavators are recorded here as [[unknown]] pending further ingest.

## Occupation Sequence

Izapa was founded in the Early Preclassic, reached its height in the Late Preclassic, and persisted into the Proto-Classic period (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Significance

Izapan art derives from Olmec models but is distinguished by cluttered, baroque, multi-figure compositions, in contrast to the single-ruler focus typical of Olmec monuments; it depicts sacred hero narratives still current at the time of Spanish contact, nearly 2,000 years later. Izapan stelae are typically paired with toad-shaped "altars" read as rain symbols; the principal gods depicted are Olmec-derived figures with elongated "tapir-trunk" upper lips, dubbed the "Long-lipped God" and understood as a prototype of the later Maya rain deity Chac (Coe & Koontz 2013).

Coe & Koontz identify Izapan civilization as "the connecting link in time and space between the earlier Olmec civilization and the later Classic Maya": the earliest Maya monuments in the northern Guatemalan lowlands appear derived from Izapan prototypes. Izapa also held priority in Long Count adoption relative to the Classic Maya — a stela in Izapan style at El Baúl, Guatemala, carries a Long Count date 256 years earlier than the earliest dated Maya monument (CE 292) (Coe & Koontz 2013).
