# Dragsholm

Paired prehistoric burials at Dragsholm, Zealand, Denmark (discovered 1973). In *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998, Ch. 2), [[bradley-richard|Richard Bradley]] uses the site as the load-bearing **Mesolithic → Neolithic worldview contrast**: two graves side by side on a slight knoll, less than three centuries apart, whose occupants “had lived in quite different worlds.”

## Discovery and Excavation History

Reported by Brinch Petersen (1974); Bradley’s account is secondary. Each grave is an isolated deposit of near-complete bodies; dating by radiocarbon and artefacts.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed via Bradley 1998.)*

- **Earlier (Late Mesolithic):** Two women; decorated bone dagger, bone awl, numerous pendants of wild pig and red deer teeth; beads of cattle and elk teeth; red ochre; amber and a single arrowhead as partial overlaps with the later grave; bone chemistry indicates marine resources; burials lacked their feet — possibly related to bone-circulation practices.
- **Later (Early Neolithic):** Man; pot, axe, battle axe, ten flint arrowheads, amber pendants; non-marine diet.

## Significance

Stages the ideological transformation that, on Bradley’s reading, made agriculture and freestanding monuments **thinkable**: Neolithic grave goods emphasize stone artefacts and culture/nature control; Mesolithic grave goods emphasize the animal kingdom and organic materials within a reciprocal natural world. Had the Neolithic burial had a mound/chamber, Bradley notes it would sit with the northern European [[long-mound]] / [[megalith]] distributions.
