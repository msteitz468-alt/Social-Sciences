## Discovery and Excavation History

Nong Nor is a coastal shell-midden and later cemetery site now behind the present shore of the Gulf of Siam, about 14 km south of [[khok-phanom-di]]. It was surveyed in 1990 and excavated over three seasons by [[higham-charles]] and Rachanie Thosarat (Higham and Thosarat 1998a, as synthesized in [[higham-early-cultures-mainland-southeast-asia-2002]]). Test pitting of the margins estimated a site area of about **700–1000 m²**, of which roughly half was uncovered. Boyd (1998), as reported by Higham, reconstructed a former landscape of indented marine embayments, with Nong Nor on a protected shore that still had open-sea access.

The excavation is methodologically important for two reasons Higham stresses: (1) fine-mesh flotation (under 1 mm) recovered tiny land snails and dense fish bone that coarser screening would miss — a caution for subsistence claims at sites excavated without fine mesh; (2) the site preserves **two stratified uses** of the same locus — a brief third-millennium BCE hunter-gatherer occupation and a later Bronze Age cemetery cut into that midden — allowing coastal forager and later ranked-village mortuary patterns to be compared on one landform.

## Occupation Sequence

### Hunter-gatherer occupation (c. 2450 BCE / ~4500 BP)

(as of Higham 2002) Higham dates the first occupation to a brief period measured in **months rather than years**, around **2450 BCE**. Geomorphology places the site on the edge of a marine inlet about 4,500 years ago. The shell midden is a few centimetres thick on the eastern margin and up to **60 cm** in the centre, dominated by *Meretrix lusoria*, a cockle adapted to sandy beaches.

**Subsistence.** Inhabitants took many fish (including tiger and bull sharks), hunted marine mammals (dolphin) and large eagle ray; few land animals are represented, and nearly all recovered land-animal bones were worked into artefacts. Higham reports **no evidence for cultivation or domestic animals**; he judges a tide-affected marine inlet unsuitable for rice and reads the evidence as marine adaptation.

**Material culture.** Clay anvils, a burnishing pebble with the single hunter-gatherer burial, sherds, and complete vessels show that these foragers made **burnished and incised pottery on-site** and used polished stone adzes — a finding Higham uses against any simple equation of pottery with farming. The only hunter-gatherer burial recovered was a woman seated or crouched under pottery vessels (with a burnishing pebble among associated items).

**Demography / duration.** From the thin occupation layer, lack of evidence for periodic abandonment, and small site size, Higham infers a **small population and brief (perhaps seasonal) occupation**; pottery may have served cooking, storage, and mortuary offerings during that stay.

### Bronze Age cemetery

(as of Higham 2002; Higham and Thosarat 1998a) One hundred sixty-six Bronze Age graves were cut into the third-millennium BCE coastal occupation over more than **400 m²**, divided into eastern and western groups (the western laid out in rows comparable to [[ban-lum-khao]]). Thinning at the margins suggests most of the cemetery was uncovered. **No Bronze Age occupation remains** were encountered — Higham reads the cemetery as set apart from settlement.

Shared Bronze Age mortuary features include easterly head orientation, pottery dominance, marble bangles, pigs’ foot bones, shell disc beads, and infant jar burials at women’s feet, plus a greater range of goods than at Ban Lum Khao. Of 50 complete graves, seven included bronze jewellery; two people had clusters of copper-socketed implements resembling Nil Kham Haeng products (form and lack of tin alloying point, Higham argues, to exchange with Khao Wong Prachan Valley production centres). Tin earrings and spiraliform tin bangles occur; exotic stone includes carnelian, talc, jade, and serpentine (serpentine bangles repaired with cast bronze ties, as at [[ban-na-di]]). Local shell manufacture is evidenced by unfinished pieces; tiger canine pendants appear; dogs’ crania regularly accompany the dead. Higham tallies about **60 bronze/copper artefacts** (38 bangles, 4 earrings, 15 socketed implements, 3 coils). He identifies the carnelian beads as the **earliest known from prehistoric Thailand** (largest 6 mm wide; as of Higham 2002).

**Social patterning.** Statistical analyses (principal components / multidimensional scaling, as reported by Higham) isolate a majority of graves with few offerings and small sets of relatively wealthy adults (marble bangles, shell pendants, dog crania; a western enclave of four women and one man with multiple exotic bangles; an eastern man with conus shells). Eastern vs western distinction is largely horizontal stratigraphy, with exotic stone almost exclusive to the later eastern sector. Higham finds no clear adult age–wealth correlation; no gender wealth bias except grinding stones with men; children receive the same ritual range as adults; and **no correlation between bronze and wealthy graves** or particular age/sex.

**Bioarchaeology.** Domett (1999), via Higham: infant mortality under one year about **11%** at Nong Nor (far below early-phase [[khok-phanom-di]]); Nordin’s score indicates good nutrition; stature and health broadly similar across Bronze Age samples (females ~154–156 cm; males ~164–168 cm).

## Significance

Nong Nor is a dual-period keystone:

1. **Coastal foragers with pottery but without farming.** The third-millennium occupation shows that burnished/incised ceramics and polished adzes can belong to a marine hunter-gatherer economy with no rice or stock — undercutting coastal-origins models of [[rice-domestication]] that Higham explicitly rejects (no rice in the first occupation phase; contrast later interaction scenarios at [[khok-phanom-di]]).
2. **Bronze Age coastal cemetery with exchange depth.** The later graves document participation in inland copper-production networks (Khao Wong Prachan / Nil Kham Haeng style implements), tin ornament use, and the earliest carnelian yet known from prehistoric Thailand (as of Higham 2002), while mortuary statistics support Higham’s broader Bronze Age model of **limited ranking without centralization** — wealth enclaves exist, but bronze does not map cleanly onto a separate elite stratum (cf. [[ban-lum-khao]], [[ban-na-di]], [[chiefdom]]).
3. **Method exemplar** for fine-mesh flotation and for reading re-use of coastal middens as dedicated cemeteries.

## Key Debates

- **Pottery ≠ agriculture.** Nong Nor is Higham’s clearest Central Thai case that ceramic technology among coastal groups need not imply cultivation.
- **Coastal rice-origins hypothesis.** Higham states that hypothesis is not supported: no rice at first occupation here; [[khok-phanom-di]] initial settlement ~2000 BCE not fifth millennium; early estuarine environments poorly suited to rice.
- **Bronze and social rank.** Absence of bronze–wealth correlation at Nong Nor feeds the regional argument that Bronze Age prestige goods circulated within flexible, non-centralized ranking systems rather than fixed chiefly monopolies — a position developed on the [[southeast-asian-bronze-chronology-debate]] and [[chiefdom]] pages.
- **Four-way non-identity.** Marine foragers, later cemetery populations, and any linguistic or genetic labels must not be silently equated; Higham treats continuity/discontinuity as open where data are silent.

## Historiography

Nong Nor enters the literature primarily through Higham and Thosarat’s 1990s fieldwork and the 1998a monograph, then through Higham’s 2002 regional synthesis. It is less famous than [[ban-chiang]] or [[khok-phanom-di]] but methodologically sharper for coastal forager–farmer transition questions. Dating of the hunter-gatherer phase rests on radiocarbon frameworks reported as ~2450 BCE / ~4500 BP (calibration status as presented in Higham 2002 should be treated carefully). The Bronze Age cemetery chronology is relative to regional phases rather than a tightly published absolute sequence in the 2002 synthesis.
