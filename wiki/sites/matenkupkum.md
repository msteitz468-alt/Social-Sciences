## Discovery and Excavation History

Matenkupkum is a rockshelter in New Ireland, Bismarck Archipelago, Papua New Guinea, excavated by J. Allen and C. Gosden as part of the 1985 Lapita Homeland Project field season, with lithic materials reported in J. Allen et al. (1989) (attributed, [[kirch-patrick-vinton|Kirch]] 2017). Kirch's text renders the site name inconsistently as "Matenkupkum" (the predominant form) and once as "Matemkupkum" — treated here as an OCR/transcription variant of the same site, with Matenkupkum used as the canonical form. As a controlled, professionally documented Lapita Homeland Project excavation, the site is a comparatively reliable witness by the standards of Pleistocene Melanesian archaeology, though the calibration status of its key radiocarbon date is not stated in the source and should be treated as uncertain.

## Occupation Sequence

The oldest occupation level is radiocarbon dated to 35,410 ± 430 years B.P. (J. Allen and Gosden 1996:186). Kirch's text does not specify whether this is a calibrated or conventional (uncalibrated) date; per the Chronological Uncertainty Protocol this is flagged as likely a conventional 14C determination pending verification, and it should not be silently relabeled cal BP. Recovered material includes simple flake/core stone tools alongside fish bone and shellfish remains.

## Significance

Kirch describes the site's faunal assemblage as "possibly the earliest evidence of marine resource exploitation anywhere in the world" (attributed claim). Matenkupkum is one of the oldest secure Pleistocene occupation sites in Near Oceania — a region of islands never land-linked to Sahul even during lowest sea-level stands — and its age is cited as evidence that early modern humans undertook deliberate, purposive open-water voyaging to reach it, rather than accidental drift.

## Key Debates

The site is invoked (with [[kuk-swamp|Kuk]]'s Highlands sequence and other Bismarck/Solomons sites) in the broader, largely resolved historiographical shift from earlier "accidental drift" models of Pacific colonization (e.g., following Mulvaney's 1962 Kenniff Cave dates) to the current consensus of purposive, repeated voyaging — Kirch presents the accidental-drift view as superseded rather than as a live scholarly dispute.
