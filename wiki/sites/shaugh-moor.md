# Shaugh Moor

Shaugh Moor, on the southwest edge of Dartmoor (Devon), is a Bronze Age relict landscape whose integrated excavation and environmental study make it a model case of how prehistoric land use and soil change combined to produce a now-abandoned moorland — used by [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) to illustrate the near-permanent human transformation of an upland environment.

## Discovery and Excavation History

A ~5 km² block was rescue-excavated in the late 1970s ahead of china-clay quarrying (Wainwright and the Central Excavation Unit; Smith et al. 1981; Balaam et al. 1982), as an integrated archaeology-plus-environmental-history project combining excavation with soil science and [[pollen-analysis|pollen]] analysis above and below the boundary walls.

## Occupation Sequence

In attributed voice, from Roberts (1998): the site's stone walls, or **reaves** — part of the wider Dartmoor prehistoric boundary system — were built around 3800 Cal. yr BP, used for roughly 1200 years, and abandoned by c. 2600 Cal. yr BP, after which the ground became indistinguishable from present-day moorland. Round stone houses attest settlement, possibly seasonal/transhumant. Pollen above and below the Wotter reave records Neolithic clearance and regeneration before reave construction. Crucially, the shift from acid brown-earth soils to podzols and gley soils began *before* Bronze Age occupation and continued under it — so [[soil-erosion|soil degradation]] was partly independent of, and partly compounded by, human land use.

## Significance

Shaugh Moor demonstrates (attributed) that upland abandonment resulted from the interaction of human land use and progressive soil deterioration, not from either alone — a key case for Roberts' argument that later-Holocene environmental change is inseparably natural-and-cultural. Its economy was pastoral (hoof-print evidence of sheep/cattle; abundant pastoral-weed pollen; phosphate levels not indicating arable manuring).

## Key Debates

Whether Dartmoor's Bronze Age upland abandonment was driven primarily by climatic deterioration ([[holocene-climate-change|late-Holocene cooling/wetting]]), by soil exhaustion and podzolization, or by social/economic change is unresolved; Roberts (1998) draws an explicit analogy between such upland abandonment and modern [[desertification]] as comparable cases of human pressure on fragile environments.

## Historiography

The Shaugh Moor project exemplifies the late-1970s British model of the integrated landscape excavation — archaeologists, soil scientists, and palynologists working one block together — that shaped [[environmental-archaeology|environmental archaeology]]'s methods.
