# Doggerland

Submerged late Pleistocene–early Holocene landscape of the southern North Sea plain that once joined south-east Britain to continental Europe. In [[cunliffe-barry|Barry Cunliffe]]’s *[[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013|Britain Begins]]* (2013), Doggerland is the huge low plain of rolling hills joining SE Britain to the adjacent continent from the Pas de Calais to Denmark — forested, gradually inundated, and for up to four millennia a land through which hunter-gatherers could roam.

**Identity caution (four-way non-identity):** Doggerland is a **submerged geographical landscape**, not a people, language, genetic population, or archaeological culture. Artefact styles shared across the plain (Pennines to Poland) are `associated_with` connectivity networks, not proof of a single ethnic or linguistic community. Genetic claims about Atlantic vs Doggerland routes of post-LGM recolonization are dated to the literature Cunliffe synthesizes (**as of Cunliffe 2013**) and must not be silently equated with “Doggerland people.”

## Discovery and Excavation History

Doggerland is known primarily through **marine geology, bathymetry, sea-level reconstruction, and incidental seabed recovery**, not through a classical terrestrial excavation campaign.

- **Continental-shelf framing.** Cunliffe reports the British Isles as submerged uplands of a western Eurasian plateau whose shelf surface typically lies 100–200 m below present sea-level, plunging beyond the shelf edge to ocean depths ≥2,000 m; emergent islands occupy less than half the shelf’s area (roughly lat. 46°–61°N to long. 12°W) (attributed, *Britain Begins* Ch. 2).
- **LGM configuration.** At Last Glacial Maximum, Cunliffe reports ice ~2 km thick on Scandinavia and ~1.5 km over much of Britain south to the west Midlands–Wash (and N Ireland); sea-level ~150 m lower exposing the shelf; a Channel River fed by the Thames, Rhine, and Seine; and the North Sea basin as polar desert/tundra (attributed).
- **Isostatic vs eustatic competition.** Postglacial isostatic uplift (Scotland still ~3 mm/yr in his account; Gulf of Bothnia ~9 mm/yr) competes with global eustatic sea-level rise from meltwater, producing place-specific land–sea outcomes — raised beaches in the north, relative rise and “fossil forests” in the south (~2 mm/yr relative rise) (attributed). Dutch-coast estimates he cites include rapid mean sea-level rise of ~7 m from ~5500 to ~2000 BC, then slower ~1.5 m from 2000 BC to present, with strong regional variation, tidal-amplitude effects, and storm surges (attributed).
- **Research mode.** Knowledge accumulates from cores, seismic imaging, trawled artefacts, and comparison with terrestrial early Holocene sites on either shore of the former plain. Cunliffe notes that more material of types linking eastern Britain with northern France, Belgium, and the Netherlands should lie under the drowned plain (attributed).

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed synthesis — Cunliffe 2013, especially Chs. 2 and 4; environmental and material evidence classes.)*

### Glacial and late-glacial prelude

- **LGM abandonment.** Intensifying Devensian ice pushed habitation south until, in Cunliffe’s account, ~25,000 years ago Britain was abandoned for ~10,000 years; southern Britain was re-populated only from ~15,000 years ago as climate improved (attributed).
- **Younger Dryas (Loch Lomond).** Windermere interstadial warmth ended ~10,900 BC with ~15°C mean-temperature drop in a generation, lasting >1,000 years, likely from Gulf Stream shut-off by meltwater; people probably mostly withdrew south; continuous British history in his narrative restarts ~9600 BC (attributed).

### Early Holocene land-bridge and forest Mesolithic

- **Recolonization after ~9600 BC.** Rapid warming from ~9600 BC toward conditions by ~8000 BC as warm or warmer than today; pioneers re-entered an emptied landscape. Cunliffe places Holocene pioneers as coming from refuges stretching from Atlantic France across to Germany, culturally indistinguishable from Ahrensburgian groups (Belgium, Netherlands, Poland, Germany; e.g. Stellmoor reindeer intercept camps), moving north with the ecological zone rather than fully retooling subsistence — “the first Britons, the ancestors of a significant component of the present-day British population” (attributed voice; genetic ancestry claims as of his 2013 synthesis).
- **Forest adjustment by ~9000 BC.** Birch/pine then hazel forced hunters either to follow traditional prey north or adapt to forest: forests support only 20–30% of the animal biomass of open landscapes; forest animals less migratory and more dispersed → smaller bands, limited territories, dietary diversification (plants, waterfowl, shore/sea) and Mesolithic tool-kits (microliths, axes/adzes, bone/antler gear) (attributed).
- **Doggerland as inhabited plain.** For up to four millennia, hunter-gatherers could roam the forested low plain joining SE Britain to the continent from Pas de Calais to Denmark while it was gradually inundated (attributed, R2-060 synthesis).
- **Material connectivity across the plain.** Artefact types show broad cultural similarity from the Pennines to Poland with regional styles; distinctive harpoon-head types link eastern Britain with northern France, Belgium, and the Netherlands — evidence of networks sharing ideas, technologies, and beliefs across what is now drowned land (attributed). Association ≠ single people.
- **Ireland separates earlier.** Cunliffe reports Ireland’s separation from the mainland around 9000 BC as rising seas flooded the meltwater-scoured Irish Sea river valley, breaking the last land bridge between northern Ireland and the Inner Hebrides (attributed). Early Holocene Channel was already a wide waterway up to ~100 km; the future Irish Sea ~50 km separating Ireland from Britain (attributed, R2-070).

### Inundation chronology (disputed)

Cunliffe presents Britain’s later separation via **submergence of Doggerland** and **flooding of the Channel River**:

| Claim | Date / process | Status in Cunliffe 2013 |
|---|---|---|
| Traditional islandization window | 7500–6500 BC | Older consensus he reports |
| Final Channel breakthrough (mollusc-habitat sequences) | 5800–5400 BC | Later revision he highlights |
| Full Channel–North Sea connection | possibly not until ~3800 BC | Possible late completion |
| Doggerland remnants as island(s) | fifth millennium BC | Possible residual land |
| Gradual inundation until North Sea and Channel tidal currents joined | “probably between 6500 and 5500 BC” in one of his formulations | Parallel phrasing in settlement chapter |

**Date precision on this page is `disputed`:** competing traditional and revised windows coexist in the same synthesis; do not promote a single islandization year to wiki voice.

### Catastrophic pulse ~5800 BC

Cunliffe reports a thick sand layer on eastern Scottish coasts dated ~5800 BC as a single exceptional storm surge, **possibly a tsunami** from a submarine landslide NW of Norway after an earthquake, with potentially devastating irreversible impact on low-lying Doggerland (attributed; concurrent environmental event — mechanism for sole causation of social change not asserted in wiki voice).

### After major inundation

- After ~2000 BC, sea-level rise slowed; coastal change became more local (long-shore drift bars, peatbogs, alluviation) (attributed).
- Cunliffe suggests post-6000 BC inundation rates and storm surges would have embedded “sea as inundator” in folk memory, and that early medieval inundation legends may derive from deep generational transmission — a **cognitive geography** thesis, not a demonstrated oral-history chain (theoretical, attributed).
- **East vs west cognitive geographies:** eastern communities experienced gradual marsh/inlet isolation with land-dominant navigation; western communities faced ocean promontories and open-sea voyaging (including to Ireland), producing different concepts of space and mobility (theoretical, attributed).

### Dual recolonization routes (Cunliffe’s redress)

Alongside eastern spread **across Doggerland** from the North European Plain, Cunliffe argues settlers from Late Glacial Cantabrian and SW French refuges spread north along the **Atlantic coastal zone** — a hypothesis frequent in early twentieth-century literature but, he holds, lost sight of in more recent east-focused discussions; he seeks to rebalance the narrative (position). Dual-route framing is a thesis about mobility corridors, not an identity equation.

## Significance

- **Bridge landscape for post-glacial recolonization** of Britain: eastern plain route from the North European Plain and Maglemosian-related forest adaptations.
- **Inundation = island Britain:** eventual drowning of Doggerland and flooding of the Channel River convert a peninsula into an archipelago, reshaping connectivity for later [[mesolithic]] and [[neolithic]] communities (timing disputed; see above).
- **Archaeological under-sample warning:** the best-preserved Mesolithic record on land (e.g. [[star-carr]]) is not a full census of the early Holocene world; substantial settlement, exchange, and ritual landscapes may lie under the North Sea.
- **Genetics vs archaeology on routes** (as of Cunliffe 2013): Cunliffe reports geneticist emphasis on an Atlantic seaboard contribution (e.g. maternal haplotype H expanding from a north Iberian refuge largely along the Atlantic; most British H genes via direct Atlantic route, some via North European Plain/Doggerland) and notes that genetic prominence of the Atlantic route **challenges archaeological preconceptions** that emphasized inward migration across Doggerland (attributed; date-stamp genetics; does not settle the [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]]).
- **Population narrative setup:** he envisages gradual colonization via Atlantic seaways and Doggerland, growth from small pioneer numbers toward possibly several hundred thousand by the Mesolithic period’s end (position vs density-based 3,000–20,000 estimate he rejects as undercount) (attributed).

## Key Debates

- **When did Britain become an island?** Traditional 7500–6500 BC vs mollusc-sequence final breakthrough 5800–5400 BC vs full Channel–North Sea connection possibly ~3800 BC, with fifth-millennium residual islands possible (Cunliffe 2013 reporting).
- **East (Doggerland plain) vs west (Atlantic littoral) recolonization** — and whether twentieth-century archaeology over-weighted the eastern forest corridor (Cunliffe’s redress; position).
- **Genetics vs material-culture preconceptions** on Atlantic vs Doggerland migration emphasis (**as of Cunliffe 2013** modern-DNA phylogeography; aDNA methods and interpretations have moved since — treat as dated synthesis). See also [[britain-invasion-continuity-debate]].
- **Tsunami/storm surge ~5800 BC** as contributor to Doggerland’s end-state vs gradual eustatic drowning alone (`concurrent_with` / possible `contributed_to` only with stated mechanism; not sole `caused_by` in wiki voice).
- **Folk memory of inundation** — Cunliffe’s speculative link from post-6000 BC experience to early medieval legends (theoretical; not established oral continuity).

## Historiography

- **Name and construct.** “Doggerland” is a modern archaeological label for a reconstructed submerged landscape (Dogger Bank as eponymous core); it is an etic survey-region construct, not an emic polity.
- **Evidence class profile.** Environmental (sea-level, isostasy, bathymetry, tsunami deposits) + material (trawled finds, shore-based type parallels) + genetic literature summarized **as of Cunliffe 2013**. Independent convergence is incomplete: genetics and lithic networks can disagree on which corridor carried which lineages or styles.
- **Relation to classic terrestrial sites.** Early Mesolithic inland lake sites such as [[star-carr]] sit on the western fringe of the same early Holocene world that included Doggerland; Mithen and others stress lost coastal and Doggerland landscapes relative to inland preservation bias (cross-link; do not collapse site = plain).
- **Source for this densification:** [[cunliffe-britain-begins-2013]] (Cunliffe 2013); thinker [[cunliffe-barry]].

### Facing the Ocean cross-check (Cunliffe 2001)

*(Attributed — [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001|Facing the Ocean]], Ch. 2 and Ch. 4; an earlier, independent Cunliffe synthesis broadly consistent with the 2013 densification above.)* Around 8000 BCE, Cunliffe (2001) reports, Britain was still part of the European mainland, the chalk ridge between Dover and Calais cut only by a wide river valley flanked by marshes; the island of Jersey was at that time a promontory of the Cotentin peninsula rather than an island. Sea-level change over the following ten millennia is attributed to eustatic readjustment following deglaciation, competing regionally with isostatic rebound; the 2001 book frames Britain's post-glacial islanding as part of a broader Atlantic-façade sea-level history (submerged forests at Mounts Bay near Penzance; partially inundated megaliths at Er Lannic, Gulf of Morbihan, and the allée couverte at Kernic, Brittany) rather than offering its own separate date range for the Channel/North Sea breakthrough — see the disputed-date table above for the specific chronology. Source: [[cunliffe-facing-the-ocean-2001]].
