# Gao-Saney

**Gao-Saney** is a tell and **cemetery** complex near [[gao-ancien|Gao Ancien]] in the Middle Niger (Mali), famous in West African Islamic archaeology for **Arabic-inscribed stelae** (including white marble pieces for which an Andalusian origin was proposed) and for fired-brick tomb structures re-excavated by Colin Flight and reviewed/re-investigated by [[insoll-timothy|Timothy Insoll]] (*[[insoll-islam-archaeology-history-gao-1996|Islam, Archaeology and History]]* 1996).

## Discovery and Excavation History

- **1931 — B. Le Pontois:** poorly documented; Mauny later listed beads, bracelets, copper torque (Insoll notes Mauny’s list is incomplete vs. Musée des Arts Africains et Océaniens holdings).
- **1939 — Chambon / Bartoli:** cemetery tomb of fired brick (Flight’s Structure F); “royal” stelae association; documentation thin (letters 1939/1941); Sauvaget (1948–50) and Viré (1958–59) published stelae.
- **1940–41 — Colonel Duboin** and later missions: additional poorly controlled work.
- **1970s — Colin Flight:** re-excavation of Structures F/G, H (grand caveau complex), K (petit caveau), J (enclosure); chronology and function revised by Insoll 1996.
- **French–Malian mission:** sole C14 from the tell (Pa 412), calibrated **AD 975–1150** (Raimbault & Sanogo 1991 as cited by Insoll); precise charcoal context poorly known.
- **1993 — Insoll:** new trenches (incl. October 1993 trench noted on his plan); re-synthesis of cemetery + occupation mound.

**Reliability:** early cemetery digs are low-quality witnesses (spoil, missing documentation); Flight and Insoll improve structural recording. Epigraphic publications are stronger for stelae texts than for findspot precision.

## Occupation Sequence

Insoll’s analytical window aligns with **ca. AD 900–1250** regional Islamization and trade. Stelae dates cluster in the **12th century** in the literature Insoll reviews. Occupation mound vs cemetery are spatially related but functionally distinct — do not collapse into one “site type.”



## 1993 Excavation and Surface Results (attributed — Insoll 1996)

- Tell heavily **looted** (~80% surface disturbance reported); Wadi Gangaber separates zones.
- Insoll trench 4 m² (14–20 Oct 1993): undivided **ash midden** to ~230 cm; occupation roughly **10th–13th c.** (as of his synthesis); manufacturing-oriented assemblage contrasts with Gao Ancien’s mercantile/elite profile.
- Cemetery: Flight Structures F/G/H (grand caveau complex), K (petit caveau), J (enclosure); Insoll revises function/chronology debates.
- **Stelae:** Arabic-inscribed stones including white marble pieces argued (Sauvaget tradition; Farias; Davidson via Insoll) as **Almería/Andalusian** imports — **hypothesis**. Ethnicity of named rulers disputed (Hunwick, Lange positions). Insoll: stelae show a **diverse-origin Muslim elite community** in the early second millennium.
- Fired-brick funerary architecture rare outside this complex in the surveyed region.

## Dual-site model

With [[gao-ancien]]: Saney as cemetery + manufacturing/tell on the north–Tilemsi approach; Ancien as river-town Islamized quartier. Do not collapse into one “capital site.”

## Significance

- Premier West African case for **Islamic epigraphy** and elite burial architecture in the Sahel.
- Material link (if Andalusian marble thesis holds) between Iberian/Islamic Mediterranean craft and Middle Niger elites — **association**, not automatic political dependency.
- Pair with [[gao-ancien]] for urban–cemetery dual structure of the Gao entrepôt.

## Key Debates

- Royal stelae provenance and Andalusian workshop hypothesis (Sauvaget tradition; Insoll’s assessment).
- Cemetery chronology vs Flight’s structural sequence (Insoll revisions).
- Relation of Gao-Saney elites to Arabic-source kings of Gawgaw / later Songhay narratives — attributed association only.
