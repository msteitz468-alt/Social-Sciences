# San Martín Huamelulpan

## Discovery and Excavation History
Linear hilltop civic-ceremonial center of five mound groups spanning ~1.5 km in the Mixteca Alta, discovered by [[caso-alfonso|Alfonso Caso]] in the 1930s and excavated by Margarita Gaxiola (1974–1976). Reported in [[flannery-marcus-cloud-people-1983|The Cloud People]] (1983) by the editors.

## Occupation Sequence
Two periods: Huamelulpan I (ca. 400–100 BCE), with ceramic ties to [[monte-alban|Monte Albán]] Ic and [[monte-negro|Monte Negro]] but heavy overlap with the Nochixtlán Cruz phase; and Huamelulpan II (ca. 100 BCE–200 CE), tied to the Ramos phase and Monte Albán II.

## Significance
A clean case of the four-way non-identity problem: the site's ceramics tie most strongly to the [[mixtec-culture|Mixtec]]-region Nochixtlán sequence, yet its hieroglyphic carvings follow the Valley of Oaxaca ([[zapotec-writing]]) canon exclusively — material-culture affiliation and writing-system affiliation diverge, and the source declines to read either as ethnic identity. The Altar de los Cráneos yielded human skulls with postmortem drilled suspension holes, which the editors linked (attributed) to a long regional history of trophy-taking and possible ritual cannibalism.

## Key Debates
[[marcus-joyce|Joyce Marcus]] rejected Charles Wicke's (1971) claim that the Huamelulpan monolith was the earliest Olmec monument, noting it bears nothing iconographically Olmec and that the site was unoccupied before 400 BCE — centuries after the San Lorenzo colossal heads.
