# Shanidar

Zagros cave (Iraq): largest Middle Eastern [[neanderthals|Neanderthal]] sample; care-for-injured interpretations; contested pollen/“flower burial.” Core Near Eastern locus in *[[stringer-gamble-in-search-of-the-neanderthals-1993|In Search of the Neanderthals]]* (1993) ch. 5 and burial discussion in ch. 7. Dates and interpretations below are **as of Stringer & Gamble 1993** unless noted.

## Discovery and Excavation History

**Zawi Chemi Shanidar (Braidwood 1959 note).** In *[[braidwood-prehistoric-men-1959|Prehistoric Men]]*, [[braidwood-robert|Braidwood]] cites **Dr. Solecki’s Zawi Chemi Shanidar** open site as slightly more “settled in” than Karim Shahir and as part of the **incipient-era** group before [[jarmo|Jarmo]]; a single radiocarbon determination **8900 ± 300 B.C.** (as of 1959) is used when discussing a possible gap to Jarmo’s preferred cluster. Distinct from the Mousterian Neanderthal cave sample — do not merge layers.

As of Stringer & Gamble (1993), Ralph Solecki’s team excavated Shanidar 1953–1960, recovering **nine Neanderthals** from very young infants to relatively “old” men, completeness ranging from a few vertebrae to fairly complete skeletons. The most exhaustive osteological study they cite is Erik Trinkaus (1983). Shanidar is one of the multi-individual samples (with La Ferrassie, La Quina, Krapina) in a total Neanderthal record they estimate under ~500 individuals.

Some individuals at least appear deliberately buried. The claim that **Shanidar 4** was buried with flowers (pollen as garlands) had, by 1993, been “seriously questioned” — S&G report alternatives of pollen percolation through sediments or animal burrowing (ch.7). Care for impaired individuals (including the famous injured older male in the wider Shanidar series as discussed in later literature) is treated in their broader Neanderthal biology as relatively well accepted compared with symbolic burial claims.

## Occupation Sequence

**(As of Stringer & Gamble 1993 Appendix and synthesis.)** Tabulated ages include Mousterian levels ~46.9–50.6 ka associated with ?Neanderthal, and Early Upper Palaeolithic 14C in the mid-30s kya. Detailed internal stratigraphy beyond that synthesis is not expanded here. Morphologically S&G group Shanidar with Tabun, Amud, and Kebara as the Neanderthal-like pole of the Middle Eastern Middle Palaeolithic dichotomy (vs Skhul/Qafzeh moderns). They note Shanidar 1 is probably younger than the more ancient modern [[qafzeh|Qafzeh]] 9 despite both associations with Middle Palaeolithic artifacts.

## Significance

- Largest Middle Eastern Neanderthal skeletal series (as of S&G 1993) — primary evidence that classic Neanderthal anatomy extends into the Zagros, not only Europe and the Levant coast.
- Comparative anatomy: Shanidar 1 vs Qafzeh 9 illustrates Neanderthal large nose and low forehead against modern morphology under similar technological association (S&G figure caption).
- Stature: smallest Neanderthal females they cite include Shanidar examples ~155 cm (with La Ferrassie); Middle Eastern Neanderthals on average a little taller than Europeans but still cold-adapted in build (S&G 1993).
- Burial and compassion debates: intentional burial accepted by many for at least some individuals; flower-burial claim contested; care for injured widely accepted — three separable inferences often conflated in popular accounts.
- One of the caves S&G cite under Gamble’s rule that complete Neanderthal skeletons cluster where carnivores made little use of caves (with Tabun, Le Moustier, Teshik Tash) — their **position** that skeleton survival tracks taphonomy more than the birth of symbolic burial.

## Key Debates

- **Flower burial (Shanidar 4):** ritual garlands vs sedimentary/bioturbation pollen (open as of S&G 1993; later literature continues to reassess — not updated here from this source alone).
- **Intentional burial vs corpse disposal / taphonomic completeness** (S&G/Gamble position vs traditional symbolic-burial readings).
- Relation of Zagros Neanderthals to Levantine chronology (Skhul/Qafzeh older moderns; Kebara ~60 ka) and to European classic Neanderthals.
- How far “care for the injured” licenses broader claims about Neanderthal social cognition or behavioural modernity ([[behavioral-modernity]]).
- Place in [[recent-african-origin]] vs [[multiregional-evolution]] arguments: multiregionalists read regional archaic continuity; replacement models treat Shanidar Neanderthals as part of a Eurasian Ancient population later displaced (positions).

### Klein — *The Human Career* (2nd ed., 1999)

*(Attributed — [[klein-human-career-1999]], [[klein-richard]]; as of Klein 1999.)*

Klein reports Shanidar Cave (Iraq, excavated 1953–60) as yielding nine partial [[neanderthals|Neanderthal]]
skeletons. He is **skeptical of the "flower burial"** (Shanidar 4): the grave fill was heavily
disturbed by rodent burrows, so the clumped pollen may be intrusive — "the Shanidar flower burial
will probably remain problematic as long as it is unique." He does accept the trauma-and-survival
evidence (Shanidar 1's crushed orbit, withered arm, and leg injuries; Shanidar 3's probable
lung-stab wound) as implying **group care for the injured** — for Klein the clearest inferable
non-material Neanderthal behavior. See the Neanderthal-burial question on [[neanderthals]].

### Roux (*Ancient Iraq*, 1992, attributed)

*(Attributed — [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]].)* Roux reports Shanidar Cave, in the Baradost mountains of the Upper Zab valley, as excavated 1951–60 by Ralph Solecki (University of Michigan): 14 m of deposit across four occupation levels — level D (lowest and thickest, at 8.5 m; Mousterian, with Neanderthal remains), level C ("Baradostian," Solecki's proposed name for an Aurignacian-type blade industry, radiocarbon-dated via hearth charcoal to between roughly 34,000 and 25,500 BCE), level B (later Aurignacian/"extended Gravettian" microlithic industry), and level B1 (Mesolithic, upper limit roughly 9000 BCE).

Level D yielded nine human skeletons (two children, seven adults). Skeleton I — an approximately 35-year-old male, 1.50 m tall — showed classic Neanderthal traits (thick bones, a massive chinless jaw, sloping forehead, brow ridges); examined by T. D. Stewart, who diagnosed a congenitally crippled arm on one individual, later amputated with a flint knife. Several individuals were killed by rockfall, not simultaneously. One body was found on a bed of branches and flowers; pollen analysis suggested death "between late May and early July." Three skeletons were radiocarbon-dated to 46,000 BP, 50,000 BP, and (stratigraphically lower) 60,000 BP.

Roux reports that Solecki considered the Baradostian industry unique in the Near East and argued that the Shanidar Neanderthals, though more recent than those of Mount Carmel, did not appear to mix with or evolve toward *Homo sapiens*, remaining physically "conservative." **This non-admixture claim is Solecki's own interpretation, as reported by Roux 1992, and must not be read in wiki voice**: it reflects the state of paleoanthropological argument current at the time (pre-dating ancient-DNA methods), and subsequent ancient-DNA research has since documented Neanderthal admixture with anatomically modern human populations, revising the no-mixing consensus this claim rested on. Flag any use of Solecki's claim on this or other pages explicitly as "(as of Solecki, per Roux 1992)," with a note that post-1992 aDNA evidence has superseded it.

Roux situates Shanidar within a broader Palaeolithic Iraqi sequence including Barda-Balka (Acheulaean/Tayacian/Mousterian hand-axe and pebble-tool industry, attributed to the end of the Riss-Würm interglacial, roughly 80,000 years ago) and Hazar Merd "Dark Cave" (Levalloiso-Mousterian, Middle Palaeolithic, discovered 1928 by Dorothy Garrod). He notes cross-regional contacts evidenced by obsidian tools at Upper Palaeolithic Shanidar sourced from the Lake Van region of Armenia, and material parallels with Syria-Palestine, the Bisitun cave (Iran), and Korain cave (Turkey).
