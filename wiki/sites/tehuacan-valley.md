# Tehuacán Valley

An arid, rain-shadowed valley in the modern state of Puebla, Mexico, requiring irrigation
(present from the first millennium BCE) for large-scale agriculture. Richard S. MacNeish
began his multi-season Tehuacán Valley project in 1960, producing the sequence long treated
as the type case for the Archaic-period transition to agriculture and, ultimately, for the
domestication of maize (Coe & Koontz 2013). See [[flannery-kent-v|Kent Flannery]]'s
contemporaneous [[guila-naquitz|Guilá Naquitz]] project in nearby Oaxaca for a parallel,
independently dated sequence.

## Discovery and Excavation History

MacNeish's project (from 1960) combined stratigraphic excavation across several caves and
open sites in the valley with associated radiocarbon dating of organic materials found near,
but not directly on, plant remains — a method later shown to be a source of significant
dating error (see Key Debates). (Coe & Koontz 2013)

## Occupation Sequence

Cultural sequence established by MacNeish, as reported by Coe & Koontz (2013):

| Phase | Approx. dates | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ajuereado (Early Hunters) | before c. 7000 BCE | Nomadic microbands of 4–8; hunted extinct horse and pronghorn with Lerma-pointed spears; camps moved 3–4 times per season |
| El Riego | c. 7000–5000 BCE | Increasing plant exploitation |
| Coxcatlán | c. 5000–3400 BCE | Bottle gourd, common bean, black sapote, warty squash join the diet; maize appears for the first time in the sequence, as tiny cobs |
| Abejas | c. 3400–2300 BCE | Tepary beans, pumpkin, and hybrid maize showing "introgression" (backcrossing) with teosinte; increased storage caching and sedentism |
| Purrón | c. 2300–1500 BCE | Precedes Formative/Preclassic village life |

In the Coxcatlán phase, the earliest Tehuacán maize cobs were small enough that
Paul C. Mangelsdorf, working from his "wild ancestral maize" reconstruction, considered them
genuinely wild specimens — a claim disputed at the time by proponents of the rival
teosinte-ancestry theory, who thought the cobs instead resembled teosinte (Coe & Koontz 2013;
see [[concepts/maize-domestication]] for the resolution of this dispute, the "Corn Wars").
A coprolite study by Eric Callen found that, even in the Abejas phase, some 70% of the diet
still derived from wild plants and animals despite growing storage and sedentism (Coe &
Koontz 2013).

**[CONTRADICTION — dating revision, date-stamped].** MacNeish originally dated the Tehuacán
maize cobs to c. 5000 BCE via radiocarbon dating of organic materials associated with, but not
directly bonded to, the cobs. Direct AMS dating of the cobs themselves has since revised this
to c. 3500 BCE and later. MacNeish contested the revision, attributing the discrepancy to
sample contamination (Coe & Koontz 2013). Coe & Koontz describe the Tehuacán sequence as
"crucial for the domestication of maize... and the Archaic period in general," such that this
revision reshapes the whole Archaic chronology of highland Mexico. Both figures are recorded
here with attribution rather than silently overwritten, per the wiki's Contradiction and
Replication Protocol.

By comparison, pollen of the genus *Zea* (apparently wild maize) at Oaxaca sites under
Flannery's project — including Gheo-Shih, near [[guila-naquitz|Guilá Naquitz]] — is dated
7400–6700 BCE, predating maize's appearance in the Tehuacán sequence (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Significance

The Tehuacán sequence anchored decades of Archaic-period Mesoamerican archaeology as the
reference chronology for the shift from foraging to food production and, specifically, for
the early history of maize. Its status as *the* type sequence has been complicated by the
direct-AMS dating revision above and by the independently dated, and in some respects earlier,
Oaxaca material from Flannery's project.

## Key Debates

- **MacNeish's original charcoal-association dates vs. direct AMS dating of the cobs
  themselves** — a methodological caution about the reliability of associated-material
  radiocarbon dates versus direct dating of the domesticate in question (see also
  [[guila-naquitz]] for the same caution applied there).
- **Mangelsdorf's "wild ancestral maize" reconstruction vs. teosinte-ancestry theory** — see
  [[concepts/maize-domestication]] for the eventual molecular-biology resolution favoring
  teosinte as sole ancestor.
