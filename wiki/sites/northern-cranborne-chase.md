# Northern Cranborne Chase

Northern edge of Cranborne Chase (West Wiltshire / northeast Dorset chalk), studied by [[tilley-christopher]] while living at Melbury Beacon (2002–2004) and published as Ch. 4 of *[[tilley-interpreting-landscapes-2010|Interpreting Landscapes]]* (partial earlier CAJ 2004). Distinct from the southern Chase / Dorset Cursus focus of *[[tilley-phenomenology-of-landscape-1994|A Phenomenology of Landscape]]* (1994).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Classic English chalk landscape archaeology (Colt Hoare to modern). Tilley's method: 18 months repeated walking of Ebble–Nadder and Ox-Drove ridges, treating monuments as mute co-authors with topography.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed, Tilley 2010.)* Neolithic long barrows reference distant hills/coombes more than each other. Early Bronze Age fills landscape with round barrows (coombe–spur transitions; scarp hugging). Late Bronze Age–early Iron Age **cross-dykes** as material metaphors continuing coombe forms; reject single "ranch boundary" universalism.

## Significance

Core thesis (attributed): barrows and dykes are **landscape metaphors** — form, placement, and walking experience encode topographic relations (height/depth, scarp/coombe). Cross-links: [[geological-identity]], [[path]], [[place-as-lived]], [[ritual-landscape]].

## Key Debates

Against form-centric dyke typology that neglects topography; against single universal function (attributed).

## Historiography

Lives in dialogue with Cranborne Chase project literature (Barrett, Bradley, Green, etc.) and Tilley's own 1994 southern Chase work.
