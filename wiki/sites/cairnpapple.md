# Cairnpapple

Multi-phase prehistoric ceremonial site at Cairnpapple Hill, West Lothian, Scotland. In *[[bradley-significance-of-monuments-1998|The Significance of Monuments]]* (1998, Ch. 9), [[bradley-richard|Bradley]] uses Cairnpapple to exemplify **closed circles**: the transformation of open circular ceremonial architecture into a place of **elite burial**, including erasure and burial of earlier stone-circle elements with the dead.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Classic excavation by Stuart Piggott (1948) established a multi-phase sequence that became a textbook example of monument biography. Bradley’s 1998 reading emphasizes process over static type: stages of construction and appropriation rather than a single "type site" identity. Primary report densification awaits dedicated ingest; claims here are attributed via Bradley after Piggott.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Summary resolution, attributed via Bradley 1998 after Piggott.)*

1. **Hilltop henge** with wide views; interior incomplete post/stone setting with semicircular focus on an oval **"cove"**; cremations in sockets and on old land surface.
2. Replaced by a ring of ~20 monoliths (possibly with larger central feature), concentric with the inner ditch — timber→stone circle change mid-third millennium BC, a pattern common in Britain.
3. **Final Neolithic form:** permeable stone circle + earthwork that hides the interior from view — combining Ch. 8’s permeable/closed contrast; "circular" landscape looking across lower ground rather than a continuous hill horizon.
4. **Early Bronze Age:** up to three rebuild phases — (a) small kerb cairn with Beaker inhumation inside the stone circle; (b–c) massive rubble cairn with upright kerb, enlarged to occupy most of the enclosure and overlay part of the ditch/bank; open ground nearly gone; cists. No monoliths in situ; number of large stones revetting the EBA cairn ≈ number in the stone circle — Piggott/Bradley hypothesis that original monoliths were **taken down and reused** (one fragment possibly matched to a socket cast).

## Significance

Load-bearing case for Bradley’s argument that surface monument "types" are often **arrested stages of shared processes**, and that Early Bronze Age burial appropriation marks increasing identification of **particular people with particular places** (and exclusion of others). Extreme case: original stone circle erased and buried with the dead. Compare Temple Wood, Dun Ruadh, Berrybrae, Arbor Low, Balbirnie/Balfarg (Bradley Ch. 9).
