# Mari

Mari (Tell Hariri), on the middle Euphrates in modern Syria, was a major Sumero-Akkadian and later Amorite city-state, an intermediary trading center between the Mediterranean/Anatolian world and lower Mesopotamia, and the source of one of the richest textual archives in ancient Near Eastern history. Its rulers appear across the Early Dynastic, Akkadian, Ur III, and Old Babylonian periods, per [[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux (1992)]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

The site's shakkanakku-period palace, with impressive underground royal tombs, and the later Old Babylonian palace and its royal correspondence archive have been recovered through excavation on the site (excavated by André Parrot, from 1933). Roux describes statues of worshippers found at Mari as "marvellous portraits, extremely well-carved" — markedly superior in quality to comparable statuary from Tell Khueira (crude), the archaic Ishtar temple at Assur (mediocre), and the "Square Temple" at Tell Asmar/[[eshnunna]] (stiff and angular, though "more impressive than beautiful"). Roux interprets Mari's sculptors as probably local artists working from Sumerian models, forerunners of the great Akkad-period sculptors — but cautions that Mari, being far from the Sumerian heartland, should not be taken as representative of "Sumerian art" proper.

## Occupation Sequence

**Early Dynastic through Akkadian period.** Mari's Early Dynastic palace shared the tripartite architectural plan (square central courtyard, chambers on three sides, a long audience hall on the fourth, twin perimeter walls with a corridor) seen also at Eridu and Kish, and included ritual installations and royal chapels; its Nini-zaza temple featured a baetyl/standing stone paralleling West Semitic open-air shrine traditions. Relations with [[lagash]] were apparently friendly under Ur-Nanshe (a hoard possibly gifted by Mesannepadda of Ur was found at Mari). An Ebla-archive letter from Enna-Dagan, *en* of Mari, to an *en* of Ebla, describes military campaigns by three of Enna-Dagan's predecessors in northern Syria — especially Iblul-Il, who devastated and occupied many towns of the Ebla kingdom; other administrative texts show Ebla sending tribute in gold and silver to Mari, at least until the reign of Ebrium of Ebla. Roux's approximate (and explicitly uncertain) synchronism places Iblul-Il of Mari, Arennum of Ebla, and Eannatum of Lagash as roughly contemporary, c. 2460–2400 BCE.

Under [[akkadians|Akkadian]] rule, Mari's palace was partially destroyed by Narâm-Sin's western campaign (mid-3rd millennium BCE); Sargon before him had claimed the Upper Region including Mari as a conquest.

**The shakkanakku line (c. 2266–1920 BCE).** After Narâm-Sin's campaign, Mari was ruled by a line of *shakkanakku* — military governors, a title originally granted by Akkadian overlords but retained by their successors as effectively kingly. A shakkanakku palace with impressive underground tombs has been excavated. The trail of shakkanakku rulers is lost after c. 1920 BCE.

**The Amorite kingdom of Mari (c. 1830–1761 BCE).** Around 1830 BCE, the Simalite Amorite chief Iaggid-Lim allied with Ilâ-kabkâbu, king of Ekallâtum on the middle Tigris, via "solemn oaths"; the alliance broke down and Ilâ-kabkâbu destroyed Iaggid-Lim's fortress, seizing his son Iahdun-Lim. Freed around 1820 BCE, Iahdun-Lim took Mari and proclaimed himself "king of Mari and of the country of the Hanaeans" (the Khabur basin). He rebuilt Mari's city wall and that of neighboring Terqa, dug canals, founded a namesake town, and built a temple to the sun-god Shamash; he led a military and economic expedition to the Mediterranean, claiming to have sacrificed to "the Ocean," had his troops bathe in it, took timber from the mountains, and imposed tribute on coastal lands — recorded on nine foundation-deposit bricks for the Shamash temple. This provoked a coalition of three Amorite chiefs plus Sumu-Ebuh, king of Iamhad (Aleppo), which Iahdun-Lim claims to have defeated.

Around 1815–1800 BCE, Narâm-Sin of [[eshnunna]] (a different individual from the earlier Akkad-dynasty king of the same name, two centuries apart) seized Ekallâtum and the Euphrates valley up to near Mari, driving Samsi-Addu into exile in Babylon; Samsi-Addu retook Ekallâtum around 1815 BCE and, five years later, liberated [[assur]]. Iahdun-Lim defeated Samsi-Addu at Nagar but was then assassinated (c. 1800 BCE) by his own successor, Sumu-Iaman, who was in turn murdered by his own servants after roughly two years. This power vacuum allowed Samsi-Addu to seize Mari without a fight (c. 1796 BCE) and install his younger son, Iasmah-Addu, as governor.

**The Mari archives.** More than 5,000 tablets of royal correspondence and administrative reports were recovered from the palace, forming the core evidentiary base for the reign of Samsi-Addu and his sons (evidence class: textual/archival). Roux notes the tablets are largely undated internally, complicating chronological reconstruction, but offer unusually direct access to individual personalities: Ishme-Dagan, viceroy of Ekallâtum, comes across as a bold warrior ("At Shimanahe we fought and I have taken the entire country. Be glad!"), in sharp contrast with his brother Iasmah-Addu of Mari, described in the correspondence as "docile, obedient, but lazy, negligent, cowardly." Samsi-Addu's paternal reprimands to Iasmah-Addu survive verbatim: "You remain a child... there is no beard on your chin..." Iasmah-Addu's marriage to the daughter of the king of Qatna increased hostility from Iamhad/Aleppo (Samsi-Addu had backed Qatna against Aleppo in a regional conflict), though no direct Aleppo–Upper Mesopotamia war is evidenced. Good relations are attested with Karkemish, whose king Aplahanda sent wine, food, ornaments, and clothing, and granted copper-mine access. Conflict with Eshnunna (kings Narâm-Sin, then Dadusha) centered on the town of Qabra, a Lower Zab crossing; an unpublished Dadusha stele (at the time of Roux's writing) suggests an eventual territorial concession or alliance with Samsi-Addu. Relations with Babylon (kings Sin-muballit, then Hammurabi) were "cold but polite" during Samsi-Addu's lifetime, with tablets and favors exchanged — but, per Roux, "some thirty years later... Hammurabi was to take and destroy Mari," ending the city's independent political history around 1760 BCE.

## Significance

Mari's archives are among the richest single textual sources for early-2nd-millennium-BCE Mesopotamian political history, offering rare direct access to personalities and diplomacy rather than only royal self-presentation. Its shakkanakku-period tombs and Early Dynastic palace anchor the site's importance for tracing continuity of Akkadian-derived governorship into the Amorite period, and its position on the middle Euphrates made it a key intermediary in trade between the Mediterranean/Anatolian world and lower Mesopotamia (see also its sculptural tradition, above).

## Key Debates

The classification of Samsi-Addu's realm — whether it should be termed the "Kingdom of Upper Mesopotamia" (Roux's preferred framing, following an unnamed Danish Assyriologist's argument that "Shamshi-Adad's empire did not originate in, and was not built upon the men and the power of the city-state Assur") or an early "Assyrian Empire" — bears directly on Mari's political history under Iasmah-Addu, since Samsi-Addu is listed on the Assyrian King List despite being, in Roux's words, "in fact a usurper, later rejected by the Assyrian tradition." See [[assur]] for the fuller discussion.

## Historiography

Excavation quality and the archive's internal dating problems bear directly on the reliability of the Samsi-Addu/Iasmah-Addu chronology: Roux notes the tablets are largely undated internally, and reconstruction depends on cross-referencing multiple correspondents. The Mari sculpture corpus is treated by Roux as evidence of a distinct, locally-inflected artistic tradition rather than as representative "Sumerian art," a caution relevant to any comparative art-historical use of the Mari material.
