# Franchthi Cave

Deeply stratified cave on the Argolid coast of Greece with **Palaeolithic–Mesolithic–Neolithic** sequence — a key witness for forager life and the local onset of farming in SE Europe. Used in *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Whittle 1996]]* Ch. 2 and related SE discussion; also prominent in [[mithen-after-the-ice-2003|Mithen 2003]] and [[farming-spread-europe-debate]].

## Discovery and Excavation History

Major twentieth-century excavation program (Jacobsen and collaborators). High-resolution for its era; specialist volumes on fauna, flora, lithics, and chronology. Whittle and Mithen are secondary syntheses.

## Occupation Sequence

Long forager occupation; Neolithic domesticates and material culture appear while forager traditions remain visible — **coexistence / gradual transition** readings are common (attributed positions vary).

## Significance

Type case for Greek Mesolithic–Neolithic transition; boat-borne domesticates into the Aegean; limited initial impact of farming on some tool and foraging practices in early contact centuries (as staged in secondary literature).

## Key Debates

Immigrant farmers vs indigenous adoption at the Greek gateway — open within [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]. Coastal sea-level change affects site catchment interpretation.

## Historiography

Flagship of Greek prehistoric cave archaeology; densified here via Whittle 1996 (and prior Mithen layer on the debate page).

## Whittle densification (1996)

*(All claims attributed to [[whittle-alasdair|Alasdair Whittle]], *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Europe in the Neolithic]]* 1996, Chs. 2–3 and synthesis. Chronology mid-1990s cal BC; pre-aDNA. Cave sequence ≠ ethnos, language, or genetic population.)*

### Mesolithic sequence and seafaring (Ch.2)

Whittle reports Franchthi (Argolid) as the **only substantial, well-investigated post-glacial Mesolithic occupation in Greece** (as of his mid-1990s synthesis): late Palaeolithic from ~**20,000 BC**; **Melian obsidian** from ~**10,000 BC** implying boats (**>100 km** sea); Mesolithic ~**2 m** of deposits with possible later decline after ~8000 BC; after ~9000 BC broad-spectrum use; after ~8000 BC more sea use, **large tunny**, more obsidian. Other Greek Mesolithic sites sparse (**Theopetra** noted as exception).

He contrasts Mediterranean European Epipalaeolithic/Mesolithic with the Levantine Natufian/PPN: the Levant shows more sites, site types, diversification, experimentation, and from ~9000 BC permanent occupations plus cereal/animal domestication—**local conditions not the same** as at Franchthi (or Uzzo). Selected human remains at non-formal-burial sites including Franchthi (small quantities of body parts carried by the living) he reads as continuous presence of the dead in a conceptual order uniting people, nature, living and dead (**interpretation**).

In his closing forager baseline, Franchthi (with Uzzo) stands among **significant places** of knowledgeable, flexible, broad-spectrum foragers with an ethic of sharing (**position**).

### Neolithic shift and transition readings (Ch.3)

Whittle notes at Franchthi a **rapid shift** from wild barley/oats and red deer/pigs/tunny to emmer/einkorn/barley and sheep/goats; lithic style continuity but more Melian cores and large flint blades; plain pottery; occupation beyond the cave. **Either** newcomers **or** rapid influence of newcomers/new practices is arguable (**finding**, not verdict).

He **rejects independent SE European domestication** of cereals and sheep/goats: Franchthi shows complete shift away from wild oats/barley; sheep/goats were **not** part of early post-glacial SE fauna (absent Franchthi and late-glacial NW Greece). Domesticates and practices are SW Asian-derived, made available by contingency.

**Continuity signals against total population replacement** (his attributed reading): lithic style/range continuity (despite stratigraphic uncertainty); continued Melian obsidian; continued collection of *Cerithium vulgatum*—odd coincidence under complete population change; plus active indigenous acquisition of pottery novelties as legitimate alternative to immigrants’ cultural baggage. Prior long-range mobility (obsidian, tunny) is part of why he thinks mobile foragers could adopt herding/crops/potting to **support an existing social ethic**—becoming Neolithic over first generations was **slow**, not fully fledged from the beginning (**position** on [[farming-spread-europe-debate]]).
