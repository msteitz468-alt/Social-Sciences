# Sanxingdui

## Discovery and Excavation History

Sanxingdui, roughly 40 km north of Chengdu, Sichuan, was the center of thirteen contemporary sites
along the Yazi and Mamu Rivers. Excavation and reporting are attributed to the Sichuan Provincial
Institute of Cultural Relics and Archaeology (Sichuansheng), with findings synthesized through
Sichuansheng 1999 (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Occupation Sequence

Occupation spans ca. 2800–1000 BCE across four phases, equivalent in the Central Plain sequence to
a span from Longshan through [[erlitou-culture|Erlitou]] and [[erligang-culture|Erligang]] to
Yinxu (late Shang). A 260-ha walled enclosure was built during the phase contemporary with Erlitou
(ca. 1700–1500 BCE) and abandoned around 1000 BCE (as of Liu & Chen 2012). Sanxingdui's contact
with the Central Plain traces back to the Erlitou period: ceramic he vessels, jade/stone ge and
zhang blades, and turquoise-inlaid bronze plaques resemble Erlitou finds; Liu & Chen state it is
likely that Sanxingdui's bronze metallurgy was first stimulated (inspired) by Central Plain
cultures (attributed).

## Significance

Sanxingdui bronzes are mostly locally made in indigenous styles: life-size bronze figures and
heads, bronze face masks in human form, and bronze "trees" decorated with birds, alongside some
ritual vessels (zun, lei) following typical Shang style. Bronze techniques combine piece-mold
casting — likely a Central Plain influence — with local soldering/brazing, a local technical
development; Liu & Chen assess the metalworking skill level as less refined than contemporary
Yellow River region products. Jade/stone ritual objects (ge, zhang blades; bi disks; huan, yuan
rings) mostly show strong Central Plain formal influence but were made predominantly of local stone
rather than imported nephrite jade — of nearly 100 stone/jade ge blades recovered from two
sacrificial pits, only three were identified as true nephrite.

Lead-isotope analysis of 53 Sanxingdui bronzes shows most contain a distinctive "highly radiogenic
lead" (HRL) isotope signature, likely sourced from ores in northeastern Yunnan; because the same
HRL signature appears in Shang bronzes at [[zhengzhou|Zhengzhou]] and Anyang, this raises the
possibility that Sanxingdui supplied metal to the Shang core (as of Jin et al. 1995, cited in Liu &
Chen 2012). A related assemblage — 710 bronzes from 19 localities along the Han and Xu Rivers in
Chenggu/Yangxian, southern Shaanxi ("Chengyang bronze assemblages"), dated to middle/late Shang —
combines Shang, Sanxingdui/Sichuan, and local stylistic elements, and is interpreted as evidence of
an exchange network connecting Sanxingdui, the Hanzhong basin (Baoshan culture), and the Shang
core; lead-isotope analysis of 31 of these bronzes found all but three contained the same HRL
signature, contemporaneous with HRL bronzes at Zhengzhou and Yinxu Phases I–III (as of Jin et al.
2006). Cowry shells recovered in quantity at Sanxingdui also evidence its early participation in a
southwestern trade route linking Sichuan via Yunnan and Burma to South Asia, traceable to at least
the second half of the first millennium BCE, carrying textiles, bamboo, and salt outward and cowry
shells, glass beads, and carnelian beads inward (Sichuansheng 1999).

## Key Debates

Many scholars identify Sanxingdui as the political center of the ancient Shu state — one of two
peoples (Ba and Shu) named in both ancient texts and Shang oracle-bone inscriptions as inhabiting
Sichuan, with the Shu apparently having more frequent Shang contact than the Ba (Tong 2004, as
cited in Liu & Chen 2012). This identification links an archaeological site/culture to a named
ancient people via later textual correlation, not direct evidence contemporary with the site itself;
it is recorded here as a widely held scholarly position (`associated_with`), not as a settled
identity — see Identity Cautions.

## Identity Cautions

The identification of Sanxingdui with the historically-named "Shu" polity rests on textual sources
(later oracle-bone and transmitted-text references) rather than on any inscription found at the
site itself; this wiki records the Shu attribution as `associated_with`, following the mandatory
four-way non-identity rule (archaeological site/culture ≠ named people). Similarly, the HRL
lead-isotope link to Yunnan ore sources and the cowry-shell evidence for a South Asian trade
connection are material/exchange-network associations, not evidence of shared population, language,
or polity with those regions.

## Shelach-Lavi (2015) densification

*(Attributed — [[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]].)*

Shelach-Lavi describes the site's pounded-earth enclosure wall as up to 40 m wide at its
base and still standing 6 m tall in places, enclosing an estimated 2.6 km² (~260 ha,
consistent with the figure above) — comparable in scale to the inner walls of the Shang
city at [[zhengzhou|Zhengzhou]] (Falkenhausen 2003). His regional chronology (Table 7)
dates the Sanxingdui period to ca. 1700–1150 BCE "(as of Shelach-Lavi 2015)."

The site's **two sacrificial pits**, dated to the final centuries of the 2nd millennium
BCE, are modest in size (4.6×3.5×1.6 m and 5.3×2.3×1.6 m) but contained more than 1,000
artifacts — bronze statues, masks, and trees, gold objects, jade, elephant tusks/ivory, and
cowrie shells. The largest bronze human figure is 2.6 m tall; the bronze-tree assemblage
stands 4.2 m (Falkenhausen 2002).

A distinctive point Shelach-Lavi draws out: of the 22 bronze vessels recovered from the two
pits, 20 are "amphora-shaped" (*zun*, *lei*, *hu*) types more typical of the middle/lower
Yangzi and Han River valley bronze traditions than of the Yellow River Shang tradition — in
tension with the "Shang-style ritual vessels" characterization above. He reads this,
together with stylistic parallels between the Xin'gan bronze head and Chenggu bronze masks
(Bagley 1999), as evidence of interregional contact "not controlled or mediated by the
Shang" — a mosaic of interacting, non-Shang-centered polities rather than a single
Shang-dominated network. Lead-isotope studies of Erligang- and Yinxu-period bronzes found a
rare radiogenic-lead isotope shared among vessels from north China, the Yangzi basin, and
Sanxingdui, suggesting a common lead (and possibly tin) source across these regions (Jin
2003) "(as of Shelach-Lavi 2015)" — consistent with, though not identical to, the HRL
signature discussed above.

Following Sanxingdui's decline, the **Jinsha site** (Chengdu suburbs, Shierqiao period)
yielded ~17 excavated houses, kilns, and ash pits, plus at least 60 ritual pits/deposits
containing elephant tusks, bronze weapons/bells/figurines (no vessels), jade, stone,
ceramics, and more than 200 gold objects.
