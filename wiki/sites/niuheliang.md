# Niuheliang

## Discovery and Excavation History

Niuheliang is a cluster of sixteen major ritual sites — cairns, altars, and other
monumental architecture — spread over roughly 50 km² of mountainous terrain in
Jianping County, Liaoning, described by
[[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen (2012)]] as "the largest ritual
complex discovered to date" for the [[hongshan-culture]]. The excavators and precise
excavation years are not specified in the source material reviewed here and are
marked [[unknown]] pending further ingest; excavation quality (a reliability fact
for reading the finds) is likewise not characterized in the reviewed material. A
thermoluminescence date on furnace remains at Niuheliang (Zhuanshanzi/Xiaofushan)
places later copper-ore smelting activity at 3494±340 to 3100±310 BP (uncalibrated
as reported, corresponding to a wide 2300–800 BCE bracket), attributed to the much
later Lower Xiajiadian period — a distinct, later reuse of the location, not part of
the Hongshan-period ceremonial construction.

## Occupation Sequence

Construction of the late-Hongshan ceremonial complex dates to ca. 3650–3150 BCE. A
radiocarbon date from the related site of Dongshanzui (within the same Daling River
ceremonial-center system) gives 5485 ± 110 cal. BP. Guo, D. (1997) reports that no
residential site has been found within 100 km² around Niuheliang, which he
interprets as marking the area as "a sacred zone designated exclusively for the
ritual life of the Hongshan people" — an attributed interpretation, not a settled
fact. Much later, copper-ore smelting activity associated with the Lower Xiajiadian
period reused the locality.

## Significance

Niuheliang anchors the archaeological case for Hongshan ceremonial centralization and
craft specialization. Cairns are built in round and square shapes, many containing
elite tombs with large numbers of jade objects, including human and animal figurines;
small clay female figurines have also been found in burials and pits near ritual
structures. The Goddess Temple, on a mountaintop at the center of Niuheliang,
comprises two semi-subterranean structures — one measuring 18.4 m long by 6.9 m wide,
the other smaller. Broken clay sculpture fragments (hand, ear, shoulder, arm, breast,
and other body parts) belong to at least seven individuals; only female anatomical
features are identifiable among them (hence the name), and the fragments show at
least three size scales, from life-size to three times life-size (per ear-fragment
measurements). A life-sized clay human face was found with green jade balls inserted
in the eye sockets. Animal figures recovered include bird-claw fragments and
pig-dragon fragments (a jaw and a head-body piece).

A jade human figurine (18.5 cm, with three interconnecting back holes, likely a
pendant) was found near the pelvis of a skeleton in the largest tomb at Locality XVI;
its posture — standing, arms folded on the chest — recurs at the Lingjiatan site,
more than 1,000 km to the south, a parallel Liu & Chen read as suggestive of
long-distance cultural contact rather than identity between the two locations. Small
chert drills and scrapers found in cairns and ritual-area pits suggest possible elite
involvement in jade manufacture (Liu, L. 2003), though the source describes this as
tentative — "more evidence is needed."

Provenance research (Wang, S. et al. 2007) indicates most Hongshan nephrite jade,
including material at Niuheliang, matches the Xiuyan jade mines on the Liaodong
Peninsula, roughly 300 km away, implying a regional exchange network supplied the
raw material for the site's craft production (as of Liu & Chen 2012).

## Key Debates

Whether Niuheliang's ceremonial centers imply a centralized regional Hongshan polity,
or instead reflect functional ritual segregation without unified political control,
is treated by Liu & Chen (2012) as an open question — the authors state explicitly
that it remains unclear what forms of regional sociopolitical organization underlay
construction of ritual landscapes "on such a magnificently extensive scale." The
degree of elite involvement in jade manufacture at the site (chert drills/scrapers
as possible evidence) is similarly flagged by the source as needing further evidence
rather than resolved. The role of Hongshan's ca. 3000 BCE collapse — linked by
several scholars to summer-monsoon weakening (An, Z. et al. 2000; Schettler et al.
2006) and by others (Li Xinwei 2008) to an elite-response mechanism of ritual
overinvestment displacing agricultural adaptation — is discussed at
[[hongshan-culture]].
