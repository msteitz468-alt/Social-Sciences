# Jenné-jeno (Djenné-Djenno)

Tell and urban settlement complex in the Inland Niger Delta (modern Mali), type-site for early West African savanna urbanism in Connah’s synthesis. Densified from [[connah-african-civilizations-2016|Connah, *African Civilizations* (2016)]], principally Ch. 6. All interpretive claims are attributed to Connah 2016 and specialists he cites (especially the McIntoshs).

**Identity caution:** Archaeological locus ≠ people ≠ language ≠ genetic population. Jenné-jeno is not “the Mali Empire,” not modern Djenné’s population, and not a single ethnic essence continuous into historic Ghana/Mali/Songhay labels. Link with `associated_with` unless identity is argued. Four-way non-identity applies to “Empire of Ghana,” Hausa city-states, and Igbo comparative parallels.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Connah (2016) frames Jenné-jeno as the excavation program that broke the “silent millennia” of West African savanna prehistory.

- **Problem setting:** Connah reports that archaeology long suffered sparse fieldwork, poor technique/publication, and strategies focused on historically known sites. McIntosh and McIntosh (1979) called the three millennia preceding earliest historical documentation the “silent millennia”; Mauny’s “siècles obscurs.” Until new work, archaeology often only confirmed stock historical interpretations drawn from post-eighth-century Islamic Arabic sources.
- **McIntosh research:** Connah reports their program tracing Inland Niger Delta urbanism from apparent origins ~third century BCE to foundation of historical Jenné early in the second millennium CE. Settlement hierarchy method is endorsed: the most effective way to investigate precolonial West African towns is not excavating the town alone but documenting the hierarchy of which the town is the summit—contrasted with older capital-hunting at historically named sites.
- **Documentary contrast:** Historians reconstruct Ghana, Mali, Songhai, Borno, and Hausaland from Arabic and later European sources (~last 1,200 years). Connah stresses that historically focused excavations (e.g. Birnin Gazargamo, Kong, Hamdallahi) are often limited and Islamic-period, while Jenné-jeno-type work rewrites indigenous trajectories preceding empire narratives.
- **Historical abundance backdrop:** Leo Africanus on Jenné and al-Sa’di (~1655) describe abundance, fertility, and markets—used to frame the Sahel paradox of modern catastrophe (UN 2012: 18 million food-short) versus past opportunity for early urbanization and [[state-formation]].

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — Connah 2016 Ch. 6, reporting McIntosh research; growth phases approximate.)*

- **Environmental frame:** Water availability is the foremost constraint (seasonal rain, northern aridity), making perennial river/lake margins attractive; floodwater/recessional cultivation is an intensification strategy (Inland Niger Delta; Lake Chad margins). Water-linked diseases (trypanosomiasis, malaria, schistosomiasis, etc.) discourage settlement near the water that attracts it. Brooks’s climatic periods (arid ~300 BCE–CE 300; wetter ~CE 300–1100; dry ~1100–1500; etc.—boundaries uncertain per Connah) shape opportunity/constraint without climatic determinism.
- **Foundation and growth:** Occupation from ~third century BCE. Settlement ≥12 ha by about the first century CE; maximum ~33 ha by about the ninth century as packed compounds of round and rectangular mud houses within a ~2 km mud-brick wall. Population estimates 4,800–12,800 for the main tell, or 10,000–26,700 including adjacent Hambarketolo and ~25 contemporary satellite sites within 1 km.
- **Economic base:** Mixed floodplain farming (especially rice and cattle) plus fishing, hunting, gathering, metallurgy, crafts, and trade ≥350 km radius. Direct evidence of African rice, *Pennisetum*, and sorghum from ~third century BCE, with diversified wild cereals, cattle, sheep/goats—economy remarkably stable ~1,600 years to a fourteenth-century end despite climatic/demographic change. Late first-millennium floodplain site density possibly greater than present under wetter climate. Recessional rice cultivation is treated as a rare African intensification that may have supported urban growth; the McIntoshs suggest Inland Niger Delta agricultural surplus fed Timbuktu, Gao, and further centres via Niger canoe transport.
- **Technology:** Iron used from late first-millennium BCE arrival despite ore ≥50 km away; copper/bronze working by late first millennium CE. Ninth-century gold earring. High technical quality first-millennium CE pottery (handmade, thin-walled, varied forms); animal/human figurines in the Inland Niger Delta tradition; drainpipes at Jenné-jeno. Textiles appear late first millennium regionally. Méma late first–early second millennium iron production beyond local need; Middle Senegal ~60,000 furnaces counted in only 15 km of Mauritanian bank—regional metallurgical scale context.
- **Walls:** City wall by ~ninth century. Connah treats settlement enclosures as multipurpose—protection under resource competition, plus governance, taxation, trade control, city–outsider distinction, and ruler prestige. Comparative early fortification: Zilum (NE Nigeria) fortified 12 ha mid-first millennium BCE.
- **Decline/shift:** Trajectory runs to foundation of historical Jenné early second millennium CE and end of the archaeological sequence around the fourteenth century in Connah’s subsistence-stability summary. Parallel first-millennium growth then arid decline is reported for Timbuktu region, Méma, and probably Middle Niger Lakes Region—environmental constraint outside Jenné-jeno’s core success story.
- **Regional hierarchy and peers:** Middle Niger material-culture homogeneity; three-tier settlement hierarchy around Jenné-jeno; site-size/artefact-diversity correlation within 4 km. Peer large sites: Kalifa Gallou ~40 ha; Dia ~50 ha; Toladie ~85 ha; Soy ~110 ha. Middle Senegal contrast: floodplain sites mostly remained small (Cubalel, Siouré) through the first millennium CE, with major scale changes only at millennium’s end with trade expansion and Takrur—attributed to even vs uneven landform/subsistence opportunity distributions.

## Significance

- **Indigenous urbanism, “too big, too early, too far south”:** Relative to Levtzion’s external-stimulus / Sahel “ports” model (cities and states as products of long-distance trade and Islam), Connah reports the McIntosh verdict that “Jenné-jeno is too big, too early, and too far south.” The site establishes a convincing case for indigenous Inland Niger Delta urbanism.
- **Reject external-stimulus origins model:** Connah concludes the long-accepted Mediterranean-contact/long-distance-trade origin model for savanna urbanism and political centralization is untenable after Jenné-jeno and related archaeology: by early first millennium CE the zone had sound agriculture with intensification potential and varied iron technology; Dhar Tichitt complexity by second millennium BCE; first-millennium CE pre-Arab stratified societies emerging via resource control partly under localized population pressures; animist ideological support; extensive pre-Arab regional and trans-desert contacts—so savanna “ports” had a vast preexisting trading hinterland. Islamic North African camel caravans later intensified commerce, city/state growth, Islam, and limited literacy (gold, ivory, slaves, spices north; salt, copper alloys, beads, cowries, textiles, manufactures south)—as intensifier, not originator.
- **[[productive-land-hypothesis]]:** Limited more-productive land (Inland Niger Delta recessional rice and other exceptional floodplain niches) under climatic variability creates stress that controllers of better land can convert into power, population concentration, surplus extraction, and pathways to stratification/urbanism. Population pressure on productive “islands” under rainfall fluctuation is one circumstance stimulating social change.
- **Pre-Islamic ideology:** Urban development by early first millennium shows pre-Islamic origins under animist diversity; tumuli, megaliths, and human sacrifice in the wider savanna suggest funerary/ancestor cults that could ideologically reinforce elite authority. Islam influenced later urbanization/state formation but did not found the earliest cities/states.
- **Pre-Arab trade antiquity:** Relay Saharan networks by Roman times (Wilson); Jenné-jeno and Kissi glass beads (some Asian/Roman) and Kissi copper alloys from North Africa indicate first-millennium CE pre-Arab trade; MacDonald and others push Saharan trade origins earlier; salt trade likely as old as food production (~3,000 years). Early iron supply to ore-poor Jenné-jeno, Saharan copper mid-first millennium CE, gold at Jenné-jeno by ~AD 800, and position at dry savanna/Sahel interface with seasonal Niger transport support the McIntosh argument that rapid Arab trade establishment keyed into already-extant indigenous sub-Saharan networks. Gold is rare archaeologically (value) but present early (ninth-century earring; Essouk-Tadmakka coin moulds; Tegdaoust hoard).
- **Regional state-formation trajectory:** Trade in gold and iron plus agricultural surplus already stimulated social/political change giving rise to Ghana before earliest Arab contact; earlier first-millennium CE ranked [[chiefdom]]s (possibly Dhar Tichitt as early as second millennium BCE) becoming stratified; second-millennium culmination in Hausa [[city-state]]s. Elite tumuli (late first–early second millennium; Koi Gourrey; El-Oualedji) with status differences and possible sacrifices parallel al-Bakri’s eleventh-century Ghana royal burial; goods may reflect groups controlling Middle Niger trade related to iron production, Jenné-jeno urban growth, and Ghana empire consolidation (suggested linkage, not proven identity of every mound with Ghana kings).
- **Comparative book role:** With [[aksum|Aksum]], Connah’s paired highland-bridge vs savanna trade-laced cases of early African [[urbanization]] and [[state-formation]] under contrasting geographies—both indigenous trajectories intensified (not solely created) by long-distance exchange and ideology.

## Key Debates

- **[[heterarchy]] vs hierarchy:** Connah reports that Jenné-jeno has not demonstrated stratified sociopolitical hierarchy or centralized authority—unless the city wall is taken to indicate the latter—thus yielding little direct evidence on state-formation origins. R.J. McIntosh (2005) argues not hierarchy but heterarchy—“highly complex society organized horizontally… multiple overlapping and competing agencies of resistance to centralization”—questioning traditional hierarchical urban evolution for Africa and elsewhere. Settlement hierarchy and site-size/artefact-diversity correlations might still suggest the city headed a larger development.
- **Connah’s caution on premature no-stratification conclusions:** He accepts reexamining theoretical assumptions but stresses excavation remains small relative to site sizes; until more extensive excavation at Jenné-jeno and peers, it is premature to assume social stratification and despotic authority were absent—especially given Ghana’s documentary appearance by eighth–ninth centuries CE and the ninth-century gold earring suggesting unequal resource distribution. Dueppen’s Kirikongo work shows social systems can transform over time. Book-level (Ch. 12) judgment: heterarchy is important but depends on lack of evidence for hierarchy rather than positive evidence for heterarchy—positive resolution needs much more extensive urban excavation.
- **External-stimulus (Levtzion) vs indigenous origins:** Documentary bias of post-eighth-century Islamic outsider sources vs archaeological deep time; Connah sides with indigenous origins plus later intensification.
- **Middle Niger vs Middle Senegal:** Uneven vs even subsistence opportunity distributions as explanation for urban concentration vs small-site persistence.
- **Climate determinism rejected:** Climate shapes dynamics but does not alone cause cities/states.
- **Wall as centralized authority?** Single ambiguous architectural indicator in the no-hierarchy argument.
- **Ghana–tumuli–Jenné-jeno linkage:** Suggested correlation of stratification, iron, urban growth, and empire consolidation—not proven one-to-one historical identity.
- **Igbo and other acephalous complexity:** Heterarchy as potential model for other complex non-centralized societies (e.g. Igbo of eastern Nigeria; forward reference including [[igbo-ukwu|Igbo-Ukwu]] regional context)—comparative suggestion, not site identity.
