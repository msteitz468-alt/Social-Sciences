# Ceren

## Discovery and Excavation History

“New World Pompeii”: Loma Caldera eruption buried a Classic village. Payson Sheets’s long-term project. [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]] use it as commoner lifeway evidence rare in elite-centered Maya archaeology.

## Occupation Sequence

*(Attributed — [[coe-houston-the-maya-2015|Coe & Houston 2015]].)*

- Rapid tephra burial preserves houses, tools, stored food, and fields.
- Date c. AD 595 places it in Early Classic / early Late Classic transition for the southeast frontier.
- Shows specialized activity areas at household scale — contrast to palace archaeology of Tikal or Palenque.

## Significance

Primary window on non-elite Classic Maya domestic economy and perishable material culture.

## Key Debates

- How far southeastern commoner patterns generalize to Petén courts.

## Densification from Sharer & Traxler 2006

*(Attributed — [[sharer-traxler-ancient-maya-2006|Sharer & Traxler 2006]]. Complements Coe & Houston synthesis above.)*

### Volcanic burial and community scale

Payson Sheets’s University of Colorado project at Ceren (Zapotitan Valley, western El Salvador): rural community exceptionally preserved by sudden volcanic ash ca. AD 600; ~100 inhabitants apparently escaped. Buildings: wattle-and-daub, pole-and-thatch on low earthen platforms. Estimated dozen+ households around a small plaza with public buildings. **Household 1:** domicile, kitchen, storage, auxiliary structure; **Household 2:** domicile, storage, sweatbath; also a community feasting house. Distinct from the earlier catastrophic **Ilopango** eruption (~1,500+ years ago in older popular accounts; calibrated midpoints vary AD 260 vs AMS average AD 429 as of their discussion), which destroyed everything within 20–30 km and made a larger zone (up to 100 km) uninhabitable for perhaps a century — Ceren is a separate nearby-vent burial during the Classic period (ca. AD 600–750 in their range statement).

### Household economy and production scales

Social economy located in household/community production: inventories include agave fiber, manos/metates, gourds, domestic pottery, censers; imports include obsidian, greenstone axes, polychrome. Three scales of production: household production, household craft surplus (part-time), and workshop full-time specialist production — **Ceren shows the first two (no workshops on-site)**, exchanging for workshop-made exotics. Monetary/media forms in the broader social economy include seashells, greenstone beads, cacao beans, copper bells. Household as basic unit: multi-building compounds; not all house mounds are dwellings (shrines, kitchens, storage); domestic identification via vessels, tools, hearths, burials, middens, manos/metates.

### Agriculture — plant inventory and anti-swidden-only argument

Plant inventory: maize, beans, squash, agave, cacao, chiles, cotton, malanga, manioc — household-garden intensification parallel to Hansen Preclassic plots at Nakbe/[[el-mirador]]. **Tilled maize rows preserved under ash** — direct field evidence. Authors reject older pure-swidden hypothesis that ancient Maya relied on swidden alone (cannot support large dense populations); Classic populations required diverse intensive methods; no direct archaeological proof of ancient swidden at most sites (pollen shows early maize). Cacao present: southern coastal plain as prime cacao production/trade zone; Ceren yielded cacao remains; word *kakaw* is Mayan. Pacific coastal plain rich volcanic soils; some of earliest permanent settlement traces in Mesoamerica along mangrove/lagoon/river settings behind Pacific beaches.
