# Los Millares

Major **enclosed Copper Age settlement** and tomb landscape in southeast Spain (Almería), type site for the Millaran phenomenon. Treated in *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996|Whittle 1996]]* Ch. 9 as a regional aggregation and **communal identity** centre — Whittle cautions against reading copper and enclosure as simple social stratification or military engines (attributed; mid-1990s synthesis).

## Discovery and Excavation History

Classic Iberian prehistoric site with long excavation and reinterpretation history. Whittle is secondary synthesis (mid-1990s radiocarbon/culture-history frame; pre-aDNA).

## Occupation Sequence

*(Descriptive package as of Whittle 1996.)* Promontory at Andarax / Rambla de Huéchar cut by **three wall lines** (outer circular cells, elaborate entrance); innermost walled area; four or five smaller circular structures conventionally labelled “forts”; ~**80 passage graves** outside the triple walls; use into Beaker; Copper Age beginning perhaps ~**3000 BC** in his horizon. SE Spain fauna (Los Millares, Terrera Ventura, Cerro de la Virgen): many sheep/goats, varying cattle/pigs; still ~20–30% game at some regional Copper Age sites; horse not domesticated till Beaker or later (as of his synthesis). Los Millares aquifer/cisterns are **not** treated as proof of an irrigation regime under extreme aridity.

## Significance

### Place and communal identity — not fort/elite (Whittle position)

Often cited in hierarchy/fortification narratives of Iberian Copper Age. Whittle argues military language (**citadel, fortification, bastion, barbican, fort**) is **misleading**; better: a process of **creating place**. Los Millares stands out regionally (Terrera Ventura unenclosed longer; other Millaran walling dispersed, roughly one per region: Cerro de la Virgen, Campos, Tarajal). Outer extensive walling vs inner smaller circular enclosures: triple lines need not be high walls (3–4 m thickness via additions); resemblances to Languedoc garrigue enclosures and (no historical connection claimed) Sarup-type palisaded “bastions” — **demarcations of space, takings of place, statements of communal identity**. Tombs + enclosures as facets of communal identity; formalised approach/entrance; circular huts inside without clear intense permanent occupation; some bastions metal-working. Small “forts”/central enclosures: possible smaller social units, stock corrals, or limited refuges if interpersonal conflict rose — but **poor support for centralised ranked/stratified society in extreme arid environment**. Portuguese parallels (Vila Nova de São Pedro; **Zambujal** plan constantly modified, central enclosure becoming solid masonry monument) reinforce monument-modification rather than endless attack-response (attributed).

### Tombs and “prestige” goods

~**1140** individuals from **58** tombs (2–100 per tomb); Tomb XXI disarticulated long bones/crania grouped; multi-level deposits (e.g. El Barranquete); selection for inclusion; grave goods include copper, beads, flint, decorated pottery, phalange idols; ivory/jet/amber/callaïs/ostrich eggshell in only **17** tombs, copper in **32** — mostly inner-monument concentration. Whittle: “prestigious objects/prestige tombs” ranked-group model is problematic — multi-generation deposition; circulation of goods/remains; copper widely available (Iceman comparison); African ivory/ostrich shell like earlier obsidian (rarity may mean casual not rank-critical); context implausible for establishing rank. Los Millares as **collectivity**, ancestral home, meeting point for **dispersed shifting communities**; creates/bonds wide sense of community. Landscape still space-rich, mobile, fluid groups in the Copper Age third millennium (attributed).

### Copper not pure hierarchy

Metallurgy-as-craft-specialisation + prestige + restricted access driving differentiation is doubted. Copper esteemed practically; valued as material — **colour and production process carrying connotations of fertility and regeneration**; knives/daggers as **icons of identity**/roles/gender/kin transmission — not qualitatively different from other artefacts; difference more circulation/supply (regionalised world) than hierarchical value monopoly (attributed, Ch. 9).

## Key Debates

Chiefdom vs corporate/communal models for Millaran society; metallurgy and social differentiation; aridity/stress vs successful adaptation — interpretive, **open**. Whittle’s mid-1990s communal place-making reading is a **position** against fort-elite orthodoxy, not field consensus.

## Historiography

Iberian Copper Age research; densified via *[[whittle-europe-in-the-neolithic-1996]]* Ch. 9 (and Ch. 10 Mediterranean synthesis). Pre-aDNA demography; absolute dates and irrigation/aridity assumptions have moved since 1996.
