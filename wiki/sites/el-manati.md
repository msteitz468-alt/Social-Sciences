## Discovery and Excavation History

El Manatí, about 10 miles (16 km) southeast of [[sites/san-lorenzo]], was discovered in 1988 during pond digging. Its waterlogged conditions produced exceptional organic preservation. Ponciano Ortiz Ceballos of the University of Veracruz directed the excavations (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Occupation Sequence

The site's ritual deposits span roughly 1600–1200 BCE. Eighteen wooden "baby-faced" figures — limbless torsos about 20 in (50 cm) tall, wrapped in mats, oriented toward the summit of a nearby hill and covered with rock mounds — were recovered; one wooden bust is radiocarbon-dated to about 1200 BCE (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Significance

Alongside the wooden figures, excavators recovered polished stone axes, jade and serpentine beads, a bloodletter in the form of a bird-headed staff fitted with a shark tooth, an obsidian knife with an asphalt handle, and seven rubber balls (3–10 in/8–25 cm) — the earliest surviving pre-Conquest Mesoamerican rubber balls known, confirming that the Mesoamerican ball game "is at least as old as the Olmec civilization" (Coe & Koontz 2013). Jade offerings were present from the earliest occupation levels, which Coe & Koontz interpret, in attributed voice, as "placing complex Olmec ceremonialism earlier than previously thought."

Terry Powis and W. Jeffrey Hurst identified theobromine residue in an Ojochi-phase bowl (c. 1600–1500 BCE) from the site — evidence that the chocolate-drink preparation process was known to the Olmec and their immediate predecessors (Coe & Koontz 2013).

## Key Debates

El Manatí's early jade offerings and ball-game evidence bear on the broader question of how early complex Olmec ritual practice began, feeding the material record underlying [[cultures/olmec-culture]] and the [[debates/olmec-mother-culture-debate]] over the antiquity and originality of Gulf Coast Olmec ceremonialism.
