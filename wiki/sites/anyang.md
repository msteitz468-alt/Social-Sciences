# Anyang (Yinxu)

## Discovery and Excavation History

Anyang — historically **Yin**, the last Shang capital, called **Yin-hsü ("Ruins of the
Yin")** by Ssu-ma Ch'ien in the 2nd century BCE — lies on both banks of the Huan River in
northern Henan. Inscribed oracle bones from the area first reached scholarly attention in
1899. From **1928 to 1937 the Institute of History and Philology (Academia Sinica) conducted
fifteen seasons of scientific excavation** under [[li-chi|Li Chi]], with Liang Ssu-yung, Tung
Tso-pin, and Shih Chang-ju; small-scale digs resumed after 1950. [[chang-kwang-chih|Chang
(1963)]] judged the importance of the Anyang excavations "cannot be exaggerated": their scale
and "scientific precision… still unsurpassed in China" as of writing, and it was Anyang that
"settled, once and for all, the controversial problem of the existence of this dynasty
previously founded exclusively on legendary history" — Ssu-ma Ch'ien's Shang royal genealogy
being "borne out almost completely" by the finds. The excavation was interrupted by the
Sino-Japanese War, leaving many sites only partly dug and not fully published.

Excavation quality is a reliability fact: the 1928–37 campaigns were, for their day, careful
stratigraphic work, but were cut short and incompletely published; Chang noted that by the
early 1960s the "center of Shang studies" had shifted toward [[zhengzhou|Cheng-chou]], where a
longer stratigraphic sequence was emerging.

## Occupation Sequence

The Anyang group spans roughly 15 km² of settlement localities on both banks of the Huan. Each
settlement was largely self-sufficient (dwellings, kilns, foundries, workshops, cemeteries),
but administratively, economically, and ritually the group formed one unit centered on the
Royal House — symbolized by the ceremonial-administrative center at **Hsiao-t'un** and the
**royal cemetery at Hsi-pei-kang**. Chang, following Tung Tso-pin's oracle-bone chronology,
placed P'an-keng's move of the capital here at 1384 BCE (a [DATED], disputed traditional date);
remains predating the move resemble the Erh-li-kang phases of Cheng-chou.

At **Hsiao-t'un**, Shih Chang-ju distinguished three sections built in sequence — Section A
(dwellings, 15 rectangular stamped-earth foundations), Section B (interpreted as royal temples,
21 foundations with burials on a N–S axis), and Section C (a ceremonial quarter) — separated
by a square stamped-earth altar, over a complex underground water-ditch system. No city wall
was found at Hsiao-t'un, though excavation was incomplete.

## Significance

- **The royal cemetery (Hsi-pei-kang).** Eleven large tombs, matching in number the eleven
  monarchs from P'an-keng to Ti-yih; Li Chi estimated earth-digging alone at no fewer than
  7,000 working-days per large grave. Great cross-shaped pit tombs with ramps, wooden chambers,
  a central sacrificial waist-pit, and human and animal sacrifice.
- **The oracle-bone archives.** Anyang is the source of the Shang scapulimancy corpus — the
  earliest securely stratified Chinese writing — that established the historicity of the Shang
  and anchors the reconstruction of its kingship, ritual calendar, and patrilineal succession.
- **Craft and sacrifice.** Bronze foundries (an Anyang square ting reached 137 cm high and
  ~700 kg), bone and stone workshops, and large-scale royal human and animal sacrifice.

## Key Debates

- **Autochthony vs. foreign origin of Shang.** Pre-1950 scholarship sometimes read Anyang's
  "sudden" civilization as intrusive; Chang argued instead for continuity from the Honan
  [[lungshan-culture|Lungshanoid]] Neolithic (see [[chinese-civilization-origins-debate]]).
- **Free farmers vs. slaves.** Whether Shang field labor was performed by free farmers or slaves
  was, Chang noted, an unresolved dispute among Shang specialists; he cautioned against reading
  a Hsiao-t'un cache of 400+ used stone sickles as decisive evidence of centralized/slave labor.

## Liu & Chen (2012) densification — Yinxu as last Shang capital, c. 1250–1046 BC

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu, Li & Xingcan Chen, The Archaeology
of China]], 2012, ch. 10, "The Late Shang Dynasty and Its Neighbors, 1250–1046 BC."
Radiocarbon/oracle-bone-anchored; supersedes Chang's 1963 pre-radiocarbon dating for the
material below. Note: the modern locality of Anyang contains two distinct loci — the
middle-Shang **Huanbei Shang City**, north of the Huan River, abandoned for unknown reasons
before the late-Shang **Yinxu** occupation began south of the river; do not conflate the two.)*

Excavation at Yinxu began in 1928 under [[li-chi|Li Chi]] and the first generation of Chinese
archaeologists; after more than eighty years of subsequent excavation, Liu & Chen note the
site's "full magnitude" remains unknown, and most royal tombs were looted anciently, limiting
direct evidence. Yinxu's core is estimated at roughly 24 km² of densely distributed Shang
remains, with the full ancient city (periphery included) approaching ~30 km². Regional survey
of the Huan River Valley shows marked population growth from middle to late Shang: site count
rose from ~20 to ~30, and the major center grew from ~500 ha to ~3,000 ha (Tang, J. and Jing
1998, 2009).

**Xiaotun and Xibeigang.** A palatial complex of ~70 ha near Xiaotun contained 53 rammed-earth
foundations concentrated in 3.5 ha — some royal residences, others royal ancestral temples
associated with sacrificial pits containing humans and animals. The royal cemetery at
Xibeigang, north of the Huan River, held royal burials plus numerous human and animal
sacrificial pits. Yinxu's population was organized into a few dozen small residential clusters
(*yi*, "district"), each with houses, storage pits, wells, roads, drainage, and water storage;
mortuary and oracle-bone evidence indicates society at Yinxu was organized by lineage groups
(Tang, J. 2004; Zhu, F. 2004). Mortuary data show at least six distinct social classes,
indicating a highly stratified society.

**Fuhao tomb.** The tomb of Fuhao, a royal consort of King Wuding, is the best-preserved
intact elite burial: 1,928 grave goods including 468 bronze objects (1,625 kg) and nearly
7,000 cowry shells — yet at 22 m² only medium-sized compared to the eleven royal tombs at
Xibeigang (107–192 m²). Tomb M56 at Huayuanzhuang, belonging to a Shang military-elite member,
held more than 1,600 grave goods, predominantly bronzes and jades.

**Craft production.** Yinxu was a major production center: three bone workshops (the Tiesanlu
workshop alone yielded 32 tons of animal bone), jade/stone grinding facilities within the
palatial complex, and six bronze foundry sites (not all contemporaneous). Liu & Chen (citing
Chinese archaeologists) argue the Miaopubeidi and early Xiaomintun foundries, sited close to
the palatial complex, were under direct royal-court control, while later, larger Xiaomintun
foundries sited farther away may signal "gradual decentralization of bronze production at
Yinxu" over the course of the Yinxu period (attributed). Lead-isotope analysis of 178 Yinxu
bronzes shows high-radiogenic-lead content declining from 78–81% in Phases I–II to 38% in
Phase III and near-disappearance by Phase IV, with likely provenance near Jinshachang,
northeastern Yunnan "(as of Jin, Z. et al. 1998)."

**Writing and dynastic anchor.** Oracle-bone and bronze inscriptions at Yinxu mark, in Liu &
Chen's account, "the inception of written historical records in China," generally supporting
the traditional dynastic genealogy of the late Shang and, in their assessment, "confirm[ing] the
late Shang as a distinct era of dynastic history" — an evidential relation between a textual/
archival evidence class and the transmitted king-list, recorded here as attributed rather than
adjudicated fact.

**Territorial control, reassessed.** Liu & Chen argue late-Shang territorial control was "much
more restricted" than a modern territorial-state model implies, endorsing Keightley's
description of the Shang state as "gruyere, filled with non-Shang holes, rather than tou-fu,
solidly Shang throughout." **[POSITION]** Scholars dispute the scale of Shang political control:
Trigger (1999) argues for a large territorial state; Keightley (1999), Lin Yun (1998b), and
Yates (1997) argue for a small city-state/segmentary state; Song Xinchao (1991) and Li Boqian
(2009) infer large territories from ceramic/bronze-style distribution; Wheatley (1971) restricts
"Shang territory" to the small core area around the middle Yellow River. Liu & Chen further
note the number of named polities in the Shang oracle-bone textual record exceeds the number of
distinguishable archaeological cultures in the same region — a discrepancy they read as
evidence that a single archaeological-culture assemblage can mask multiple, decentralized
political groups (a caution against equating material culture with polity).

## Shelach-Lavi (2015) densification

*(Attributed — [[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi 2015]]. Shelach-Lavi
uses "Yinxu" for the Late Shang capital locus and "Anyang" for the modern city/area under which
it lies; both names are treated here as the same locus, following his usage.)*

Oracle-bone inscriptions were first traced to the village of Xiaotun, near Anyang, around the
turn of the twentieth century; large-scale scientific excavation of Yinxu began in 1928 under
[[li-chi|Li Ji]] (National Research Institute of History and Philology), running fifteen field
seasons until halted in 1937 by the Japanese invasion, then resuming after the founding of the
PRC under the Institute of Archaeology. In Shelach-Lavi's adopted Shang chronology, Yinxu and
its oracle bones typify the Late Shang phase (ca. 1300–1050 BCE); Huanbei, the Middle Shang
center, was abandoned ca. 1300 BCE, after which the center shifted to Xiaotun/Yinxu — an
unwalled settlement covering an estimated 3,000 ha (30 km²) (Zhongguo Shehui 2003).

The **Xibeigang royal cemetery** contains eight large cross-shaped tombs (identified as Shang
kings' graves), 107–192 m² in size and up to 12 m deep; grave M1001's main chamber is 19×16 m,
10.5 m deep, and 66 m long including its ramps, and contained the remains of roughly 150 human
victims along with twelve horses and eleven dogs (Chang 1980; Bagley 1999). More than a
thousand small earthen sacrificial pits are arranged in orderly rows in the cemetery's eastern
section, each holding up to twelve human skeletons, many decapitated. Twenty-five "chariot
pits" — chariots, horses, human drivers, and equipment — have also been found at Yinxu.

The **Fuhao tomb** at Xiaotun/Yinxu, identified by inscriptions as belonging to Fuhao, consort
of Shang king Wuding, is 22 m² and 7.5 m deep — a tenth the size of the largest royal tombs but
the richest and largest intact Shang grave known. Shelach-Lavi records roughly 1,600 burial
goods, sixteen human victims, a sacrificed dog, ~6,820–7,000 cowrie shells (figures vary by
source: Zhongguo 1980 gives 6,820), 195 bronze ritual vessels and 271 other bronze items
(combined weight ~1,600 kg), 755 jade artifacts, and 564 carved bone artifacts — figures that
differ somewhat from Liu & Chen's (2012) count of 1,928 grave goods and 468 bronze objects
above; both counts are attributed to their respective sources rather than reconciled. Of 95
tested bronze vessels/tools/weapons from the tomb, most contained roughly 15% tin, some over
20% (Zhongguo 1980).

Yinxu is the source of 200,000+ oracle-bone fragments. Craft production included six bronze
workshops (the largest, near Xiaomintun, covering more than 5 ha across three subdivided
workshops) and the Tiesanlu bone workshop, which yielded 34,000 kg of worked animal bone from
only a small excavated portion of its area (Campbell et al. 2011). The Si Mu Wu Fangding, found
at Yinxu, weighs 875 kg — described by Shelach-Lavi as the largest bronze vessel cast anywhere
in the ancient world.

Chariots appear fully formed in Yinxu sacrificial pits dated to ca. 1250 BCE, with no local
antecedent stages; the earliest known chariots elsewhere come from the Sintashta-Petrovka
culture (modern Kazakhstan), dated ca. 2000–1800 BCE, and it is generally agreed (Anthony and
Vinogradov 1995) that the Yinxu chariots reflect transmission of chariot technology from
Central Asia given the absence of local antecedents.

On the territorial-extent debate, Shelach-Lavi argues against a bordered "territorial state"
model for the Shang, favoring a concentric-zones/network-of-towns model (Chang 1980; Keightley
1978–80), and explicitly disagrees with Liu and Chen's (2003) reading of the Late Shang
political system (as reflected in the oracle bones) as the weakened remnant of a more
centralized Early Shang system — he argues the available archaeological evidence does not
support a "weaker Late Shang" reading.

## Chang — *Shang Civilization* (1980) densification

*(Attributed — [[chang-shang-civilization-1980|Chang, *Shang Civilization*]], 1980, Ch.1 and
passim. Social reconstruction from pre-war IHP reports, post-1949 PRC publications, and oracle
bones; absolute dates as of 1980, partly superseded.)*

**Capital structure.** Chang reconstructs Yin as a **royal capital whose core is the An-yang
cluster** of localities on both banks of the Huan — Hsiao-t'un ceremonial-administrative center,
Hsi-pei-kang royal cemetery, craft villages, and dependent hamlets — not a single continuous
walled city. He argues the effective capital catchment may extend tens to ~200 km to sites such as
Ch'ao-ko, Hui Hsien, and Hsing-t'ai (attributed network model). Whether the oracle-bone toponym
*Ta yi Shang* equates with An-yang remains disputed in his account.

**Hsiao-t'un.** Predynastic occupation with pit dwellings, water-ditch drainage, Period I pottery,
and uninscribed oracle bones, comparable in art/technology level to late Cheng-chou. Dynastic
hang-t'u (rammed-earth) house foundations in three groups (Shih Chang-ju's reconstruction), with
foundation and dedication sacrifices. **Fu Hao (Tomb 5):** intact elite burial of a royal consort
active in Wu Ting–period warfare and ritual (named on bronzes and bones); rich bronze, jade, and
cowrie inventory — Chang's type case for high-ranking women in the royal apparatus.

**Hsi-pei-kang.** Large cross-shaped and ramped royal tombs (M 1001 etc.), repeatedly robbed;
floor sacrifices (humans, dogs, weapons) under wooden chambers; second-level platforms for goods
and victims. 1934–35 seasons alone: 10 large tombs + 1,221 small graves (many skull-only or
body-only pits in parallel rows) — Chang's primary quantitative window on sacrificial classes
beside the principal burials.

**Oracle-bone loci.** Multiple findspots beyond a single "archive"; pit H-127 as a major storage
dump; Tung Tso-pin's five-period *chen-jen* chronology as the relative time spine of late Shang
kingship (Period I vs IV assignments contested). See [[oracle-bone-inscription]],
[[scapulimancy]].

**Urban thesis (attributed).** An-yang is a multi-nodal **urban network** of self-sufficient
settlement nodules integrated by royal ritual, craft specialization, and lineage cemeteries — a
model later writers (Keightley; [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu & Chen]];
[[shelach-lavi-archaeology-early-china-2015|Shelach-Lavi]]) rework rather than discard.
