# Saqsawaman (Sacsahuamán)

Ceremonial-fortress complex on the rocky promontory above [[cuzco]], described by [[daltroy-terence-n|D'Altroy]] (as of [[daltroy-the-incas-2002|D'Altroy 2002]]) as the grandest architectural complex in the [[inca-empire]]—multi-function religious architecture, fortress, magazine, and ceremonial landscape rather than a simple citadel.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Spanish eyewitnesses (Pedro Sancho, Cieza de León) described towers, chambers, zig-zag walls of enormous fitted stones, and storehouses before much was dismantled for colonial Cuzco building stone—Cieza already saw a partially stripped complex. Nineteenth- and early twentieth-century travelers and Cuzco excavators treated it as a monumental focus of Inca archaeology (alongside the public impact of [[machu-picchu]] after 1912). Valcárcel’s 1930s work recovered substantial pre-imperial Killke pottery; Rowe used the Killke fraction (about one-quarter to one-third of pottery) to argue pre-imperial hilltop occupation. Recent excavations have confirmed sumptuary goods (tupu pins, knives, metals, polychrome pottery). Masonry technique studies (Protzen; Lee; Gasparini and Margolies, as of D'Altroy 2002) use Saqsawaman’s irregular “marvel” joints as a case of upper-course-first trimming and lower-course shaping by pounding with hammerstones, ropes, and scaffolds—not abrasion primary, and not exotic technology.

## Occupation Sequence

**Pre-imperial.** Most known Killke basin sites sit on slight rises; only Saqsawaman and Old Chogo were defensibly situated (Dwyer 1971, as of D'Altroy 2002). Ethnohistory places early settlement on swampy ground at the foot of the hill (Betanzos); Killke pottery under Cuzco and at Saqsawaman documents pre-imperial use before the imperial building program, 1536 warfare, the 1650 earthquake, and Spanish remodeling destroyed much surface architecture in the basin.

**Imperial complex.** Tradition attributes inauguration to Pachakuti or Thupa Inka Yupanki, sometimes as the “head” of a puma-shaped city—Sarmiento’s more literal layout (body between the rivers, Saqsawaman as head) vs. Betanzos’s metaphorical “lion’s body” with ruler as head; scholars debate allegory vs. plan (Hyslop 1990, as of D'Altroy 2002). Cieza: Pachakuti began, successors continued; 20,000 provincial workers on rotation (4,000 quarry, 6,000 haul, others foundations). Three zig-zag cyclopean walls face the less steep approach; Sancho described a multi-story round tower, many chambers, capacity for 5,000 Spaniards, and magazines of arms, cloth, and metals. Cieza: founded as a Sun temple but actually religious architecture, fortress, magazine, and ceremonial complex combined. North across a plaza, Suchuna (“slide”) holds aqueducts, cisterns, terraces, and carved features including the “Throne of the Inca” and “Tired Stone” (Collaconcho), part of Cuzco’s carved-stone/*wak'a* corpus.

**Colonial war.** Few truly fortified heartland sites exist; Saqsawaman and [[ollantaytambo]] may have been only partly designed as fortifications but served that purpose in colonial wars. In the 1536 siege of Cuzco, Spaniards reduced pressure by capturing the Saqsawaman citadel (Juan Pizarro died in the assault); night-of-new-moon ceremonial pauses on campaign were among practices Spaniards could exploit (as of D'Altroy 2002).

## Significance

Saqsawaman crowns the capital’s northern rim as the supreme statement of Inca monumental labor and multi-purpose sacred-military space, inseparable from [[cuzco]]’s plan, *wak'a* landscape, and *mit'a* logistics. It is a primary comparative foil for unfortified provincial centers ([[huanuco-pampa]], [[cajamarca]]) and for estate walls at [[machu-picchu]] read as non-military.

## Key Debates

- **Temple vs. fortress vs. magazine.** Cieza’s multi-function reading vs. popular “fortress only” label.
- **Puma-head layout.** Literal urban zoomorph vs. metaphor for social body and sovereignty.
- **Pre-imperial occupation intensity.** Rowe’s Killke-hilltop argument vs. limits of early excavation samples.
- **Design intent of fortification.** Primary military architecture vs. ceremonial complex secondarily defensible (with 1536 use as evidence of capability, not sole original purpose).
- **Stereotomy.** Experimental pounding/scribe-and-cope models vs. folkloric “impossible” masonry explanations.
