# Easter Island (Rapa Nui)

Easter Island (Rapa Nui) is the remote southeast-Pacific island whose monumental statues (*moai*) and documented total [[deforestation]] have made it a much-cited — and much-contested — case of anthropogenic environmental change and possible societal collapse. [[roberts-neil|Roberts]] ([[roberts-holocene-1998|1998]]) uses it as a compact illustration of forest destruction driven by a monument-building society on a fragile island ecosystem.

## Discovery and Excavation History

Studied archaeologically by Katherine Routledge (1914–15) and the Heyerdahl expedition (1955–56), the island's environmental history was reconstructed by John Flenley and colleagues from [[pollen-analysis|pollen]] cores taken from three crater lakes (Rano Raraku, Rano Kau, Rano Aroi) in the late 1970s–1980s (Flenley and King 1984; Flenley et al. 1991).

## Occupation Sequence

The pollen record shows (attributed) that at the time of Polynesian settlement — roughly 1500 years ago in Roberts' rendering, though the settlement date is now disputed and often placed several centuries later — the island was covered in palm forest. Forest was cleared progressively for statue-transport timber, canoes, dwellings, and fuel; complete deforestation, documented by pollen, coincided with the peak of monument construction (dated by Roberts to c. 850–300 Cal. yr BP / AD 1200–1650), when population may have reached ~7000. The first Europeans (AD 1722) found a barren, treeless island with many statues toppled.

## Significance

Rapa Nui is a canonical case in the study of human-induced [[deforestation]], island ecological vulnerability, and resource-driven societal stress — frequently invoked (including by Roberts) as a cautionary microcosm of larger human–environment dynamics.

## Key Debates

The "ecocide" narrative — self-inflicted collapse through deforestation — is contested. Later research has emphasized the roles of introduced Pacific rats in suppressing palm regeneration, European contact, slave raiding, and introduced disease in population decline, and has questioned both the settlement chronology and the scale of pre-contact collapse. Roberts (1998) presents the deforestation record as secure while treating the causal statue-building link in attributed voice; the identity of the [[austronesian-expansion|Polynesian]] settlers (against Thor Heyerdahl's rejected South American hypothesis) is corroborated by mtDNA (Hagelberg et al. 1994) — a genetic signal that converges with, but is not identical to, the linguistic/cultural Polynesian category.

## Historiography

Because the island is small, remote, and richly instrumented (crater-lake cores, dense monuments), it has served as a "model system" for environmental archaeology — which also makes it prone to over-generalization; the shift from Flenley's forest-loss record to the contested collapse narrative is itself a case study in how environmental data are read into social history.
