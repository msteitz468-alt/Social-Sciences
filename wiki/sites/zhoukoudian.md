## Discovery and Excavation History

Zhoukoudian Locality 1 ("Dragon Bone Hill"), near Beijing, was excavated in two main
campaigns: 1921–1937 (Johan Gunnar Andersson, Otto Zdansky, Davidson Black, Pei
Wenzhong, and Franz Weidenreich) and 1949–1966. The first campaign recovered fossils
attributed at the time to "Sinanthropus pekinensis," now classified as [[homo-erectus]],
representing more than 40 individuals. The original fossils were lost in transit during
World War II: they disappeared in 1941 while in the custody of U.S. Marines who were
interned by the Japanese (Klein, [[klein-human-career-1999]]). Weidenreich's detailed
plaster casts and measurements, made before the loss, survive at the American Museum of
Natural History and remain the primary basis for study of the original material — an
excavation-history fact that materially affects the reliability of every subsequent
claim about the Locality 1 fossils, since scholars work from casts and descriptions
rather than the bones themselves.

## Occupation Sequence

Dating of the Locality 1 human fossils is provisional. [[paleomagnetic-dating]] placed
the Brunhes/Matuyama polarity boundary (conventionally ~780 ka) below the earliest H.
erectus fossils and artifacts at the site, and correlation of the deposits' climatic
stratigraphy with marine oxygen-isotope stages implies the sequence spans roughly
500–230 ka (as of Klein 1999). Uranium-series, thermoluminescence, and electron-spin-
resonance dates support a depositional span of about 200–300 thousand years beginning
around 550–500 ka. All of these figures should be read as convergent estimates rather
than a single precise chronology; Klein treats the dating as "provisional."

## Significance

Zhoukoudian anchors the East Asian record of [[homo-erectus]] and, via Weidenreich and
von Koenigswald's 1939 judgment that the Javan (Trinil) and Chinese ("Sinanthropus")
fossils were conspecific, contributed directly to the taxonomic decision by Ernst Mayr
(1950) and W. E. Le Gros Clark (1955, 1964) to sink both into a single species, Homo
erectus. The site also provides among the oldest widely accepted archaeological
evidence for [[control-of-fire]], via ash and hearth deposits — though Klein cautions
this claim rests partly on a preservation bias, since open-air sites of comparable age
are less likely to preserve unambiguous hearths than cave sites (Klein 1999).

## Key Debates

Whether the site's abundant deer and other animal bones reflect H. erectus hunting or
scavenging prowess, versus accumulation by hyenas (whose bones, coprolites, and
tooth-damaged prey bones are also present at the site), is unresolved. Klein explicitly
rejects the older "proficient deer stalkers" interpretation of Zhoukoudian as
unsupported by the current evidence, concluding the site "cannot unambiguously
illuminate the predatory or scavenging prowess of H. erectus" pending fresh excavation
of a large bone sample (Klein 1999).

Separately, claims of morphological continuity between the Zhoukoudian H. erectus
sample and living East Asian populations (e.g., shovel-shaped incisors) form part of
the wider [[modern-human-origins-debate]] over regional continuity versus replacement
models of modern human origins; this wiki records that dispute on the debate page
rather than adjudicating it here.

## Chang — Sinanthropus and the Upper Cave (1963, attributed)

[[chang-kwang-chih|K. C. Chang]] (*[[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963|The Archaeology of
Ancient China]]*, 1963) synthesized the earlier Choukoutien reports as of ~1960. He described
**Sinanthropus (Peking Man)**, known skeletally only from Locality 1, as a toolmaker (a pebble
chopper-chopping-tool tradition and a flake tradition) and fire-user with a small mean cranial
capacity (~1075 cc) but near-modern limb bones. He recorded Weidenreich's claim of twelve
morphological traits (including **shovel-shaped incisors**) allegedly transmitted to modern
"Mongoloid" inhabitants of the region — while noting "few human palaeontologists consider this
evidence as being conclusive," a caution linking the site to the wider
[[modern-human-origins-debate]] over regional continuity vs. replacement.

Chang also reported the **Upper Cave** (latest Pleistocene / earliest Holocene): warm-climate
fauna, an industry of stone tools plus abundant bone/antler artifacts, perforated teeth and marine
shells (read as long-distance trade or migration), and ~7 individuals whose skullcaps all showed
depressions or fractures — over which he recorded three competing, unadjudicated explanations
(Weidenreich: violent attack; W. C. Pei: rockfall; Chang: epidemic death and kin burial). He noted
Weidenreich had classed three Upper Cave skulls as three different "racial elements" ("primitive
Mongoloid, Melanesoid, and Eskimoid"), against a later reanalysis (Woo/Wu 1961) reading them as
"altogether Mongoloid" — a position dispute Chang left open.

**Caution:** the racial-type vocabulary ("Mongoloid," "Melanesoid," "Negroid") is 1920s–1960s
physical-anthropology idiom carried from Chang's sources; it is recorded here as attributed, dated
terminology, not wiki-voice fact, and does not license any equation of a skeletal "type" with a
language or a self-conscious people. Chang's absolute dating throughout is pre-radiocarbon glacial
correlation. Source: [[chang-archaeology-ancient-china-1963]].

## Liu & Chen (2012) — discovery, nationalist reception, and the fire-control challenge

*(Attributed — [[liu-chen-archaeology-of-china-2012|Liu, Li & Xingcan Chen, The Archaeology
of China]], 2012, ch. 1, "Chinese Archaeology: Past, Present, and Future.")*

Zhoukoudian ("Dragon Bone Hill"), a limestone-hill site in Fangshan County ~48 km southwest
of Beijing, was first noted as a fossil locality ("dragon bones," *longgu*) in 1918;
large-scale excavation began in 1927 under the Geological Survey of China. A hominid lower
molar found that year was named *Sinanthropus pekinensis* ("Peking Man," now classified
[[homo-erectus]]) by Davidson Black; in 1929 Pei Wenzhong found the first complete Peking
Man skullcap. Excavation removed 500,000+ tons of deposit before being interrupted by World
War II in 1937; post-1950s resumed excavation has yielded hominid fossils from more than 40
individuals dated 550,000–250,000 years ago, 100,000+ stone artifacts, and abundant
mammalian fossils.

**Weidenreich's multiregional claim.** J. F. Weidenreich (1943), based on twelve
morphological features shared between Peking Man fossils and modern East Asian populations,
concluded some Peking Man genetic traits were transmitted to modern Mongoloid populations —
a claim Liu & Chen describe as controversial but later adopted by many Chinese archaeologists
in support of multiregional human-evolution theory. This claim is recorded here as attributed,
dated racial-typological vocabulary (see the parallel Chang-1963 note above), not as a settled
finding; it feeds the wider [[modern-human-origins-debate]].

**The Binford–Ho fire-control challenge and its nationalist reception.** Lewis Binford and
Chuan Kun Ho (1985) challenged the established view that Peking Man controlled fire and that
the Zhoukoudian cave was Peking Man's habitual "home." Liu & Chen report this drew a strong
defensive reaction from Chinese archaeologists, notably excavator Jia Lanpo (1991); they
attribute the strength of that reaction to rising nationalist sentiment that framed Peking Man
as a direct ancestor of the nation within the multiregional evolutionary model — an explicit
reflexivity point: how nationalist stakes shaped scientific reception of a methodological
challenge, not merely its substance.
