This is the standard regional period system used for Central Andean prehistory, recorded here on its first use in this wiki (per the wiki's convention of logging regional period systems in `timelines/` at first use). Periods in this scheme correlate, per Scarre ed. 2013 synthesis, with intervals of relatively wetter coastal conditions and local prosperity, while "Horizons" correlate with drier intervals and the spread of sierra (highland) cultural influence — an attributed pattern, not an independently verified causal claim; environmental correlations should be read as `concurrent_with`/`contributed_to` rather than sole `caused_by` per the Evidence-Class Framework.

**Two source frameworks are recorded below.** The Scarre handbook table remains the wiki’s quick-reference spine. [[moseley-incas-and-their-ancestors-2001|Moseley 2001]] uses the same named periods with some date and subdivision differences (attributed notes). Where they diverge, **do not silently average** — keep both attributions.

Calibration status of radiocarbon dates is not consistently specified in either secondary source and should be treated as unstated pending confirmation (Chronological Uncertainty Protocol). Moseley sometimes distinguishes radiocarbon vs calendar years (e.g. Monte Verde).

## Moseley 2001 period notes (attributed; do not replace Scarre table)

### Lithic vs Preceramic subdivision

Moseley subdivides the long prepottery era using an environmental turning point:

- **Lithic Period:** coincides with Ice Age retreat dynamics; ends around **5,000 years ago** when post-Pleistocene sea levels stabilized and climatic/environmental conditions assumed modern configuration (Ch. 4 colonization).
- **Preceramic (sometimes Late Archaic):** begins with abatement of glacial meltback and relative sea-level stabilization as marine/meteorological currents enter contemporary regimes **~3000 BC**. Better site survival; "preceramic foundations of civilization."

By **2000 BC**, some Preceramic populations erected the continent’s largest architectural monuments of the time under religious guise, with scant evidence of hereditary *kurakas*.

### Farming and ceramic spread (Moseley)

- Colombia/Ecuador tropics: agriculture well before 3000 BC.
- Arid Cordillera lag: farming takes hold N–central Peru **~1800 BC** after a dusty/dry episode; Titicaca agropastoralism after **~1600 BC** rainfall increase; further lag into hyper-arid Atacama.
- **Initial Period** (north): intensive farming + ceramics; in Titicaca region the same era is often called the **Formative Period**.
- Southward expansion of intensive agriculture correlates with climatic amelioration (N. Peru by 1800 BC; Titicaca after 1600 BC). Maize and llama rare/absent in most early coast settings; root crops primary staples.

### Initial Period peak and ~900 BC crisis

- Great age of coastal civic-ceremonial architecture (La Leche through Pisco); Sechín Alto underway by **1400 BC**.
- Beginning **~900 BC**, virtually all coastal centers abandoned within a century or two; coincides with several hundred years of severe drought in Lake Titicaca sediment cores — "agrarian bubble burst."
- After rainfall returned, littoral populations lost much confidence in temple-mound construction.

### Early Horizon

- Associated with [[chavin-de-huantar]] founded by **~800 BC** (Burger phases).
- Horizon **technically begins** when Chavín artistic influence appears in Ica ceramic arts **~400 BC**, coincident with another protracted drought (**~400–200 BC**).
- Janabarriu Phase 400–200 BC: cult spread, textile and metallurgical revolutions, class formation (Kuntur Wasi, Paracas necropolis).
- Southern sphere: Yaya-Mama, Pukara, [[paracas-culture]] as counter-pole to northern Chavín sphere.

### Early Intermediate Period

- Gauged by **Ica Valley ceramic sequence**: about **200 BC–AD 600** (Moseley).
- Also called "Mastercraftsmen Period" / "Regional Developmental Period"; kuraka class, wealth finance, residential communities outnumber ceremonial centers; fortifications common.
- North coast: Salinar → [[gallinazo-culture]] → [[moche-culture]] (Larco); Moche polity upheaval shortly before AD 600 (next chapter).
- South coast: Paracas → Nazca polychrome slips ~200 BC; Nazca Phase 8 end estimate AD 600 but some ¹⁴C to AD 755+.
- Drought episodes: Christ–~AD 200; Nazca Phase 5 response to drought beginning **AD 560**; Tiwanaku Phase 3–4 expansion after AD 200 rainfall recovery.

### Later periods (brief, as framed in Moseley EIP close)

- Tiwanaku five-phase occupation from ~400 BC; Classic Phase 4 c. AD 375–600/700; decline with protracted drought ~AD 1100.
- While Moche and Nazca fall and Huari (Wari) arises, Tiwanaku’s political longevity exceeds other ancient Andean empires (Moseley).
- Inca Late Horizon and Spanish conquest framed in Ch. 1–2 (see [[inca-empire]]); Andean cyclical time vs Spanish lineal history affects historical reconstruction (Moseley).

## Notes on Correlated Pattern

Scarre’s synthesis: "Periods" tend to correlate with coastal prosperity/wetter conditions; "Horizons" with drier intervals and sierra influence. Moseley repeatedly ties drought (Titicaca cores; AD 560 Nazca; first-centuries-AD Gallinazo/sierra encroachment; 400–200 BC Chavín spread) to settlement, political, and religious change — always treat climate as contributor/concurrent constraint, not sole cause. Maritime-Oasis vs Arid Montane poles (Moseley) organize north-coast vs south-highland demographic cores across periods.
