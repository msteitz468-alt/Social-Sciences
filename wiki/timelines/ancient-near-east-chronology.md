# Ancient Near East Chronology (ca. 3000–323 BCE)

A working chronology of the ancient Near East from the first cities to Alexander,
after [[van-de-mieroop-history-ancient-near-east-2016|Van De Mieroop 2016]] (with
[[roux-ancient-iraq-1992|Roux 1992]] and [[postgate-early-mesopotamia-1994|Postgate 1994]]
for detail). **All dates BCE, on the conventional Middle Chronology unless noted** — see
the chronology problem below; treat every second- and third-millennium date as
approximate and convention-dependent, not exact.

## The Chronology Problem (Van De Mieroop 2016, attributed)

Absolute dates for Near Eastern history are, in Van De Mieroop's words, "a vexing and
controversial problem," and the precise-looking numbers "give a false impression of
certainty." The Mesopotamians documented sequences of rulers well, but fixing that
relative sequence to an absolute point is the difficulty.

- **First-millennium chronology is secure**, anchored by Ptolemy of Alexandria's king-list
  (back to 747 BCE) and a **solar eclipse of 15 June 763 BCE** recorded in the Assyrian
  eponym (*limmu*) list, which anchors a long sequence of Assyrian dates.
- **Second-millennium and earlier chronology is not.** Scholars reconstruct a relative
  sequence from Assyrian and Babylonian king-lists but cannot anchor it with confidence.
  A single omen text — the **Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa** (a 7th-century copy of planetary
  observations from that king's reign) — fits several early-second-millennium moments,
  yielding rival systems. For Hammurabi of Babylon:

| Chronology | Hammurabi's reign | Fall of Babylon (First Dynasty) |
|---|---|---|
| Long | 1848–1806 | 1651 |
| **Middle (convention)** | **1792–1750** | **1595** |
| Short | 1728–1686 | 1531 |
| Ultra-short (Gasche et al. 1998) | — | 1499 |

Most scholars adopted the **Middle Chronology** in the 1950s; later work (the Venus
tablet's reliability questioned; an ultra-short case built on a "too-long Dark Age";
Anatolian tree-rings, eclipses, omens) has not produced certainty, so the Middle
Chronology remains "too convenient to discard" (Roaf 2012, cited by Van De Mieroop, whose
Assyrian/Babylonian dates follow Pruzsinszky's list). This page follows the same
convention for cross-wiki consistency.

## Regional Period Systems

- **Mesopotamian political periodization** (used above): Uruk → Jemdet Nasr → Early
  Dynastic (I–III) → Akkad → Ur III → Old Babylonian → Kassite/Middle Assyrian → Neo-
  Assyrian → Neo-Babylonian → Achaemenid. Boundaries are conventional and, before ~911
  BCE, chronology-dependent.
- **Prehistoric ceramic sequence** (southern Mesopotamia): [[hassuna-culture|Hassuna]] →
  [[samarra-culture|Samarra]] → [[halaf-culture|Halaf]] → [[ubaid-culture|Ubaid]] →
  [[uruk-culture|Uruk]] (see those pages; dates pre-calibration in the older sources).

## Notes on Method

The "ancient Near East" is itself a modern construct with conventional geographic and
temporal bounds (Van De Mieroop opens his survey by questioning them). Dates here serve
sequence and comparison, not precision: a bare year drawn from a chronology-dependent
range is a false certainty. Where a page's argument turns on absolute dating, state the
chronology and dating method (see [[radiocarbon-dating]] for prehistoric anchors and the
Chronological Uncertainty Protocol).
